Chapter 10

Self-Hosting Math: When OSS + GPUs Beat APIs

The 2026 break-even analysis for renting GPUs and running open-source models instead of calling an API.

The siren song of self-hosting

Every few months, a developer looks at the API invoice, squints at an open-weights model on Hugging Face, and thinks:

“Wait. The model is free. GPUs are, like, $2/hr. Why am I paying Anthropic $3,000 a month?”

It’s a reasonable thought. The numbers look seductive. An H100 at $2.99/hour sounds cheaper than a bill with five figures on it.[1]

Owning a car feels cheaper than Uber until you add up insurance, gas, parking, and the Saturday you spent at the mechanic. Self-hosting LLMs follows the same script.

This chapter does the math honestly. By the end, you’ll know — for your workload — whether self-hosting wins, loses, or ties in 2026.

The 2026 plot twist: API prices have fallen so fast — DeepSeek V3.2 is now $0.28/M in, $0.42/M out[2] — that self-hosting rarely wins on pure cost anymore. When it wins, it’s usually because of non-cost drivers: data residency, latency, guaranteed throughput, or custom fine-tunes.

The four drivers of the self-hosting decision

Before touching a spreadsheet, figure out which of these four things is actually pushing you toward self-hosting. The answer changes everything downstream.

🔒 Privacy

PII, medical records, legal docs that can’t leave your VPC. Often non-negotiable. Cost isn’t the point.

⚡ Latency

On-prem or edge deployment for sub-50ms round trips. APIs add 100–300ms of round trip + queue time.

💰 Cost

The one this chapter focuses on. Winnable only at very high volume in 2026.

🎛️ Control

Custom fine-tunes, guaranteed throughput, specific sampling tricks the APIs don’t expose.

If your driver is privacy or control, the math almost doesn’t matter — you’re going to self-host and you’re just deciding how. If your driver is cost, the rest of this chapter will either convince you or save you a quarter of wasted work.

Note the spread. An H100 costs anywhere from $1.99 to $3.99 depending on provider and commitment. A B200 ranges nearly 5x. This matters a lot for break-even math.

Shop around, seriously. The hyperscalers (AWS, GCP, Azure) are usually 2–3x more expensive than specialist clouds (Lambda, RunPod, CoreWeave, Crusoe, Vast.ai) for identical hardware. If your driver is cost, starting with AWS H100s will make self-hosting look like a bad idea when it’s actually the provider that’s the problem.

The OSS models worth deploying in 2026

You don’t self-host just any model — you pick one that matches both your task and the GPUs you can afford. Here’s the April 2026 shortlist.

Notice a pattern: every model worth deploying in 2026 is a mixture-of-experts. That’s not a coincidence.

MoE is the quiet cost revolution

Mixture-of-experts matters because you pay compute for the active parameters, not the total. DeepSeek V3.2 has 685B total params but only activates 37B per token.[7] That’s an ~95% compute reduction versus a dense model of the same total size.

Translation: an MoE model that would embarrass a dense 200B model costs roughly the same to run as a dense 37B model. That’s how OSS caught up to frontier APIs in a year.

MoE architecture → ~85–95% compute reduction vs a dense model of equal total size. It’s the single biggest reason OSS self-hosting is even in the conversation in 2026.

It also means VRAM is still the bottleneck, not compute. You need to fit all 685B params in GPU memory even if you only activate 37B at a time, because which 37B you activate changes token by token.

The break-even calculation

Here’s the honest math. To self-host economically you need:

Cost per 1M tokens, self-hosted
Hourly GPU cost$X/hr × N GPUs
Tokens per hourthroughput × 3600s × utilization
Ops multiplier× 1.3 to 1.6 (engineer time, failover, monitoring)
Cost / 1M tokens= (GPU $/hr × ops × 1,000,000) / tokens-per-hour

Then compare that number to the API price per 1M tokens. If your self-hosted cost is lower and you’re confident you’ll hit the assumed utilization, self-host. Otherwise, don’t.

Worked example: serving Llama 4 Scout on 1× H200

Let’s price out a realistic deployment.

Llama 4 Scout on a single H200 — monthly
H200 (reserved, Jarvislabs-ish)$3.80/hr
Hours / month× 730
Base GPU cost= $2,774
Ops overhead (engineering, failover, Grafana, on-call)× 1.4
Realistic monthly cost≈ $3,884
Assumed throughput with vLLM + speculative decoding~3,000 tokens/sec sustained
Effective utilization (not peak!)× 0.5
Tokens / month at 50% utilization≈ 3.94B
Effective cost per 1M tokens≈ $0.99

Now compare that to API prices for rough-equivalent quality (2026):

  • Claude Haiku 4.5: $1/$5 per 1M (in/out)
  • Gemini 2.5 Flash: ~$0.30/$2.50 per 1M
  • GPT-5.4 Mini: ~$0.40/$1.60 per 1M
  • DeepSeek V3.2 API (higher quality than Scout!): $0.28/$0.42 per 1M[2]

Yes, you read that right. DeepSeek charges you less to run a bigger model on their hardware than it costs you to rent an H200 and run a smaller model. That’s 2026 in a nutshell.

The 50% utilization trap. In production your GPU spends nights, weekends, and the dip between spikes mostly idle. If your traffic isn’t 24/7 heavy, your real utilization is 20–40%, not 50%. That inflates cost-per-token by 1.25–2.5x — and usually pushes self-hosting back above API pricing.

When self-hosting does win on cost

There are still scenarios where self-hosting beats the API bill on pure dollars. Here’s the decision chart.

❌ API wins

  • < 2B tokens / month total
  • Bursty traffic (GPU idle > 50% of hours)
  • Multiple model sizes needed (Haiku + Sonnet + Opus)
  • No in-house ML platform team
  • Need latest model within weeks of release

Net: APIs cheaper and simpler. Don’t self-host.

✅ Self-hosting wins

  • > 10B tokens / month steady
  • Heavy batch workloads (fill the GPU)
  • Single fine-tuned model serves most traffic
  • Dedicated ML platform team already in place
  • Locked-in reserved GPU capacity at $2–$3/hr

Net: 50–70% savings vs API at scale, assuming ops go well.

Verdict: The cost break-even in 2026 sits roughly at 10 billion tokens per month sustained. Below that, API wins. Above it — and only if utilization stays high — self-hosting can save 50–70%.

Serving frameworks: the 2026 big three

Picking a model is only half the job. The serving framework is the other half — and it determines whether your expensive H200 runs at 500 tokens/sec or 3,000 tokens/sec.

⚡ vLLM

The default open-source choice. PagedAttention, continuous batching, P-EAGLE speculative decoding since v0.16.[9] Easiest on-ramp. Run vllm serve and go.

🚀 SGLang

Often faster than vLLM on long-context workloads. Structured-output engine for JSON/regex built in. Speculative decoding supported.[9]

🏎️ TensorRT-LLM

NVIDIA’s own serving stack. Best raw throughput on NVIDIA hardware. Steepest learning curve. Speculative decoding up to 3.6x speedup.[10]

In 2026 these three are the only serving frameworks serious teams run in production for LLMs. Ollama is great for laptops, not for multi-tenant serving.[9]

Start with vLLM. It’s the shortest path from “I have a GPU” to “I have an OpenAI-compatible endpoint.” Move to SGLang or TensorRT-LLM only after you’ve proven the deployment works and you’re chasing the last 30% of performance.

Two cost multipliers everyone forgets

Speculative decoding

A small “draft” model guesses the next few tokens, and the big model verifies them all in one forward pass instead of one at a time. In 2026 this is a standard feature of vLLM, SGLang, and TensorRT-LLM, delivering 2–3x (occasionally up to 3.6x) faster generation with no quality loss.[10]

More tokens per GPU-hour means lower cost per 1M tokens. If you’re not enabling this, you’re leaving 50–70% of your GPU on the table.

Quantization

Run the model in INT8 or 4-bit (GGUF, AWQ, GPTQ). You give up 0.5–2 points of benchmark quality and get ~2x throughput and ~4x VRAM headroom. For most production tasks, that trade is obvious.

Stacking speculative decoding + 4-bit quantization can double to triple your effective tokens-per-GPU-hour — cutting self-hosted $/1M-tokens by 40–60% with minimal quality loss.

Ops overhead nobody budgets for

The thing that kills most self-hosting cost pitches is the stuff that doesn’t show up in the per-GPU-hour spreadsheet.

What GPU-hour pricing hides:
  • Reliability engineering. GPUs fail. Nodes crash. CUDA versions fight. You need on-call.
  • Model updates. When Llama 5 drops in August, someone has to re-quantize, re-benchmark, re-deploy, re-eval.
  • Serving infra. Load balancer, autoscaler, rate limiter, logger, tracing — all the stuff the API provider built for you.
  • Capacity swings. Scaling down is hard when you’re on reserved capacity. API providers absorb that.
  • Safety layers. Moderation, PII redaction, jailbreak resistance — built-in with APIs, DIY with self-hosting.

A rule of thumb: take your raw GPU bill, multiply by 1.3–1.6. That’s your realistic monthly cost. If 1.6× your GPU bill still beats the API, self-hosting probably wins. If it doesn’t, you’re deluding yourself.

The hybrid architecture that usually wins

The cleanest answer in 2026 is rarely “all API” or “all self-hosted.” It’s hybrid:

1
Route cheap traffic to self-hosted. The 70–80% of requests that are easy — classification, extraction, summarization — go to your own Llama 4 Scout instance.
→
2
Route hard traffic to the API. The 20–30% that need frontier reasoning go to Sonnet, Opus, or GPT-5.4. Pay for quality when it matters.
→
3
Spill over to the API. When your GPUs saturate, overflow traffic automatically spills to the cheap API so users don’t see errors.

This pattern uses the router we built in Chapter 7 and the capacity planning from Chapter 8. You get the cost advantage of self-hosted on steady traffic and the elasticity of the API on spikes.

Hidden driver: fine-tuning lock-in

If you fine-tuned a model, self-hosting stops being optional. Most providers don’t host arbitrary fine-tuned open weights; the ones that do charge dedicated-capacity prices that can exceed plain GPU rental.

So when you decide whether to fine-tune (Chapter 11, coming up), you’re also implicitly deciding whether to self-host. Those two decisions are linked.

Don’t self-host and fine-tune in the same sprint. Each is its own ops adventure. Ship the self-hosted base model first, get it stable for a month, then add a fine-tune on top.

Exercise: price your biggest workload

🛠️ Do it yourself (45 min)

  1. Pick your biggest AI workload — the one eating the most API dollars today.
  2. Measure sustained throughput. Tokens/day, steady-state. Multiply by 30 for monthly tokens.
  3. Pick a candidate OSS model that could plausibly handle the task (Llama 4 Scout for most; DeepSeek V3.2 if you need reasoning).
  4. Estimate GPU count. Rule of thumb: Scout fits on 1× H100 at 4-bit; DeepSeek V3.2 needs 4× B200 or 8× H200.
  5. Compute monthly GPU cost. GPUs × $/hr × 730 × 1.4 (ops multiplier).
  6. Estimate effective throughput. 3K tokens/sec per H200 at 40% utilization is a reasonable starting point.
  7. Divide monthly cost by monthly tokens. You have your $/1M self-hosted.
  8. Compare to what you’re paying the API today for the same task. Write down the winner and by how much.

Bonus: redo the math at 2× and 10× your current volume. Break-even usually flips at 3–5×.

The checklist before you pull the trigger

  • ✅ Usage is > 10B tokens/month steady (not bursty)
  • ✅ You have (or are hiring) an ML platform engineer on-call
  • ✅ A single OSS model can cover > 70% of your traffic
  • ✅ You have reserved GPU capacity at under $3/hr or a plan to get there
  • ✅ Your eval suite can tell you if a model update regresses quality
  • ✅ You have a hybrid fallback to an API when GPUs saturate
  • ✅ You’ve priced the ops multiplier (1.3–1.6×) honestly
  • ✅ You understand latency tradeoffs for your use case

If you can’t tick every box, self-hosting is probably a distraction. Stick with APIs, optimize with caching (Ch. 4), batching (Ch. 5), compression (Ch. 6), and routing (Ch. 7). That stack can deliver 80–95% cost reduction without a single GPU procurement meeting.
