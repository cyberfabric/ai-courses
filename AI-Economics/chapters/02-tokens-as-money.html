Chapter 2

Tokens Are Money: The Cost Equation

How tokens map to dollars — input vs output, reasoning tokens, and why longer context costs more

Money with words on it

In Chapter 1 we said a prompt is "money with words on it." This chapter is where we turn that metaphor into arithmetic. If you can look at a prompt and instinctively see the dollars in it — before you call any API — you're already operating at a level most teams don't. That's the goal for the next 20 minutes.

The unit of all this accounting is the token. Not a request, not a sentence, not a page — a token. Every provider bills by it. Every cost equation rests on it. Tokens are the kilowatt-hours of AI. And just like electricity, what you pay depends on when you spend, how you spend, and which plan you're on.

The cost equation, in one line: cost = input_tokens × input_price + output_tokens × output_price. Every optimization in this course is a variation on shrinking one of those four factors.

What a token actually is

A token is a chunk of text the model processes as a unit. It's not a character, not exactly a word, and not a syllable. For English, a useful rule of thumb: 1 token ≈ 4 characters ≈ 0.75 words. Code, URLs, and non-English text tokenize less efficiently — we'll see why in a minute.

Every provider has its own tokenizer (the little program that slices text into tokens). OpenAI uses tiktoken. Anthropic uses its own. Google Gemini uses SentencePiece. A prompt can have different token counts on different providers, which means the same text literally costs different amounts depending on where you send it.

If you want the full "how does a tokenizer work" story, the AI-Intro course covers the mechanics. For cost engineering, you only need to know three practical things: tokens are what you pay for, the count depends on the tokenizer, and you can ask every provider to count for you before you call.

Input vs output: the 4–5x asymmetry

Here's the first surprise every developer hits: input and output are priced separately, and output is almost always several times more expensive than input. Typically 4x–5x, sometimes more.

Why? Because output generation is sequential and compute-intensive — each token forces another forward pass through the model. Reading your 10,000-token prompt happens in one chunk; writing a 500-token answer happens one token at a time. Providers price for the compute, not the character count.

Reasoning tokens: the invisible bill

Reasoning models are the 2026 specialty. They "think" internally before producing the visible response — and you pay for every thought. Those internal-reasoning tokens are billed as output tokens on both Anthropic and OpenAI, but they never appear in your response. The API just sends you the final answer and a bigger invoice.3

How much more? In our own measurements and in public benchmarks: reasoning tokens typically run 2–10x the visible output tokens, depending on the task. A hard math problem can burn 15,000 reasoning tokens to produce a 400-token explanation. That's a 40x multiplier on what you "see" coming back.

Watch out for silent reasoning costs. Anthropic's extended thinking has a minimum budget of 1,024 tokens per call when enabled.3 If you turned extended thinking on "to see what happens" and forgot, every single request is now carrying an extra $0.015–$0.030 in hidden output tokens at Sonnet rates. Audit the flag.

OpenAI's o3 and o4-mini work the same way: the reasoning tokens are billed as output and not returned. response.usage.reasoning_tokens tells you exactly how many. Watch that number in every production trace — it's often the single biggest lever on the bill.

Tuning the reasoning budget

Both Anthropic and OpenAI let you throttle how much "thinking" a reasoning model does. This is effectively a dial that trades quality for cost — and on most tasks, the default is tuned for hard problems, not yours.

  • Anthropic (Sonnet 4.6, Opus 4.6/4.7) — set thinking.budget_tokens explicitly; minimum 1,024, default off. If you don't turn it on, you don't pay for it.3
  • OpenAI (o3, o4-mini) — reasoning_effort takes "low", "medium", "high", or "max". Low can cut reasoning tokens by 70–90% on easy tasks.
  • Opus 4.6+ adaptive thinking — the model decides how much reasoning each task deserves. Still caps at your budget, but avoids "max effort on a trivial prompt" by default.

Practical pattern: set reasoning_effort: "low" as your baseline. Bump it up only for request classes where you've measured a quality win. Most teams leave it on "medium" or "high" forever and quietly pay the multiplier.

Reasoning tokens in action — one "hard" o4-mini request

The 200K (and 1M) context tax

Most providers quote a single rate like "$3 per 1M input tokens." What the sticker doesn't say: once your prompt crosses a threshold, the rate often doubles.

As of April 2026:

  • Gemini 2.5 Pro / 3 Pro — above 200K input tokens, input price roughly doubles (Gemini 2.5 Pro goes from $1.25/M to ~$2.50/M).4
  • GPT-5.4 and GPT-5.4 Pro — above 272K input tokens, OpenAI charges 2x input and 1.5x output for that session.2
  • Anthropic — no context-size tier surcharge on standard models as of April 2026; the flat rate holds up to the model's max context.1

This matters enormously for RAG and long-document pipelines. A prompt that sits at 199K tokens is priced one way; the same prompt at 201K costs almost double per input token — and you rarely notice in your dashboard because the bill just "creeps up" as contexts grow.

Tip: When you're near a context threshold, it's often cheaper to split the request into two smaller calls (each under the threshold) than to send one over-sized call. Run the math on your specific shape — but treat 200K and 272K as bright lines worth engineering around.

Case study: the $4,300 base64 accident

A team shipped an image-description feature. The prototype worked beautifully on their laptops. They sent the image as a base64 string inside the user message because it was "simpler than handling vision APIs properly." The prompts averaged 28,000 tokens each — most of it base64 garbage — and cost about $0.12 per call on Sonnet 4.6.

That's fine at 10 requests/minute. At 10,000 requests/minute over a weekend launch, the feature burned through $4,300 before anyone noticed the bill was climbing. The fix took four hours: send the image via the proper vision endpoint (which handles its own encoding), drop the base64, watch cost per call drop to $0.004. A 30x improvement, on the same feature, from a single tokenization mistake.

Never inline binary-encoded data into a prompt. Images, PDFs, audio — always use the provider's dedicated input channel (Files API, vision API, audio endpoint). Base64 in a text prompt is a bill multiplier that will surprise you on Monday.

Tokenization quirks worth knowing

Not all text tokenizes the same way. Grocery analogy: when you buy flour, a kilo is a kilo. When you buy by the token, the same content can weigh very different amounts depending on what's in it.

Typical token counts (illustrative, tiktoken-style)
  • "hello world" → 2 tokens
  • "The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog." → 10 tokens
  • "https://analytics.example.com/v2/users?tag=cohort_42&region=EU-west" → ~25–35 tokens
  • A typical 500-word English paragraph → ~650 tokens
  • Same paragraph in Japanese → often 1.5–3x more tokens
  • Minified JSON (no whitespace) → ~1.2x more tokens than pretty-printed
  • Base64-encoded 50KB image blob inlined in a prompt → tens of thousands of tokens 😱

Three practical implications. One: URLs and IDs are token-hungry; keep them out of system prompts unless they earn their place. Two: non-English languages cost more per "character of meaning" — budget accordingly. Three: never, ever inline raw base64 blobs into a prompt. That's one of the most common "oh god the bill" stories we've ever heard.

You don't have to guess token counts. Every major provider ships a free counter — use it before shipping any feature that touches a variable-length prompt.5

Streaming, retries, and "phantom" tokens

A few billing edge cases surprise people every quarter. None of them are exotic — they're just the gaps between the mental model and what actually hits your invoice.

  • Streaming doesn't lower cost. A streamed response and a non-streamed response with the same input and output length cost exactly the same. Streaming is a UX feature, not a cost lever. If the user disconnects mid-stream, you still typically pay for tokens the model already generated.
  • Retries compound. A failed request that you retry once bills you twice. If the first call returned 800 output tokens before erroring, those may not be billed (provider-dependent), but most networking-level retries on a 200 OK response that your client botched do double the bill. Idempotency keys help; blind retries hurt.
  • Aborted requests still cost. If you cancel a generation after 500 of 800 output tokens, you pay for the 500. User-visible "Cancel" buttons are fine, but don't assume they zero out the spend.
  • Function-calling tokens add up. The model's tool-call JSON counts as output tokens. Several function calls in a row can quietly triple your output-token usage on a single agent turn.
Gotcha: If your SDK auto-retries on 529 ("overloaded") errors with the same request body, an outage day can silently quintuple your bill. Configure your retry policy with exponential backoff and a max-attempts cap. Two retries is usually plenty.

The token-budget mindset

Electricity-tariff thinking: you don't plug in a space heater without knowing the monthly bill impact. Treat prompts the same way. Every AI feature should have a per-call token budget written down in the design doc — and a corresponding dollar budget that falls out of it.

A good token budget has four parts:

  1. Max input tokens per call — cap the system prompt + retrieved context + user message at a hard number. Reject overruns.
  2. Max output tokens per call — use max_tokens / max_completion_tokens on every request. This is the #1 missed setting in the wild.
  3. Max reasoning tokens per call — cap the thinking budget where the provider exposes it (Anthropic: thinking.budget_tokens; OpenAI: reasoning_effort).
  4. Max cost per call — the derived number. Log it. Alert on p99 spikes.
Tip: Default every new AI feature to max_tokens = 800 unless someone explicitly justifies more. If the feature really needs a 2,000-token answer, that's fine — but it's a deliberate choice, not a blank check. Uncapped output is the single most common cause of a five-figure surprise invoice.

Wrapping the equation in a 10-line helper

Build this once, reuse forever. The idea is a single function every engineer on the team calls before shipping a prompt change. Don't overthink it — a tiny estimator beats no estimator:

Minimal cost-estimator sketch (pseudocode)
  • estimate(prompt, model, expected_output_tokens) → {tokens_in, tokens_out, usd_per_call, usd_at_volume}
  • Look up the current input_price and output_price from a small pricing table in code, dated in a comment.
  • Count input tokens with the provider's SDK.
  • Multiply and return the four numbers.
  • Bonus: write a unit test that fails if the pricing table is more than 60 days old — a forcing function for keeping rates fresh.

Order-of-magnitude intuition

One thing experienced cost engineers develop is a feel for cost without a calculator. Here are rough benchmarks, in April 2026 dollars, for "one 1M-token request at this model tier" — not exact, but close enough to sanity-check any new feature in your head.

  • Tiny/nano tier (GPT-5.4 Nano, Gemini Flash-Lite, similar): roughly $0.10–$0.30 per 1M mixed tokens. At this price, a high-volume classification feature is effectively free compared to the engineering effort of caching it.
  • Mini tier (Haiku 4.5, GPT-5.4 Mini, o4-mini, Gemini Flash): roughly $1–$3 per 1M mixed tokens. The sweet spot for most production features in 2026 — good enough for ~80% of jobs.
  • Standard tier (Sonnet 4.6, GPT-5.4 Standard, Gemini 2.5 Pro): roughly $5–$15 per 1M mixed tokens. The default "looks-good" choice; the one most teams default to and overpay for.
  • Premium tier (Opus 4.7, GPT-5.4 Pro, o3): $10–$80 per 1M mixed tokens. Reach for it when quality is the constraint, never by default.

Memorize those four brackets and you can sketch the cost of nearly any new AI feature on a napkin. Exact numbers come next chapter; the mental brackets are what makes planning meetings productive.

Comparing reasoning vs non-reasoning on the same task

One more pattern worth the ink: on many production tasks, a smaller non-reasoning model is competitive with or cheaper than a reasoning model — and only the eval tells you which one to use. Cost estimate for a classification task, 100,000 calls/month, 2,000 input + 300 output visible tokens:

  • Haiku 4.5 (non-reasoning): 100K × (2,000 × $1/M + 300 × $5/M) = $350/month.
  • o4-mini with reasoning_effort: low (~500 reasoning tokens avg): 100K × (2,000 × $1.10/M + 800 × $4.40/M) = $572/month.
  • o4-mini with reasoning_effort: high (~5,000 reasoning tokens avg): 100K × (2,000 × $1.10/M + 5,300 × $4.40/M) = $2,552/month.

Same task. 7x cost spread depending on model and effort dial. If your eval says Haiku 4.5 passes, you've just left $2,200/month on the table by going with o4-mini-high "because reasoning is smarter." Run the eval first, then pick the tier.

Exercise: tokenize a real prompt of yours

Exercise: Price one real prompt on three providers

Grab a prompt you wrote this week — a system prompt, a retrieval template, anything you actually run in production. Then, in under 20 minutes:

  1. Count the input tokens with tiktoken (for OpenAI), the Anthropic SDK count_tokens endpoint, and Gemini's countTokens. Record all three numbers — they won't match exactly.
  2. Sample 10 real outputs from your logs for that prompt. Record the average and p95 output-token count.
  3. Compute the per-request cost on Claude Sonnet 4.6, GPT-5.4 Standard, and Gemini 2.5 Pro using April 2026 rates.
  4. Multiply by monthly call volume. Now you have a live cost estimate — probably off from your actual bill, which is useful information.
  5. Compare to the actual invoice line for that feature (if you have per-feature tags from Chapter 1's tip). Within 10%? Congrats, your mental model works. Way off? Investigate before Chapter 9.

Bonus: Re-run the math using the model one tier down (Haiku, GPT-5.4 Mini, Gemini Flash). Write down the theoretical savings. You've just pre-computed the ROI for Chapter 7's routing chapter.
