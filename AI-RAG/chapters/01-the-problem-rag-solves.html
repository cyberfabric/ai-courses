Chapter 1

The Problem RAG Solves

Why LLMs make things up, and the elegant pattern that fixes it

Your AI Is Confidently Wrong

You've probably had this experience. You ask ChatGPT, Claude, or some other AI assistant a question about your company's data — your internal policies, your API docs, last Tuesday's meeting notes — and it replies with absolute confidence. Except the answer is completely made up.

It doesn't say "I don't know." It doesn't hesitate. It just... invents something that sounds right. And that's the scary part — it sounds so plausible that you almost believe it.

This isn't a bug. It's a fundamental feature of how large language models work. And understanding why they do this is the first step toward fixing it.

The Core Problem

LLMs are trained on vast amounts of public internet text. They know a lot about the world in general — but they know nothing about your company's internal wiki, your product database, your customer support tickets, or anything that happened after their training data was collected.

Why LLMs Hallucinate

Let's get one thing straight: LLMs don't "think." They predict the next word in a sequence based on patterns they learned during training. When you ask a question, the model doesn't look up the answer in some internal database. It generates the most statistically likely continuation of your prompt.

This works amazingly well for general knowledge. "What's the capital of France?" — easy, the training data is full of that answer. But "What's our company's vacation policy?" — the model has never seen your employee handbook. So it does what it always does: it predicts words that look like a plausible vacation policy.

LLMs don't know what they don't know. Unlike a database query that returns "no results found," an LLM will always produce an answer. It has no concept of "I don't have this information." It's a prediction engine, not a knowledge retrieval system — and that distinction is everything.

Think of it this way. Imagine you have a well-read friend who has devoured thousands of books, articles, and blog posts. You ask them about general history, science, or cooking — brilliant answers. Now ask them what was discussed in your team's standup meeting yesterday. They'll either say "I wasn't there" (if they're honest) or make something up that sounds reasonable (if they're an LLM).

The Three Flavors of Hallucination

Not all hallucinations are created equal. Understanding the types helps you appreciate why RAG is so effective:

Type What Happens Example
Factual fabrication The model invents facts that don't exist "Your refund policy allows returns within 90 days" (it's actually 30)
Knowledge gap The model doesn't have the data and fills the gap "The Q3 revenue was $4.2M" (it has no idea)
Outdated info The model's training data is stale "The latest version of React is 18" (it's actually 20)

In enterprise settings, these hallucinations aren't just annoying — they're dangerous. A customer support bot that invents refund policies. An internal assistant that fabricates compliance procedures. A code assistant that suggests deprecated APIs. Organizations using RAG report a 60–80% reduction in hallucinations and up to 3x improvement in answer accuracy for domain-specific questions.

The Naive Solution That Doesn't Work

Your first instinct might be: "Just train the model on our data!" This is called fine-tuning, and while it has legitimate uses, it's a terrible solution for the hallucination problem. Here's why:

Fine-tuning for factual knowledge
1. Collect your company's documents 2. Fine-tune an LLM on them 3. Hope the model memorizes the facts 4. Repeat every time your data changes

Fine-tuning teaches the model style and behavior, not specific facts. It's like training a chef in French cuisine — they'll cook French-style, but they won't memorize your restaurant's specific menu.

The problems with fine-tuning for factual knowledge:

  • Expensive: Fine-tuning costs real money and takes hours or days to complete
  • Stale instantly: Your data changes daily. You can't fine-tune every day.
  • Still hallucinates: Fine-tuning doesn't guarantee the model will recall specific facts — it adjusts weights, not memories
  • No source attribution: Even if the model gets it right, you can't point to which document the answer came from
  • Catastrophic forgetting: Fine-tuning on new data can make the model worse at things it previously knew
Fine-tuning is great for teaching a model how to respond (tone, format, domain terminology). It's terrible for teaching a model what to say about your specific data. The 2026 best practice is to use fine-tuning for style and RAG for facts — not one or the other.

The Core Insight: Open-Book Exams

Here's the million-dollar idea behind RAG, and it's beautifully simple:

Instead of teaching the model your data, hand it the relevant pages right before it answers.

Think about exams in school. In a closed-book exam, you have to memorize everything. You'll get some things right, some things wrong, and you'll definitely make stuff up when you don't remember. That's an LLM without RAG.

In an open-book exam, you have the textbook right there. You still need to understand the material well enough to find the right pages and synthesize an answer — but you're not relying on memory for the facts. That's RAG.

RAG in One Sentence

Retrieval-Augmented Generation means: find the relevant documents first, then let the LLM read them and answer. The model generates from evidence, not from memory.

The term "Retrieval-Augmented Generation" was coined in a 2020 paper by researchers at Meta (then Facebook). Let's break the name down:

  • Retrieval — search your knowledge base for relevant documents
  • Augmented — stuff those documents into the LLM's prompt as context
  • Generation — the LLM reads the context and generates an answer grounded in your actual data

A Simple RAG Walkthrough

Let's trace through a concrete example. Say you're building a support bot for your company, and a customer asks: "What's your refund policy for annual subscriptions?"

User asks question
→
Find relevant docs
→
Feed docs to LLM
→
LLM answers from docs

Here's what happens behind the scenes:

  1. The question comes in: "What's your refund policy for annual subscriptions?"
  2. The system searches your knowledge base — your help center articles, policy documents, terms of service — and finds the 3–5 most relevant chunks of text
  3. Those chunks are inserted into the LLM's prompt: "Based on the following documents, answer the user's question..."
  4. The LLM reads the actual policy text and generates an answer grounded in your real data
  5. The answer includes references so the customer (or your team) can verify it
What the LLM actually sees
System: Answer the user's question based ONLY on the provided context. If the context doesn't contain the answer, say "I don't have that information." Context: [Document: refund-policy.md] Annual subscriptions may be refunded within 14 days of purchase. After 14 days, refunds are prorated based on remaining months. Contact support@example.com to initiate. [Document: billing-faq.md] For annual plans, we offer a 14-day full refund window. After that, you'll receive a prorated credit... User: What's your refund policy for annual subscriptions?

The LLM doesn't need to "know" your refund policy. It reads it right there in the prompt and answers accurately, citing the specific documents.

That's it. That's the core pattern. Everything else in this course — embeddings, vector databases, chunking strategies, advanced retrieval — is about making this search step faster, smarter, and more accurate.

Why RAG Is the #1 Production AI Pattern

RAG isn't just a clever research idea — it's the dominant pattern in production AI systems today. If you've used any of these, you've used RAG:

90%+ Enterprise AI chatbots use RAG
60–80% Reduction in hallucinations
3x Better accuracy on domain questions

Real-world examples of RAG in action:

  • Customer support bots — Answer questions from your help center and knowledge base
  • Internal knowledge assistants — Search company wikis, Confluence, Notion, and Slack history
  • Documentation search — Developer docs that actually answer your specific question
  • Code assistants — Cursor, GitHub Copilot, and Claude Code all use RAG to pull relevant code context
  • Legal document analysis — Search through contracts and case law for relevant precedents
  • Medical information systems — Retrieve relevant research papers to support clinical decisions
Where this fits in the RAG pipeline: This chapter gives you the "why." The full RAG pipeline has four stages: Ingest (load and prepare your documents), Index (create embeddings and store them), Retrieve (find relevant chunks for a query), and Generate (let the LLM answer from the retrieved context). We'll build each stage throughout this course.

RAG vs Fine-Tuning vs Long Context

RAG isn't the only approach to getting LLMs to work with your data. Here's how the three main strategies compare:

Approach Best For Weakness
RAG Factual Q&A over large, changing datasets Requires retrieval infrastructure; quality depends on search
Fine-tuning Teaching style, tone, domain behavior Expensive, stale, doesn't guarantee factual recall
Long context Small knowledge bases (<200K tokens) Accuracy drops for info in the "middle"; expensive at scale
The retrieval advantage: RAG's secret weapon is that it retrieves only the relevant information. Long context stuffs everything in and hopes the model finds what it needs — but research shows accuracy drops 10–20 percentage points when the answer is buried in the middle of a long context. RAG sidesteps this entirely by surfacing just the relevant chunks.

Here's a simple decision tree for choosing your approach:

Is your knowledge base small (< 200K tokens)?
├── YES → Try long context + prompt caching first
│         (simpler, no retrieval infra needed)
└── NO → Does the data change frequently?
    ├── YES → Use RAG (retrieval keeps data fresh)
    └── NO → Do you need specific factual recall?
        ├── YES → Use RAG
        └── NO → Consider fine-tuning for style/behavior

In 2026, the best practice is often:
Fine-tune for STYLE + RAG for FACTS = best of both worlds
(96% accuracy vs 89% RAG-only or 91% fine-tuning-only)

What Can Go Wrong

RAG isn't magic. It's a powerful pattern, but it has failure modes you need to understand before diving in:

Garbage In, Garbage Out

If your source documents are poorly written, outdated, or contradictory, your RAG system will faithfully retrieve and present that garbage. RAG doesn't fix bad data — it surfaces it faster.

Irrelevant Retrieval

The system might retrieve chunks that are topically related but don't actually contain the answer. This is the biggest quality challenge in RAG, and we'll spend several chapters on solving it.

Context Window Overflow

Retrieving too many chunks can overflow the LLM's context window or dilute the signal with noise. The art is in retrieving just enough relevant context.

The LLM Ignores the Context

Sometimes the LLM will generate an answer from its training data instead of the provided context. Good prompt engineering (which we'll cover in Chapter 8) mitigates this.

RAG doesn't eliminate hallucinations — it dramatically reduces them. If the retrieval step returns irrelevant documents, the LLM may still hallucinate based on that faulty context. Poor retrieval quality is the #1 cause of RAG hallucinations. That's why most of this course focuses on getting retrieval right.

The Librarian Analogy

Here's the analogy that ties everything together, and the one we'll come back to throughout this course:

RAG Is a Librarian

Imagine you need to write an essay. Without RAG, you're trying to write from memory — you'll get some things right, but you'll fill gaps with guesses. With RAG, you have a skilled librarian who listens to your question, runs to the right shelves, pulls the most relevant books, and hands them to you before you start writing. You still write the essay (that's the LLM's job), but now you're working from actual sources instead of memory.

The quality of your RAG system depends on three things — the same three things that make a librarian great:

  1. How well-organized is the library? (Your indexing and chunking strategy)
  2. How good is the librarian at understanding your question? (Your retrieval approach — embeddings, hybrid search, re-ranking)
  3. How good is the writer at using the sources? (Your LLM and prompt engineering)

We'll master all three throughout this course.

A Taste of the Code

We'll build a complete RAG system in Chapter 3, but here's a preview to show you how simple the core pattern is. This is pseudocode — just 15 lines that capture the entire idea:

# The entire RAG pattern in pseudocode
def answer_question(user_question, knowledge_base):
    # Step 1: Turn the question into a vector
    question_embedding = embed(user_question)

    # Step 2: Find the most similar chunks in your knowledge base
    relevant_chunks = knowledge_base.search(
        question_embedding,
        top_k=5
    )

    # Step 3: Build a prompt with the retrieved context
    prompt = f"""Answer based ONLY on this context:

    {relevant_chunks}

    Question: {user_question}"""

    # Step 4: Let the LLM generate an answer from the context
    answer = llm.generate(prompt)
    return answer

That's it. Every RAG system — from a weekend prototype to a production system serving millions of users — is a variation on these four steps. The rest of this course is about making each step work really, really well.
