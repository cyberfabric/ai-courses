Chapter 10

Types of AI Models & The Industry Map

Text, image, multimodal, speech — open vs closed source, and who makes what

Over nine chapters, you've learned how AI works under the hood — tokens, transformers, generation, embeddings. All of it focused on one type of model: the Large Language Model.

But the AI world is much bigger than LLMs. There are models that generate images, transcribe speech, compose music, and fold proteins. There are tiny models that run on your phone and massive ones that require data center clusters. Some are open source, some are locked behind APIs.

This final chapter gives you the complete map. By the end, you'll be able to navigate any AI conversation with confidence — whether your team is debating which model to use, which provider to choose, or whether to run something locally.

Text Models (LLMs): The Ones You Know

These are the models we've been studying. They process text, generate text, and power the tools you use daily. But not all LLMs are created equal.

Model Family Company Key Characteristics
GPT-4 / GPT-4o OpenAI The model that started the mainstream LLM era. Strong all-around, widely integrated.
Claude Anthropic Known for following instructions carefully, long context (up to 1M tokens), strong at code.
Gemini Google Natively multimodal (text, images, video). Deeply integrated with Google services.
Llama Meta Open-weights. The most popular base model for fine-tuning and self-hosting.
Mistral / Mixtral Mistral AI European AI lab. Efficient models that punch above their weight class.
DeepSeek DeepSeek Chinese lab producing competitive open models, especially strong at code and reasoning.

Each of these uses the transformer architecture you learned in Chapter 5. The differences come from training data, model size, fine-tuning approach, and engineering decisions. Same engine, different builds.

Image Generation Models: Pictures from Words

Image models take a text description ("a cat wearing a space suit on Mars") and generate a matching image. The technology behind them is different from LLMs.

Most image generators use a technique called diffusion. The simplified explanation: start with pure noise (random pixels), then gradually remove noise in a way guided by the text prompt, until a coherent image emerges. It's like a sculptor starting with a block of marble and chipping away until a statue appears — except the text prompt guides what shape to carve.

Model Company Notes
DALL-E 3 OpenAI Integrated into ChatGPT. Good at following detailed prompts.
Midjourney Midjourney Known for highly aesthetic, artistic output. Accessible via Discord.
Stable Diffusion / SDXL Stability AI Open source. Can run locally on your own GPU. Huge community.
Flux Black Forest Labs From former Stability AI researchers. Fast, high-quality, flexible.

Tip

Image models and LLMs are fundamentally different architectures solving different problems. LLMs predict the next token. Diffusion models iteratively denoise an image. But they share a common foundation: both are neural networks trained on massive datasets, and both use the concepts (layers, weights, training) you learned in Chapters 2–3.

Multimodal Models: See, Hear, and Write

The latest frontier is multimodal models — models that can handle multiple types of input and output: text, images, audio, and video.

When you upload a screenshot to ChatGPT and ask "What's wrong with this UI?", that's a multimodal model processing both the image and your text simultaneously. When you ask Claude to analyze a chart from a PDF, same thing.

  • GPT-4o — handles text, images, and audio natively in a single model
  • Gemini — Google's natively multimodal model (text, images, video, code)
  • Claude — processes text, images, and PDFs; generates text

Multimodal capability is rapidly becoming the default. A model that can only handle text increasingly feels limited when developers need to work with screenshots, diagrams, mockups, and multimedia content.

Code Models: Specialized for Programming

While general LLMs like GPT-4 and Claude are excellent at code, there are also models trained specifically (or primarily) on code:

  • Codex (OpenAI) — the model that originally powered GitHub Copilot
  • CodeLlama (Meta) — Llama fine-tuned specifically on code, open-weights
  • DeepSeek Coder (DeepSeek) — open model optimized for code generation and understanding
  • StarCoder (BigCode) — open-source, trained on permissively-licensed code

Specialized code models can be smaller and cheaper than general models while still performing well on programming tasks. A 7B code model might write better Python than a 70B general-purpose model, because its training data was focused.

Aha Moment

This is the specialization trade-off you see everywhere in AI. A general model (GPT-4, Claude) can do everything reasonably well. A specialized model (CodeLlama, DeepSeek Coder) can do one thing extremely well. When you're choosing a model for a specific task, a smaller specialist often beats a larger generalist — and costs a fraction of the price.

It's the same trade-off in software development: a Swiss Army knife vs. a dedicated tool. Both have their place, and the right choice depends on your use case.

Speech Models: Text to Speech and Back

AI can also convert between speech and text:

  • Whisper (OpenAI) — speech-to-text. Open source, remarkably accurate, supports 100+ languages. You've likely used it indirectly through apps that transcribe audio.
  • TTS models (various) — text-to-speech. Models from OpenAI, ElevenLabs, and others can generate human-sounding speech from text, with controllable voice, tone, and emotion.

The pipeline for voice assistants (like ChatGPT's voice mode) typically chains these together: Whisper converts your speech to text, an LLM generates a response, and a TTS model converts it back to speech. Three separate AI models working in sequence.

Open Source vs. Closed Source: The Great Divide

One of the biggest debates in AI right now is about openness. Different companies take very different approaches:

Closed Source (Proprietary)

You access the model through an API. You can't see the weights, can't modify the model, and can't run it yourself. The company controls everything.

  • OpenAI — GPT-4, DALL-E (API only)
  • Anthropic — Claude (API only)
  • Google — Gemini (API, integrated into Google products)

Open Weights (Semi-Open)

The model weights are published, so you can download and run the model yourself. But the training data and training code are usually not shared. You can use and modify the model, but you can't fully reproduce it.

  • Meta — Llama (weights available under a license)
  • Mistral AI — Mistral, Mixtral (open weights)
  • DeepSeek — DeepSeek models (open weights)

What "Open" Really Means

Open Weights ≠ Open Source ≠ Open Training Data

This is a common source of confusion. "Open weights" means you can download and use the model. It does NOT mean the training data, training code, or data curation process is shared. True "open source" AI (where everything is reproducible) is rare. When people say "open-source AI," they usually mean "open weights."

Closed source (GPT-4, Claude)

✓ Cutting-edge performance ✓ Managed infrastructure ✓ Regular updates from provider ✗ No customization of model ✗ Data sent to third party ✗ Dependent on provider's pricing ✗ Can't run offline

Open weights (Llama, Mistral)

✓ Run on your own hardware ✓ Full control, no data sharing ✓ Fine-tune for your use case ✓ No per-token API costs ✗ You manage infrastructure ✗ Generally behind closed models ✗ Requires GPU expertise ✗ No automatic updates

Model Sizes: What the "B" Means

You'll see model sizes like "7B," "13B," "70B," "405B." The "B" stands for billion parameters (the weights and biases from Chapter 2).

Analogy: The Car Lineup

Think of AI models like cars. There are economy cars (7B), sedans (13B-30B), SUVs (70B), luxury cars (200B+), and supercars (1T+).

Economy cars are cheap, fast, and handle daily commutes perfectly. You don't need a supercar to go to the grocery store. SUVs handle more complex terrain. Supercars deliver peak performance but cost a fortune to operate.

Similarly, a 7B model handles simple code completion and chat perfectly. A 70B model handles complex reasoning. A trillion-parameter model handles the most nuanced, difficult tasks. Pick based on your actual needs, not on bragging rights.

Size What It Can Do Well Hardware to Run It Example Models
1B–3B Simple tasks, classification, basic chat Phone, laptop CPU Llama 3.2 1B, Phi-3 mini
7B–13B Good chat, code completion, summarization Consumer GPU (8-16GB) Llama 3.1 8B, Mistral 7B

Running Models Locally vs. in the Cloud

You have two options for running AI models: call a cloud API, or run the model on your own hardware.

Cloud APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google)

You send requests to a provider's servers. They handle the GPUs, the infrastructure, and the scaling. You pay per token.

Best for: production applications, frontier models, when you don't want to manage hardware, when you need the absolute best quality.

Local Inference (Ollama, llama.cpp, vLLM)

You download an open-weights model and run it on your own machine or server. No per-token costs, full privacy, complete control.

OL
Ollama
The easiest way to run models locally. One command to download and run a model. Works on Mac, Linux, Windows. Great for experimentation.
LC
llama.cpp
Highly optimized C++ inference engine. Can run models on CPU (no GPU needed, just slower). The foundation that Ollama is built on.

Best for: privacy-sensitive data, offline use, high-volume applications where per-token costs add up, experimentation and learning, fine-tuned models.

Tip

A practical setup many developers use: cloud APIs (Claude, GPT-4) for complex tasks that need frontier quality, and a local 7B-13B model (via Ollama) for simple tasks, quick iterations, and privacy-sensitive work. You don't have to choose one or the other.

The Business of AI

How do AI companies make money? Several models (pun intended):

  • API access (pay-per-token): OpenAI, Anthropic, Google sell API access. You pay for what you use. This is the primary revenue model for most AI companies.
  • Subscriptions: ChatGPT Plus, Claude Pro — monthly fees for enhanced access, higher limits, and premium features.
  • Enterprise contracts: Custom deployments, dedicated capacity, enhanced privacy, compliance features. The biggest revenue stream for AI companies.
  • Open source + services: Meta gives away Llama to build ecosystem dominance. Mistral offers open weights but sells managed APIs and enterprise support.

The AI industry is moving fast. Pricing drops rapidly as competition increases and hardware improves. What costs $10 today might cost $0.50 next year.

Who Uses What (and Why)

Different users gravitate to different solutions:

  • Individual developers: Claude/ChatGPT subscriptions for daily coding, Ollama for experimentation. Minimal infrastructure needed.
  • Startups: Cloud APIs for fast iteration (no infrastructure overhead). Often love open-weights models because they avoid vendor lock-in and can fine-tune for specific use cases.
  • Enterprises: Closed-source APIs with enterprise agreements (SLAs, data privacy, compliance). Or self-hosted open-weights models for maximum control. Often both, depending on the use case.
  • Researchers: Open-weights models for experimentation, reproducibility, and ablation studies. Access to model internals is essential for research.

Putting It All Together

  • LLMs (GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama) are the most prominent AI models, but the landscape includes image, speech, code, and multimodal models.
  • Image generation uses diffusion (noise → image), not next-token prediction.
  • Multimodal models handle text + images + audio in a single model — rapidly becoming the default.
  • Closed source (OpenAI, Anthropic) offers cutting-edge quality via API. Open weights (Llama, Mistral) offers control and customization.
  • "Open weights" ≠ "open source" ≠ "open training data." Know the distinction.
  • Model sizes range from 1B (runs on a phone) to 1T+ (requires data centers). Pick based on your actual task.
  • Cloud APIs for convenience and quality, local models for privacy and cost control. Many developers use both.
  • The AI industry is moving fast — prices are dropping, capabilities are rising, and the landscape changes every few months.

Exercise: Choose Your Model

For each of the following scenarios, pick which model type and deployment approach you'd recommend. Consider: quality needs, privacy, cost, and complexity.

  1. A startup building a customer support chatbot that answers questions about their product. Needs to be accurate about product details. Budget is limited.
  2. An enterprise processing sensitive legal documents to extract key clauses. Data cannot leave the company's infrastructure.
  3. A solo developer building a side project that generates creative social media posts. Quality doesn't need to be perfect, but it needs to be cheap.

Suggested approach:

  1. RAG with embeddings + a cloud API (Claude or GPT-4). Embed your product docs, retrieve relevant ones, let the LLM answer. Cost-effective because you only pay per query.
  2. Self-hosted open-weights model (Llama 70B or similar) on company infrastructure. Complete data control. Higher upfront infrastructure cost, but no data leaves the building.
  3. Local 7B model via Ollama for zero ongoing cost, or a small/cheap cloud model. Creative text doesn't need frontier models — a smaller model with higher temperature works great.
