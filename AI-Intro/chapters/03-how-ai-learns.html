Chapter 3

How AI Learns

Training data, loss functions, and backpropagation — simplified with visual analogies

In Chapter 2, you learned that a neural network is made of neurons, weights, and layers. But we left a huge question hanging: how do all those trillions of weights end up with the right values?

Nobody sits down and types in 1.8 trillion numbers by hand. Instead, the weights start as random numbers — total nonsense — and the network learns by adjusting them over millions of iterations.

This chapter explains how that learning process works. By the end, you'll understand why training AI costs millions of dollars, why you can't just "fix" a wrong answer, and why the quality of training data matters more than the size of the model.

The Starting Point: Pure Randomness

Before training begins, every weight in the network is a random number. The model knows literally nothing. Ask it to complete "The capital of France is..." and it might output "banana sandwich purple 7."

This is important to internalize: there's no built-in knowledge. No one hardcodes facts about France. Every piece of "knowledge" the model eventually displays was learned by adjusting those random weights during training.

So how do random numbers become a system that can write code, explain quantum physics, and debate philosophy? Through a surprisingly simple loop, repeated an absurd number of times.

The Training Loop: Predict, Compare, Adjust, Repeat

The entire training process boils down to four steps, repeated millions of times:

1. Predict
Make a guess
→
2. Compare
Check how wrong
→
3. Adjust
Tweak the weights
→
4. Repeat
Millions of times

Let's walk through each step.

Step 1: Predict

The model receives some input — let's say the text "The cat sat on the" — and makes a prediction about the next word. Early in training, the prediction is basically random. Maybe it says "refrigerator."

Step 2: Compare

We know the actual next word (from the training data) is "mat." So we compare the prediction ("refrigerator") with the truth ("mat") and calculate a score for how wrong the model is. This score is called the loss.

High loss = very wrong. Low loss = pretty close. Zero loss = perfect (which basically never happens).

Step 3: Adjust

Based on how wrong the prediction was, we nudge the weights slightly in a direction that would make the answer less wrong next time. Not by a lot — just a tiny adjustment. Millions of weights get tiny adjustments every single step.

Step 4: Repeat

Do this again. And again. And again. For millions or billions of examples. Each time, the weights get nudged a little bit, and the model's predictions get slightly less terrible.

After billions of iterations, "refrigerator" becomes "mat." The model has learned.

Analogy: Learning to Paint

Imagine teaching someone to paint by showing them millions of paintings and saying "more like this, less like that."

They start with random brushstrokes — complete garbage. You show them a Monet and say: "You're very far off." They adjust slightly. You show them a Van Gogh and say: "Getting warmer." They adjust again. After seeing millions of paintings and getting feedback each time, they start producing things that look like art.

At no point did you explain color theory, perspective, or composition. They learned entirely from examples and feedback. That's exactly how neural networks learn.

Loss Functions: The "How Wrong Am I?" Score

The loss function is the model's report card. After every prediction, it calculates a single number: how far off was the answer?

Think of it like a grade on an exam:

  • Loss = 0 — perfect answer (basically never happens)
  • Loss = 0.1 — very close, minor errors
  • Loss = 5.0 — way off, terrible prediction
  • Loss = 15.0 — the model might as well have guessed randomly

The goal of training is simple: make the loss as small as possible. Every adjustment to the weights is aimed at reducing this number.

For language models specifically, the loss measures: "how surprised was the model by the actual next token?" If the model assigned a high probability to the correct token, the loss is low (it wasn't very surprised). If the correct token was ranked unlikely, the loss is high (it was very surprised).

Tip

When AI researchers say a model is "better trained," they usually mean it has a lower loss on new, unseen data. Everything in training comes down to minimizing this one number.

Gradient Descent: Walking Downhill Blindfolded

OK, so we need to adjust the weights to reduce the loss. But how do we know which direction to adjust them? With trillions of weights, you can't just try random changes and hope for the best.

The answer is gradient descent, and it has the best intuitive analogy in all of AI:

Analogy: Walking Downhill Blindfolded

Imagine you're standing on a hilly landscape, blindfolded, and your goal is to reach the lowest valley. You can't see anything, but you can feel the slope of the ground beneath your feet.

Your strategy is simple: feel which direction slopes downward, and take a step that way. Then feel the slope again, take another step downhill. Repeat.

Eventually, you'll end up in a valley — a low point. Maybe not the absolute lowest point on the entire landscape, but a pretty good one.

That's gradient descent. The "landscape" is the loss function (how wrong the model is for different weight values). The "slope" is the gradient (which direction to adjust weights). Each "step" is a weight update. You keep walking downhill until you find a valley where the loss is low.

The "gradient" is just a fancy word for "which way is downhill." For each weight, the gradient tells you: "if you increase this weight, will the loss go up or down? And by how much?" Then you nudge the weight in whichever direction reduces the loss.

Do this for every weight, every iteration, and the model gradually finds its way to a combination of weights that produces good predictions.

Backpropagation: The Blame Game

There's a practical problem with gradient descent: a large language model has trillions of weights spread across dozens of layers. When the model makes a wrong prediction, which weights are responsible?

Enter backpropagation (or "backprop" for short). It's the algorithm that figures out how much each weight contributed to the error.

Think of it like this: when a software project fails, you do a post-mortem. You trace the failure backward. "The deployment crashed because the config was wrong. The config was wrong because the merge was bad. The merge was bad because the tests didn't catch the bug. The tests didn't catch it because..."

Backpropagation does the same thing for neural networks. It starts at the output (where the error happened) and traces backward through every layer, assigning blame to each weight: "You contributed 0.3% to the error. You contributed 0.001%. You contributed 2.1%."

Then each weight is adjusted in proportion to its blame. Weights that contributed a lot to the error get bigger adjustments. Weights that barely contributed get tiny nudges.

Aha Moment

This is how the network actually learns. Every training step, the model makes a prediction, calculates the error, traces backward through every layer to assign blame, and adjusts every weight accordingly. It's a feedback loop at an incomprehensible scale: trillions of tiny blame assignments and adjustments, billions of times.

And this is also why deeper networks take longer to train — the blame signal has to travel through more layers, and it gets weaker along the way. (This is the "vanishing gradient problem" you might have heard about.)

Learning Rate: The Size of Your Steps

When adjusting weights, you have to choose how big each adjustment should be. This is the learning rate.

Learning rate too high

Weights change too much each step. The model overshoots the valley and bounces around wildly. It never settles on good values. Like running downhill — you miss the valley and fly past it.

Learning rate too low

Weights barely change each step. Training takes forever to converge. You might get stuck in a shallow dip instead of finding the real valley. Like baby steps — you'd reach the bottom eventually, but not in your lifetime.

Finding the right learning rate is one of the most important practical decisions in AI training. Too high and training is unstable. Too low and it takes forever (and costs a fortune in GPU time).

Modern training systems usually start with a higher learning rate (take bigger steps early when you're far from the valley) and gradually decrease it (take smaller, more careful steps as you get closer to a good solution).

Overfitting: Memorizing the Exam vs. Understanding the Subject

Here's a trap that plagues every machine learning project: overfitting.

Imagine a student who memorizes every answer from past exams word-for-word. On practice tests (using old exams), they score 100%. But on a new exam with new questions, they fail — because they memorized specific answers instead of understanding the underlying concepts.

Neural networks can do exactly the same thing. If you train a model too long on the same data, it starts memorizing specific examples rather than learning general patterns. It performs brilliantly on training data and terribly on anything new.

Well-trained model (generalization)

Learns that functions need error handling and return types. Can write error handling for functions it has never seen.

Overfitted model (memorization)

Memorizes that "function parseJSON" always has a specific try/catch block. Can only produce that exact block — falls apart on new function names.

Researchers use various techniques to prevent overfitting: testing on held-out data, adding randomness during training (dropout), and stopping training before the model starts memorizing. The goal is always the same: learn the patterns, not the specific examples.

Tip

This is why AI tools sometimes feel oddly familiar with certain patterns. If a specific coding pattern appeared thousands of times in the training data, the model learned it very well. But if your specific use case is rare or unique, the model has to generalize — and that's where errors creep in.

Training Data: Garbage In, Garbage Out

Everything the model knows comes from its training data. There's no other source of knowledge. If something isn't in the training data — or is rare in the training data — the model can't learn it.

This has massive implications:

  • Data quality trumps model size. A smaller model trained on clean, high-quality data often beats a larger model trained on noisy, messy data. The garbage-in-garbage-out principle has never been more true.
  • Biases in data become biases in the model. If the training data contains mostly Python code, the model will be better at Python than at Kotlin. If the training data underrepresents certain topics, the model will be weak on those topics.
  • Cutoff dates matter. The model only knows about things that existed in its training data. New frameworks, new APIs, recent events — if they're not in the data, the model doesn't know about them.

This is why the same architecture (transformer) produces very different models depending on training data. Train it on books and Wikipedia — you get a general knowledge model. Train it on code repositories — you get a coding model. Train it on medical papers — you get a medical model.

The architecture is the engine. The training data is the fuel. The fuel determines where you can go.

Major AI labs are extremely secretive about their training data, and for good reason — it's one of their biggest competitive advantages. We know that LLMs are generally trained on a mix of web pages, books, academic papers, code, and conversation data, but the exact composition is proprietary.

Pre-training vs. Fine-tuning: Two Phases of Learning

Modern LLMs don't learn in a single phase. They go through at least two distinct stages:

Phase 1: Pre-training — "Learn to Read"

This is the expensive phase. The model sees massive amounts of text (trillions of tokens) and learns to predict the next word. It absorbs grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, code syntax — everything it can extract from the data.

After pre-training, the model is incredibly knowledgeable but not very useful as an assistant. It can continue any text, but it doesn't know how to follow instructions, be helpful, or have a conversation. It's like a brilliant person who will just keep talking without listening to you.

Phase 2: Fine-tuning — "Learn to Be Helpful"

This phase takes the pre-trained model and teaches it to be a good assistant. Using a combination of techniques:

  • Supervised fine-tuning (SFT): Human trainers write examples of ideal question-answer pairs. The model learns to imitate this format.
  • Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF): Human raters compare different model responses and pick the better ones. The model learns to produce responses that humans prefer.

Fine-tuning is much cheaper and faster than pre-training because it's working with a model that already "knows" things — it just needs to learn how to present that knowledge helpfully.

Analogy: Education to Career

Pre-training is like general education — you spend years learning to read, understanding math, absorbing general knowledge from school. It's expensive (years of time, lots of money) and gives you a broad foundation.

Fine-tuning is like professional training — you've already got the general education, now you're learning to be a doctor, lawyer, or software engineer. It's faster, more focused, and builds on everything you already know.

You wouldn't send a first-grader to law school. Likewise, you can't fine-tune a model that hasn't been pre-trained. The foundation comes first.

Training a frontier LLM isn't something you do on your laptop over a weekend. It requires warehouse-scale data centers filled with specialized hardware, running for months, consuming enormous amounts of electricity.

This is why there are only a handful of companies training frontier models (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta). The barrier to entry isn't knowledge — the techniques are well-published. It's cost.

Why You Can't Just "Fix" a Wrong Answer

A common frustration: "Claude just told me something wrong. Why can't they just fix it?"

Now you understand why. The model's "knowledge" isn't stored in a database you can edit. It's distributed across trillions of weights that were learned during training. There's no "Paris is the capital of France" stored in a specific location. That knowledge is encoded as a complex pattern of weights across millions of neurons.

To "fix" one wrong answer, you'd need to retrain or fine-tune the model, which means:

  • Adjusting many weights, not just one
  • Making sure the fix doesn't break other things the model gets right
  • Spending significant compute resources
  • Testing extensively to avoid new errors

Aha Moment

This is fundamentally different from a traditional software bug. In your code, if a function returns the wrong value, you find the line, fix it, done. But in a neural network, there's no "line" to fix. The wrong answer emerges from the interaction of billions of weights, and changing one might fix this answer while breaking ten others.

It's like trying to change one memory in a human brain without affecting any other memories. The knowledge is interconnected, not isolated.

Why This Matters for You

When AI tools get something wrong, the fix isn't to "tell the model it's wrong." The model won't remember your correction in the next conversation. Instead, work around it: provide more context, rephrase your prompt, or give explicit examples of what you want. You're giving the model better input patterns to match against, not "teaching" it.

Putting It All Together

Here's the complete picture of how AI goes from knowing nothing to writing your code:

  • Training starts with random weights — the model knows absolutely nothing.
  • The training loop (predict → compare → adjust → repeat) runs billions of times, gradually reducing the loss.
  • Gradient descent is "walk downhill" — adjust weights in the direction that reduces error.
  • Backpropagation assigns blame to each weight, so the adjustments are targeted, not random.
  • Overfitting happens when the model memorizes examples instead of learning patterns. Researchers actively fight this.
  • Training data quality matters more than model size. Garbage in, garbage out.
  • Pre-training teaches the model to understand language. Fine-tuning teaches it to be helpful.
  • Training frontier models costs $100M+ and takes months on thousands of GPUs. This is why you can't just "retrain" to fix an error.
