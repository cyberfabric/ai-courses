Chapter 2

Neural Networks Without the Math

Neurons, layers, and weights — the intuition behind it all, using analogies not equations

In Chapter 1, we learned that AI is really just sophisticated pattern matching. But how does it match patterns? What's the machinery that makes this possible?

The answer is neural networks — the engine inside every AI tool you use. And despite the intimidating name, the core idea is surprisingly simple.

By the end of this chapter, you'll understand what neurons, layers, and weights actually are. You'll know why "deep" learning is called "deep." And you'll be able to trace, at a high level, what happens to your prompt as it flows through the network. No equations required.

The Biological Inspiration (and Why It Breaks Down)

Neural networks are loosely inspired by the human brain. The keyword is loosely.

Your brain has roughly 86 billion neurons. Each neuron receives signals from other neurons, does some processing, and either fires a signal onward or stays quiet. Neurons connect to each other in vast networks, and from these connections emerge your thoughts, memories, and ability to recognize your friend's face in a crowd.

Artificial neural networks borrow this idea: they're made of simple units (also called "neurons") connected together in a network. Each unit takes in numbers, does a tiny calculation, and passes its result forward.

Don't Take the Analogy Too Far

Artificial neurons are not brain cells. They don't "think." They don't have consciousness. A single artificial neuron is just a tiny math operation. The brain analogy is useful for understanding the structure (lots of connected units) but misleading for understanding the experience (there is none). Think of it like how airplanes are "inspired by birds" — the principles overlap, but the mechanism is completely different.

A Single Neuron: The Voting System

Let's start with the smallest building block: one single artificial neuron. Forget the biology — think of it as a tiny decision-maker.

Here's what a neuron does:

  1. It receives inputs — numbers coming from somewhere (raw data, or the output of other neurons).
  2. Each input has a weight — a number that says "how important is this input?" High weight = "I care about this a lot." Low weight = "this barely matters."
  3. It adds up the weighted inputs — multiply each input by its weight, then add them together.
  4. It adds a bias — a constant offset that shifts the result up or down (think of it as the neuron's default leaning).
  5. It applies an activation function — a rule that decides "given this total, what should I output?" Usually this means: if the total is high enough, fire a strong signal. If it's low, output nearly nothing.

That's it. That's the whole neuron. Inputs × weights + bias → activation → output.

Analogy: The Committee Vote

Imagine a hiring committee deciding whether to approve a candidate. Each committee member (input) gives a score. But not all members have equal influence — the CTO's opinion (high weight) matters more than the intern's (low weight).

The weighted scores get added up. There's also a company policy bias: "we generally prefer to hire" (positive bias) or "we're very selective" (negative bias).

Finally, a threshold is applied: if the total score is above a certain level, the candidate is approved (neuron fires). Below it, they're rejected (neuron stays quiet).

One artificial neuron works exactly like this — a weighted vote followed by a threshold decision. Simple on its own, but when you connect thousands of these together, things get interesting.

What Are Weights, Really?

Weights are just numbers. Positive, negative, large, small — they're the knobs that control what the neuron pays attention to.

A weight of 0.9 means "this input is very important." A weight of 0.01 means "I mostly ignore this." A weight of -0.5 means "the presence of this input actually pushes me away from firing."

Here's the critical part: no human sets these weights by hand. They start as random numbers, and the network learns to adjust them during training. We'll cover exactly how in Chapter 3, but for now, just know this: training a neural network is basically the process of finding the right weights.

What's the Activation Function?

The activation function is the neuron's decision rule. Without it, a neural network would just be a chain of additions and multiplications — which can only compute straight lines. That's useless for real-world problems.

The activation function introduces non-linearity — which is a fancy way of saying "it lets the network learn curves, not just straight lines." Think of it as the neuron saying: "Given everything I've heard, am I excited enough to pass the signal forward?"

The most common one these days is called ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit), which has the simplest possible rule: if the input is positive, pass it through. If it's negative, output zero. That's it.

Tip

You don't need to memorize activation function names. The key intuition is: activation functions let neurons make non-obvious decisions. Without them, the whole network would be no more powerful than a single neuron — no matter how many you chain together.

Layers: Where the Magic Happens

A single neuron can make a tiny decision. But the power of neural networks comes from connecting many neurons together in layers.

A typical neural network has three types of layers:

  • Input layer — receives the raw data. For a language model, this is your tokenized text. For an image model, it's the pixel values.
  • Hidden layers — the middle layers where the actual computation happens. The "hidden" name just means you don't directly see their input or output — they're internal.
  • Output layer — produces the final result. For a language model, this is the probability of each possible next token.

Each neuron in one layer connects to every neuron in the next layer. The output of layer 1 becomes the input of layer 2, which becomes the input of layer 3, and so on.

The Abstraction Ladder

Here's where things get fascinating. Each layer in the network learns to recognize patterns at a different level of abstraction. The deeper you go, the more complex the patterns become.

Let's use image recognition as an example, because it's the most visual:

Layer 1
Edges & lines
→
Layer 2
Shapes & textures
→
Layer 3
Parts (eyes, ears)
→
Layer 4
Whole objects (faces)
→
Output
"It's a cat"

The first layer learns to detect simple edges and color transitions. The second layer combines edges into shapes like circles and corners. The third layer recognizes parts — something that looks like an eye, an ear, a whisker. The fourth layer puts those parts together into whole objects. And the output says: "cat."

No one programs these layers to look for edges or ears. The network discovers these abstractions on its own during training. That's what makes it so powerful — and so hard to predict.

Aha Moment

Neural networks automatically build their own hierarchy of concepts. Low-level layers handle simple patterns, high-level layers handle complex ones. This is exactly how your brain works too — your visual cortex processes edges first, then shapes, then objects, then meaning.

The same principle applies to language models. Early layers might recognize character patterns and word boundaries. Middle layers understand grammar and syntax. Later layers capture meaning, tone, and context. By the time your prompt reaches the output layer, the network has built up an incredibly rich understanding of your text — all through layers of increasingly abstract pattern recognition.

The Same Idea for Language

For LLMs processing your code or text, the abstraction ladder works similarly, just with language instead of images:

Layer Depth What It Recognizes Example
Early layers Token patterns, common sequences "func" often followed by a name
Middle layers Grammar, syntax, structure This looks like a function definition
Deeper layers Meaning, intent, context This function calculates a total
Deepest layers Relationships, reasoning This function has a bug in the loop

What "Deep" in Deep Learning Actually Means

Here's one of the most anticlimactic reveals in all of AI:

"Deep" learning just means "lots of layers."

That's it. A neural network with 2 layers is "shallow." A network with 50, 100, or 1,000 layers is "deep." The term "deep learning" literally means "learning with many layers." No philosophical depth involved.

Early neural networks (1990s) might have had 2-3 layers. Modern large language models have dozens to over a hundred layers. GPT-3 has 96 layers. The more layers, the more abstract and complex the patterns the network can learn.

Tip

When someone says "deep learning model" at a meeting, mentally translate it to "neural network with many layers." That's all it means. The word "deep" refers to the architecture, not to the quality of understanding.

Parameters: The Knobs That Make It Work

In Chapter 1, we mentioned that GPT-4 has roughly 1.8 trillion "parameters." But what are parameters?

Parameters are all the weights and biases in the network. Every connection between every neuron in every layer has a weight. Every neuron has a bias. Add them all up, and you get the total parameter count.

Here's how the scale has evolved:

~100K
Early neural nets (1990s)
175B
GPT-3 (2020)
~1.8T
GPT-4 (estimated, 2023)

That's a jump from one hundred thousand to one point eight trillion in about 30 years.

What Does "1.8 Trillion Parameters" Actually Mean?

Let's make this concrete. Each parameter is a single number (a decimal like 0.0472 or -1.338). If you printed every parameter of GPT-4 on paper, one number per line:

  • You'd need about 3.6 billion pages (500 numbers per page)
  • Stacked, that paper would reach roughly 360,000 kilometers — almost the distance to the Moon
  • At one number per second, reading them all would take about 57,000 years

And every single one of those numbers was learned during training — adjusted tiny bit by tiny bit, millions of times, until the network could predict text well.

Analogy: The Massive Phone Tree

Imagine a neural network as a massive phone tree. Your prompt enters at the top, and it gets passed through millions of people arranged in rows (layers).

Each person receives calls from multiple people in the row above. They listen to all of them, but they don't trust each caller equally — they have a personal rating for each one (weights). They combine what they hear, add their own gut feeling (bias), and make a quick decision: "Based on all this, here's what I'll pass to the people in the next row."

The first row of people handles simple observations: "This seems to be about code." The next row gets more specific: "It looks like Python." Deeper rows understand context: "It's a function that parses JSON." The final row says: "Here's the most likely next token."

No single person understands the full picture. But collectively, through millions of tiny weighted decisions, the phone tree produces something that looks like understanding.

Why Size Matters (But Isn't Everything)

More parameters generally means the model can capture more complex patterns. A 7 billion parameter model can handle straightforward tasks well. A 70 billion parameter model can handle nuance and ambiguity. A model in the trillions can do things that surprise even its creators.

But size alone isn't the whole story. How the model is trained, what data it sees, and how the architecture is designed all matter enormously. A well-trained 70B model can outperform a poorly-trained 200B model. Size is the starting budget; training decides how it's spent.

Bigger models excel at:

Nuanced reasoning Ambiguous instructions Complex code generation Multi-step tasks Understanding context

Where bigger doesn't help much:

Simple factual lookups Basic formatting tasks Following clear templates Tasks with clear right/wrong answers

The Universal Approximator (In Plain English)

There's a famous result in AI called the universal approximation theorem. Sounds terrifying, but the idea is simple:

A neural network with enough neurons can learn to approximate any pattern.

Any input-output relationship you can imagine — no matter how complex — a sufficiently large neural network can learn to mimic it. Image recognition? It can learn that. Language translation? That too. Code generation? Yep.

Think of it like LEGO bricks. One LEGO brick can't do much. But with enough bricks, you can build anything — a house, a car, the Eiffel Tower. Individual neurons are the bricks. Given enough of them, connected in the right way, the network can represent any pattern.

The catch? "Enough neurons" might mean a lot of neurons. And finding the right weights for all of them requires enormous amounts of data and computing power. The theorem says it's possible — it doesn't say it's easy or practical.

A neural network is a general-purpose pattern learner. It doesn't know anything about language, images, or code. It just learns whatever patterns exist in the data you show it. Show it text — it learns language patterns. Show it images — it learns visual patterns. Show it code — it learns coding patterns.

The architecture is the same. Only the training data changes.

How This Connects to the LLMs You Use

Let's trace what happens when you type a prompt into Claude Code or ChatGPT. At a high level, here's the journey:

Step 1
Your prompt is tokenized
→
Step 2
Tokens enter the network
→
Step 3
Through dozens of layers
→
Step 4
Probabilities come out
→
Step 5
Next token is picked
  1. Tokenization: Your text is broken into tokens (pieces of words). We'll cover this in detail in Chapter 4.
  2. Input: Each token is converted into a list of numbers (an embedding — Chapter 9) and fed into the first layer.
  3. Processing: The data flows through layer after layer. Each layer detects more abstract patterns, building on the previous layer's work. With each layer, the network "understands" more about your prompt.
  4. Output: The final layer outputs a probability for every possible next token. "The next token is 42% likely to be 'function', 15% likely to be 'const', 8% likely to be 'class'..."
  5. Selection: One token is chosen from these probabilities. It gets appended to your prompt, and the whole process repeats for the next token.

Every word you see appearing in Claude Code's response went through this entire pipeline. Every. Single. Token. That's why responses appear one word at a time — each one requires a full pass through the network.

Aha Moment

When you see Claude Code streaming a response token by token, you're literally watching the network run in real time. Each word that appears represents a complete journey through billions of parameters, dozens of layers, and millions of tiny weighted decisions.

This is also why longer responses take longer — the model has to do a full forward pass through the entire network for every single token it generates. A 500-word response means roughly 700 full passes through the network.

Tip

This is why giving the model good context matters so much. All of your prompt — every file you include, every instruction in CLAUDE.md — flows through the same layers. Better input means the patterns the network matches in those layers are more relevant to your actual problem. Garbage in, garbage out applies at every single layer.

Putting It All Together

Here's what you now understand about the engine behind every AI tool you use:

  • A neuron is a tiny decision-maker: weighted inputs + bias + activation = output.
  • Neurons are organized in layers. Each layer learns progressively more abstract patterns.
  • "Deep" learning just means many layers — nothing philosophically deep about it.
  • Parameters (weights + biases) are the learned values. Modern LLMs have billions to trillions of them.
  • Neural networks are universal pattern learners — the same architecture learns language, images, or code depending on the training data.
  • Your prompt flows through all these layers, and each layer builds a more abstract understanding. The output is a probability distribution over all possible next tokens.

Exercise: Trace a Tiny Network

Grab a piece of paper and draw a tiny neural network with 3 layers:

  • Input layer: 3 neurons (imagine they receive: "how complex is the task," "how urgent is the deadline," "how many people are involved")
  • Hidden layer: 2 neurons
  • Output layer: 1 neuron (outputs: "how many hours to estimate for this task")

Draw lines connecting every neuron in one layer to every neuron in the next layer. Count the total connections — that's your parameter count (weights only, ignoring biases for simplicity).

How many connections did you get? (3 × 2) + (2 × 1) = 8 weights. Now imagine GPT-4 with trillions of these connections. The architecture is the same — it's just the scale that's mind-boggling.

Bonus: Imagine one input neuron has a weight of 0.9 and another has a weight of 0.1. What does that tell you about which input the hidden neuron "cares about" more?
