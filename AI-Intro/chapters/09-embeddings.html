Chapter 9

Embeddings: How AI Understands Meaning

Vectors, similarity, semantic search — the bridge to RAG and beyond

Throughout this book, we've been saying that AI converts text into numbers. Tokens become IDs. IDs become "numerical representations." Those representations flow through the transformer.

But we never explained what those numbers actually are. What does a "numerical representation of meaning" look like? How can a list of numbers capture the difference between "dog" and "democracy"?

The answer is embeddings — one of the most elegant and practically useful ideas in all of AI. By the end of this chapter, you'll understand how AI represents meaning, why similar things end up "near" each other, and why this concept is the foundation of tools you'll use every day as a developer.

The Problem: Computers Can't Understand "Similar"

For a computer, the strings "dog" and "puppy" are completely different. Different characters, different lengths, different byte values. A simple string comparison says they have nothing in common.

But you and I know they're deeply related — they refer to the same animal, just at different ages. How do you teach a computer that "dog" and "puppy" are more similar to each other than "dog" and "refrigerator"?

You can't hardcode every relationship. There are too many words, too many concepts, too many nuances. "Happy," "joyful," "glad," and "content" are all related but slightly different. "Bank" is related to "money" in one context and "river" in another.

We need a way to represent meaning that captures these relationships automatically. That's what embeddings do.

The Solution: Turn Meaning into Numbers

An embedding is a list of numbers (a vector) that represents the meaning of a piece of text. Not the spelling or the sound — the meaning.

What does this look like? A typical embedding for one word might be a list of 768 to 3,072 numbers:

What an embedding looks like (simplified — real ones have 768+ numbers)

"dog" → [0.21, -0.45, 0.89, 0.03, -0.67, ...] "puppy" → [0.19, -0.42, 0.91, 0.05, -0.63, ...] "car" → [-0.71, 0.33, -0.12, 0.88, 0.45, ...] Notice: "dog" and "puppy" have very similar numbers. "car" has completely different numbers.

Each number in the embedding represents some learned aspect of meaning. Nobody programmed what each dimension means — the model figured it out during training. One dimension might roughly correspond to "is this animate?", another to "is this positive?", another to something so abstract it doesn't have a human name.

The key insight is this: things with similar meanings get similar numbers. The embedding for "dog" is numerically close to the embedding for "puppy," "canine," and "pet." It's far from the embeddings for "algebra," "democracy," and "pizza."

Analogy: GPS Coordinates for Meaning

Embeddings are like GPS coordinates for meaning. Just as GPS gives every location on Earth a pair of numbers (latitude, longitude), embeddings give every concept a set of numbers that pin it to a location in "meaning space."

"Dog" and "puppy" have similar GPS coordinates — they're in the same neighborhood. "Dog" and "democracy" are on different continents. "King" and "queen" are in the same city but different buildings.

The difference? GPS uses 2 numbers. Embeddings use 768 to 3,072 numbers. More dimensions mean more nuance — the model can capture subtle distinctions that 2D coordinates never could.

The Classic Example: King - Man + Woman = Queen

When embeddings were first discovered, one result blew researchers' minds:

If you take the embedding for "king," subtract the embedding for "man," and add the embedding for "woman," the result is closest to the embedding for "queen."

king − man + woman ≈ queen

The model learned that "king" and "queen" have the same relationship as "man" and "woman" — purely from reading text. Nobody taught it this. It emerged from the patterns in the data.

This works for other relationships too:

  • Paris − France + Japan ≈ Tokyo (capital city relationship)
  • bigger − big + small ≈ smaller (comparative relationship)
  • walking − walk + swim ≈ swimming (tense relationship)

The embedding space has structure. Relationships between concepts are encoded as directions in this high-dimensional space. The "gender direction" points from king to queen, from man to woman, from uncle to aunt. The model discovered these directions on its own.

Aha Moment

This is what people mean when they say AI "understands" language. It doesn't understand the way you do — it has no experience of royalty or gender. But it has learned to represent these concepts as points in a space where relationships are preserved as geometric patterns.

"Understanding" in AI is geometric. Similar meanings are nearby. Relationships are directions. Analogies are parallelisms. The model didn't learn dictionary definitions — it learned the shape of meaning.

From Words to Sentences (and Beyond)

Word embeddings are fascinating, but modern AI goes much further. You can compute embeddings for entire sentences, paragraphs, or even documents.

A sentence embedding takes the whole meaning of a sentence and encodes it as a single vector. This means:

  • "How do I sort a list in Python?" and "What's the best way to order an array in Python?" would have very similar embeddings — same meaning, different words.
  • "How do I sort a list in Python?" and "What's the weather like today?" would have very different embeddings — completely unrelated topics.

This is enormously powerful because it lets you compare the meaning of text, not just the words. Two questions can use completely different vocabulary but get nearly identical embeddings because they're asking the same thing.

Cosine Similarity: Measuring "How Close?"

If embeddings are GPS coordinates, we need a way to measure distance between them. The most common measure is cosine similarity.

You don't need the formula. Just understand what it tells you:

Cosine Similarity What It Means Example
1.0 Identical meaning "dog" vs. "dog"
0.85–0.99 Very similar "dog" vs. "puppy"
0.5–0.85 Somewhat related "dog" vs. "cat"
0.0–0.5 Unrelated "dog" vs. "democracy"
< 0 Opposite or very different "hot" vs. "cold" (sometimes)

Think of it as a similarity score from -1 to 1. The higher the number, the more similar the meanings. This one number lets you answer the question "how related are these two pieces of text?" for any two texts in any language.

Practical Use #1: Semantic Search

Traditional keyword search is literal. Search for "fix array sorting bug" and it finds documents containing those exact words. If a document says "resolve list ordering issue" — same meaning, different words — keyword search misses it.

Semantic search uses embeddings instead. It converts your query into an embedding, then finds documents whose embeddings are closest to yours. Since embeddings capture meaning, it finds relevant results regardless of the specific words used.

Keyword search

Query: "fix array sorting bug" ✓ "Fixed array sorting bug in utils.js" ✓ "Array sorting bug report #1234" ✗ "Resolved list ordering issue in helpers" ✗ "Corrected the sequence arrangement problem" Misses relevant results that use different words for the same concept.

Semantic search (embeddings)

Query: "fix array sorting bug" ✓ "Fixed array sorting bug in utils.js" ✓ "Array sorting bug report #1234" ✓ "Resolved list ordering issue in helpers" ✓ "Corrected the sequence arrangement problem" Finds results by meaning, not just keywords. Different words, same concept = match.

This is how Cursor finds relevant code files when you ask a question about your codebase. It doesn't just grep for keywords — it computes embeddings of your question and of your code, then finds the code that's semantically closest to what you're asking about.

Practical Use #2: Vector Databases

If you want to do semantic search over a large dataset, you need a place to store all those embeddings. Enter vector databases — databases specifically designed to store, index, and search high-dimensional vectors efficiently.

PC
Pinecone
Cloud-hosted vector database. Store millions of embeddings and find the nearest neighbors in milliseconds. Popular for production RAG systems.
CH
Chroma
Open-source, lightweight vector database. Great for local development and prototyping. Can run embedded in your Python application.
WV
Weaviate
Open-source vector database with built-in embedding generation. Supports hybrid search (combining keyword and semantic search).

The workflow is straightforward: compute embeddings for all your documents once, store them in a vector database, then at query time, compute the embedding of the user's question and ask the database "find me the 5 most similar documents." The database returns them in milliseconds.

Why This Matters: The Foundation of RAG

There's a widely-used pattern in AI applications called RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation). It solves a critical problem: how do you get an LLM to answer questions about your data — data the model has never seen in training?

The answer uses everything from this chapter:

1. Embed
Your documents → vectors
→
2. Store
Vectors → database
→
3. Query
User question → vector
→
4. Retrieve
Find similar docs
→
5. Generate
LLM + relevant docs → answer
  1. Embed: Convert all your documents (product docs, codebase, knowledge base) into embeddings.
  2. Store: Put those embeddings in a vector database.
  3. Query: When a user asks a question, embed their question.
  4. Retrieve: Find the most similar documents using cosine similarity.
  5. Generate: Pass those documents to an LLM as context, along with the user's question. The LLM generates an answer grounded in your actual data.

This is how AI chatbots that "know about your company's products" work. They're not trained on your data — they use embeddings to retrieve relevant information and paste it into the context window (Chapter 8) before the model generates a response.

Aha Moment

RAG is the most common way to connect an LLM to your private data, and it's built entirely on embeddings. Without embeddings, there's no way to figure out which documents are relevant to a user's question. Without that retrieval step, the LLM would have to guess (and hallucinate).

This is also how coding tools like Cursor search your codebase. They embed your code files, embed your question, find the most semantically relevant files, and include those in the context. Every time Cursor magically finds the right file to reference, embeddings are doing the heavy lifting.

Tip

If you ever build an AI-powered feature that needs to answer questions about specific data (documentation, knowledge bases, internal wikis), RAG with embeddings is almost certainly the right approach. It's cheaper than fine-tuning, easier to update (just re-embed new documents), and keeps your data private (it never becomes part of the model's weights).

How Embeddings Are Created

Embeddings come from neural networks (surprise!). Specialized models are trained specifically to produce good embeddings. Some popular embedding models:

768
Dimensions in OpenAI's text-embedding-3-small
3,072
Dimensions in text-embedding-3-large
1,024
Dimensions in Cohere's embed-v3

These models are trained with a simple objective: make sure that texts with similar meanings produce similar embeddings, and texts with different meanings produce different embeddings. The training data is typically pairs of texts that are known to be similar (like question-answer pairs, or paraphrases).

You don't need to train your own embedding model. You call an API, send it text, and get back a list of numbers. It's one of the simplest AI APIs to use.

Visualizing High Dimensions

You might be wondering: how do you visualize 768 dimensions? You can't, really. Humans can visualize 2 or 3 dimensions. But the concept still works.

Think of it this way: in a 2D space, you can position cities on a flat map. Nearby cities are close on the map. But a flat map can't capture elevation, climate, population, or culture. In a 3D space, you get elevation. In 768 dimensions, you get 768 independent aspects of meaning that can all be represented simultaneously.

Researchers use dimensionality reduction techniques (like t-SNE or UMAP) to squish 768 dimensions down to 2D for visualization. The result is a "map of meaning" where you can see clusters: all the programming-related words cluster together, all the food words cluster together, all the sports words cluster together. It's a beautiful visual confirmation that embeddings really do capture meaning.

Putting It All Together

  • Embeddings are lists of numbers (vectors) that represent the meaning of text.
  • Similar meanings produce similar numbers — "dog" and "puppy" are close; "dog" and "democracy" are far apart.
  • Relationships are encoded as directions: king − man + woman ≈ queen.
  • Cosine similarity measures how close two embeddings are (0 = unrelated, 1 = identical meaning).
  • Semantic search uses embeddings to find relevant results by meaning, not just keywords.
  • Vector databases (Pinecone, Chroma, Weaviate) store and search embeddings at scale.
  • RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) uses embeddings to connect LLMs to your private data.
  • Embedding models are easy to use — send text to an API, get back numbers.

Exercise: Think Like an Embedding

Without using any tools, think about these word pairs and rate their semantic similarity from 0 (unrelated) to 1 (identical meaning):

Word A Word B Your Rating (0–1)
car automobile
happy joyful
Python snake
array list
bug insect

Now consider: would a keyword search find the connection between "car" and "automobile"? Between "array" and "list"? That's exactly the problem embeddings solve.

Bonus: Notice how "Python/snake" and "bug/insect" are tricky. In a programming context, these pairs are unrelated. In everyday English, they're synonyms. This illustrates why context matters for embeddings — and why modern embedding models take the surrounding text into account, not just the word in isolation.
