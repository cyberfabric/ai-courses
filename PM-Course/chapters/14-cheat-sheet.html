The Complete PM Workflow

Your daily routine in ten steps (Chapter 12):

  1. Receive ticket from your project tracker
  2. Open project in Windsurf
  3. Create branch: mob456-feature-name
  4. Create change: openspec new change mob456-feature-name
  5. Propose specs: /opsx:propose in Cascade
  6. Review and refine the generated proposal and specs
  7. Validate: openspec validate
  8. Commit: stage all, write message with ticket number, commit
  9. Push: Publish Branch
  10. Create PR on BitBucket with title, description, and reviewers

What to Learn Next (If You're Curious)

This course taught you everything you need for the PM workflow. But if you want to explore further, here are some natural next steps:

  • Markdown — the formatting language used in proposal.md and spec files. It's simple (headings, bold, lists) and takes 15 minutes to learn.
  • Reading Pull Requests — start reading other people's PRs on BitBucket. You don't need to understand the code, but you'll learn how the team communicates.
  • Git branching strategies — understand how your team organizes branches (e.g., feature branches, release branches). Ask a developer to explain your team's approach.
  • YAML basics — the format used in OpenSpec's config.yaml. If you ever need to tweak settings, knowing YAML's indentation rules helps.

None of these are required. They're just directions you might naturally drift toward as you get more comfortable.

One Final Thought

You Did It

Fourteen chapters ago, you didn't know what a terminal was. Now you can navigate folders, track changes with Git, push to BitBucket, create Pull Requests, and write structured specs with OpenSpec. You can take a ticket from the backlog and deliver a complete set of behavioral specifications — ready for developers and AI to act on.

That's not "kind of technical." That's a real, tangible skill that makes you a more effective PM. You're not on the outside looking in anymore — you're part of the workflow.

Every expert was once a beginner who kept going. The first few times you run through the workflow, you'll check this cheat sheet. After a few weeks, you won't need it. After a few months, you'll be the one explaining it to the next PM who joins the team.
