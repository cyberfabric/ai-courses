Video Understanding & Analysis

Memory Map

The Big Idea

Video = images + audio + time. A single frame tells you what's in the scene. The sequence tells you the story. You don't need every frame — clever sampling and the right model let you understand hours of video with a handful of images and a transcript.

🎬 Why Video Is the Hardest Modality

🖼️ Visual Stream (frames)
🔊 Audio Stream (speech, sounds)
🎬 Video
⏱️ Temporal Structure (time)
🔗 Cross-Modal Links
📊
Scale of Data
1 image = 6M numbers. 1 min of video at 30fps = 10.8 billion numbers. Almost 2,000x more data than a single image.
⏱️
Temporal Reasoning
No single frame shows "the ball was thrown." Only the sequence reveals actions, causation, and narrative. This is what makes video uniquely hard.

🎥 Analogy: Understanding a single image = looking at a photograph. Understanding video = watching a movie with subtitles in a foreign language.

🖼️ Frame Extraction Strategies

  1. 📐 Uniform Sampling

    Grab 1 frame every N seconds. Simple, predictable. Good default: 1fps for action, 1 per 10s for talking heads.

  2. 🎬 Scene Detection

    Detect camera cuts and visual changes with PySceneDetect. Sample one frame per scene — no wasted tokens on identical frames.

  3. 🔑 Keyframe Extraction

    Video codecs already store reference frames (I-frames) at scene boundaries. Natural sampling points, built into the file.

🧩 Core Concepts

🎯
Action Recognition
What activity is occurring? Walking, cooking, typing? LLMs work for one-off analysis; specialized models (SlowFast, VideoMAE) for high-volume/real-time.
👁️
Object Tracking
Follow a specific object across all frames. SAM 2 (Meta): click on any object in one frame, it tracks through the entire video. Like keeping your eye on one player during a match.
❓
Video Q&A
Ask specific questions: "How many people?", "What brand of laptop?", "At what point does the demo fail?" Surprisingly detailed answers from frame sequences.
📋
Two-Pass Summarization
Pass 1: Break video into segments, analyze each. Pass 2: Combine segment descriptions into a coherent summary. Works for any video length.
🔗
Audio-Visual Fusion
Analyze visual frames AND audio transcript together. Timestamps are the glue. "At 2:30, speaker says X and screen shows Y" beats separate lists.
⚠️
Video Hallucinations
LLMs fill gaps between sampled frames with invented details. Standing → sitting = model invents "sat down gracefully." Always validate critical findings.

🧰 Your Video AI Toolkit

🎬 FFmpeg
Swiss Army knife: extract frames, separate audio, resize, convert formats. Non-negotiable for video work. Free, open-source.
🔍 PySceneDetect
Detect scene changes for intelligent frame sampling. Sample at boundaries, not blindly.
🎯 SAM 2
Meta's object segmentation & tracking. Click object in frame 1 → tracked through entire video. Open source, state-of-the-art.
📊 Twelve Labs
Dedicated video understanding API. Natural language search across videos: "find the moment the CEO mentions revenue."
🐍 OpenCV (cv2)
Classic computer vision library. Low-level frame manipulation, face detection, optical flow (motion between frames).

⚡ Tips & Pitfalls

✅ Do ❌ Don't
Include context: "45-min product demo" Send frames with just "What's in this video?"
Label frames with timestamps (t=0:05) Send unlabeled frames (model can't reason about timing)
1 frame/30-60s for talking heads 1fps on a 30-min meeting (1,800 frames, massive cost)
Validate critical findings against footage Trust model's invented gap-filling between frames
Spend token budget on transcript for talk-heavy videos Over-sample visually static content

🧪 Quick Quiz

Test Your Understanding

Why can't you understand video by looking at a single frame?

A single frame shows what's in the scene, but temporal reasoning — understanding actions, causation, and narrative — requires seeing how frames change over time. "The ball was thrown" only emerges from the sequence.

What are the three frame sampling strategies?

1) Uniform sampling (every N seconds), 2) Scene detection (sample at visual changes, using PySceneDetect), 3) Keyframe extraction (use the codec's built-in I-frames). Scene detection is most token-efficient.

Which model currently leads for video understanding and why?

Gemini 1.5 Pro — native video input (no frame extraction needed), supports up to 1-hour videos, built-in audio analysis, excellent temporal reasoning, 1M+ token context window.

What's the "two-pass" video summarization approach?

Pass 1: Break video into 30-60s segments, analyze each with frames + audio. Pass 2: Collect all segment descriptions, send to LLM for a coherent unified summary with key moments and timestamps. Scales to any video length.

Why should you combine visual and audio analysis rather than doing just one?

Visual frames show what's on screen; audio transcript captures what's said. Together with timestamps as the glue, you get full understanding. Example: lecture transcript + slide frames = complete notes.

Key Takeaways

  • Video = visual stream + audio stream + temporal structure. The sequence tells the story, not any single frame.
  • Frame extraction is the practical workhorse: FFmpeg + sampling strategy + vision LLM. Scene detection is the most efficient sampling.
  • Gemini = native video (easiest). Claude/GPT-4o = frame extraction (great for structured analysis). ~20 frames max per request.
  • Combine Whisper transcript + frame analysis + timestamps for the richest video understanding. Timestamps are the glue.
  • LLMs for one-off analysis; dedicated models (SAM 2, SlowFast) for high-volume real-time tasks like tracking and action recognition.
  • Watch out for video hallucinations — models fill gaps between sampled frames with invented details. Always validate critical findings.
