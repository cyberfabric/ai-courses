CHAPTER 7

Speech-to-Text & Audio Understanding

From sound waves to structured text — how AI learns to listen

From Eyes to Ears

For the last three chapters, we've been all about vision — sending images to LLMs, extracting data from documents, even generating images from text. Now we switch senses entirely. It's time to talk about sound.

You already use speech-to-text every day, even if you don't think about it. Dictating a text message, asking Siri a question, watching YouTube with auto-generated captions, joining a meeting that gets auto-transcribed — all of it is powered by the same fundamental technology we're about to explore.

The Big Idea

Speech-to-text used to require expensive, specialized systems trained on narrow domains. Today, a single model — OpenAI's Whisper — can transcribe speech in 100 languages, handle accents, filter background noise, and even add punctuation. It's open source, free to run locally, and it changed everything about how developers work with audio.

But modern audio AI goes far beyond just typing out words someone said. It can identify who is speaking, classify sounds (is that music or speech?), detect emotions in voice, and extract meaning from audio that has nothing to do with words. By the end of this chapter, you'll understand how all of it works and be ready to build with it.

How Speech-to-Text Actually Works

At its core, speech-to-text solves a deceptively hard problem: turn vibrations in the air into written words. Here's how modern systems do it, step by step.

Step 1: Sound Becomes Numbers

Sound is a continuous wave — pressure changes in the air over time. To process it digitally, we sample the wave at regular intervals. Standard audio is sampled 16,000 times per second (16kHz) for speech, or 44,100 times per second (44.1kHz) for music. Each sample is just a number representing the air pressure at that instant.

So a 10-second audio clip at 16kHz is literally a list of 160,000 numbers. That's your raw input.

Step 2: Numbers Become a Spectrogram

A raw list of numbers is hard to work with. Instead, we transform the audio into a spectrogram — a 2D image that shows which frequencies are present at each moment in time. Think of it as a heat map: the x-axis is time, the y-axis is frequency (pitch), and the brightness shows how loud each frequency is.

Audio as an Image

Here's something beautiful: once audio is converted to a spectrogram, it literally looks like an image. And that means all the vision techniques we learned in earlier chapters — encoders, transformers, attention — can be applied to audio too. A spectrogram of someone saying "hello" has a distinct visual shape, just like the letter "A" has a distinct shape on a page. The model learns to recognize these visual patterns in sound. This is why audio AI and vision AI share so much DNA.

Step 3: An Encoder Extracts Features

The spectrogram goes into an audio encoder — a neural network (usually a Transformer) that looks at the spectrogram and extracts meaningful features. It learns things like: "This pattern of frequencies is the sound 'sh'," "This rise in pitch means a question," "This frequency range is background noise, ignore it."

The encoder produces a sequence of audio embeddings — dense vectors of numbers that represent what's happening in the audio over time. Sound familiar? It's the same concept as image embeddings from Chapter 2, just for a different modality.

Step 4: A Decoder Generates Text

The audio embeddings go into a decoder — essentially a language model that takes audio features as input and produces text tokens as output. It uses attention to "look at" the audio embeddings while generating each word, deciding what was said based on the acoustic evidence and its language understanding.

The Speech-to-Text Pipeline

Sound Wave → Sample (16kHz) → Spectrogram → Audio Encoder → Text Decoder → Transcription

The analogy from the chapter outline is spot-on: speech-to-text is like a court stenographer who also understands context — they don't just type what they hear, they understand sentence structure, fill in unclear words from context, and even add punctuation. Modern models do exactly this.

Whisper: The Model That Changed Everything

Before 2022, speech-to-text was a fragmented world. Google had their model, Amazon had theirs, and they were all trained on narrow, curated datasets. Then OpenAI released Whisper, and the game changed overnight.

What Makes Whisper Special

Whisper was trained on 680,000 hours of audio from the internet — podcasts, YouTube videos, audiobooks, interviews in dozens of languages. That's 77 years of continuous audio. This massive, diverse training set is why Whisper handles accents, background noise, technical jargon, and code-switching (mixing languages mid-sentence) far better than anything before it.

Whisper is also open source. You can download the model weights, run it on your own hardware, and integrate it into your pipeline with zero API costs. This is huge — it means you can build production speech features without paying per-minute transcription fees.

Using Whisper in Practice

Let's get hands-on. You have two main paths: the OpenAI API (easy, pay-per-minute) or running Whisper locally (free, requires setup).

Option 1: OpenAI Whisper API

The simplest path. Upload an audio file, get text back.

from openai import OpenAI

client = OpenAI()
with open("meeting.mp3", "rb") as audio_file:
    transcript = client.audio.transcriptions.create(
        model="whisper-1",
        file=audio_file,
        response_format="verbose_json",
        timestamp_granularities=["word"]
    )
print(transcript.text)
# Each word with its timestamp:
for word in transcript.words:
    print(f"{word.start:.1f}s: {word.word}")

The API costs $0.006 per minute of audio. A one-hour meeting costs about $0.36. That's remarkably cheap for production-quality transcription.

Option 2: Run Whisper Locally

import whisper

model = whisper.load_model("small")
result = model.transcribe("meeting.mp3")
print(result["text"])
# With timestamps per segment:
for seg in result["segments"]:
    print(f"[{seg['start']:.1f}s] {seg['text']}")
Use faster-whisper for local deployments. The original Whisper implementation is in PyTorch and can be slow. The faster-whisper library uses CTranslate2 under the hood and runs 4x faster with the same accuracy. Same API, just pip install faster-whisper and swap the import. It's the de facto choice for self-hosted Whisper.

Beyond Basic Transcription

Whisper doesn't just give you raw text. Depending on how you call it, you get:

  • Timestamps: Word-level or segment-level timing — essential for subtitles, searchable audio, and syncing text to playback
  • Language detection: Whisper auto-detects the spoken language (useful for multilingual apps)
  • Translation: Whisper can translate from any of its 100 languages directly to English during transcription — no separate translation step needed
  • Punctuation and formatting: Unlike older systems that output ALL CAPS WITH NO PUNCTUATION, Whisper produces properly cased, punctuated text
Old-school STT output
SO I THINK WE SHOULD MOVE THE DEADLINE TO FRIDAY BECAUSE THE CLIENT SAID THEY NEED MORE TIME AND I AGREE WITH THAT

No punctuation, no casing, no structure. You'd need a separate NLP pipeline just to make this readable.

Whisper output
So I think we should move the deadline to Friday, because the client said they need more time, and I agree with that.

Proper casing, punctuation, natural sentence structure. Ready for downstream processing or human reading.

Speaker Diarization: Who Said What?

Transcription tells you what was said. Diarization tells you who said it. If you're transcribing a meeting with five people, you don't want one giant block of text — you want to know that Alice said the first paragraph and Bob responded with the second.

How Diarization Works

Speaker diarization is a separate task from transcription. It works by analyzing the voice characteristics (pitch, timbre, speaking rate) in different segments of audio and clustering them — segments that sound like the same voice get grouped together.

Think of it like sorting laundry by color. You don't need to know whose shirt is whose — you just group the red shirts together, the blue ones together, and the whites together. Diarization assigns labels like "Speaker 1," "Speaker 2," etc. It doesn't know their names (unless you tell it), but it can reliably separate different voices.

Transcription + Diarization Pipeline

Audio File → Whisper (text + timestamps) → Diarization Model (speaker labels) → Align & Merge → Speaker-Tagged Transcript

Whisper itself doesn't do diarization — you need a separate model. The most popular open-source option is pyannote.audio, which works well with Whisper's output. The typical approach: run Whisper to get timestamped text, run pyannote to get speaker timestamps, then align them.

For best results, use a recording setup where each participant has a separate audio channel (like separate Zoom tracks).

Beyond Speech: Audio Classification

Not all audio contains speech. Sometimes you need to answer different questions: Is this music or speech? Is that a fire alarm or a doorbell? Is the baby crying or laughing? Is the crowd cheering or booing?

Audio classification takes an audio clip and assigns it a category. It uses the same spectrogram-to-encoder approach as speech-to-text, but instead of producing text tokens, it produces class labels.

Practical Audio Classification Tasks

Task Example Labels Real-World Use
Sound detection glass breaking, dog bark, siren Security systems, smart home
Music classification genre, mood, tempo, instruments Spotify recommendations, content tagging
Speech/non-speech speech, music, silence, noise Podcast editing, audio preprocessing
Emotion detection happy, sad, angry, neutral Customer service analytics, UX research
Acoustic scene office, street, restaurant, park Context-aware apps, accessibility

For most of these tasks, you don't need to build from scratch. Pre-trained audio classification models are available on Hugging Face, and many can be fine-tuned on your specific domain with relatively little data.

LLMs can classify audio too. Gemini 1.5 Pro and GPT-4o both accept audio input natively. You can upload an audio clip and ask "What sounds are in this recording?" or "What language is being spoken?" This is great for prototyping, though dedicated models are more efficient at scale.

Multilingual Transcription

One of Whisper's killer features is multilingual support. It doesn't just transcribe English — it handles 100 languages with a single model. And it can translate any of them to English in the same pass.

How Multilingual Models Handle Language

Whisper was trained on audio-text pairs in many languages. During transcription, it first detects the language (or you can specify it), then generates text in that language. For translation, it detects the source language and generates English text instead.

import whisper

model = whisper.load_model("small")
# Auto-detect language and transcribe
result = model.transcribe("french_podcast.mp3")
print(f"Detected: {result['language']}")
print(result["text"])  # French text

# Translate to English
result = model.transcribe(
    "french_podcast.mp3", task="translate"
)
print(result["text"])  # English translation
Quality varies by language. Whisper works best for languages with lots of training data (English, Spanish, French, German, Chinese, Japanese). For lower-resource languages, accuracy drops significantly. Always test with real audio in your target language before committing to a pipeline.

Code-Switching: Mixing Languages

In many real-world scenarios, speakers mix languages mid-sentence. A developer in Bangalore might say: "Let's deploy the service to production, lekin pehle we need to run tests." Whisper handles code-switching reasonably well because it saw plenty of mixed-language audio in training, though it's not perfect.

Real-Time vs Batch Transcription

There are two fundamentally different modes of speech-to-text, and the right choice depends on your use case:

Batch Transcription
Upload a complete audio file, get the full transcript back. Higher accuracy because the model sees all context at once. Latency: seconds to minutes depending on file length.

Best for: meeting recordings, podcasts, video subtitles, call center archives, voicemail.

Real-Time / Streaming
Audio is processed as it arrives, producing text with minimal delay (200-500ms). Less context available, so accuracy is slightly lower. But you see words as they're spoken.

Best for: live captions, voice assistants, real-time translation, call center monitoring, dictation.

Whisper was designed for batch processing. It processes 30-second audio chunks, which means there's inherent latency. For truly real-time streaming transcription, you'll want to look at services like Deepgram, AssemblyAI, or Google Cloud Speech-to-Text, which are built for streaming from the ground up.

The hybrid approach works well. Use a fast, lightweight streaming model (like Deepgram) for real-time display, then re-process the complete audio with Whisper after the session ends for a higher-quality final transcript. This gives you the best of both worlds: instant feedback during the conversation and polished output afterward.

Building a Meeting Notes Pipeline

Let's put everything together. Here's a practical pipeline that takes a meeting recording and produces structured, actionable notes:

Meeting Notes Pipeline

Audio File → Whisper (transcribe) → Diarization (speakers) → LLM (summarize) → Structured Notes 
from openai import OpenAI

client = OpenAI()
# Step 1: Transcribe
with open("meeting.mp3", "rb") as f:
    transcript = client.audio.transcriptions.create(
        model="whisper-1", file=f,
        response_format="text"
    )
# Step 2: Summarize with an LLM
summary = client.chat.completions.create(
    model="gpt-4o",
    messages=[{
        "role": "user",
        "content": f"Summarize this meeting. List "
        f"key decisions, action items with owners, "
        f"and open questions:\n\n{transcript}"
    }]
)
print(summary.choices[0].message.content)

This simple pipeline already provides enormous value. A one-hour meeting gets transcribed in minutes, then summarized into a page of key points. Add diarization and you can tag action items to specific people.

Preprocessing Tips

Real audio is messy. Here are tips for better results:

  • Chunk long files: Whisper processes 30-second chunks. For files over 10 minutes, split them first to avoid memory issues and improve parallel processing.
  • Normalize audio: Adjust volume levels so the speech is consistent. Quiet audio produces worse transcriptions.
  • Reduce noise: If you have background noise, apply noise reduction before transcription. Libraries like noisereduce or services like Adobe Podcast's "Enhance Speech" can help significantly.
  • Choose the right format: Whisper accepts mp3, wav, m4a, and other common formats. WAV gives the cleanest input (no compression artifacts) but larger files. MP3 is fine for most cases.

Common Challenges (and How to Handle Them)

Speech-to-text is impressive, but it's not magic. Here are the most common issues you'll face and how to deal with them:

Background Noise

Noisy environments — coffee shops, open offices, construction — degrade accuracy significantly. Solution: Preprocess with noise reduction. For real-time use, directional microphones and noise-canceling hardware help more than any software fix.

Accents and Dialects

Whisper handles common accents well (British, Indian, Australian English) because it saw them in training. Thicker regional accents or dialects with non-standard vocabulary can cause errors. Solution: Fine-tune Whisper on your target dialect if accuracy is critical. Even a few hours of domain-specific data can make a big difference.

Technical Jargon and Proper Nouns

Whisper doesn't know your company's product names, internal acronyms, or niche terminology. It might transcribe "Kubernetes" as "Cooper Netties" or your colleague "Prateek" as "Practic." Solution: Post-processing with a custom dictionary — find-and-replace known misspellings, or use an LLM to correct the transcript with a list of expected terms.

Overlapping Speech

When multiple people talk at once, transcription quality drops sharply. The model can't separate tangled audio streams. Solution: This is a hardware problem more than a software one. Use separate microphone channels when possible, or accept that overlapping segments will be lower quality.

Audio + LLMs: The Power Combo

Here's where things get really interesting. Transcription alone gives you text. But feeding that text into an LLM unlocks a whole new level of audio understanding:

  • Summarization: "Summarize this 2-hour podcast in 5 bullet points"
  • Q&A: "What did the CEO say about the Q3 roadmap?"
  • Action extraction: "List all action items with who's responsible"
  • Sentiment analysis: "How was the customer's tone in this support call?"
  • Translation: Transcribe in one language, summarize in another
  • Searchable audio: Transcribe your entire podcast library and make it full-text searchable

And with models like GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Pro that accept audio natively, you can skip the separate transcription step entirely — send the audio directly and ask questions about it.

G
Gemini 1.5 Pro — Native Audio
Accepts up to 9.5 hours of audio in a single prompt. You can upload a full podcast episode and ask questions about it directly — no transcription step needed. It understands tone, music, sound effects, and speech simultaneously.
O
GPT-4o — Native Audio
Processes audio natively as part of its multimodal input. Available through the Chat Completions API and the Realtime API. Can understand speech, music, and environmental sounds. Powers ChatGPT's voice mode.
