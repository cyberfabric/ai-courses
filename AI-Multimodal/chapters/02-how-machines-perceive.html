CHAPTER 2

How Machines See, Hear, and Read

Pixels, waveforms, and tokens — turning the real world into numbers

Everything Is Numbers

In Chapter 1, we opened the door from the mail slot. AI can now see images, hear audio, and watch video. But here's the thing that should bother you:

Computers only understand numbers.

A computer doesn't "see" a photo of your cat. It sees a giant grid of numbers. It doesn't "hear" your voice. It processes a long sequence of numbers representing air pressure changes. It doesn't "read" your code. It converts characters into numerical tokens.

Every modality — images, audio, text, video — must be converted into numbers before any AI model can process it. The art and science of multimodal AI starts here: how do you turn the messy real world into clean lists of numbers?

This chapter is about that conversion process. No math formulas — just intuition for how raw sensory data becomes something a model can work with.

The UN Interpreter Analogy

Imagine the United Nations. Delegates from every country speak different languages — French, Mandarin, Arabic, Spanish. They can't communicate directly. So what do they do?

They use interpreters. Each interpreter specializes in one language and translates everything into a common working language that everyone shares.

Multimodal AI works the same way:

  • Images "speak" in pixels
  • Audio "speaks" in waveforms
  • Text "speaks" in characters and words

Each modality has a specialized encoder — a neural network that acts as an interpreter. The encoder takes raw data in its "native language" (pixels, waveforms, characters) and translates it into a common numerical language called an embedding.

Once everything is in the same numerical language, the AI model can reason about all of it together. That's the magic trick behind every multimodal model.

Key vocabulary: An encoder converts raw data into numbers. The output is called an embedding — a dense list (vector) of numbers that captures the meaning of the input. Think of embeddings as the "diplomatic language" at the UN — they capture the essence of what was said, regardless of which language it was originally in.

How Machines See: Images as Numbers

Let's start with vision, because it's the most intuitive modality to understand.

A Photo Is Just a Spreadsheet

Take any digital photo. At its core, it's a grid of tiny colored dots called pixels. Each pixel has three numbers: how much Red, Green, and Blue it contains (RGB). Each number ranges from 0 to 255.

Inside a Digital Image

A single pixel might be: (142, 87, 201) — that's a shade of purple.

A 1920×1080 HD image has 1,920 columns × 1,080 rows = 2,073,600 pixels.

Each pixel has 3 color values, so the image is really ~6.2 million numbers.

A 4K image? About 25 million numbers. Your phone camera at 48MP? Around 144 million numbers per photo.

That's a LOT of numbers. And most of them are redundant — neighboring pixels are usually very similar. The encoder's job is to compress this flood of numbers into a much smaller, more meaningful representation.

So a photo is already numbers. But raw pixel values are almost useless for understanding. The number 142 in one pixel tells you nothing about whether the image contains a cat, a car, or a coffee cup. The meaning is in the patterns across millions of pixels.

From Pixels to Understanding: Vision Encoders

A vision encoder takes that giant grid of pixel numbers and compresses it into a much smaller list of numbers (an embedding) that captures what's actually in the image.

The Vision Encoding Pipeline

Raw Image

6.2 million numbers (RGB pixel values) — no meaning, just colors at positions

→
Low-Level Features

The encoder first detects edges, corners, textures, and color gradients

→
Mid-Level Features

Edges combine into shapes: circles, rectangles, curves, patterns

→
High-Level Features

Shapes combine into objects: "face," "car," "tree," "text"

→
Embedding

A compact vector (e.g., 768 numbers) that encodes the image's meaning

Think of it like how you perceive a photo yourself. You don't look at individual pixels. Your brain automatically detects edges, groups them into shapes, recognizes objects, and understands the scene — all in a fraction of a second. Vision encoders do the same thing, layer by layer.

Two Types of Vision Encoders

CNNs (Convolutional Neural Networks)

The original approach. Slide small filters across the image to detect patterns. Each layer detects increasingly complex features — edges → textures → objects.

Analogy: Like reading a page with a magnifying glass, scanning across systematically, building up understanding of larger patterns from small local details.

Used in: older models, mobile/edge applications where speed matters

ViT (Vision Transformers)

The modern approach. Chop the image into patches (e.g., 16×16 pixel squares), treat each patch like a "word," and use the same Transformer architecture that powers LLMs.

Analogy: Like looking at a mosaic — you see each tile (patch) and how it relates to every other tile, understanding the big picture from the relationships between pieces.

Used in: GPT-4V, Claude vision, Gemini, CLIP — basically all modern multimodal models

The Vision Transformer is the dominant approach today. And here's the beautiful part: it's the same architecture as the text Transformers you already know. Images get chopped into patches, patches get treated like tokens, and the Transformer does what Transformers do best — finds relationships between them.

Why this matters to you: When you send an image to Claude or GPT-4, the model's vision encoder chops your image into patches, converts each patch into an embedding, and feeds those embeddings into the language model alongside your text tokens. That's why image inputs cost tokens — each patch becomes a token.

How Machines Hear: Audio as Numbers

Audio feels more abstract than images, but the same principle applies: sound must become numbers before any model can process it.

What Is Sound, Really?

Sound is vibrations in the air. When you speak, your vocal cords create pressure waves that travel through the air and hit a microphone. The microphone measures how the air pressure changes over time and records it as a sequence of numbers — this is called sampling.

Inside a Digital Audio File

Standard audio quality (CD) samples the air pressure 44,100 times per second. Each sample is a single number representing the pressure at that instant.

One second of CD-quality audio = 44,100 numbers.

A 5-minute song = about 13.2 million numbers.

A 1-hour podcast = about 159 million numbers.

Unlike images where you can "see" the pixels, a raw audio waveform — a squiggly line going up and down — is almost impossible for humans to interpret visually. But it contains everything: words, tone, emotion, background noise, and music.

The Spectrogram: Turning Sound into a Picture

Here's the clever trick that makes audio processing work: convert the sound into an image.

A spectrogram is a visual representation of sound. The horizontal axis is time, the vertical axis is frequency (pitch), and the brightness represents volume. Low rumbles are at the bottom, high squeaks at the top, and louder sounds are brighter.

The Audio Encoding Pipeline

Raw Waveform

44,100 numbers per second — a squiggly line of air pressure changes

→
Spectrogram

Convert to a 2D image: time × frequency. Sound is now a picture you can "look at"

→
Mel Spectrogram

Adjust frequency scale to match how humans perceive pitch (we hear differences better at low frequencies)

→
Audio Encoder

Process the spectrogram image with a neural network (often a Transformer)

→
Embedding

A compact vector capturing what was said, by whom, in what tone

This is why audio AI and image AI are surprisingly similar under the hood — both ultimately process 2D patterns. Spectrograms turn the "how does the machine hear?" problem into a "how does the machine see?" problem. Elegant, right?

Fun fact: This is exactly how Whisper works. OpenAI's speech recognition model converts audio to mel spectrograms, then processes them with a Transformer encoder. The same architecture that reads text and sees images also hears audio — just with different preprocessing.

How Machines Read: Text as Numbers

You already know this one from working with LLMs, but let's recap briefly so we can see the common thread across all modalities.

Tokenization → Embeddings

Text goes through two steps:

  1. Tokenization: Split text into chunks (tokens). "Multimodal" might become ["Multi", "modal"]. Each token gets a numeric ID from a vocabulary.
  2. Embedding: Each token ID is converted into a dense vector of numbers (e.g., 768 or 1536 numbers). These vectors capture the token's meaning, not just its identity.

The word "bank" gets a different embedding depending on context — "river bank" vs "bank account." That's the power of contextual embeddings.

What You Write

"The cat sat on the mat"

6 words — human-readable text

What the Model Sees

[464, 3857, 3290, 319, 279, 5765]

6 token IDs → 6 embedding vectors → a matrix of numbers

The key insight: by the time text reaches the Transformer's attention layers, it's already a sequence of numerical vectors — exactly the same format as image patch embeddings or audio frame embeddings.

The Common Thread: Everything Becomes Embeddings

Step back and notice the pattern:

Every modality follows the same path:
Raw data → Specialized encoder → Embedding (a list of numbers that captures meaning)

Images: pixels → vision encoder (ViT) → embedding vectors
Audio: waveform → spectrogram → audio encoder → embedding vectors
Text: characters → tokenizer → text encoder → embedding vectors

The output format is always the same: a sequence of embedding vectors. And that's the whole trick. Once every modality speaks the same "language" (vectors of numbers), you can:

  • Compare them — is this image related to this text?
  • Combine them — feed image embeddings and text embeddings into the same model
  • Search across them — find images that match a text query
  • Translate between them — generate text that describes an image, or an image that matches text

This is the foundation everything else in the course builds on. The reason multimodal AI works is that every type of data can be encoded into the same numerical format.

Pre-Trained Encoders: Why You Never Start from Scratch

Here's the practical reality: you will almost never train an encoder yourself. Just like you don't train your own LLM — you use GPT-4 or Claude — you don't train your own vision encoder. You use one that's already been trained on millions or billions of examples.

ImageNet
14M images, 21K categories — the classic vision training dataset
LAION-5B
5.8 billion image-text pairs — used to train CLIP and Stable Diffusion
680K hrs
of audio data used to train Whisper — multilingual speech from the web

Training an encoder from scratch requires enormous datasets and compute. But using a pre-trained encoder? That's just an API call or a library import. The encoder has already learned what edges, shapes, objects, phonemes, and words look like. You just give it your data and it produces embeddings.

Using a pre-trained vision encoder in Python

from transformers import ViTModel, ViTImageProcessor
from PIL import Image

processor = ViTImageProcessor.from_pretrained("google/vit-base-patch16-224")
model = ViTModel.from_pretrained("google/vit-base-patch16-224")

image = Image.open("photo.jpg")
inputs = processor(images=image, return_tensors="pt")
outputs = model(**inputs)

# outputs.last_hidden_state → image embeddings!
# Shape: [1, 197, 768] → 197 patch embeddings, each 768 numbers

197 patches × 768 numbers each. That's your image, translated into the common language of embeddings. From here, you can feed these into a language model, compare them against text embeddings, or use them for classification.

What Do Encoders Actually Capture?

An embedding isn't just a random pile of numbers. Each dimension captures something about the input. Not something you can point to and name ("dimension 42 = cat-ness"), but the collective pattern encodes rich meaning.

Here's how to think about it:

A Good Embedding For...

  • A photo of a golden retriever
  • A photo of a labrador

...would be very close together in embedding space (similar dogs)

A Good Embedding For...

  • A photo of a golden retriever
  • A photo of a bulldozer

...would be very far apart in embedding space (completely different things)

The same principle applies to audio and text. The embedding for "hello" spoken in a quiet room and "hello" spoken in a noisy cafe should be close (same word). The embedding for "hello" and the sound of a car horn should be far apart.

Encoders aren't perfect. They can be biased by their training data. A vision encoder trained mostly on photos from the US might struggle with objects common in other cultures. An audio encoder trained on English speech might produce worse embeddings for tonal languages. Understanding these limitations is important when building real features.

See Encoders in Action

Try It Now: Watch Encoding Happen

You can see encoding in action without writing code. Open Claude or ChatGPT and try this:

  1. Upload a photo of something on your desk
  2. Ask: "Describe this image in detail. What objects do you see, what are their spatial relationships, and what can you infer about the setting?"
  3. Now upload a completely different photo — maybe a landscape or a screenshot
  4. Ask the same question

Notice how the model's understanding goes from low-level ("I see a rectangular object") to high-level ("This appears to be a software developer's workspace"). That progression — raw visual data to semantic understanding — is exactly what the vision encoder does internally, layer by layer.

Bonus: Try uploading a very blurry or dark photo. Notice how the model struggles — just like a human, when the raw data quality is poor, even a great encoder produces poor embeddings.

Bonus: What About Video?

Video isn't really a separate modality — it's a combination of modalities over time:

  • A sequence of images (frames), typically 24-60 per second
  • An audio track (speech, music, sound effects)
  • Sometimes subtitles or overlay text

Processing video usually means encoding frames with a vision encoder and audio with an audio encoder, then figuring out how they relate across time. It's the most data-dense modality — a 1-minute 1080p video at 30fps contains 1,800 frames × 6.2M numbers per frame = ~11 billion numbers, plus the audio track.

We'll dive deep into video understanding in Chapter 9. For now, just know: video = images + audio + time.

Hands-On Exercise

Exercise: Think Like an Encoder

For each of these inputs, think about what information an encoder would need to capture to be useful. What details matter, and what can be thrown away?

  1. A photo of a handwritten shopping list — what does the encoder need to "notice"? (The text content, the handwriting style, the paper, the ink color... which matters most?)
  2. A 10-second voice recording saying "Turn left at the next intersection" — what does the audio encoder capture? (The words, the speaker's gender, background noise, urgency in the voice...)
  3. A screenshot of a mobile app with a crash dialog — what does the vision encoder extract? (The error message text, the UI layout, the app name, the button labels...)

Key insight: The answer depends on what you're trying to do. A general-purpose encoder tries to capture everything. A specialized encoder prioritizes what matters for its task (OCR encodes text details, speaker-ID encodes voice characteristics, etc.).

Bonus: Take one of these inputs and actually send it to Claude or GPT-4. Compare what the model focuses on to what you predicted.

The Big Picture

Let's zoom out. Here's what you now understand:

  • AI only understands numbers — every modality must be converted
  • Images are grids of RGB values, encoded by vision encoders (ViT) into embeddings
  • Audio is a waveform, converted to a spectrogram (2D image of sound), then encoded into embeddings
  • Text is tokenized and embedded — you already knew this one
  • The output of every encoder is the same format: a sequence of embedding vectors
  • Pre-trained encoders are available off the shelf — you rarely train from scratch
  • Embeddings capture meaning, not raw data — similar inputs produce similar embeddings

This is the foundation. But knowing how each modality becomes numbers only solves half the problem. The harder question is: how do you combine them?
