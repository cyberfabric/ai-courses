Chapter 5

Setting AI Policies & Guidelines

Write policies developers will actually follow — not 50-page documents nobody reads

Why "Just Be Careful" Isn't a Policy

In Chapter 4, you rolled out AI tools with a phased approach. Your team is using them. Champions are evangelizing. Adoption is climbing. Everything looks great — until a developer pastes your proprietary API into ChatGPT on a free tier, another commits AI-generated code with a copyleft license buried in it, and a third submits a 2,000-line PR with "Claude wrote it" as the only description.

Welcome to the "we need a policy" moment. Every team gets here. The only question is whether you arrive proactively or after an incident.

The core analogy: AI policies are like your team's coding standards — too strict and nobody follows them, too loose and you get chaos. The goal is a short, practical document that developers reference because it's useful, not because compliance made them.

Here's what "just be careful" actually sounds like to developers: "I haven't thought about this, so the risk is on you." That's not leadership. A real policy does three things: it protects the company (legal, IP, security), it protects the developer (clear expectations, no surprises), and it enables productivity (explicit green lights for approved use cases).

The Policy Spectrum

Before writing your policy, understand where your organization sits on the spectrum. Every company lands somewhere between these extremes:

🚫 AI Banned

No AI tools for any work code.

  • Maximum control
  • Zero productivity gains
  • Drives usage underground (shadow AI)
  • Talent leaves for AI-friendly companies

Who does this: Some defense contractors, heavily regulated industries (temporarily).

✅ AI Governed

Approved tools with clear guardrails.

  • Balanced control and productivity
  • Clear expectations for developers
  • Audit trail and accountability
  • Adapts as tools evolve

Who does this: Most mature engineering orgs in 2026.

🌊 AI Free-for-All

Use anything, any time, any way.

  • Maximum speed (short term)
  • No oversight or consistency
  • Security and IP risks everywhere
  • Impossible to audit or manage

Who does this: Startups that haven't thought about it yet.

Your target is the middle — AI Governed. This is where you get the productivity benefits while managing the risks. The rest of this chapter shows you exactly what that looks like.

The Five Policy Areas

A complete AI usage policy covers five areas. You don't need a novel — each area can be covered in half a page. The entire policy should fit on two pages.

Policy Area 1: What Goes INTO the AI

This is the most critical area. Every time a developer sends a prompt to an AI tool, they're sending data to a third party. The question is: what data is okay to send?

Data Classification for AI Context

✅ Always safe to include:

  • Open-source code your team maintains
  • General coding questions (no proprietary context)
  • Public documentation and API references
  • Code patterns and architecture discussions (abstracted)

⚠️ Requires approved tools with enterprise data retention policy:

  • Proprietary source code (with zero-data-retention plans)
  • Internal API schemas and database models
  • Business logic and algorithms
  • Internal documentation

🚫 Never put into any AI tool:

  • API keys, secrets, credentials, tokens
  • Customer PII (names, emails, payment info)
  • Database connection strings or production URLs
  • Compliance-sensitive data (HIPAA, PCI, SOX)
  • Unreleased product features or strategic plans

Tip: The "Newspaper Test" for AI Prompts

Before pasting something into an AI tool, ask: "Would I be comfortable if this appeared in a news article about our company's data practices?" If the answer is no, don't paste it. This simple test catches 90% of data classification edge cases.

Policy Area 2: What AI Code Needs Before Merging

The single most important rule in your policy: the developer who submits the PR owns every line in it, regardless of who or what generated it. This isn't optional. It's the foundation of AI code accountability.

Leadership Insight: The Ownership Principle

In 2026, the industry consensus is clear: the human who commits AI-generated code is fully accountable for its correctness, security, and compliance. "The AI wrote it" is not a defense — not for bugs, not for security vulnerabilities, and not in court.

Make this explicit in your policy and reinforce it in every code review. This isn't about punishment — it's about ensuring developers engage critically with AI output instead of rubber-stamping it. The developer is the engineer of record, and the AI is a tool they used.

Merge requirements for AI-generated code:

AI Code Pre-Merge Checklist

  • Developer has reviewed and understands every line (not just "it compiles")
  • Tests exist and pass — including edge cases the AI might have missed
  • No hardcoded values, dummy data, or placeholder comments left behind
  • Follows team coding conventions and architectural patterns
  • No security anti-patterns (SQL injection, XSS, hardcoded secrets)
  • Dependencies are from approved sources (no unknown packages)
  • Standard code review by at least one other team member
  • For security-critical code: senior engineer review required

Warning: Prohibited Zones for AI Code

Define areas where AI-generated code requires extra scrutiny or is outright prohibited without expert review. Common prohibited zones include:

  • Authentication and authorization logic
  • Cryptographic implementations
  • Payment processing and financial calculations
  • Database migrations on production data
  • Infrastructure-as-code for production environments

AI can assist in these areas, but the output must be reviewed by a domain expert before merge. No exceptions.

Policy Area 3: Approved Tools

In Chapter 3, you chose an "approved list" of 2–3 tools. Your policy formalizes this. Be specific:

Sample Approved Tool List

Tool Tier Approved For Account Type
GitHub Copilot Business Standard All work code Company SSO
Claude Code (Max plan) Standard All work code Company SSO
Cursor Pro Opt-in All work code Company SSO
ChatGPT (free tier) 🚫 Banned Not for work code N/A
Any personal AI account 🚫 Banned Not for work code N/A

Key distinction: Company-managed accounts with SSO, audit logs, and zero-data-retention agreements are approved. Personal accounts — even for the same tools — are not approved for work code.

Why personal accounts are banned for work code: Even if the tool is the same, a personal account has different data retention terms, no audit trail, no SSO, and no enterprise security features. It's the account type, not the tool name, that matters.

Policy Area 4: Code Ownership & IP

This is where it gets legally interesting. Who owns AI-generated code? The short answer: it depends on the tool, the jurisdiction, and the level of human involvement.

Scenario: The IP Question That Stumped Legal

Your lead backend developer uses Claude Code to generate a novel caching algorithm. It works brilliantly and becomes core to your product's performance advantage. Six months later, your company wants to patent it.

Legal asks: "Who invented this? Can we claim copyright?" The developer says the AI wrote most of it, but they guided the design, reviewed the output, and modified key parts. Is this patentable? Is it copyrightable?

The 2026 reality: In the US, copyright requires human authorship. If the developer provided "sufficient creative input" — iterative prompting, editing, and refining — copyright likely attaches to the human author (and through employment agreements, to the company). Pure AI output with no human creative involvement may not be copyrightable.

Discussion prompts: Does your company's employment agreement cover AI-assisted work? Have you consulted legal about AI-generated IP? What would happen if a competitor generated similar code using the same AI?

What your policy should say about IP:

  • AI-generated code created during work, using company tools, belongs to the company (same as human-written code)
  • Developers must provide meaningful creative input — not just "generate a caching system" but iterative design, review, and modification
  • Employment agreements should explicitly cover AI-assisted work (talk to legal)
  • For code intended for patent filing, document the human creative process

The Licensing Landscape in Numbers

The legal environment around AI-generated code is evolving rapidly:

  • Only GitHub Copilot Business+ currently offers IP indemnity — they cover legal costs if AI-generated code causes IP disputes
  • The EU AI Act enforcement for high-risk AI begins August 2, 2026 — vendors using GPAI models in the EU must publish training data summaries and copyright compliance policies
  • The Doe v. GitHub lawsuit (alleging Copilot reproduces licensed code without attribution) is on appeal to the Ninth Circuit — the outcome could reshape AI code licensing
  • Anthropic's terms assign Claude Code output ownership to the user — but this is a contractual right, not copyright law

Bottom line: The legal landscape is unsettled. Your policy should be conservative now and include a quarterly review to adapt as case law develops.

Policy Area 5: Attribution & Transparency

Should developers disclose when code is AI-generated? This is one of the most debated policy questions in 2026. Here are the two schools of thought:

Mandatory Disclosure

  • PRs must tag AI-generated sections
  • Commit messages include AI tool used
  • Enables tracking and auditing
  • Helps reviewers calibrate scrutiny

Downside: Creates bureaucratic overhead. Hard to enforce accurately when AI assists vs generates. Can stigmatize AI usage.

Ownership-Based (No Disclosure)

  • Developer owns all code regardless
  • Same review standards for all code
  • No distinction between AI and human code
  • Simpler, less overhead

Downside: Loses audit trail. Can't measure AI impact. No signal for reviewers to increase scrutiny.

Tip: The Practical Middle Ground

Most teams in 2026 land here: disclose the tool, not the lines. Commit messages or PR descriptions mention "Assisted by Claude Code" or "Used Copilot for initial scaffolding" — but don't require line-by-line attribution. This gives you an audit trail without creating busy work.

Example PR description: Refactored payment service to async pattern. Used Claude Code for initial implementation, manually adjusted error handling and retry logic.

The Licensing Minefield

AI tools are trained on code from across the internet, including code under various open-source licenses. This creates a real risk: the AI might generate code that's substantially similar to copyleft-licensed code (like GPL), which could contaminate your proprietary codebase.

What your team needs to know:

  • Copyleft risk: If AI-generated code closely matches GPL code, using it in a proprietary project could trigger copyleft obligations (sharing your source code)
  • Attribution requirements: Some licenses require attribution. AI tools don't always flag when generated code matches a licensed source
  • Training data provenance: Most AI tools are trained on code from GitHub, Stack Overflow, and other public sources. The legal status of this training is still being litigated

Licensing Guardrails

  • Enable code duplicate detection in your CI/CD pipeline (tools like FOSSA, Snyk, or Black Duck can scan for license conflicts)
  • Use AI tools that offer IP indemnity where available (currently only Copilot Business+)
  • For critical code paths, run an originality check before committing
  • Document that AI-generated code has been reviewed for licensing compliance
  • When in doubt about a large code block that looks too "perfect," search for it on GitHub — the AI may have reproduced an existing project's code

Writing Policies Developers Actually Follow

The difference between a policy that works and one that's ignored comes down to how you write it and how you roll it out.

Good Policy Characteristics

  • Short: 2 pages maximum. If it's longer, nobody reads it.
  • Specific: "Use company SSO accounts only" beats "use appropriate security measures."
  • Green-light focused: Emphasize what IS allowed, not just what isn't. Developers need to know what they CAN do.
  • Example-heavy: Include real examples of good and bad behavior, not just abstract rules.
  • Living document: Include a review date. Outdated policies are ignored policies.

Bad Policy Characteristics

  • 50+ pages: Written by legal for legal, not for developers.
  • Vague language: "Exercise appropriate judgment when using AI tools." (What does that mean?)
  • All restrictions, no permissions: A wall of "don'ts" that makes developers feel like they can't do anything.
  • No examples: Abstract principles with no practical application.
  • Never updated: Written once in 2024 and never revisited despite tools evolving monthly.

The format that works: Write your policy as a one-page quick reference (the "TL;DR" developers will actually use) backed by a longer reference document (for edge cases and legal questions). Keep the quick reference in your repo's root — right next to your README.

The Two-Page Policy Template

Here's a complete, adaptable policy template. Customize the bracketed sections for your team.

AI Usage Policy — [Your Team/Company Name]

Last updated: [Date] — Next review: [Date + 3 months]

1. Approved Tools

Only use company-managed accounts on approved tools for work code: [Tool 1], [Tool 2], [Tool 3]. Personal AI accounts (including free tiers) are not approved for work code. Request new tools through [process/link].

2. Data Rules

Never send to any AI tool: API keys, customer data, credentials, database connection strings, compliance-sensitive information. Approved tools only: proprietary source code, internal APIs, business logic. Any tool: general coding questions, open-source code, public references.

3. Code Ownership

You own the code you commit. AI-generated code must meet the same quality, security, and review standards as human-written code. "The AI wrote it" is not a justification for bugs, vulnerabilities, or style violations.

4. Review Requirements

All AI-generated code requires standard code review. Security-sensitive areas ([list areas]) require senior engineer review. No AI-generated code may be committed without the developer understanding every line.

5. Attribution

Note the AI tool used in PR descriptions (e.g., "Assisted by Claude Code"). Line-by-line attribution is not required.

6. Prohibited Uses

AI output must not be used without expert review for: authentication/authorization, cryptography, payment processing, database migrations, production infrastructure changes.

7. Reporting

If AI generates output that includes secrets, sensitive data, or appears to reproduce copyrighted code, report to [person/channel] immediately.

Getting Buy-In Without a 6-Month Committee

Your policy needs three stakeholder groups to sign off: engineering (that's you), legal, and security/compliance. Here's how to get all three without creating an endless committee.

Week 1 Draft

You write the first draft. Use the template above.

→
Week 2 Security Review

Security team reviews data classification and tool list.

→
Week 3 Legal Review

Legal reviews IP, licensing, and attribution sections.

→
Week 4 Team Feedback

Share with developers. Incorporate feedback. Ship it.

The key insight: Write the draft yourself. Don't ask legal or security to write the policy — you'll wait months and get something developers can't follow. Write it in developer-friendly language, then have the specialists review it. They'll tell you what to add or change.

Leadership Insight: Policies as Enablers, Not Blockers

Frame your policy as "here's how we use AI safely and effectively" — not "here's everything you can't do." When you present the policy to your team, lead with the permissions: "You're approved to use Copilot and Claude Code for all work code, with company accounts. Here are the guardrails to keep us safe." Developers are far more receptive to guardrails when they come with explicit green lights.

The Quarterly Review Cadence

AI tools evolve monthly. Your policy should evolve quarterly. Set a recurring calendar event and use this review framework:

Quarterly Policy Review Checklist

  • Tool check: Are approved tools still the best options? Any new tools worth evaluating?
  • Pricing check: Have pricing or data retention terms changed?
  • Incident review: Were there any AI-related security or quality incidents this quarter?
  • Legal update: Any new court rulings, regulations, or vendor term changes? (EU AI Act enforcement in August 2026 is a big one)
  • Developer feedback: What policy friction are developers experiencing? What's unclear?
  • Adoption metrics: Is the policy being followed? What violations have occurred?
  • Industry benchmarks: What are comparable companies doing that you're not?

Tip: Version Your Policy in Git

Check your AI usage policy into your repo. Treat changes like code changes — PRs, reviews, and a changelog. This gives you version history, a clear audit trail, and makes the policy easy for developers to find. Store it at docs/ai-policy.md or alongside your team's CLAUDE.md and .cursorrules files.

Handling the Edge Cases

No policy covers every scenario. Here are the edge cases that come up most often, and how to handle them:

?
"Can I use AI for personal projects at work?" Yes, on personal time with personal accounts. Never mix personal and work AI accounts.
?
"Can I use AI to write commit messages?" Yes. Low risk, high convenience. Just review what it says.
?
"Can I paste error logs into AI?" Only if you strip PII and secrets first. Error logs often contain customer data, stack traces with internal paths, and environment variables.
?
"What about AI in Slack/Teams bots?" Same rules as any AI tool. If it processes work data, it needs to be on the approved list with appropriate data handling.
?
"A client requires no AI in their project. Now what?" Respect it. Tag the repo as AI-restricted. Ensure tools are disabled for that project. Document the exception.
?
"AI suggested a dependency I've never heard of." Verify it exists. Check its license. Check its maintenance status. AI hallucinates package names — don't npm install blindly.

Exercise: Draft Your AI Policy

Try It: Create Your Team's AI Usage Policy

Using the template above, draft a policy for your team. This should take 30–45 minutes. Then run it through the buy-in process (security → legal → team feedback) over the next 2–3 weeks.

Step 1: Customize the Template

  • Fill in your approved tools (from Chapter 3's evaluation)
  • List your security-sensitive code areas by name (auth module, payment service, etc.)
  • Define your attribution format (PR description, commit message, or both)
  • Add any industry-specific compliance requirements (HIPAA, PCI, SOX, etc.)

Step 2: The "Developer Sniff Test"

  • Read your draft as if you were a developer seeing it for the first time
  • Is it under 2 pages?
  • Can a developer read it in 5 minutes?
  • For every "don't," is there a corresponding "do" or "here's how"?
  • Are the examples concrete, not abstract?

Step 3: Schedule the Reviews

  • Book a 30-minute meeting with your security team: [date]
  • Book a 30-minute meeting with legal: [date]
  • Plan a team feedback session: [date]
  • Set the "go live" date: [date]
  • Set the first quarterly review date: [date + 3 months]

Pro tip: Share the draft with your AI champions first. They'll spot practical issues that others miss, and their endorsement makes the team feedback session smoother.
