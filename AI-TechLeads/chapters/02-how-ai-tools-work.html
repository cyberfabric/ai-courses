Chapter 2

How AI Coding Tools Really Work

The mental model tech leads need — without the PhD

You Can't Lead What You Don't Understand

In Chapter 1, we established that AI adoption is a leadership challenge. But here's the thing about leading a technology shift: you need to understand the technology well enough to make good decisions.

You don't need to understand transformer architectures or attention mechanisms. You don't need to know how backpropagation works. But you do need to understand:

  • Why AI gives different answers to the same question
  • Why it sometimes "makes stuff up" with total confidence
  • Why it's brilliant at some tasks and terrible at others
  • Why the way you provide context changes everything about the output quality
  • How much it costs and what drives those costs

Without this mental model, you'll make bad decisions about tooling, set unrealistic expectations for your team, and struggle to troubleshoot when things go wrong.

This chapter's goal: Give you a practical understanding of how AI coding tools work — enough to make informed decisions, evaluate tools, and guide your team. Think of it as the "How Cars Work" chapter for someone who needs to manage a fleet, not build an engine.

LLMs in 5 Minutes

Every AI coding tool your team uses — Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code, ChatGPT — is powered by a Large Language Model (LLM). Here's what that means in plain English.

An LLM is a neural network trained on massive amounts of text. We're talking trillions of words: books, websites, open-source code, documentation, forum posts, academic papers. During training, the model learns statistical patterns in this text. Given a sequence of words (or code), it predicts what comes next.

That's it. At its core, an LLM is a very, very sophisticated prediction engine.

When you type a prompt like "Write a Python function that validates email addresses," the model doesn't understand email validation the way you do. It has seen thousands of email validation functions during training and learned the statistical patterns of what typically follows that kind of request. It generates code token by token, each one predicted based on everything that came before.

Tip: The Key Insight for Tech Leads

LLMs don't reason from first principles. They pattern-match from training data. This means they're excellent at tasks where the pattern is well-represented in their training data (common API usage, standard algorithms, popular frameworks) and unreliable at tasks that require novel reasoning or deep domain knowledge.

When your developer says "the AI wrote perfect code for this REST endpoint but completely botched our custom authentication flow" — this is why.

Why LLMs Hallucinate

You've probably heard the term "hallucination" — when an AI confidently generates something that's completely wrong. A function that calls an API that doesn't exist. A library method with the wrong signature. An import for a package that was deprecated two years ago.

This happens because the model is always predicting the most likely next token. It doesn't have a fact-checking mechanism. It doesn't verify that the function it's calling actually exists in the library version you're using. It generates what looks statistically plausible based on its training data.

For tech leads, this has a critical implication: AI-generated code that compiles and looks correct can still be wrong. The AI doesn't know it's wrong. It has no doubt. This is why code review of AI output is non-negotiable (more on that in Chapter 7).

Warning: The Confidence Trap

AI-generated code is always presented with 100% confidence, whether it's correct or not. There's no uncertainty indicator, no "I'm not sure about this" flag. Your team needs to understand that confidence in presentation does not equal correctness in content. Train your developers to verify, not trust.

The Context Window: Your Most Important Lever

If there's one technical concept you need to deeply understand as a tech lead, it's the context window.

The context window is the amount of text (measured in tokens) that the model can "see" at once. Think of it as the model's working memory. Everything you send to the model — your prompt, your code, your project files, the conversation history — needs to fit in this window.

A 1M token context window sounds enormous, and it is — that's roughly 750,000 words, or about 10 novels. But for codebases? A medium-sized project can easily be 500,000+ lines of code. You're not fitting the whole thing in one prompt.

Why "Just Paste the Whole Codebase" Doesn't Work

Even with million-token windows, cramming everything in is a bad strategy for three reasons:

  1. Attention dilution: The more context you provide, the harder it is for the model to focus on what matters. Relevant information gets buried in noise. Studies show that LLM performance on specific tasks degrades when the context is stuffed with irrelevant material.
  2. Cost: More tokens = more money. Sending your entire codebase with every request would be astronomically expensive.
  3. Quality: Models perform best when they receive focused, relevant context. A well-curated 10K token prompt often produces better results than a 500K token dump.

Leadership Insight: Context Quality Is a Team Responsibility

The quality of AI output is directly proportional to the quality of context provided. This is why investing time in project-level context — rules files, architecture docs, coding conventions — pays enormous dividends. It's not one developer's job to "figure out prompting." It's a team capability you build together.

The best tech leads treat context engineering like they treat CI/CD setup: infrastructure that makes everyone more productive, maintained as a team asset.

Tokens and Costs: The Economics Your Team Should Understand

Every interaction with an AI coding tool costs money. Whether your team sees the bill directly (API usage) or it's hidden in a subscription (Copilot, Cursor), someone is paying per token.

A token is roughly 3/4 of a word in English, or about 3-4 characters of code. The string function validateEmail(input) is about 7 tokens.

Tip: The Model Tiering Strategy

Smart teams use different models for different tasks. Use a smaller, cheaper model (like Haiku) for simple completions, test generation, and documentation. Save the expensive models (like Opus) for complex architectural decisions, security-sensitive code, and hard debugging. This can cut your AI costs by 50-70% with minimal quality impact.

Temperature: Why the Same Prompt Gives Different Results

Your developers will notice this quickly: ask the AI the exact same question twice, and you might get different answers. This isn't a bug. It's a feature called temperature.

Temperature controls how "creative" or "random" the model's responses are:

Low Temperature (0.0 – 0.3)

  • More deterministic, predictable outputs
  • Model picks the highest-probability tokens
  • Good for: code generation, factual queries, structured output
  • Think: exam answers

High Temperature (0.7 – 1.0)

  • More varied, creative outputs
  • Model explores less likely tokens
  • Good for: brainstorming, creative writing, exploring alternatives
  • Think: improv comedy

Most coding tools default to a low temperature for code generation. But when a developer complains "I got a completely different implementation when I asked again," now you know why. Even at low temperatures, there's some randomness.

What this means for your team: Don't treat AI output as deterministic. Two developers asking the same question may get different (but equally valid) implementations. This is fine — what matters is whether the output meets your standards, not whether it's identical.

The Training Data Question

Every LLM has a knowledge cutoff date — the point at which its training data stops. After that date, the model doesn't know what happened. New frameworks, new API versions, new security vulnerabilities, new best practices — all invisible to the model unless provided in context.

Scenario: The Outdated Dependency

Your team uses a popular state management library that released a major version update three months ago. The new API is completely different from the old one. A developer asks the AI to implement a complex state management pattern.

The AI generates clean, well-structured code — using the old API. It works if you're on the legacy version. It completely fails on the version your project actually uses.

The developer doesn't notice because the code looks correct. It passes the syntax check. It even compiles if the old types happen to be compatible. The bug only surfaces during integration testing, two days later.

Discussion prompts: How could this have been prevented? What project context would have helped the AI generate correct code? Should developers be expected to catch this kind of error?

This is where context engineering becomes critical. The model's training data is frozen, but the context you provide is live. By including your project's dependency versions, API documentation, and coding standards in the context, you bridge the gap between what the model knows and what your project needs.

What AI Is Great At (and What It Isn't)

This might be the most practical section in this chapter. Print this out and pin it to your team's wall.

Where AI Excels

  • Boilerplate code: CRUD operations, API endpoints, data models, config files. The stuff nobody enjoys writing.
  • Pattern-heavy tasks: Form validation, data transformations, serialization/deserialization. Tasks with well-known patterns.
  • Test generation: Unit tests, integration tests, edge case identification. AI is surprisingly good at testing code it didn't write.
  • Documentation: Docstrings, README files, API documentation, code comments. The stuff developers never want to write.
  • Language translation: Porting code between languages, converting SQL dialects, translating error messages.
  • Code explanation: Explaining legacy code, summarizing complex functions, onboarding assistance.
  • Refactoring: Extracting methods, renaming variables, simplifying complex expressions, applying known design patterns.

Where AI Struggles

  • Novel architecture: Designing systems that don't look like anything in the training data. The AI will default to common patterns even when they're wrong for your use case.
  • Business logic: Complex domain rules that are specific to your company. The AI hasn't read your requirements doc (unless you provide it).
  • Security-critical code: Authentication flows, encryption, access control. AI can generate plausible-looking security code that has subtle, exploitable flaws.
  • Cross-codebase consistency: Maintaining consistent patterns across a large codebase. The AI sees the files you show it, not the ones you don't.
  • Performance optimization: Real-world performance tuning requires understanding your specific load patterns, data sizes, and constraints.
  • Judgment calls: Trade-offs between approaches, deciding when "good enough" is actually good enough, knowing when to break the rules.

Two Mental Models: Which Is More Useful?

Your team will gravitate toward one of two mental models for AI coding tools. Both have value, but one is significantly more useful.

"Autocomplete on Steroids"

  • AI suggests the next few lines of code
  • Developer stays in control at all times
  • Low risk, low reward
  • Works well for inline suggestions (Copilot-style)
  • Underestimates what modern AI can do

"Brilliant Intern"

  • AI can tackle entire tasks with guidance
  • Needs clear instructions and code review
  • Higher risk, much higher reward
  • Works well for agent-style tools (Claude Code, Cursor Agent)
  • Better matches how modern AI tools actually work

The "brilliant intern" model is more useful because it sets the right expectations. An intern who has read millions of codebases is incredibly valuable — but you wouldn't let them merge code without review. You'd give them clear requirements, check their work, and gradually trust them with more complex tasks as they prove themselves.

That's exactly how your team should use AI tools. Clear requirements (prompts with context). Check their work (code review). Gradually increase trust (from autocomplete to agent-mode).

Leadership Insight: Set the Mental Model for Your Team

How your team thinks about AI tools determines how they use them. If they see AI as "autocomplete," they'll underuse it. If they see it as "magic," they'll over-trust it.

Introduce the "brilliant intern" framing in a team meeting. It immediately makes clear why: (a) AI output needs review, (b) better instructions lead to better output, and (c) the tool isn't replacing anyone — it's amplifying everyone.

Context Engineering: The Game Changer

Here's the concept that separates teams that get mediocre results from AI from teams that get exceptional results: context engineering.

Context engineering is the practice of systematically providing AI tools with the right information about your project, your conventions, and your requirements. It's the difference between asking the intern "write some code" and giving them your coding standards, architecture docs, and a detailed brief.

Three Levels of Context

Level 1 Prompt Context

What the developer types in each request. The bare minimum.

→
Level 2 Project Context

Rules files (CLAUDE.md, .cursorrules), architecture docs, coding standards. Shared team knowledge.

→
Level 3 Tool Context

MCP servers, connected databases, live API docs, real-time project state. Dynamic, always current.

Most teams are stuck at Level 1. Their developers write prompts from scratch every time, providing no persistent project context. Each request starts from zero.

Teams at Level 2 have invested in shared rules files. These tell the AI about your project's tech stack, coding conventions, naming standards, and common patterns. Every AI request automatically includes this context. The result? Dramatically more consistent and correct output.

Teams at Level 3 are connecting their AI tools to live data sources through the Model Context Protocol (MCP). MCP has become the industry standard for connecting AI agents to tools and data — with 50+ official servers and 150+ community implementations covering databases, dev tools, communication platforms, and cloud infrastructure. Your AI tool can query your actual database schema, read your real API docs, and check your current deployment state.

Scenario: Context Engineering in Action

Two developers on the same team are asked to build a new API endpoint for user profile updates.

Developer A (no context engineering): Opens ChatGPT, types "Write a PATCH endpoint for user profile updates in Node.js." Gets generic Express.js code with no authentication, no input validation matching team standards, and a different error handling pattern than the rest of the codebase.

Developer B (with context engineering): Opens Claude Code in a project with a CLAUDE.md that specifies: "We use Fastify, not Express. Auth middleware is in src/middleware/auth.ts. All endpoints use the ValidationSchema pattern from src/utils/validation.ts. Error responses follow RFC 7807." The AI generates an endpoint that fits seamlessly into the existing codebase.

The difference: 5 minutes of review vs. 45 minutes of rewriting. Same model, same capability — different context.

What Your Team Needs to Know vs. What They Don't

You're not training ML engineers. Here's what matters for your developers:

Must understand:

  • AI predicts, it doesn't reason — always verify output
  • Better context = better output (invest in rules files)
  • Different models for different tasks (cost vs. quality tradeoff)
  • Training data has a cutoff — provide current information in context
  • AI is great at patterns, bad at novel judgment

Nice to know:

  • What tokens are and roughly how pricing works
  • Why temperature affects output variation
  • What the context window is and why it has limits

Don't need to know:

  • Transformer architecture, attention mechanisms, embeddings
  • Training methods (RLHF, constitutional AI, DPO)
  • Model benchmarks and leaderboard rankings
