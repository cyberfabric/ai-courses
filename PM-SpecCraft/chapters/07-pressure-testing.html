Chapter 7

Pressure-Testing Your Spec

Be your own toughest reviewer before engineering is. Use AI to wear six different hats — engineer, designer, legal, support, security, finance — and find the holes while the spec is still cheap to change.

The cheapest review pass you’ll ever run

You closed chapter 6 with a clean Acceptance Criteria section, a triaged edge-case list, and a Definition of Done the team agreed to. The spec is, on paper, in good shape. The version on your screen could be shared right now and would survive a polite reading. That’s the moment to stop and not share it yet.

Every spec ships through the same gauntlet. An engineer reads it and asks what happens at the edges. A designer reads it and asks where the empty state lives. Legal reads it and asks where the data goes. Support reads it and asks who picks up the phone when the flow breaks. You can meet that gauntlet in a one-hour review with seven people in a room, or you can simulate most of it in twenty minutes with an AI tool the night before. The simulated pass won’t catch everything. It will catch enough that the real review becomes a conversation about substance, not a scramble to patch obvious holes.

That’s pressure-testing. It’s the cheapest review pass you’ll ever run, and it’s entirely yours — no calendars to coordinate, no apologies for taking people’s time, no ego on the line because no one else has read the draft yet. The whole point is that mistakes are private here. You find them, you fix them, and the version that lands in your team’s inbox is already a draft or two better than the one you would have sent.

Why this earns trust with engineering

Engineers can tell, almost on the first paragraph, whether a spec has been pressure-tested. The signal is small — assumptions are named explicitly, the unhappy paths get airtime, the risk section has actual risks instead of placeholders. The signal isn’t that the spec is perfect. It’s that someone tried to break it before sending it. That signal does most of the work in the first ten minutes of a review.

The opposite signal is also easy to spot. A spec that hasn’t been pressure-tested reads as a happy-path narrative with the word “robust” sprinkled near the end. Engineering reads that, files the questions in their head, and either burns review time surfacing them in writing or quietly builds around them. Either way, you pay for the missing pass — in trust, in cycle time, or in scope creep at the worst moment.

Doing the pressure-test pass yourself, with AI as the reviewer, flips the dynamic. You arrive at engineering review with a list of the holes you already found, the ones you decided to leave open, and the reasons. That’s a meeting that runs short.

What pressure-testing isn’t

It’s easy to confuse pressure-testing with two adjacent activities, both useful but different. Pressure-testing is not polishing: you’re not asking AI to tighten the prose, fix typos, or improve the headings. Polishing is a separate pass that comes later, and the prompt for it looks completely different. Asking a polish prompt to do a pressure-test gives you a beautifully worded spec with the same holes it had before.

It’s also not a scope review. Scope reviews ask “is this the right thing to build?” Pressure-testing assumes you’ve already answered that question and asks “will this thing, as written, survive contact with the team and the user?” Mixing the two muddles the output: every reviewer hat will start drifting into “maybe you shouldn’t build this at all” mode, which is a fair question for a different conversation.

The cleanest way to keep pressure-testing focused is to say so in the prompt: “Assume the decision to build this has been made. Don’t question scope. Find what’s missing, risky, or underspecified about the spec as written.” Two sentences of framing, much less drift in the output.

The cast of reviewers

Pressure-testing works because real specs get reviewed by people with very different jobs. AI is happy to play any of them, one at a time, if you tell it which hat to put on. Six roles cover most products. Not every spec needs all six — pick the three or four that map to your domain.

🛠️
Senior engineer
Sniffs for state machines, race conditions, missing error handling, scope hidden behind innocent verbs (“sync”, “refresh”, “just”).
🎨
Designer
Sniffs for empty states, error states, mid-flow interruptions, mobile vs. desktop differences, accessibility gaps.
⚖️
Legal & compliance
Sniffs for personal data flows, cross-tenant leakage, consent prompts, retention rules, terms-of-service implications.
🎧
Customer support
Sniffs for unrecoverable states, who picks up the phone, what the macro will say, how to undo a wrong action.
🔐
Security
Sniffs for token expiry, permission escalation, replay risk, who can see what, what happens when an account is compromised.
💰
Finance & ops
Sniffs for billing edges, refund paths, seat-count drift, currency rounding, what shows up on the invoice.

Each role is a lens, not a person. Even if your team has no dedicated legal or finance reviewer, the lens still finds things, because the lens is a different question shape, not a different mood.

The shape of a red-team prompt

A working reviewer prompt has four ingredients: a clear role, a clear job (not just “review this”), the spec itself as input, and an output shape that’s easier to triage than free-form prose. The same four-part shape works in Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot Chat, or anything else. Vary the wording; keep the structure.

Underspecified

“Review this spec and tell me what’s wrong.”

Pressure-test ready

“You’re a senior backend engineer at a SaaS company. Read this spec for a workspace invitation flow. Give me three lists: what’s missing (questions an engineer would ask before estimating), what’s risky (assumptions that look fragile), and what’s underspecified (sentences that could justify two different builds). Be specific. Quote the line you’re reacting to. No general advice.”

The underspecified version produces a polite, generic critique that blends “you should add a metrics section” with “what about accessibility?” with “consider performance.” It’s not wrong; it’s not actionable. The pressure-test version produces a list of questions tied to specific lines, which is something you can either fix or consciously defer.

One more rule that earns its keep: ask for the AI to quote the line it’s reacting to. That single instruction turns vague feedback into traceable feedback, and it makes the next step — converting findings into edits — almost mechanical.

Reviewers in sequence: a worked example

Let’s walk through a small spec under three different reviewer hats, back-to-back. The product is a feature that lets workspace admins invite new members by email. The PRD is roughly one page. We’ll use Gemini for this run, but the same dialogue would unfold in Claude or ChatGPT with no changes.

You’re a senior backend engineer at a B2B SaaS. Here’s the workspace invitation spec [pasted]. Give me three lists: missing, risky, underspecified. Quote the line you’re reacting to. No general advice.
Missing: (1) “Invite expires after a reasonable period” — what counts as reasonable? Hours? Days? (2) Nothing on what happens if the same email is invited twice. (3) No mention of rate limits on the invite endpoint. Risky: (4) “The invitee accepts and is added to the workspace” assumes the invitee already has an account — what about new sign-ups via invite? (5) The role assigned at acceptance is implied to be “Member” but never stated. Underspecified: (6) “Admin can revoke an invite” — revoke before acceptance, after, or both?
Good. Same spec. Now you’re a legal & compliance reviewer at a company subject to GDPR. Same three lists. Same rule: quote the line.
Missing: (1) Nothing about consent — the invitee’s email is processed without their prior knowledge. (2) No retention policy on pending invites. Risky: (3) “Invite includes the inviter’s name and workspace name” — if the invite is sent to a wrong address, you’ve disclosed company affiliation to a stranger. (4) No mention of data residency for invitee email storage. Underspecified: (5) “Audit logs are kept” — for how long, accessible to whom?
Now: customer support lead. You’ve been on the support desk for five years. Same three lists.
Missing: (1) No story for “I never got the email.” Resend? Check spam? Generate a link to share manually? (2) Nothing on lockout: if the only admin loses access, can support recover the workspace? Risky: (3) “Invite link is single-use” — a frequent support ticket will be “I clicked the link from my work computer to preview, then tried on phone and it was dead.” Underspecified: (4) “Members can be removed” — does the removed person see anything? Get an email? Lose their drafts?
One more hat: security engineer at a company that’s had one breach already this year. Same three lists.
Missing: (1) No mention of token format, length, or entropy — the spec just says “invite link.” (2) Nothing on what happens if an invite is accepted from an unexpected IP or geography. Risky: (3) “Invite includes a link the user clicks to accept” — phishing payload, indistinguishable from a real one to an automated email scanner. (4) Pending invites tied to email but not to device; a stolen mailbox grants workspace access. Underspecified: (5) “Audit log records acceptances” — does it record failed acceptance attempts too? That’s the signal you actually want.
Combine all four passes. Give me a deduped list of the top eight findings, ranked by “how badly does this hurt if we ship without addressing it.”
[returns ranked list]

Twenty-five minutes of conversation. Four reviewer hats. A combined list of eight specific, line-traceable findings, ranked by impact. None of the findings are wrong; some you’ll act on, some you’ll defer, a couple you’ll consciously decline. That’s the work of the next pass.

Reusable reviewer-hat templates

The four-part prompt shape doesn’t change between hats — only the role and the “what they smell for” clause do. That makes the prompts easy to keep as templates. Save them in a notes file, a project memory, or wherever you keep your prompt library (chapter 11 builds the kit out properly). Three templates cover most of what you’ll do.

Engineering reviewer template You’re a senior engineer reviewing a product spec at a {company shape}. Your job: find what’s missing, risky, or underspecified about the spec as written. Don’t question scope. Quote the line you’re reacting to. Output three lists: MISSING, RISKY, UNDERSPECIFIED. Keep findings concrete and traceable. No general advice. Spec: {paste}
Legal & compliance reviewer template You’re a legal & compliance reviewer at a company subject to {GDPR / CCPA / HIPAA / your regime}. Read this spec for {personal data flows, cross-tenant access, consent, retention, third-party sharing, audit obligations}. Output three lists: MISSING, RISKY, UNDERSPECIFIED. Quote the line. No general advice. Spec: {paste}
Customer-support reviewer template You’re a customer support lead with five years on the desk for a product like {brief product description}. Read this spec and find the unrecoverable states, the support tickets you’ll get on day one, and the moments where the user has no clear path forward. Three lists: MISSING, RISKY, UNDERSPECIFIED. Quote the line. Spec: {paste}

The three other hats — designer, security, finance — follow the same shape with the role and “smells for” clause swapped. Keep the templates short. The temptation is to make them longer over time; resist it. A short template is one you’ll actually paste at midnight before a Monday review.

Surfacing implicit assumptions

Most spec problems are assumptions in disguise. The line reads cleanly because both you and the reader supplied the same unstated context — until someone supplies different context, and the spec breaks. AI is unusually good at extracting these, because it has no shared context with you and has to make every assumption explicit to read the spec at all.

The prompt is short and reusable: “List every assumption this spec is making, stated or unstated. Number them. For each, say whether it’s explicit in the text or inferred.” What comes back is often unsettling in a useful way.

Implicit assumptions in the workspace invitation spec 1. The invitee has a valid email address that they actively read. (inferred) 2. The invitee speaks the same language as the inviter. (inferred) 3. The workspace is on a paid plan with seats available. (inferred) 4. The inviter has admin permission at the moment of sending. (inferred — not re-checked at acceptance) 5. The invitee will accept within the same calendar month for billing purposes. (inferred) 6. The email service provider is configured and not bouncing. (inferred) 7. The invitee’s email domain is not blocked by an enterprise rule. (inferred) 8. There is exactly one workspace per organization. (inferred from silence on multi-workspace)

Eight assumptions, none stated in the original spec. Each one is a place the build could surprise you. You probably won’t address all eight in this release. You will choose which to make explicit, which to leave alone, and which to add to the open-questions list at the top of the spec. That choice is the value of running the prompt.

The risk register

Once the assumptions are out, fold the most important ones into a small risk register at the bottom of the spec. Four columns is the right shape. Anything more elaborate gets ignored; anything less elaborate gets confused with the open-questions list.

Risks | # | Assumption / risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation | |---|----------------------------------------------------|------------|--------|-----------------------------------------------------| | 1 | Inviter loses admin role between send & accept | Low | Medium | Re-check role at acceptance; reject if missing. | | 2 | Invite link forwarded to a third party | Medium | High | Bind link to invitee email; require email match. | | 3 | Invite email lands in spam, never read | High | Medium | In-app “resend” from admin view; surface in audit log. | | 4 | Workspace runs out of paid seats at acceptance | Medium | Medium | Reserve a seat at send; release on expiry/decline. | | 5 | Two admins invite the same email simultaneously | Low | Low | First write wins; second invite returns “already pending.” |

Five rows is plenty. The discipline isn’t completeness — it’s naming the few risks that, if they materialize, you want a paper trail showing you saw them coming. The mitigation column is what makes the risk register useful in engineering review: each row already proposes a path forward, so the conversation moves from “is this a risk?” to “is this the right mitigation?”

“What would a senior PM ask me about this?”

One last prompt earns its keep on almost every spec. After the reviewer-hat passes, ask the model to play someone in your job, two levels above you. “You’re a senior PM with ten years in this domain. Read my spec. Give me five questions you’d ask me in a one-on-one before signing off.”

The output usually surprises in two ways. First, the questions sound nothing like the engineer or legal questions; they probe motivation, scope, and second-order effects. Second, two or three of the questions are ones you’ve been quietly avoiding. Sample output for the workspace invitation spec:

  • What’s the success metric for this feature in 90 days, and is it actually measurable today?
  • Why now? What changes in the business if we ship this next quarter instead?
  • Who wins if this works — the inviter, the invitee, the workspace owner, the billing team? Are their interests aligned?
  • What’s the cheapest version of this we could ship in a week, and why aren’t we shipping that?
  • If this gets adopted three times faster than expected, what breaks first?

None of these are technical. All of them are the questions a PM gets asked in the meeting they didn’t know was coming. Catching them in your own draft is worth twenty minutes.

The gap matrix

If the spec covers more than one user surface or more than one phase of a flow, a tiny matrix helps you see what each reviewer hat already addressed and what they didn’t. Scope rows down the side, reviewer columns across the top, a checkmark or a one-word note in each cell. Empty cells are unaddressed concerns — not necessarily bugs, but places to either go fill in or to consciously decide are out of scope.

Gap matrix — workspace invitations | Surface / phase | Engineer | Designer | Legal | Support | Security | Finance | |---------------------|----------|----------|-------|---------|----------|---------| | Send invite | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | seat reservation? | | Pending state | ✓ | empty | retention? | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | | Accept (existing) | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | | Accept (new signup) | ✓ | ✓ | consent? | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | | Revoke | ✓ | empty | ✓ | ✓ | ✓ | refund? | | Audit / history | ✓ | n/a | ✓ | empty | ✓ | empty |

Six empty cells. Three of them probably matter for this release; three you’ll decline politely. Either way, the matrix made the decision visible instead of accidental. It also makes a great artifact to share with engineering: “here’s what we covered, here’s what we explicitly chose not to, here’s where we’d like your read.”

What’s yours: triage, not generation. AI will produce findings forever. It will keep playing reviewer hats until you stop asking. None of that is the work. The work is deciding which findings warrant a spec edit this release, which go on the open-questions list, which get logged as known risks, and which you politely decline. That triage call is grounded in things AI doesn’t have: your release date, your team’s capacity, the conversation you had with sales last Tuesday, the customer who’s waiting. A reviewer pass that produces fifty findings and zero decisions hasn’t pressure-tested anything — it’s just a longer to-do list. The decision is yours; that’s the seat you’re sitting in.

From findings to edits, in the same session

The single biggest mistake when pressure-testing isn’t in the prompts; it’s in the gap between finding things and fixing them. If you collect a list of twelve findings and close the laptop, half of them won’t make it into the spec. Some will lose context; some will look smaller in the morning; some will get re-discovered by engineering anyway. The fix is to keep the session open and convert findings into edits while the model still has the spec in working memory.

After each reviewer-hat pass, before moving to the next hat, take five minutes to triage the findings: act-on, defer, decline. For act-on findings, ask the model to draft a one- or two-sentence spec patch you can drop into the relevant section. You’ll edit them in your voice anyway, but starting from a draft beats starting from a blinking cursor.

If the spec edits start to balloon — you’re rewriting whole sections, not patching sentences — that’s a signal to pause, save the current version, and write a one-line decision-history note at the top before continuing. Big edits made invisibly are how through-lines get lost.

Common stumbles, named without blame

Three patterns show up often enough to be worth naming. Each is easy to step around once you’ve seen it.

Uniform-confidence findings. AI returns a list of twelve items in the same authoritative tone whether item one is a release-blocker and item twelve is a typo. Nothing in the output ranks them. The fix is to ask explicitly for ranking by impact and to ask the model to say which findings it’s least sure about. That second question often surfaces the two or three findings that were guesses.

Invented reviewer concerns. Sometimes the model will produce a finding that sounds plausible but doesn’t actually map to anything in the spec — a designer concern about a screen the spec never mentioned, a legal worry about data the feature doesn’t collect. The quote-the-line rule catches most of these; if the model can’t cite the line, the finding is probably hallucinated. Drop it without ceremony.

Reviewer drift. By the third or fourth hat, the model starts producing findings that all sound the same — the engineer hat starts giving you legal-flavored answers, or the support hat starts talking like a designer. The fix is to start each hat in a fresh thread, or to re-anchor with a short reminder of the role at the top of each new prompt. Costs nothing; preserves the lens.

Length-as-effort. A model that returns eighteen findings looks more thorough than one that returns four. It usually isn’t. The marginal findings past five or six tend to be either restatements, low-value style notes, or polite invented concerns. Cap each hat’s output at the top of the prompt — “give me at most six findings, and only ones you’d defend in a review” — and the average quality climbs immediately.

Calibrating how picky the reviewer is

Default reviewer prompts produce a mid-pickiness output: not nitpicky, not lazy, somewhere in the middle. Both extremes are useful at different moments, and you can ask for them explicitly.

Early in a draft, when you genuinely want a wide net, dial up: “Be the pickiest version of this reviewer. Flag anything you’d push back on, even small things. I’ll triage afterward.” Expect twice as many findings, and expect to drop most of them. The point isn’t to act on all of them; it’s to make sure you’re not missing a category.

Late in a draft, near a sharing deadline, dial down: “Be a senior reviewer who only flags issues that would change the build, the timeline, or the risk profile. Skip stylistic and minor issues entirely.” You’ll get four to six findings, and they’ll be the ones worth reading at 11pm. Both modes use the same template; only the calibration sentence changes.

When the spec passes too easily

Sometimes you run three reviewer hats and get back lists that feel suspiciously light: a couple of vague suggestions, no specifics, no quoted lines. It’s tempting to declare the spec battle-tested and move on. Pause for a second. A clean pass usually means one of two things, and only one of them is good news.

The good version: the spec is genuinely tight because earlier passes did the heavy lifting — you sharpened the problem in chapter 3, you co-wrote a careful PRD in chapter 5, you triaged AC in chapter 6. By the time pressure-testing arrives, the obvious holes are already plugged. This is real, and you should believe it when the rest of the spec earns it.

The other version: the prompt is too soft, the model is being polite, or the spec is too short for the model to grip. Re-run with a pickier calibration, or paste the spec in a fresh thread and ask the model to first describe the spec back to you in its own words. If the description has gaps or invents details, those gaps are the findings; the original review just didn’t surface them.

A second, smaller example

Pressure-testing isn’t only for big features. A short spec for a refund flow benefits from the same pass, often more, because short specs hide more in their assumptions. Imagine a one-page spec saying: “Customers can request a refund from their order page. Refunds under $50 are automatic; over $50 go to a support reviewer. The refund hits their original payment method within 5 business days.”

Two reviewer hats, fifteen minutes total. Finance: “What about partial refunds? Refunds for orders that span two billing periods? Currency at refund time vs. at order time? Refunds for orders paid by gift card?” Customer support: “What does the customer see while it’s pending? What if the original card is expired or canceled? What’s the macro for ‘my refund didn’t arrive in 5 days’ on day 6?” The original spec was three sentences. The reviewer pass produced eight specific questions. Half of them probably belong in the spec; the other half belong on the open-questions list. Either way, the cost was a quarter-hour and a coffee.

Five questions to run before you share the spec.

  • Can you name the riskiest assumption in the spec out loud, in one sentence?
  • Did each reviewer hat produce at least one finding you ended up acting on?
  • Does the spec now name what’s explicitly out of scope, not just what’s in?
  • If a stranger read the open-questions list, would they understand what you’re still figuring out?
  • Could you point an engineer at a specific paragraph and say “this is the part I’m least sure about” without hesitating?

If any answer is “not yet”, you’ve found the next twenty minutes of work. None of the questions are about the spec being perfect; all of them are about you knowing where the spec stands.

Same prompts, any tool

This whole pass is tool-agnostic on purpose. The reviewer-hat shape, the assumption extractor, the risk register, the gap matrix — they all work in Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot Chat, Windsurf’s Cascade, or whatever your team standardized on. The differences across tools live in attachment handling and context window, not in the prompts. Some tools accept a PDF spec; others want the markdown pasted in. Some have project memory; others reset every conversation. Pick the tool whose attachment shape matches the form your specs already live in, and you’ll spend zero time on plumbing.

If you have access to two tools, one trick worth a try: run the same reviewer-hat prompt in two different models and compare the lists. They’ll overlap by maybe sixty percent. The forty-percent gap is interesting — not because one model is right and the other isn’t, but because the gap usually contains the findings you most want to think about.

Giving the reviewer enough context

A reviewer hat with no domain context produces generic findings. A reviewer hat with a paragraph of context produces sharp ones. Before running the reviewer pass, take two minutes to attach or paste the smallest set of context that grounds the AI in your specific product:

  • One paragraph on the product. What is it, who pays for it, what’s the main job it does. Three or four sentences.
  • One paragraph on the user. Who is the spec for, what surface they’re on, what they were doing right before they hit this flow.
  • The constraints that aren’t obvious from the spec. Mobile-first? Enterprise? Self-serve only? Compliance regime? One or two lines.
  • What you’ve already decided. So the model doesn’t spend reviewer effort questioning settled calls.

Most tools accept this as a system prompt, a project memory, or just a preamble pasted before the spec. Whichever your tool prefers is fine. The point is that the reviewer is now reviewing your product’s spec, not a generic SaaS spec, and the findings reflect that immediately. The same prompt, with and without this context, produces visibly different output.

Try It: the three-hat pressure-test

Pick a spec you’re actively working on. It doesn’t need to be finished — pressure-testing a draft is more useful than pressure-testing a final. Then:

  1. Choose three reviewer hats from the cast of six. Pick the ones whose concerns most map to your spec’s domain.
  2. Run the four-part red-team prompt for each hat in sequence: clear role, clear job, the spec, three-list output shape (missing / risky / underspecified). Insist on quoted lines.
  3. After the third hat, ask the model to dedupe and rank the combined findings by impact.
  4. Triage the ranked list into three buckets: act-on this release, defer with an open-question note, decline (and say why in one sentence).
  5. Pick the single finding that most changed the spec. Write one sentence about what it changed and why. That sentence is the proof you ran the pass.

Time-box the whole thing to forty-five minutes. Most of the value lands in the first thirty.

When to re-run the pass

Once you’ve acted on the first round of findings, the spec on your screen is materially different from the one the reviewers saw. New sentences, new sections, new mitigations. A small voice in the back of your head will say: are these new edits any good? The answer is to re-run the pass — but lighter, faster, and only against the parts that changed.

A short re-run prompt does the trick: “Same spec as before, but here’s the diff — the parts that changed since your last review. Just re-review these specific edits as the same reviewer hat. Did I introduce new risks? Did the edit actually address the original finding?” Five minutes per hat. Often you’ll find that one or two of your fixes introduced a small new wrinkle — which is the entire reason the second pass exists.

Two re-runs is usually the right ceiling. Past that, returns drop sharply, and you’re into territory where real human reviewers add more value than another simulated pass. Pressure-testing is a sharpening tool; it’s not a replacement for the conversation that comes next.

What you should walk away with

By the end of a pressure-test pass, the spec on your screen should look slightly different than it did an hour ago. A few sentences will be sharper. The risk section will have actual rows. The open-questions list at the top of the document will be shorter or longer, but it will reflect a real conversation rather than placeholders. Somewhere in your notes, there will be a small list of findings you decided not to act on, each with a one-line reason. That list is not a regret; it’s the proof that triage happened.

You should also feel a quiet shift in confidence. Not the false confidence of having addressed everything — that confidence is brittle and breaks on the first real reviewer comment. The other kind: the confidence of knowing where the spec is weak, where you chose to leave it weak, and what you’d say if someone asked you why. Engineering review goes very differently when you walk in with that kind of confidence.

One more thing usually shows up by the end of the pass: a slightly clearer view of which parts of the spec are most likely to change. Some sections will have survived four reviewer hats almost untouched — those are the stable parts, the ones you can build on. Others will have absorbed most of the edits — those are the unstable parts, where the team should probably plan for a second iteration after the first slice ships. Knowing which sections fall into which bucket is itself useful information; engineering can plan around volatility much more easily than around uncertainty.

The spec is now, finally, ready to share. Not perfect — nothing reviewable is — but ready in the sense that you’ve done the work that’s yours to do before asking anyone else to spend their attention on it. That’s the boundary between drafting and shipping a draft. Pressure-testing is what walks you across it.

The five-minute version

Some specs don’t earn an hour. A small change to a settings page, a copy tweak, a feature flag rollout — the full multi-hat pass would take longer than the build. There’s a tighter version that still adds value, and it fits in the time it takes to make a cup of coffee.

Pick the one reviewer hat that maps best to the spec’s biggest unknown. If the spec touches money, finance. If it touches data, legal. If it touches a flow with state transitions, engineering. Run the standard four-part prompt with one calibration line added: “Limit yourself to the three findings most likely to embarrass us if we ship without addressing them.” Three findings, one hat, five minutes. You won’t catch everything; you’ll catch the worst things, which is the right ratio when time is short.

Use the five-minute version as a default, not as a corner-cut. A team that runs a five-minute pass on every spec is meaningfully ahead of a team that runs a one-hour pass on the big ones and nothing on the rest, because the small specs are where assumption-shaped bugs love to live.

A note on solo work and team norms

Pressure-testing is a solo activity by design. The whole value proposition is that you find things in private and arrive at the team review with answers. That said, two small habits make the practice play well with the rest of your team.

First, surface the artifact, not the conversation. The risk register, the gap matrix, and the open-questions list all belong in the spec; the chat transcript with the AI does not. Engineering doesn’t need the prompt history to evaluate the result, and including it tends to invite a meta-conversation about the tool rather than about the spec.

Second, when a teammate asks how you arrived at a particular finding, just say so. “I role-played a security reviewer with the model and this came out of it” is a perfectly normal sentence in 2026. It builds trust. It also normalizes the practice for teammates who haven’t yet picked it up — the next person to write a spec on your team is more likely to try the same pass because you mentioned it casually.
