Chapter 11

Your Personal AI PM Kit

Spec one took a week. Spec two doesn’t have to. The kit is what turns the craft from “I figured it out for this one” into “I have a way of working that I bring to every spec.”

Spec two is on your plate

The first spec shipped on Tuesday. The retro went well. By Thursday morning a new ticket landed in your queue: a different surface, a different team, a different problem. You open a blank doc, you open your AI tool of choice, and you notice the same small irritation: you’re about to redo all the setup work. You’ll re-explain the product context to the assistant. You’ll re-type the “sharpen this problem statement” prompt from memory. You’ll rebuild the PRD outline from scratch. By the end of the morning, half of what you’ve done is recreating scaffolding you already had on the last spec.

That gap — between “the craft I figured out on spec one” and “the craft I have ready for spec two” — is what a personal AI PM kit closes. The kit is small. It’s a handful of templates, a one-page context pack, a list of reusable prompts, and a place where all of it lives. Built once, refined monthly, it shaves hours off every spec from then on.

This chapter is about building that kit and keeping it portable across whichever AI tool you happen to be using this quarter. The kit is yours, not the team’s — teams come in chapter 12. Right now we’re building the version of the kit that lives on your laptop and travels with you between Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, or Windsurf, depending on what your day looks like.

Why a kit beats freelancing every spec

Freelancing each spec means starting from a blank page every time. Every time you sit down with the assistant, you re-explain the product, re-prompt the same questions, and re-discover the same edge cases. The work feels productive because you’re typing, but most of the typing is recreating things you already have somewhere — in last quarter’s spec, in a slack thread from October, in the back of your head.

A kit beats freelancing for three reasons that compound. First, less re-explanation: the assistant gets the product context in one paste rather than ten back-and-forths. Second, more consistency: AC across your specs start to look like AC, not like fourteen different shapes from fourteen different sessions. Third, faster ramp on each new spec: by the time you’ve written your fifth spec with the kit, the kit is doing maybe a third of the typing for you and the spec still reads as yours.

None of this is about cleverness. It’s about not wasting the work you already did. The first time you wrote a sharp problem statement with the assistant’s help, the prompts you used were probably good. Saving them turned a one-off win into a reusable tool. The kit is just the box those tools live in.

The four parts of the kit

A working kit has four parts. They’re small — together, they fit in fewer pages than a single PRD. The discipline is to keep them small; the temptation is to grow each part until it’s too heavy to maintain.

Templates. Reusable document skeletons. PRD outline, AC checklist, edge-case prompt, risk register, retro one-pager. Each is a structure you fill in, not prose you copy. Templates carry the shape of good output without forcing it into a single voice.

Context packs. A one-page brief about your product that you paste into the assistant before doing real work. Product overview, customer segments, glossary, prior decisions, “what we don’t do.” The context pack is the assistant’s onboarding doc; it makes every prompt afterwards sharper.

Prompt library. Ten to fifteen named prompts you reuse. Each has an input shape (what you paste in), an expected output (what you should get back), and a when-to-use note. The library beats remembering every prompt from scratch.

Naming conventions and storage. Where the kit lives, how files are named, how versions are tracked. Boring on the surface; load-bearing in practice. A kit you can’t find at 9am on a Monday is not a kit.

Part 1: Templates

Templates are the shapes you reuse. Five usually cover the ground: a PRD outline, an AC checklist, an edge-case prompt, a risk register, and a retro one-pager. None of them is fancy. The PRD outline is the headings you put on every PRD; the AC checklist is the question list you run on every set of acceptance criteria; the edge-case prompt is the question shape you use every time you need to find what you missed.

Here’s a PRD outline template, the way it might look as a saved file in your kit. It’s the same skeleton from chapter 5 — the value here is just having it pre-saved so you don’t reconstruct it every time.

Template: PRD outline (kit v0.3) # [Spec name] Status: [draft / open for review / frozen vN] Decision log: (one-line entries, newest on top) ## Problem - One sentence on what is broken or missing - Evidence (numbers, quotes, observations) ## Users - Primary segment, secondary segment - What changes for them when this ships ## Goals & non-goals - Goals (max 3) - Explicit non-goals (what we are not doing) ## Scope - In scope: bullet list - Out of scope: bullet list with one-line reason each ## UX shape - Key surfaces, key states, the “happy path” ## Acceptance criteria - Testable, observable, unambiguous ## Risks & open questions - Each with owner and rough date ## Metrics - The one number we’ll be judged on; the supporting set

Notice what’s in the template and what isn’t. The headings are there. The structure is there. The actual content is yours to fill in for each spec. No template should ever read as “here’s the spec, just change the names” — that’s a recipe for specs that all sound the same and none of which sound like you.

The other four templates have the same shape: a skeleton with field labels, a one-line note on what each field is for, no prose to inherit. The AC checklist has fields like “is each AC observable from outside the system?” and “does each AC name a single behaviour?”. The risk register has columns: assumption, probability, impact, mitigation, owner. The retro one-pager has three boxes: what shipped, what surprised us, what changes for the next spec. Pre-saved, each takes ten seconds to copy and start filling.

Part 2: Context packs

The context pack is the part of the kit that does the most work for the least effort. It’s a one-page brief about your product that you paste into the assistant at the start of any non-trivial session. The assistant goes from “random helpful generalist” to “helpful generalist who knows your product enough to skip the basic questions.” The lift in answer quality is immediate and large.

Five sections, kept short. Together they fit on one screen.

Context pack — [Product name] (updated 2026-04-15) ## What we are - One paragraph: what the product does, who pays for it, how big the team is, what stage we’re at. ## Who uses it - Two to four customer segments, one line each. Names, rough sizes, what they care about, what they avoid. ## Glossary - Ten to twenty product-specific terms with short definitions. Internal nicknames count. Don’t make the assistant guess. ## Prior decisions - Five to ten one-line entries. “We chose X over Y in Q1 because Z.” The history that constrains today’s spec. ## What we don’t do - Five to ten things we have explicitly decided are out of scope as a product. (“We don’t do real-time chat.” “We don’t store payment data ourselves.”)

The “what we don’t do” section is the one most people skip and the one that pays back the most. Without it, the assistant cheerfully suggests features you’ve already ruled out, and you spend half the session waving them off. With it, the assistant knows the boundaries of your product and stays inside them.

The context pack lives in one file, named clearly (e.g. context-pack-2026Q2.md), and gets refreshed once a quarter or whenever a load-bearing fact changes. You don’t need to push the file anywhere fancy — a Notion page, an Obsidian note, a Google Doc, a markdown file in a personal repo, all work. What matters is that you can find it in five seconds and paste it into Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot Chat, or Windsurf without thinking about it.

Part 3: Prompt library

Ten to fifteen named prompts is the right size. Fewer than ten and you’re still writing them from scratch most of the time. More than fifteen and you can’t remember what’s in there, which means you write them from scratch anyway. Aim for the shelf where you actually browse the contents.

Each prompt in the library has the same three-part shape, so future-you can scan the file and find what you need without re-reading prompts.

Prompt: sharpen-problem-statement (v2) When to use: Early discovery; you have a fuzzy brief and need to push it through 3–4 rounds of sharpening before writing AC. Input shape (paste under the prompt): - The fuzzy brief, verbatim - One paragraph of relevant context - Any data points you have (numbers, quotes) Expected output: - 3 candidate sharper problem statements - For each: what evidence would confirm it, what would reframe it, who the primary user is The prompt: “You are helping me sharpen a problem statement. I’ll paste the fuzzy version, the context, and the data below. Don’t propose solutions yet. Give me three candidate sharper problem statements. For each, name the evidence that would confirm it, the reframe that would invalidate it, and who the primary user is. Push back if any of them is the same statement in different words.”

Three sections: when to use, input shape, the prompt itself. The when-to-use line is the one that saves time later — you scan the library by use case, not by prompt text. The input shape forces you to gather the right material before pasting, which is half of what makes a prompt produce a good answer. The prompt itself is short, specific, and ends with one explicit constraint (here: “push back if any of them is the same statement in different words”).

A useful starter library covers the loop you walk every spec: sharpen-problem-statement, draft-prd-section, generate-edge-cases, classify-comments, propose-diff-edits, translate-for-audience, run-smell-tests, draft-handoff-summary, summarize-decision-log, prune-stale-questions. Ten prompts, each in the same template. That’s most of the kit’s daily value.

For comparison, here are two more entries from the library — both shorter than the sharpening prompt, both used several times a week. Notice how the shape stays the same; only the contents change. That uniformity is what makes the library scannable.

Prompt: generate-edge-cases (v3) When to use: After AC is drafted, before pressure-testing. Surfaces edge cases you missed in the happy-path framing. Input shape: - Current AC, verbatim - One paragraph on the user’s real workflow Expected output: - 12 edge cases grouped by category (empty, error, slow, permissions, multi-language, accessibility, time zones, large data, concurrent actions) - For each: a one-line probability/impact gut-check The prompt: “Below is the current AC for [feature]. Generate 12 edge cases grouped by category. For each, give a one-line gut-check on probability and impact. Don’t suggest solutions; just surface the cases. Flag any that contradict an existing AC.”
Prompt: translate-for-audience (v2) When to use: After the spec is drafted, when you need a briefing email for leadership, sales enablement, or support. Input shape: - The relevant spec section - The audience (leadership / sales / support / legal) - The form (email / slack message / 3 bullets) Expected output: - One version of the section reshaped for the audience - Same facts, different lead, different vocabulary - A note flagging any qualifier or number that shifted The prompt: “Rewrite the section below for [audience] as a [form]. Lead with what they care about most. Keep every number and constraint exactly as written; flag anything you simplified or rounded. End with the one open question they’re most likely to ask.”

Three prompts, three different jobs, identical template shape. The shape is what makes the library easy to scan and easy to extend — new prompts slot in without forcing you to invent a new format. After a few months, you’ll find that you’ve internalised the shape so well that drafting a new entry takes about five minutes.

Part 4: Naming conventions and storage

The kit lives somewhere. The choice matters less than the consistency. Notion, Obsidian, a Google Drive folder, a private Git repo, a single Apple Notes folder — any of them works if you actually open it on Monday morning when the new spec lands.

Three naming habits make the kit easy to live with. First, prefix files with their type: template-prd-outline.md, context-pack-2026Q2.md, prompt-sharpen-problem.md. Second, put the version date in the name when something changes meaningfully: template-ac-checklist-2026-03.md. Third, keep an index.md at the top of the kit folder that lists every file in one line each. The index is your menu; without it, you scroll.

Versioning doesn’t need git unless you already use git. A simple convention works: when a template or prompt changes meaningfully, save it with a new date in the filename, and keep the previous one for two months in case the change turns out to be wrong. Then prune. The point is to have a small recent history, not an archive.

The compounding payoff: same prompt, different spec

The clearest way to see why the kit pays off is to watch the same prompt do work on two different specs in two different weeks. Same prompt, same context pack format, different surface. The assistant ramps up faster the second time because the prompt is sharper and the context is already in shape.

[Pasted: context pack — product overview, segments, glossary, prior decisions, what-we-don’t-do]

[Pasted: prompt template — sharpen-problem-statement v2]

Fuzzy brief: “Notifications feel noisy for power users. Can we do something about that?”
Three candidate sharper problem statements:
1. Power users on the Pro plan receive 40+ in-app notifications per day, of which they act on fewer than 10%; the rest are read-and-dismissed friction. Confirmed by: notification engagement metrics on the Pro segment. Reframe risk: the issue is not volume but routing — the right notifications are buried among the wrong ones. Primary user: Pro-plan workspace admins managing 5+ projects.
2. The notification preferences UI hasn’t kept pace with the feature surface; users can mute categories but not granular triggers. Confirmed by: support tickets requesting per-trigger mutes. Reframe risk: the demand is small and concentrated in a few enterprise accounts. Primary user: enterprise admins managing notification policy.
3. Power users have no “quiet hours” concept; notifications keep firing during focus blocks. Confirmed by: Slack-status integration usage data. Reframe risk: users want this from us but actually solve it via OS-level Do Not Disturb. Primary user: individual contributors on Pro and Enterprise.

None of these three is the same statement in different words; they each name a distinct lever.
Two weeks later, different spec: invite flow.

[Pasted: same context pack]
[Pasted: same prompt template]

Fuzzy brief: “Team invitations have a high drop-off. Can we improve the funnel?”
Three candidate sharper problem statements, scoped to your product’s constraints (especially the “we don’t store payment data” and SSO-routing notes from your context pack):
1. Forty percent of invited teammates never make it into the workspace; most failures are silent. Confirmed by: end-to-end funnel instrumentation on invite tokens. Reframe risk: some failures are intentional (invitee changed jobs, declined). Primary user: workspace admins doing onboarding.
2. The 7-day expiry window is too short for enterprise customers running quarterly batch invites. Confirmed by: recurring re-send patterns from a small set of accounts. Reframe risk: small denominator; not worth a config knob. Primary user: enterprise admins.
3. SSO-routing edge cases produce silent failures invisible to the inviter. Confirmed by: incident postmortems Q1 2026. Reframe risk: the SSO failures are a separate spec. Primary user: SSO-enabled workspace admins.

Two specs, two weeks apart, ten minutes of setup each. The output is sharp out of the gate because the assistant already knows the product and the prompt already knows what shape of answer to produce. Without the kit, both sessions would have started with twenty minutes of context-rebuilding before the first useful answer. The kit didn’t make the assistant smarter; it made the starting position better.

Tool-agnostic portability

The whole kit is plain text. Every part of it — templates, context packs, prompts — is a markdown-friendly document with no tool-specific markup. That portability is the design constraint, because the AI tool you use this quarter is probably not the one you’ll use next quarter, and you don’t want to rebuild the kit each time you switch.

Three concrete attachment shapes cover most tools:

Paste-into-chat. Works in every chat-style assistant: Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot Chat. You paste the context pack at the top of the session, then paste the prompt template, then paste the input. Three blocks, in that order. Verbose but universal.

Project files / system prompts. Tools like Claude Projects, ChatGPT Custom GPTs, and Gemini Gems let you pin a context document so it’s always available without re-pasting. Drop the context pack in once, and every prompt in that project starts with the context already loaded. The prompt library still pastes per session, but the heavy lifting is pre-attached.

Repo-attached docs. Tools like Windsurf and Copilot in IDE mode read files from a repo. If your kit lives in a personal repo (or a shared product-docs repo), you can reference the context pack and prompt files directly. The assistant pulls them in on demand. This shape suits PMs who already work in a code-adjacent environment.

The point of the three shapes is that the kit’s content doesn’t change — only the wrapper does. A context pack written for paste-into-chat works just as well as a Claude Project file or a repo-attached markdown doc. You don’t maintain three versions; you maintain one and adapt how you load it.

Maintaining the kit: the 30-minute monthly review

Kits decay. A prompt that worked great in February drifts out of step with how the product evolved by May. A context pack written in Q1 misses the segment you launched in Q2. Without maintenance, the kit becomes the AI equivalent of an old browser bookmarks folder: full of dead links and forgotten purpose.

Thirty minutes a month is enough to keep it healthy. Pick a recurring slot (last Friday afternoon, first Monday morning — whatever sticks). Open the kit folder. Walk three quick passes:

Prune. Any prompt or template you haven’t used in the last 30 days, archive it. Not delete — move it to an archive/ subfolder so you can fish it out if you need it. The kit gets lighter; the daily-use parts surface to the top.

Add. Think back over the last month of specs. Any prompt you typed twice from memory? Save it as a template entry in the library. Any context fact you re-explained to the assistant more than once? Add it to the context pack. The new wins from the month get crystallised into the kit.

Refresh. Open the context pack. Scan it for staleness. Did any “what we don’t do” items become things we now do? Did any prior decisions get overturned? Did the team grow or change shape? Update the file with a new date in the name; archive the old one for two months.

Thirty minutes, three passes, once a month. The discipline is small; the difference between a kit you trust and a kit you don’t is exactly this maintenance loop.

The tell. You open the kit on Monday morning, scroll through the prompt library, and notice a prompt you’ve never used — you don’t even remember writing it. Or you spot two templates that do the same thing in slightly different words. Or the context pack still mentions a customer segment you sunset two quarters ago.

The check. Ask of each item: have I used this in the last 30 days? Would I describe it the same way today? Does it duplicate something already in the kit? If the answer to any of those is no, the item is owing you a decision.

The fix. Archive aggressively. A small kit you use is worth ten times a big kit you don’t. Move the unused items to archive/, keep the ones that earn their place, and trust that anything you genuinely need will come back in the next monthly review.

What goes in the kit, and what doesn’t

One thing worth naming: not everything from your spec work belongs in the kit. The kit is the reusable layer. Anything that’s genuinely one-off — the actual PRD for the team-invitation flow, the slack thread about the Q2 metric — lives in your normal product docs, not in the kit. The kit holds the shapes and the system prompts; the specs hold the substance.

The clearest test is portability. If a thing you’re considering adding to the kit only makes sense for one specific spec, it doesn’t belong. If it’d be useful on the next three specs you’ll write, it does. Templates pass this test by definition. Context packs pass it because the product context is the same across all your specs. Prompts pass it when they’re generic enough to work on multiple problem shapes.

What fails this test, occasionally: clever one-off prompts that worked great on one weird spec but won’t come up again. Capture them in the spec’s notes if you want; don’t bury them in the kit where they’ll add noise to every monthly review.

What’s yours: this kit, not the team’s standard. The kit you build in this chapter is personal. It reflects your taste, your voice, your judgment about which prompts produce output you can actually use. It is not the team’s style guide, not a standard your colleagues should adopt, not something to push into a shared template library before you’ve lived with it for three months. Teams come in chapter 12, and they have their own dynamics. Right now, the work is to build the kit that makes your spec work faster and more consistent, in your voice. If a teammate wants to copy a prompt from your library later, that’s a happy side-effect — not the goal. The kit you maintain for yourself is always sharper than a kit you maintain for an audience.

The maturity arc: from v0.1 to v1.0

Kits don’t arrive fully formed. They start rough, they get better through use, and after about six months they reach a steady state where most weeks involve no changes and the monthly review is mostly a quick scan. The arc is worth knowing because it sets expectations — if your kit at week two is still rough, that’s normal; if your kit at month six is still being rebuilt every Friday, something is off.

Kit v0.1 (week one). You have a context pack that’s mostly right, five prompts saved, three templates. The index file is bare. Half of your sessions still feel like freelancing because you forget the kit is there. The kit is helping but you’re not yet leaning on it.

Kit v0.3 (month two). The prompt library is up to ten entries. The context pack has a glossary section that actually matches your team’s slang. You’ve archived two prompts that didn’t earn their place and added three new ones from recent specs. Reaching for the kit is starting to feel automatic. Sessions ramp faster.

Kit v0.7 (month four). The library is at its soft cap of fifteen prompts. The context pack has been refreshed once for a product change. You’ve switched between two AI tools without rebuilding anything because the kit is plain markdown. The monthly review takes twenty minutes instead of thirty. You start noticing that new specs feel substantially easier than the equivalent ones from six months ago.

Kit v1.0 (month six and beyond). The kit is steady. You add a new prompt every month or two, retire one every month or two, refresh the context pack quarterly. The monthly review is mostly “yep, still good.” The kit is doing maybe a quarter of the typing on every spec, the spec still reads as yours, and you can’t imagine working without it. Your colleagues have started asking about it.

The arc isn’t guaranteed. The thing that makes it happen is the monthly review. PMs who skip the review usually plateau at v0.3 — a kit that’s helpful but never quite gets sharp. PMs who run the review consistently hit v1.0 by the second quarter and stay there. The discipline is small; the compounding is real.

When you switch AI tools mid-quarter

The AI tool landscape shifts fast. The chat assistant you preferred in January may not be the one you reach for in June. A new release lands that’s suddenly better at one of the things you do daily. Your employer rolls out a new tool with stronger privacy guarantees. A pricing change makes your old default less attractive. Tool switches happen.

The kit’s portability is the thing that makes those switches cheap. Because everything is plain markdown, the migration is mostly mechanical: paste the context pack into the new tool’s project-file slot, drop the prompt library into a folder the new tool can see, and you’re running. The first week in the new tool feels rough because you’re calibrating to its tone and quirks; by week two, the kit’s scaffolding is doing the same work it did before.

One small gotcha: prompts that depend heavily on a specific tool’s personality (Claude’s tendency to push back, ChatGPT’s default formality, Gemini’s preference for structured lists) sometimes need a one-line adjustment when they move. If the sharpening prompt produced great pushback in Claude and bland agreement in a different tool, add an explicit pushback instruction to the prompt itself: “Disagree with at least one of my framings if you can defend the disagreement.” The fix is small and the prompt is now more portable than before. Those small calibrations are usually the only migration cost.

Two anti-patterns worth knowing

The bloated kit. Once you start saving prompts, the temptation is to save every prompt you’ve ever written. Six months later, the prompt library has 47 entries, you remember about 8 of them, and the rest are dead weight that slows you down every time you scan the file. The cure is the monthly prune. The prevention is a soft cap: 15 prompts in the active library, the rest in archive. If you want to add a 16th, retire one first.

The brittle kit. Some kits get over-tuned to a single AI tool. The prompts assume Claude’s exact tone of voice. The context pack uses ChatGPT’s system-prompt syntax. The templates rely on Gemini’s formatting quirks. When the tool changes — or when you switch jobs and lose access to it — the kit needs a full rewrite. The cure is to keep everything in plain markdown, with prompts that work in any chat assistant. Test occasionally by pasting a prompt into a tool you don’t usually use; if the answer quality drops sharply, the prompt is too brittle.

Sharing the kit (or not)

Eventually a colleague will see you working with the kit and ask if they can have a copy. Two paths work; one path doesn’t.

The first path that works: send them your kit as a snapshot, with the explicit framing that it’s your kit and they should fork it, prune ruthlessly, and adapt to their own voice. They’ll keep maybe a third of what you sent and rewrite the rest. That’s a healthy outcome — they get a head start, you don’t take on a maintenance burden.

The second path that works: extract two or three of the most generic templates from your kit (the PRD outline, the AC checklist), and contribute those to a shared team folder as standalone documents. The shared folder is small and obvious; everyone benefits; nobody owns the maintenance.

The path that doesn’t work: declaring your personal kit the team standard and trying to maintain it for everyone. Personal kits live or die by how much they reflect their owner. The moment a kit has multiple owners with different tastes, it converges to a lowest-common-denominator that helps nobody. Keep your kit yours; let the team work out its own conventions in chapter 12 territory.

A worked example: building kit v0.1 in an afternoon

Here’s what it looks like to build the first version. The whole thing takes about three hours of focused work, often spread across two afternoons.

Hour one is the context pack. Open a fresh markdown file, name it context-pack-v01.md. Write the five sections from memory; don’t research, don’t look anything up. Get a rough draft on the page. Read it through; ask Claude or ChatGPT to flag any section that feels thin or contradictory; tighten the parts the assistant called out. By the end of the hour, you have a one-page brief that captures your product accurately enough to load into any AI session.

Hour two is the prompt library. Pull up your last three or four spec sessions. Find the five or six prompts you used most heavily. Copy each one into a fresh markdown file with the three-part shape: when-to-use, input-shape, the prompt itself. Don’t over-edit; the prompts that worked already worked. Save them with the prompt-* naming convention. By the end of the hour, you have eight or so prompts saved, which is a workable starting library.

Hour three is templates and the index. Pull the PRD outline, the AC checklist, and the risk register from your last shipped spec; strip them of spec-specific content; save them as templates. Then write a one-page index.md at the top of the kit folder that lists every file in one line. The index is what makes the kit usable on Monday morning.

That’s kit v0.1. It’s rough, it has gaps, and it will get sharper with each monthly review. The first version doesn’t need to be polished; it needs to exist. From there, the kit improves itself through use — you notice what’s missing on real specs and you add it next month.

Try It: build kit v0.1 this week

Block three hours on your calendar this week. Don’t need them to be consecutive; two ninety-minute sessions work fine. Then walk the three steps:

  1. Context pack (60 min). Open a fresh file. Write the five sections from memory: what we are, who uses it, glossary, prior decisions, what we don’t do. One page total. Have Claude or Gemini read it and flag thin spots; fix them.
  2. Prompt library (60 min). Pull up your last three spec sessions. Pick the five most-used prompts. Copy each into a separate file with the when-to-use / input-shape / prompt template. Save with a prompt-* prefix.
  3. Templates and index (60 min). Strip the PRD outline, AC checklist, and risk register from your last shipped spec. Save each as a template. Write a one-page index file listing everything in the kit.

By Friday you’ll have a kit that’s usable on Monday. Don’t aim for polish; aim for existence. The first time you reach for the kit on a real spec, you’ll learn more about what it needs than you would from another hour of editing in the abstract.

Where the kit shines, where it doesn’t

The kit makes the predictable parts of spec work faster. Sharpening a fuzzy brief, drafting a PRD section, generating edge cases, classifying review comments, translating a paragraph for a different audience — all of these are jobs the kit was designed for, and on all of them the kit shaves real time off the session. By the third or fourth spec, you’ll notice that those phases feel almost frictionless because the templates and prompts have absorbed the muscle memory.

Where the kit doesn’t help, and shouldn’t try to: the unique-judgment moments. Whether the problem is worth solving. Which user matters this quarter. When to push back on a senior reviewer. When to declare the spec frozen even though three more comments are still pending. None of these belong in a template; none of them get easier with a saved prompt. The kit speeds up the mechanical layer so you have more attention for the layer where attention is the only thing that matters.

That split — mechanical work in the kit, judgment work outside it — is the same split this whole course has been about. AI accelerates the mechanical layer; you stay in the driver’s seat for the judgment layer. The kit just makes the acceleration repeatable. It’s the practical artefact of a craft that is otherwise easy to lose between specs.
