Chapter 10

Iterating After First Feedback

The spec is back, covered in comments. How to absorb feedback fast with AI’s help, without losing the through-line that made the spec yours in the first place.

The spec comes back covered in marks

The handoff is done. You hit share, sent the link, and waited the long twenty-four hours that follow. Now the spec is back. There are eighteen comments in the margin, three slack threads referencing it, and one email from leadership with the subject line “quick thoughts.” Some of the feedback is sharp. Some is contradictory. Some is a small thing that turns out to change everything. And the sprint planning meeting is on Tuesday.

This is the moment most specs lose their shape. Not because the feedback is bad — usually it’s good. The shape gets lost because the iteration happens piecemeal: comment by comment, edit by edit, with the AI drafting little patches in between. Each individual change feels reasonable. By Tuesday morning, the spec is twenty percent longer, the original problem statement has drifted a quarter-turn, and you can’t quite say what changed since Friday.

The high-leverage move in this phase is to slow down for ten minutes before speeding up. Capture everything first. Classify before you change anything. Then iterate in batches, with AI as your draft partner and you as the editor who guards the through-line. That sequence — capture, classify, draft, edit, log — is what this chapter is about.

Step one: capture all the comments before changing anything

The instinct when comments arrive is to start replying. A comment comes in, the answer feels obvious, you make the edit, you tick the box. By comment six, you’ve already shifted three things in the spec without seeing the full set. That ordering trades calm for speed and almost always loses both.

What works better is a five-minute capture pass. Open a blank doc, your AI tool of choice — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, whichever you have at hand — and a kettle. Read every comment, every thread, every email. Type them into the doc verbatim. Don’t respond, don’t edit the spec, don’t even decide whether you agree yet. Just collect.

The capture pass does two things. It gives you the full surface area of the feedback before you touch anything, which prevents the “each edit looks fine, the spec ends up incoherent” failure mode. And it surfaces contradictions early — engineering wants the scope smaller, sales wants it bigger, and leadership wants it shipped sooner. You can’t resolve a contradiction you haven’t noticed.

If the feedback is spread across docs, slack, email, and a Loom video, paste it all into one place before you start. Specs lose coherence when feedback lives in five tools. The unified comment list is your iteration workspace; treat it as the source of truth for the next two hours.

Step two: classify into three buckets

Once everything is captured, the next move is classification. Every comment goes into one of three buckets, and the bucket decides what happens next.

Must-fix. The comment names something that blocks shipping. A factual error in a load-bearing claim. An AC that contradicts how the system actually works. A scope item that turns out to be impossible in the timeline. A constraint you didn’t know about. These are the comments where the spec goes out wrong if you don’t change it.

Should-consider. The comment names a real point, but you’re not sure yet whether to change the spec. A reviewer prefers a different framing. An engineer suggests a smaller v1. A designer wants an additional state. The point is legitimate; the answer depends on weighing it against the spec’s through-line, the timeline, and the other comments. These are the comments that get a thoughtful reply, sometimes a change, sometimes a respectful no.

Out-of-scope. The comment is interesting but belongs to a different spec, a different release, or a different problem entirely. “What if we also added X?” comments often live here. So do “you should rethink the whole approach” comments that arrive after the approach is locked in. These get acknowledged, logged as follow-ups, and explicitly placed outside the current spec.

The classification is fast — usually a minute per comment if you don’t overthink it. Don’t agonize. The bucket can change as you learn more, but having an initial label on every comment turns an overwhelming pile into a manageable list.

Here are 14 comments from the eng review of the team-invitation spec. Help me classify each one as must-fix, should-consider, or out-of-scope. For each, give me a one-sentence reason. I’ll override your call on any I disagree with.
Quick first-pass classification, with one-line reasons:
1. “7-day expiry too short for enterprise” — should-consider (real workflow issue, but a config knob is a real scope addition).
2. “Permissions inheritance is recursive, not flat” — must-fix (factual error in the spec).
3. “Could we add SSO-bypass for invited users?” — out-of-scope (different problem).
4. “Spam attribution isn’t separately tracked” — must-fix (your 40% claim depends on this; needs annotation).
5. “Should we A/B-test the email subject?” — out-of-scope (post-launch optimization).
...continuing through 14...
Override on 5: that’s actually a should-consider for me, because the email subject is part of the spec’s success metric. Re-classify and continue.
Updated. Comment 5: should-consider — subject line is a lever for the conversion metric, not pure post-launch tweak. Continuing 6 through 14...

Notice the pattern: the assistant proposes a classification, you override the ones where your judgment differs, and you keep moving. The AI’s value here is speed of triage, not authority on the call. The call stays yours.

Step three: handle the must-fixes first, in one pass

Once classification is done, work the must-fix bucket end to end before touching anything else. These are the changes that don’t require deliberation — they’re corrections to facts, not negotiations of taste. Working them in a batch is faster than alternating between buckets, and it gets the spec into a state where the rest of the iteration sits on solid ground.

For each must-fix, write a one-line decision-log entry, make the change, and move on. Don’t over-explain in the spec; the explanation goes in the log at the top, not in a parenthetical at the change site. The spec stays clean; the receipt sits in the log.

Decision log — after must-fix pass 2026-04-30 (15:20): Corrected permissions model description — inheritance is recursive, not flat (per K.’s review). Updated AC 4 and the constraint section. 2026-04-30 (15:25): Added [ASSUMPTION] marker to the spam-attribution claim. The 40% number is real; the “mostly spam” cause is a guess until tracking lands. 2026-04-30 (15:30): Removed the “recursive permission inheritance” phrasing from edge case 5; it didn’t match how permissions actually work. Replaced with the literal behavior K. described.

Three log entries, three corrections, one tight pass. The spec is now factually accurate. The should-consider work that follows can happen against a clean baseline rather than a moving target.

Step four: should-consider — AI as draft partner, you as judge

The should-consider bucket is where iteration takes the most thought. These comments are real points, but the answer depends on context the comment author doesn’t fully see. The shape of the work is: read the comment, decide whether the spec changes, and if it does, draft the change cleanly.

This is where AI earns its keep, used carefully. The prompt that works well is not “rewrite this section to address this comment” — that’s the prompt that produces drift. The prompt that works well is “here’s the section, here’s the comment, propose two alternative edits and name the trade-offs.” You stay the decision-maker; the AI gives you options.

Section: AC 3 of the team-invitation spec (pasted below).
Comment: “7-day expiry feels short for enterprise customers who do batch invites quarterly. Could we make this configurable per workspace?”

Propose two alternative edits: one minimal, one more ambitious. For each, name what we’d add to scope and what trade-off we’d be making.
Option A — minimal: keep the 7-day default, but add a one-line note that workspace owners can extend expiry by re-sending the invite. No new scope; relies on existing re-send flow. Trade-off: enterprise customers still hit the friction point quarterly; we just gave them a manual workaround.

Option B — configurable knob: add a workspace-level expiry override (default 7 days, max 90 days). Adds a settings UI surface and a new validated input on the API. Trade-off: real engineering scope addition (~1–2 days), and a new permission question (who can change the workspace expiry?). Solves the underlying workflow.

If the enterprise workflow is a known pattern, Option B is worth it. If it’s a single anecdotal comment, Option A is honest about the cost.
Option B, but cap default-max at 30 days, not 90 — security will object to 90. Draft the AC change in the spec’s existing voice. One paragraph, no bullets unless they were already there.
Updated AC 3 (drop-in replacement):

“The invite link remains valid for the workspace’s configured expiry window. The default is 7 days, configurable by workspace owners up to 30 days; security review pending on whether the upper bound should be lower for SOC 2–regulated workspaces. After clicking, the new member sees the workspace name and the inviter’s name, then a single ‘Accept and Sign In’ button.”

Three turns. The spec section is updated, the trade-off is captured, and security is flagged as an open question rather than ignored. The PM’s judgment shows up at every step — choosing Option B, capping the max at 30 days, naming the security gap. The AI did the drafting; the calls were the PM’s.

The diff prompt: a small thing that compounds

One specific prompt shape is worth naming because it removes a lot of friction. Call it the diff prompt: “Here’s the current section. Here’s the comment. Show me the proposed change as a before/after diff so I can see exactly what’s shifting.” Tools like Claude or ChatGPT will happily produce this; Copilot Chat will too if you ask plainly. Gemini handles it best when you label the before and after explicitly.

Why this prompt matters: it forces the change to be visible. A “rewrite this section” prompt produces a new block of prose that you compare against the original by reading both side-by-side, slowly. A diff prompt highlights exactly what changed, which is what you actually need to evaluate. The lazy version of the iteration loop accepts the new prose because it sounds reasonable. The rigorous version reads the diff and asks “is this single change worth the trade?”

Some PMs go further and ask the assistant to color-code the diff with a one-line rationale per changed sentence: “changed because of K.’s comment on the spam attribution.” The rationales then become first drafts of the decision log entries. One pass, two outputs.

Reading your own reaction before you respond

One small habit that pays off across iteration rounds: notice your own reaction to a comment before you act on it. Some comments land cleanly — you read them, you agree, you make the change, no internal weather. Other comments land sharply — the wording felt dismissive, the criticism felt unfair, the senior reviewer skipped the context and went straight to a verdict. Those comments are the ones where iteration most often goes wrong, and not because the comment is bad.

The pattern is this: a comment that lands sharply triggers either over-correction (you make a change you don’t actually agree with, just to make the discomfort go away) or defensiveness (you write a long reply explaining why the comment is wrong, which costs goodwill you’ll need later). Both are reactions to the feeling, not to the substance.

The fix is mechanical. When you notice the sharp landing, leave the comment alone for an hour. Move to a different bucket. Make a coffee. When you come back, re-read the comment without the first-reaction tightness, and decide what you’d do if the same point had been raised by a teammate you trusted in a tone you liked. Often the substance is fine and the tone was just blunt; you can address the substance and ignore the tone. Sometimes the substance is also off, and the calmer read makes it easier to decline politely. Either way, the decision gets made on the merits rather than on the temperature of the moment.

This is exactly the kind of judgment that AI can’t do for you. The assistant has no read on whether the comment landed sharply, and no stake in how you reply. You’re the one with the relationship to maintain and the spec to defend — both of which are easier to protect when the reply waits a beat.

The same paragraph, three audiences

One thing comments often surface is that the spec needs to read for different audiences. Engineering wants precision: exact behavior, exact constraints, exact edge cases. Leadership wants outcome: the problem, the call, the risk, the metric. Sales wants narrative: who this is for, what changes for them, when they can talk about it.

The same paragraph can’t serve all three well. The high-leverage move is not to write three specs — it’s to write one spec well, and to use AI as a translator when you need to brief a different audience. The translation lives in a slack message or an email; the spec stays the spec.

Watch the same paragraph re-shaped for engineering and for leadership. Same facts, different framing.

For engineering — precise, behavioral Goal: reduce overall invite-acceptance failure rate from 40% (measured March, full-funnel) to under 15% by Q3. Failure modes in scope: expiry timeout, spam-folder routing, SSO mis-routing, expired-workspace edge case. Out of scope: multi-account collisions (separate spec). Instrumentation required: per-step funnel events on the invite token, with timestamps and termination reason. New events: invite_sent, invite_clicked, invite_failed (with cause enum), invite_accepted.
For leadership — outcome and call Today, four out of ten invited teammates never make it into the workspace. Most of those failures are silent — we don’t see them, the inviter doesn’t see them, and the invitee assumes the product is broken. We’re going to fix the four most common failure modes in one release, with the goal of cutting that failure rate to fewer than two in ten by Q3. The biggest unknown is how much of the gap is spam filtering vs. flow friction; we’ll know within three weeks of launching the new instrumentation.

The two paragraphs sit on the same facts. The first is the spec section. The second is the briefing email. Generating the second from the first is a thirty-second prompt to Claude or ChatGPT — “rewrite this for a leadership audience: lead with the outcome, name the call, end with the biggest unknown” — followed by a careful read to make sure no facts shifted.

The careful read matters. AI translation has a small but consistent failure mode: it tightens prose by quietly dropping a qualifier, or rounds a number, or softens a constraint. The spec said “under 15% by Q3”; the translation said “fewer than two in ten by Q3.” That’s the same thing, expressed for a different reader. But if the translation had said “close to 10%,” that would be a fact change masquerading as a translation. Always re-read against the source.

The third audience — sales — usually wants the same facts re-shaped a third way: who can use this, when can they talk about it, and what’s the one-sentence pitch. A separate Gemini or Windsurf prompt — “rewrite for a sales-enablement audience: who, when, and the one-line pitch” — tends to produce a clean briefing that sales can drop into a deck or a customer email. Same source paragraph, third frame, and again the careful read against the source to catch any drift.

The reason to do all three from the same source — rather than letting each audience get a separately-edited version — is that the source stays the source of truth. When sales asks engineering a question two months from now and they compare notes, they discover they’ve been working from the same numbers. The translations are presentation; the spec is the contract. Don’t let translations quietly become forks.

When to patch, when to rewrite: the 30%-rule

One question that comes up around comment three of any iteration: do I patch this section in place, or do I rewrite it? Patching is faster but produces lumpy prose. Rewriting is cleaner but takes longer and risks losing the original voice.

A useful rule of thumb: if more than about 30% of the section needs to change, rewrite. If less than 30%, patch in place. The number isn’t magic; it’s the threshold above which patches start to read worse than rewrites. A patched paragraph that’s 60% original sentences and 40% new ones almost always reads as two voices stitched together — the original yours, the new ones the assistant’s. A clean rewrite, drafted in one pass and then re-voiced, reads as a single authorial voice even if it took longer to produce.

The other reason to rewrite past the 30% threshold: patches accumulate. By the third round of iteration, a heavily patched section has had three rounds of additions stitched in, and the prose starts to show its seams. A rewrite resets the section to a clean state. The trade-off is one more pass through the assistant; the gain is a section that still reads as written, not assembled.

The decision log earns its keep here

Every change you make in this iteration phase deserves a one-line entry at the top of the spec. Date, what changed, why. The discipline takes about a minute per change; skipping it is the single largest cause of through-line drift across iterations.

Decision log — after iteration round 1 2026-05-01: Updated AC 3 to support workspace-level expiry override (default 7d, max 30d). Reason: K.’s comment about enterprise quarterly batches; security cap kept tight. Open question added: SOC 2 upper-bound review. 2026-05-01: Reframed problem statement for clarity. No fact change — same metrics, same scope, tighter prose. Reason: M.’s comment that the original buried the load-bearing number. 2026-04-30 (15:30): Removed “recursive permission inheritance” edge case — didn’t match actual behavior. Replaced with K.’s description. 2026-04-30 (15:25): Added [ASSUMPTION] marker to spam-attribution claim. 2026-04-30 (15:20): Corrected permissions model description. 2026-04-26: Initial draft. AI-assisted; sections marked [DRAFTED-WITH-AI] reviewed and edited by me.

Six lines tell the story of two days. A reviewer who saw the original on the 26th can scan the log on the 1st and know exactly what shifted, in what order, and why. An engineer reviewing the spec a week later doesn’t need to play archaeology. Future-you, looking at this spec in October, will remember the SOC 2 review you queued up. The log is small; what it buys is large.

Drafting comment-thread responses without sounding like a bot

Comment threads need replies. The temptation is to paste each comment into ChatGPT or Claude, ask for a thoughtful response, and post the result. The result reads polished and reasonable. It also reads as not-you, and engineering will spot it within the first two replies.

The two-sentence rule from the handoff chapter applies here twice as hard. Sentence one: acknowledge the specific point, in plain words. Sentence two: state your call. Agree and update; disagree and explain; or defer with a date. That’s the entire reply.

Comment from M. on the problem statement: “The 40% number is buried in paragraph three. Most readers won’t make it that far. Lead with it.”
Fair — rewriting now. The new opening leads with “Four out of ten invited teammates never make it into the workspace,” with the source citation moved to the same sentence. Will land in the next push.

Two sentences. The comment is acknowledged precisely; the call is named; the next action is concrete. There’s no AI prose to give it away — not because the AI is bad at writing replies, but because a four-paragraph reply to a small comment reads as overengineered, and engineers can tell.

For comments that genuinely need a longer answer, the right response isn’t a longer comment — it’s a fifteen-minute call. “This is worth a quick chat — can we do fifteen minutes tomorrow?” Then have the chat. The async thread is not the right venue for unresolved disagreement.

What does NOT change across iterations

What’s yours: the through-line. The comments will move things around. AC will get sharper; scope will narrow or stretch; metrics will get re-cut; phrases will tighten. What does not move — what cannot move without you saying so explicitly — is the original problem you set out to solve, the user you said this was for, and the call you made about the shape of the answer. Those three are the spec’s through-line, and they’re yours. If a round of feedback ends with one of them subtly shifted and no decision log entry recording the shift, stop the iteration and reconcile. Either the through-line should have changed (in which case log it loudly) or it shouldn’t have (in which case revert the drift). Iteration is supposed to sharpen the spec around its center, not redraw the center.

A smell test for iteration

The tell. You re-read the spec after a round of edits and feel a small disorientation — the words are different, the AC are different, but you can’t quite point to what shifted. The decision log has three entries; the spec feels like it has six entries’ worth of changes.

The check. Open the original problem statement and the current one in two windows. Read them aloud. Do they describe the same problem? Do they name the same user? Is the success criterion still the metric you’d defend at a quarterly review? If any of those answers wobbles, the through-line moved while you weren’t looking.

The fix. Reconcile out loud, in the log. Either: “through-line updated — new problem statement reflects the workflow K. surfaced; old framing was too narrow,” and live with the new through-line. Or: “reverted problem-statement edits made during iteration round 1 — the original framing still holds, the AC tightening can stand,” and restore the original. Both are valid. Silent drift is what isn’t.

When feedback trickles in over a week

The capture-classify-draft sequence assumes the comments arrive in a batch. Sometimes they don’t. The spec goes out, two comments land in the first hour, then nothing for a day, then four more, then nothing for two days, then a final one from the senior leader who only just got around to reading it. The trickle pattern is harder to handle than the batch pattern, because the temptation is to respond to each comment as it arrives, in real time, and lose the benefit of seeing the full surface.

A small habit helps: agree with yourself on a daily “iteration window” while the spec is open for review. Twenty or thirty minutes, late afternoon, every day. New comments accumulate during the day; you don’t respond to them when they land. At the daily window, you run a mini version of the full sequence on whatever arrived since yesterday: capture, classify, must-fix the urgent ones, queue the rest, log everything. Then close the laptop until tomorrow.

The window does two things. It prevents the comment-by-comment edit drift that’s the trickle pattern’s main failure mode. And it gives reviewers a predictable cadence: they know the spec changes daily, not in real time, so they don’t expect instant replies and they don’t see ghost edits appearing while they’re still typing their own comments. The discipline costs nothing; the trust margin is real.

The exception is the must-fix that genuinely blocks reviewers — a factual error that’s actively misleading the conversation, or a permissions misread that’s about to send a senior reviewer down the wrong path. Those get fixed inside an hour, with a one-line slack heads-up to the people who saw the original. Everything else can wait for the daily window.

Rounds two, three, and beyond

The first iteration round absorbs the loudest feedback — the load-bearing corrections, the framing shifts, the scope negotiations. By round two, the comments narrow. They’re tighter, more specific, often about wording rather than substance. By round three, you’re mostly polishing, and the assistant’s diff prompts come back with options that all read as “equivalent, slightly differently phrased.”

Two things change as the rounds progress. First, the bar for “must-fix” gets higher. A comment that would have been a must-fix in round one becomes a should-consider in round three, because the spec is already closer to being right and the cost of late changes goes up. The discipline is to actually move the bar — not pretend round three is round one.

Second, the AI’s value shifts. In round one, the assistant earns its keep on triage and drafting alternatives. By round three, triage is trivial (the comments are a smaller pile) and drafting alternatives often produces three options that say the same thing. What the assistant is still useful for in late rounds is sanity-checks: “here’s the current spec; identify any places where the prose drifted from the original problem statement,” or “list any inline annotations that have become stale because the underlying assumption was confirmed since.” The assistant becomes a proofreader rather than a co-author.

One late-round failure mode worth naming: the spec accumulates parenthetical clarifications across rounds, each one added to address a single comment, and by round three the prose is dense with qualifiers. The fix is a single “clarity pass” in round three: ask Claude or ChatGPT to identify sentences with three or more parentheticals and propose tighter rewrites. Most of the parentheticals were warranted when added; many are redundant once the rest of the spec evolved around them. A clarity pass at the end of iteration is the moment to clean them up.

When the right answer is to stop iterating

One pattern worth recognizing: the third round of iteration produces almost no improvement, but it’s tempting to keep going because more comments keep coming. Each comment is reasonable in isolation. None of them, individually, would change the spec meaningfully. Collectively, they’re polishing prose that’s already good enough.

The signal that you’ve reached this point is when the diff prompts come back with options that all read as “equivalent, slightly differently phrased.” The assistant isn’t producing alternatives because there aren’t meaningfully different alternatives left. That’s the moment to declare the spec frozen for this review cycle, push the version, and let engineering start.

Specs don’t need to be perfect; they need to be good enough to build against. Knowing when to stop iterating is part of staying in the driver’s seat. The assistant will happily keep refining until you tell it to stop. You’re the one who knows that the marginal value of a fourth iteration round is now lower than the cost of the delay.

A useful self-check: before each new iteration round, write down what specifically would have to change in the spec for the round to be worth doing. If the answer is “not much, but I want one more pass,” the round isn’t worth doing. Ship the version, log the decision to freeze it, and move forward. You can always iterate again after engineering surfaces something concrete.

A worked example end to end

Here’s the team-invitation spec, two iteration rounds in, told as a short timeline. It’s a composite of how this phase actually feels.

Monday afternoon, the spec went out. Monday evening, eight comments landed in the doc. Tuesday morning, six more arrived — two from leadership, four from the eng lead. Total of fourteen comments by Tuesday noon. The PM made coffee, opened a fresh doc, and pasted every comment in verbatim, no replies yet.

Twenty minutes of classification: four must-fix (a permissions misread, a metric source error, a missing edge case, a wrong AC), seven should-consider (most about scope and timing), three out-of-scope (interesting follow-ups for a separate spec). The PM ran the classification past Claude in a single prompt for a sanity check; the assistant flagged one comment as should-consider that the PM had marked out-of-scope, and on re-read the PM agreed and re-classified.

Thirty minutes of must-fix work: each correction made, each one logged with a one-line entry at the top. By 11am the spec was factually correct.

Two hours on the should-considers, one at a time, with the diff prompt. For each one, two alternative edits, the trade-offs laid out, the PM’s call recorded in the log, the change made. Of the seven, four made it into the spec; two were declined with reasons; one was deferred to a follow-up.

Fifteen minutes on the comment threads: two-sentence replies on each, written by the PM, no AI. Three threads got a “let’s do fifteen minutes tomorrow” reply because the underlying disagreement needed a real conversation.

Ten minutes on the out-of-scope bucket: each one logged into a follow-ups list at the bottom of the spec, with a sentence noting why it didn’t belong here. The reviewers who raised them got a thank-you reply and a pointer to the follow-ups list.

Five minutes on the smell test: open original problem statement, open current one, read both aloud. Same problem, same user, slightly tighter framing — one log entry covers the framing tightening explicitly. No silent drift.

By 2pm the spec was done. Total time invested: about three hours. Output: a spec that absorbed fourteen comments, made eight changes, declined two with reasons, deferred four, and kept its through-line intact. The version went out at 2:15pm. Sprint planning ran cleanly on Tuesday.

Keeping your authorial voice across rounds

One of the quieter risks across multiple iteration rounds is voice drift. The original spec sounded like you. By round three, after the assistant has drafted half a dozen alternative phrasings and you’ve picked from them, the spec sounds like a slightly competent stranger. The facts are right, the structure holds, the AC are testable — and yet a colleague who’s read your specs for a year would notice that this one reads differently from the others.

The drift happens because each individual diff prompt asks the assistant to produce text in its own default register, and most PMs accept the option that reads cleanest rather than the one that sounds most like them. Over three rounds, the spec tilts toward the assistant’s register and away from yours.

The fix is a deliberate “voice pass” before the spec freezes. Read three or four paragraphs aloud. If they sound like you, ship it. If they sound generic, pick the two paragraphs that feel most off and rewrite them yourself, no AI, in your own words. Two paragraphs of authorial voice in a long spec is enough to anchor the rest — readers calibrate on tone quickly, and even a partial recovery shifts the whole document back toward yours.

One small heuristic: search the spec for the words your assistant tends to over-produce — seamless, robust, leverage, comprehensive, ensure, facilitate — and replace them with whatever you’d actually say. The replacements are usually shorter and punchier. The vocabulary shift alone moves the spec back toward your voice without needing a full rewrite.

Versioning and the “frozen” signal

Iteration ends when the spec freezes. Frozen doesn’t mean immutable — it means engineering can start building against it, and any further changes go through a different process (a follow-up edit with a stakeholder check, not an open-comment iteration round). Naming the freeze explicitly matters because reviewers need to know when their input is still shaping the document and when it isn’t.

The lightest convention that works is a single line near the top of the spec, updated at each version: “Status: open for review through 2026-05-03 EOD; freezes for sprint planning Tuesday 2026-05-05.” Three pieces of information: it’s open, here’s the deadline, here’s what happens after. Everyone reading the spec knows where it is in the cycle.

Pair the status line with a version label in the decision log: “v1.0 frozen for sprint planning. Subsequent edits logged as v1.1, v1.2, etc., with explicit rationale.” The label gives reviewers and engineers a stable reference point. When someone asks “is the version we discussed Monday still the current one?”, the answer is “v1.0 was Monday; we’re on v1.2 now — here are the two log entries explaining what changed.” Specs without version labels accumulate a fog of which-version-am-I-reading questions; specs with labels skip that fog entirely.

The convention is purely textual — no tooling required. It works in any doc tool, any wiki, any ticket. Tools like Copilot Chat or Claude can be helpful for drafting the version-label rationale (“summarize the diff between v1.0 and v1.2 in two sentences”) but the convention itself is yours to enforce. The freeze line is one of the cheapest trust-building moves in the whole iteration phase.

What the assistant is good at, what it isn’t

Three places where AI earns its keep in this phase: triaging large comment batches by proposing a first-pass classification, drafting diff-style alternatives so you can see the trade-off explicitly, and translating the same content for a different audience without changing the underlying facts. Each of these is a high-leverage use because each replaces a manual task that’s tedious but not judgment-heavy.

Three places where AI tends to introduce friction if you let it lead: writing comment-thread replies (the polish gives you away), deciding which bucket a comment belongs to (the call is yours; the assistant can suggest, not decide), and rewriting the problem statement on the fly during iteration (drift risk, every time). Knowing where to lean on AI and where to keep both hands on the wheel is most of the craft of this phase.

A specific anti-pattern worth naming: feeding the entire comment batch plus the entire spec into the assistant and asking for a “revised spec.” This produces a document that looks reasonable, addresses most of the comments, and has subtly drifted on the through-line. The drift is invisible without a careful side-by-side, and most PMs don’t do the side-by-side because the new spec reads fine. Capture, classify, and edit in batches, with the human in the loop on each call. Resist the “rewrite the whole thing” temptation; the speed gain is real but the trust loss is larger.

Multi-stakeholder iteration when stakeholders disagree

Sometimes the comments contradict each other. Engineering wants the scope smaller; sales wants a feature added; leadership wants the launch sooner; design wants a state added that engineering wants removed. Each comment is reasonable on its own. None of them can all be true.

The PM job here is not to pick a side; it’s to surface the contradiction explicitly so the right people can resolve it. AI helps by stating the contradiction in clean prose — “eng comment 3 reduces v1 scope; sales comment 1 expands v1 scope; both can’t be true. Possible resolutions: A, B, C, with trade-offs.” — which gives you a one-paragraph summary to take to the people who need to weigh in.

The resolution conversation usually happens with three people in a room (or a slack thread), not in the spec doc. Once it resolves, you log the decision, update the spec, and reply to the original commenters with the call and the rationale. The contradiction stops being a stalemate and becomes a logged trade-off. Stakeholders who saw the contradiction get the resolution; stakeholders who didn’t see it get a clean spec.

Try It: a real iteration round in three hours

Take a spec you shipped in the last month that got at least eight review comments. Pull it up alongside the comments. Then walk the full iteration loop, with a timer:

  1. Capture (5 min). Paste every comment into a fresh doc, verbatim, no replies.
  2. Classify (15 min). Mark each one must-fix, should-consider, or out-of-scope. Run your classification past Claude or ChatGPT for a sanity check; override anything you disagree with.
  3. Must-fix pass (30 min). Make each correction, log each one in a decision log at the top of the spec.
  4. Should-consider pass (60 min). Use the diff prompt for each one: “here’s the section, here’s the comment, propose two alternative edits with trade-offs.” Pick, edit in your voice, log.
  5. Smell test (5 min). Read the original problem statement aloud, then the current one. Confirm the through-line held, or log the shift explicitly.
  6. Wrap (15 min). Two-sentence replies on each comment thread; out-of-scope items moved to a follow-ups list with reasons.

Time-box the whole exercise to three hours. The point isn’t to produce a perfect iteration — the point is to feel where AI saved you time and where it tried to take over a call. Notice both. Most PMs come out of this exercise with a clearer sense of which prompts to keep and which ones to drop.

The “stop the world” comment

Most comments are tactical — a sharper AC, a missed edge case, a clearer phrasing. Once or twice in a year, a comment is structural. A senior engineer says the approach can’t work because of a constraint you didn’t know about. A senior PM says the problem you’re solving isn’t actually the highest-priority slice. Leadership says the timeline is wrong by a quarter. These comments don’t patch into the spec — they invalidate it, or at least invalidate enough of it that the iteration loop can’t absorb them.

The right response is to stop the iteration loop, not to push through it. Trying to absorb a structural comment with diff prompts and decision-log entries produces a spec that’s patched on top of a broken foundation. The patches read fine in isolation; the document collapses on close inspection.

What works instead is a short pause and a conversation. Reply to the comment with: “This is bigger than an iteration. Can we find thirty minutes to talk through the implications? I want to understand whether the spec needs a rewrite or a re-scope before I touch the rest of the comments.” Have the conversation. Decide together whether the spec rewrites, re-scopes, or stands. Then update the decision log loudly — “v1.0 paused 2026-05-02 after K.’s structural comment on permissions. v2.0 underway with revised scope; v1.0 archived for reference” — so the rest of the reviewers see the pause explicitly rather than wondering why their comments aren’t getting answered.

Stop-the-world comments are rare but they’re also a signal that the rest of the iteration was about to be wasted. Catching them early saves a round of comments on a doc that’s about to be rewritten anyway. AI can’t spot these for you — they require domain knowledge or organizational context the assistant doesn’t have. Recognizing one when it arrives, and respecting it instead of patching past it, is part of the craft.

One subtle tell that a comment is structural rather than tactical: the commenter is arguing about something upstream of what the spec is trying to do. Comments about AC, edge cases, scope, metrics — those are tactical, inside the spec’s frame. Comments about the problem statement, the user, or whether this work should happen at all — those are structural, challenging the frame itself. When you see a comment that challenges the frame, treat it with extra weight. The frame is harder to defend on slack than the AC are; if the frame is wrong, fix the frame before another round of AC-polishing makes the spec feel more finished than it actually is.

Iterating the open-questions block

The open-questions block from the handoff chapter deserves its own iteration discipline. Open questions get answered across the review week — security weighs in, design lands a call, leadership picks a default — and the block needs to evolve in lockstep, not ossify as a snapshot of week-one uncertainty.

The simple rule: every time an open question gets answered, move it from the open-questions block to a one-line entry in the decision log, and update any AC or scope that depended on it. The block shrinks across the review week; the log grows. By the time the spec freezes, the open-questions block ideally has zero or one items left, and each item that remains has an explicit owner and a date.

Items that don’t get answered before freeze deserve special handling. They go in a “deferred decisions” section near the top of the spec, with the default behavior the team will use until the answer lands. “SOC 2 expiry-cap review still pending; default behavior is the 30-day cap for all workspaces until security reviews. Will revisit by 2026-05-15.” Engineering can build against the default; security can review on their own timeline; nobody is blocked. The deferred-decisions section is a small piece of plumbing that prevents open questions from quietly killing momentum.

Iteration as a habit, not a fire drill

The first time you run the capture-classify-draft-edit-log sequence, it feels slow. By the third or fourth time, the steps compress. The capture pass becomes ten minutes instead of twenty; the classification becomes fast because you recognize the comment shapes; the diff prompts become muscle memory. What was a fire drill on spec one becomes a habit on spec four.

The habit is what compounds. PMs who treat iteration as ad-hoc — respond to whatever’s loudest, edit when convenient, log when remembered — spend the same total time but produce specs that drift. PMs who run the sequence on every spec produce specs that stay tight across rounds, and they buy back the time on the rounds where the habit lets them move fast through what would otherwise feel chaotic.

It’s also worth noting how the sequence feels different on a stressful spec versus a relaxed one. On a relaxed spec — small change, low stakes, friendly reviewers — the steps feel almost ceremonial. You could probably skip half of them and still ship something fine. On a stressful spec — high-visibility launch, contradictory feedback, tight deadline — the steps feel essential. The discipline is to run the same sequence on both, because the habit you build on the relaxed specs is exactly what you’ll lean on when the stressful one arrives. Iteration is a craft you practice in calm weather so it works in storms.

None of the steps require AI. The capture, the classification, the editing, the logging — all of these can be done without an assistant. AI accelerates the parts where speed matters and judgment is shallow: triage, draft alternatives, translation. The judgment-heavy parts — what counts as must-fix, when to hold the line, when the through-line drifted — stay yours. That balance is what keeps the spec yours through every iteration round.
