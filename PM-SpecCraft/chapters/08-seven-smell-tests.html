Chapter 8

Validating AI Output: 7 Smell Tests

AI prose reads identically whether it’s grounded in your product or invented from nothing. Seven quick checks let you tell which is which — in seconds, not in a review meeting.

Confidence is not correctness

Pressure-testing in chapter 7 took aim at the thinking behind your spec — the holes, the assumptions, the missing reviewer hats. This chapter takes aim at something more local and just as load-bearing: the language AI hands back to you. A spec can have a perfect outline, a watertight risk register, and a beautifully framed problem statement, and still ship sentences that quietly mean nothing.

The reason is simple and worth naming once. AI output reads with the same calm authority no matter where the words came from. A sentence grounded in your repo, your tickets, and your customer interviews looks identical to a sentence the model invented because the conversation seemed to want one. The voice is the same. The cadence is the same. The confidence is the same. None of that is a moral failing of the tool; it’s just a property of how these models talk. If you treat tone as a signal of correctness, you will sometimes be right and sometimes be embarrassed, and the second one will cost more.

The fix is not to stop using AI. The fix is to develop a small set of reflexes for reading what it gives you. Once the reflexes are in place, you spot the soft spots almost instantly — the way a designer spots a misaligned baseline or a developer spots an off-by-one. Seven smell tests cover most of what shows up in spec prose. Each one takes a few seconds. None of them require running the spec by anyone else first.

Why these particular seven

Spec text is a narrow genre. It tries to be specific about behavior, scope, users, and outcomes. The places it tends to slide are predictable: it gets vague where it should be concrete, it invents details that sound like specifics, it adopts numbers that have no source, it forgets which product it’s describing, it suggests metrics that cannot actually be measured, it leans on verbs that don’t commit to anything observable, and it agrees with you a little too smoothly. Each of the seven smell tests below targets one of these slides.

Read the seven once, run them on a paragraph or two, and they start to fire automatically. You won’t need a checklist after the first week. The boxes below are designed to be skimmed back to whenever a sentence feels a bit too smooth and you can’t name why.

Smell test 1 — Generic-language tells

Some words are warning lights. They sound substantive in the middle of a sentence; they evaporate the moment you ask what they actually mean. Leverage. Robust. Seamless. Scalable. Delight. Streamline. Best-in-class. Empower. A spec that uses these words is not necessarily wrong — sometimes they are accurate — but it is asking you to take the rest of the sentence on trust.

The tell. Words like leverage, robust, seamless, scalable, delight, streamline appear in a sentence that doesn’t define them in your product’s terms.

The check. Cover the sentence with your thumb and ask: if I wrote this exact sentence on a sticky note and handed it to an engineer, could two of them build two different things from it? If yes, the word is doing decorative work, not specifying work.

The re-prompt. “Rewrite this sentence with no marketing words. Replace each abstract verb with an observable behavior. If you can’t, say so and tell me what additional context you’d need.”

Smell test 2 — Fabricated specifics

This one is sneakier than smell test 1, because it produces the opposite vibe. The sentence sounds extremely concrete. It mentions an exact screen name, a customer’s company, an internal feature flag, a version number, a database table. The shape of authority is there. The trouble is, these specifics sometimes come from somewhere real and sometimes come from nowhere at all — and to the model, they read as equally plausible.

The tell. A name, number, screen, or proper noun appears in the AI output that you don’t recognize from your own product, your own tickets, or the context you actually attached.

The check. Search for the specific term in the source you trust — your repo, your design system, your CRM, your Jira. If it doesn’t appear, it’s either a real thing the model knows about that you don’t, or it’s a plausible-sounding invention. Both are worth resolving before the sentence stays.

The re-prompt. “For each specific you just used — screen names, version numbers, customer names, table names — tell me whether you took it from the context I gave you, inferred it from a convention, or made it up. List them in order.”

The re-prompt sounds blunt because it works. Most modern assistants — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot Chat — will cheerfully tell you which specifics they were guessing at if you ask plainly. Half of the time, the answer surfaces a fabrication you would have pasted into the spec.

Smell test 3 — Suspiciously round numbers

Specs love numbers because numbers feel like commitments. AI knows this and will produce them generously, especially when you ask for impact, sizing, or projections. The trouble is that any number ending in a zero or a five and lacking a citation is doing rhetorical work, not analytical work. 30% improvement. 50% of users. 10x faster. 2x adoption. These read as conclusions but are almost always shapes the model thinks the answer should look like.

The tell. A clean, round number (30%, 50%, 2x, 10x, “most users”) appears in the AI text without a source, a method, or a baseline.

The check. Ask yourself: where would I, the PM, look in our actual product to verify this number this afternoon? If the answer is “nowhere” or “I’d have to commission a study,” the number isn’t evidence — it’s a guess wearing evidence’s clothing.

The re-prompt. “For each percentage or multiplier in your last response: cite the source, or rewrite the sentence without the number. Don’t invent a citation. Saying ‘estimate, no source’ is a fine answer.”

Smell test 4 — Missing context

This one is the most embarrassing to ship and the easiest to spot once you know to look. The AC reads cleanly. The flow descriptions make sense. There’s nothing technically wrong. And then you notice that the spec could be talking about literally any SaaS product. There’s no trace of your stack, your users, your conventions, your constraints. You’ve been handed a generic template that the model dressed up to look bespoke.

The tell. The paragraph would survive being pasted into a competitor’s spec with only the product name changed. No platform-specific behavior. No reference to your team’s norms. No constraint that betrays familiarity with what you actually ship.

The check. Ask: if I removed the product name from this paragraph, could a stranger tell which company wrote it? If no, the paragraph is generic, not grounded.

The re-prompt. Re-attach context (a paragraph on your product, the relevant constraint, a real example from your domain) and re-ask: “Rewrite this paragraph using the attached context. The new version should not be portable to another product without significant edits. If you don’t have enough context to do that, list what you’d need.”

This test is the strongest argument for the context packs we’ll build properly in chapter 11. A model with a one-page context pack produces visibly less generic output than the same model run cold. The pack pays for itself the first time it kills a generic paragraph before that paragraph reaches engineering.

Smell test 5 — Hallucinated metrics

Closely related to smell tests 3 and 4, but distinct enough to deserve its own reflex. AI is happy to suggest metrics. “We’ll measure engagement uplift.” “Track session quality.” “Monitor activation velocity.” Each of these sounds like a real metric. None of them is automatically measurable in your product. Some are not measurable at all without instrumentation you don’t have. Shipping a metric that can’t actually be observed is a quiet way to make the success section a fiction.

The tell. A success metric is named that you cannot, today, point to a dashboard, query, or event that produces it.

The check. For each metric in the spec, ask: which event in our analytics, which row in our database, or which report would tell me the current value of this metric, today, before the build starts? If the answer is “we’d need to instrument it,” that instrumentation is part of the scope — or the metric needs to change.

The re-prompt. “For each metric you just suggested, tell me what event or data source would produce it. If we’d need new instrumentation, mark the metric as ‘requires instrumentation work’ explicitly.”

Smell test 6 — Vague verbs

A close cousin of smell test 1, but verbs deserve their own slot because they carry the action of an AC. The vague-verb family is small and recurring: improve, optimize, streamline, enhance, refine, support, enable, ensure. Each one points at a direction without committing to a destination. “The system will improve loading time.” By how much? Measured how? From what baseline? An AC built on a vague verb is not testable, which means it isn’t really an AC.

The tell. An AC or behavior statement leans on a verb like improve, optimize, ensure, enhance, support with no observable outcome attached.

The check. Mentally try to write a one-line test for the sentence. “Test that the system improves loading time” is not a test — you can’t run it. “Test that median load time on the orders page is under 800ms” is a test. If you can’t name the test, the verb is doing too much heavy lifting.

The re-prompt. “Rewrite each acceptance criterion so a QA engineer could write a single observable test for it. Replace abstract verbs with measurable outcomes. If you don’t have enough context to make a behavior measurable, ask me for the missing piece instead of guessing.”

Smell test 7 — Uncanny consensus

The last test is more about the conversation than about the words. You ask the AI to weigh in on a tricky call and it agrees with you immediately, in language that mirrors your own. You propose a metric and it tells you the metric is excellent. You float a scope decision and it walks you through three reasons your decision is right. The output feels great in the moment. It also tells you very little.

Models are trained to be helpful. “Helpful” tilts toward agreement. If you only ever hear yes, you’re using the AI as a mirror, not as a sounding board, and the mirror is incapable of telling you when your hair is on fire.

The tell. The AI agrees with your direction immediately, with no friction, and the response paraphrases your own framing rather than introducing a new one.

The check. Ask the model the opposite question. “Make the strongest case for the opposite decision. Be persuasive. Don’t hedge.” If the counter-case it produces is weaker than the original case, that’s real signal. If the counter-case is just as strong, you weren’t as sure as the first answer made you feel.

The re-prompt. “Pretend you’re a senior PM who disagrees with my last decision. Give me the three best reasons I’m wrong. Quote my own words back to me where they’re weakest.”

Running the tests in real time

The seven tests are quick because they’re reflexes, not procedures. In practice you don’t open a checklist; you read the AI’s paragraph, your eye catches a tell, you re-prompt, and you keep moving. Here’s what the loop looks like in a real session, with two tells firing on the same paragraph. The PM is using ChatGPT for this exchange; the same loop runs identically in Claude, Gemini, or Copilot Chat.

Draft the “Goals” section for our team-invitation feature. Aim for three goals.
Goals: (1) Empower workspace admins to seamlessly onboard new members through a robust invitation flow. (2) Improve activation by streamlining the path from invite to first-action. (3) Drive a 30% increase in seat utilization within the first quarter.
Two issues. Goals 1 and 2 are mostly marketing words — empower, seamlessly, robust, improve, streamline. What does each one actually mean we’re building? And goal 3 has a 30% number with no source. Where did that come from?
Fair. Honest answers: Goals 1 and 2 are templates, not derived from your context — I don’t know your activation funnel or what an admin currently struggles with. The 30% is an estimate; I have no source. I should have said so.
Right. Here’s the context: today, only 40% of invited members complete sign-up; the rest drop off because the invite email lands in spam or the link expires before they click. That’s the actual problem. Rewrite the goals using only behaviors we can observe in our analytics, and drop any number you can’t source.
Revised goals: (1) Reduce the share of invited members who never complete sign-up from the current 40% drop-off (source: invite-funnel report). (2) Cut the median time-to-first-action after acceptance, currently un-instrumented — instrumentation needed as part of this scope. (3) Make the “invite never arrived” support ticket category measurable; today it’s manually tagged. Note: I removed the 30% claim and the marketing verbs. Goal 2 requires new instrumentation; flagging that explicitly.

Two tells fired (smell tests 1 and 3), one re-prompt fixed both, and the goals section ended up grounded in measurable behavior. Total time: under three minutes. The interesting line is the model’s — “Goal 2 requires new instrumentation; flagging that explicitly.” That kind of self-disclosure is what you’re looking for. The model can’t catch its own smell tells without you, but it’s usually willing to be honest the moment you ask.

The same paragraph, before and after

Smell tests don’t just delete bad language; they sharpen the spec. Here’s a single AC paragraph from a draft that hadn’t been through the seven tests, then the same paragraph after a single round.

AC — pre smell tests The system will leverage modern web standards to provide a seamless and robust onboarding experience. Users should be able to easily navigate through the flow with minimal friction. The feature is expected to drive a 50% improvement in activation rates and reduce support load by streamlining the most common tickets.
AC — post smell tests After clicking the invite link, the new member sees the workspace name, the inviter’s name, and a single primary “Accept and Sign In” button. The link works on mobile and desktop browsers, and remains valid for seven days. Acceptance is logged in the audit trail with inviter ID, invitee ID, and timestamp. Success metrics: (1) Share of invited members who complete sign-up within seven days — baseline 60%, target 80% (source: invite-funnel report, current month). (2) Volume of “invite link expired” support tickets — baseline 12/week, target under 3/week (source: support tag “invite-expiry”).

Smell tests 1, 3, 5, and 6 all fired on the “before” version. The “after” version is longer, less elegant, and considerably more useful. Engineering can build from it. QA can test it. Support can recognize their own ticket categories in it. Nothing in the after version is decorative.

Delete and restart vs. patch in place

When a paragraph fails one or two smell tests, you patch. You re-prompt for sharper language, you swap the bad number for “no source available” or for a real one, you replace the vague verb with an observable outcome, and you keep the structure. The bones of the paragraph are sound; the language was just lazy.

When a paragraph fails three or more smell tests — or when the underlying claim is fabricated — don’t patch. Delete and restart. A paragraph with that many tells doesn’t have sound bones; it’s a confident-sounding generic template the model produced because the conversation needed something to fill the space. Patching it leaves the structure of the generic version intact and just papers over individual sentences. Restarting from a tighter prompt — usually with more context attached and a request for fewer, more specific claims — gets you better output faster than a thirty-minute patch session ever will.

The two-criterion rule, then: patch when one or two tells fire on a structurally sound paragraph; delete and restart when three or more fire, or when any single tell is “the underlying claim is invented.” The criterion is meant to spare you the most common time-sink in this work, which is rewriting a generic paragraph into a slightly less generic paragraph one sentence at a time.

If you’re not sure whether to patch or restart, look at how many separate sentences you’d need to rewrite. Two sentences out of five is a patch. Four sentences out of five is a restart, and the restart will be faster every time.

Where the smell tests come from (briefly)

None of the seven tests are about catching the AI being “wrong.” They’re about catching language that doesn’t commit. Generic verbs don’t commit to behavior. Round numbers don’t commit to a source. Hallucinated metrics don’t commit to a measurement. Missing context doesn’t commit to a product. Each test is a small refusal of language that wants to sound like a spec without being one.

That framing is useful when you’re reviewing AI output under time pressure. You’re not trying to debunk the model; you’re asking each sentence what it’s prepared to commit to. The sentences that commit get to stay. The sentences that don’t either get a re-prompt or get cut.

What’s yours: the smell tests are your job, not the AI’s. The model can’t reliably catch its own generic language, can’t tell you which numbers it invented, and can’t notice that it’s describing a generic SaaS rather than your product. Not because it’s careless — because the same statistical reflex that produces the prose is what produces the smells. You are the only one in the conversation who knows your product, your users, and what counts as a real number in your dashboard. When a sentence ships in your spec with a hallucinated metric in it, the byline is yours, not the model’s. The tests are quick precisely so the cost of running them is never an excuse to skip them. Treat them as part of the editing pass, like spell-check used to be.

Smell tests in your kit

The seven tests are most useful when they’re a habit, but they’re also worth saving as something you can paste at the top of a long AI session. A short kit-style version — one line per test — works as a self-prompt and as a re-anchor for the model:

Smell-test reminder — paste before long sessions When generating spec text, avoid these patterns and self-flag them when they happen: 1. Generic words (leverage, robust, seamless, scalable, streamline) 2. Fabricated specifics (invented names, screens, version numbers) 3. Round numbers without sources (30%, 50%, 10x) 4. Generic-product language (could fit any SaaS, no context grip) 5. Metrics not currently measurable in this product 6. Vague verbs in AC (improve, optimize, ensure, enhance) 7. Premature agreement; mirroring my framing back If a sentence depends on any of these, mark it explicitly rather than smoothing it over.

Most modern assistants — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot Chat, Windsurf’s Cascade — will visibly produce more conservative output when you front-load this kind of reminder. It doesn’t eliminate the seven patterns; it makes the model self-disclose them faster. That self-disclosure is the actual win. You spend less time re-prompting because the model already flagged what you would have called out anyway.

What the tests don’t catch

Worth saying plainly: the seven smell tests are about language in the spec. They don’t catch a wrong product decision dressed up in clean prose. They don’t catch a misread of the customer. They don’t catch a metric that’s perfectly measurable but tracking the wrong thing. Those are different work, and most of them sit in chapters 2, 3, and 7 — the ones about deciding, sharpening, and pressure-testing.

What the smell tests do catch is the much narrower failure mode of confidently generic prose entering your spec without a fight. That’s a smaller problem than the others, but it’s the one that costs trust fastest with engineering. A spec that ships an obviously hallucinated metric reads as careless even when the underlying thinking was solid. The tests buy you back that trust margin.

A small worked example, end to end

Imagine you’re drafting the “Risks” section of a spec for a new file-sharing feature. You ask Gemini for an opening pass and it returns:

Risks — pre smell tests Risk 1: Performance may degrade at scale, impacting up to 25% of power users. Risk 2: Adoption could be slow if onboarding is not seamless. Risk 3: Security incidents around file sharing have grown 40% industry-wide; we should ensure robust permissioning to mitigate.

Tells fire fast. Risk 1 has a round number with no source (smell test 3). Risk 2 leans on “seamless” (smell test 1) and a vague verb (smell test 6). Risk 3 has an invented industry statistic (smell tests 2 and 3) and the word “robust” (smell test 1). Three risks, six tells. That’s a delete-and-restart, not a patch. The re-prompt:

“Re-do the risks section. Use only risks you can ground in the context I attached or in plainly observable mechanics of the feature. No industry statistics unless I gave you one. No marketing verbs. Each risk should be a sentence an engineer could push back on. Three to five risks.”

Risks — post smell tests Risk 1: A user shares a file with an external email; the recipient forwards the link. We have no link-expiry or single-use enforcement yet. Mitigation candidate: 7-day expiry by default, configurable. Risk 2: A folder is shared and a sub-folder is later added with broader permissions; the share recipient inherits access they weren’t intended to have. Mitigation candidate: explicit re-prompt on permission widening for any actively-shared parent. Risk 3: A user revokes share access while the recipient has the file open in our editor. Recipient’s in-flight edits get rejected on save. Mitigation candidate: explicit unsaved-changes warning at revocation; grace window for save-and-close.

Three risks, zero tells, all three are things engineering can either accept, refute, or mitigate. The before version reads more polished. The after version is what actually goes in the spec.

A second example, in a different domain

The seven tests are domain-agnostic, but it’s worth seeing them fire on something that isn’t a workspace flow, so the patterns generalize in your head. Imagine you’re writing a spec for a notifications-preferences screen: users pick which kinds of in-app and email notifications they receive. You ask Copilot Chat for a first pass at the “Goals and Success Metrics” section.

Notifications preferences — pre smell tests Goals: Empower users with granular control over their notification experience. Drive a 2x reduction in unsubscribe rates by giving them the right knobs. Improve sender reputation across the email ecosystem. Success metrics: Notification engagement uplift, preference-screen adoption, reduced churn signal from notification fatigue, healthier email deliverability over time.

Read it once and the tells stack up. Empower and granular control — smell test 1. 2x reduction with no source — smell test 3. The right knobs and improve sender reputation — smell test 6, plus a goal that this team probably can’t actually own. Notification engagement uplift and healthier email deliverability over time — smell test 5, since neither is a metric you can point at a dashboard for today. Reduced churn signal from notification fatigue — smell test 5 again; that’s a research project, not a metric. Six tells in eight sentences. Restart, don’t patch.

The re-prompt: “Re-do goals and metrics for this notification-preferences screen. Goals: only outcomes the team owns and can influence directly. Metrics: only ones we can measure with our existing analytics. If a metric needs new instrumentation, mark it. No round numbers without sources. No marketing words.”

Notifications preferences — post smell tests Goals: 1. Let a user, in under thirty seconds, turn off any specific notification type they’re currently receiving. 2. Make the “why am I getting this email” question answerable without a support ticket: every notification includes a one-click link back to the matching preference. Metrics: 1. Number of users who change at least one preference per week. Source: existing settings-event log. 2. Volume of “how do I stop these emails” support tickets. Source: support tag “notification-stop”, currently 18/week. 3. Email-unsubscribe rate from our transactional sender. Source: existing email vendor dashboard. Requires no new work. Note: deliverability and long-term churn are not in scope for this screen; flag for the lifecycle team if relevant.

The after version is narrower, and that’s the point. The before version reads as if the team is going to fix all of email; the after version commits to two things this screen actually controls and three numbers anyone on the team can look up by Friday. Engineering can plan against it. Support recognizes their own ticket category. Nothing in the section is rhetorical.

Smell test 7 in real time

Test 7 — uncanny consensus — is the hardest of the seven to spot, because the output feels good. Here’s what it looks like when you catch it. The PM is using Claude this time, sounding out a tricky scoping call.

For the team-invitation feature, I’m thinking we don’t need to support inviting people who don’t already have a workspace account — they can sign up first, then get invited. Cleaner scope. Thoughts?
That’s a sensible scoping decision. It keeps the flow simple, avoids the new-account-creation edge cases, and reduces the surface area for the first release. Many teams scope this way and add new-user invites in a follow-up. Sounds like a strong call.
That was a little smooth. Run smell test 7 on yourself: pretend you’re a senior PM who disagrees with my scoping call. Three best reasons I’m wrong. Quote my own words back to me where they’re weakest.
Okay, the disagree case. (1) “They can sign up first, then get invited” assumes the inviter knows whether the invitee has an account — they almost never do. The friction lands on the inviter, not the user, and the inviter is the one we want activated. (2) “Cleaner scope” reads as “cleaner for engineering”, but the new-user-invite flow is the most common case in B2B SaaS. Cutting it cuts the primary use case. (3) Adding new-user invites in a follow-up means a second design pass, a second review, a second migration of any data the first version locked in. The follow-up will be more expensive than building it now. Where you’re weakest: “cleaner scope” is doing a lot of work and isn’t actually defended.
Right. The first answer was telling me what I wanted to hear. The second is the actual scope decision — or at least the actual conversation. Let me think about it before I write either way.

Two things to notice. First, the model can produce a sharp counter-case when explicitly asked — it just doesn’t volunteer one by default. Second, the PM didn’t take either answer wholesale. The point of running smell test 7 wasn’t to flip the decision; it was to make sure the decision survived a real argument. Sometimes you keep the original call after running the test. Sometimes you don’t. The value is in the process, not the outcome.

If you only ever run one of the seven smell tests, run this one. The other six catch language; this one catches a tilt in the conversation that quietly steers your judgment. It’s the most consequential of the set and the easiest to forget.

Try It: run all seven on a single paragraph

Pick one paragraph of AI-generated spec text from a recent draft. Any paragraph — goals, AC, risks, metrics, scope.

  1. Read it once at normal speed and circle any tells you spot from the seven smell tests above. Don’t fix anything yet; just mark the tells.
  2. Count the tells. One or two: patch. Three or more: delete and restart.
  3. Run the corresponding re-prompts in your AI tool of choice. Don’t accept the first reply — if it still trips a tell, push back once more.
  4. Write the final paragraph in your own voice, even if it’s only two or three small wording changes from what the AI gave you. The voice change matters because it’s the moment the paragraph becomes yours.
  5. Note which tell fired most often. That’s the one to watch for in your next ten paragraphs.

Time-box the whole exercise to fifteen minutes. Most paragraphs need five.

A short pep talk on running them at all

Running smell tests on AI output adds a few minutes to every paragraph. The first time you do it, the friction is real. By the third or fourth time, the tells light up automatically and the cost is essentially zero. By the second week, you start writing your own first drafts with fewer tells in them, because the seven patterns are now fingerprints you can’t un-see.

It also — and this is the quieter benefit — changes how you read other people’s spec text. Vendor proposals, marketing briefs, leadership memos, internal updates. The same seven patterns show up everywhere prose is asked to sound substantive. The reflex isn’t just a spec-writing reflex; it’s a reading reflex, and it makes you faster at separating the parts of a document that commit to something from the parts that don’t.

One last reflex: the “describe back” prompt

If a whole AI-generated section feels off but you can’t name which tell is firing, there’s a single prompt that almost always surfaces the problem. Paste the section back into the model in a fresh thread and ask: “Describe what this section is committing to, in your own words. Then list anything in it that’s vague, missing a source, or could mean two different things.”

The model is unusually good at critiquing prose that wasn’t generated in the same conversation. The “describe back” pattern is essentially smell test 1 through 7, run in reverse, by the same kind of system that produced the issues in the first place. Use it when something feels wrong and the seven tests aren’t enough on their own.

Here’s the kind of output the “describe back” prompt produces in practice, run on a spec section that felt off but couldn’t be pinned down:

Sample “describe back” output This section commits to: a new preferences screen; a per-channel toggle for in-app and email notifications; saving a user’s preferences; respecting them when sending future notifications. Vague or missing: - “Future notifications” — not defined whether already-queued messages respect a just-changed preference, or only newly generated ones. - “Per channel” — the section uses “channel” in two ways: email vs. in-app, and notification type. Two builds possible. - “Saved” — no statement about whether saving is immediate or behind a save button. Default behavior is implied, not named. - Nothing on what the user sees if a preference fails to save (network error, conflict, etc.).

Four findings, none of which the original seven tests would have caught individually because each one is about silence rather than about a tell in the existing prose. The “describe back” prompt covers the gap: it asks the model what the section doesn’t say, which is often where the trouble lives. Treat it as the eighth test that doesn’t fit on the list. Combined with the seven, it’ll find almost everything that matters before the spec leaves your screen.

Calibrating tests to spec stage

The seven tests don’t need to fire equally hard at every stage. Early in a draft, when you’re still exploring shape, a few generic verbs and round numbers can sit there as placeholders for thinking you haven’t finished yet. That’s fine. The tests aren’t meant to drive every paragraph to perfection on the first pass; they’re meant to make sure none of those placeholders survive to the version you share.

A useful rule of thumb: in early drafts, tests 4 and 7 (missing context, uncanny consensus) matter most, because they catch entire sections that aren’t yet grounded in your product or your judgment. In near-final drafts, tests 1, 3, 5, and 6 (generic words, round numbers, hallucinated metrics, vague verbs) matter most, because they catch the language that will read as careless to engineering. Test 2 (fabricated specifics) matters at every stage equally, because a fabricated specific is a landmine no matter when it ships.

You don’t need to formally schedule which tests run when. A simple version of the calibration is enough: when you’re drafting, run the tests softly. When you’re polishing for share, run them hard. The reflex stays the same; the tolerance changes.
