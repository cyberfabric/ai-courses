Chapter 5

Writing the PRD With AI (and Owning It)

Co-author a complete spec without becoming a passive editor — outline-first, attach context, fill skeletons, and keep your voice on the page where it matters.

Wednesday morning, blank page

Yesterday you finished a discovery loop. Twelve angles, three families, one cheap test, one chosen candidate — the inline upgrade flow at the moment a free-tier user hits the project-count limit. The discovery doc sits open in another tab. Today the job is different: turn that chosen candidate into a PRD that the team can build against.

You open a fresh document. The cursor blinks. Somewhere in the back of your head a small voice says: just paste everything into Claude and ask for a PRD. The model will fill the page in thirty seconds. It will look complete. The temptation is real, and it’s the first thing this chapter is going to talk you out of.

Not because AI can’t write a PRD — it can. The trouble is what you become when it does. You become the editor of someone else’s draft, smoothing prose that wasn’t yours to begin with. The PRD ends up plausible, fluent, and quietly hollow. Reviewers feel it before they can name it. Engineers find the soft spots in code review. The author of the spec is supposed to be the one who knows which sentences are load-bearing — and after a full-PRD-from-prompt draft, you genuinely don’t.

The move that high-leverage PMs use instead is small and almost embarrassingly simple. Write the outline yourself. Hand the AI one section at a time. Rewrite the two or three sentences that contain your actual judgment. Save versions as you go. The whole chapter is a long form of that single instruction.

Why “give me a full PRD” is the wrong unit of work

The full-PRD prompt feels efficient. One ask, one output, ten minutes saved. The cost is hidden in the shape of what comes back.

When a model writes a whole PRD from a paragraph of context, it has to fill nine or ten sections with plausible content. It will lean on patterns it has seen across thousands of specs. The Goals section will sound like other Goals sections. The Risks section will list reasonable-sounding generic risks. The Metrics section will propose KPIs that any product in your category might track. Every section will be on-topic. Few sections will be specific. The result is a PRD that looks done at a glance and falls apart on a careful read.

The deeper problem is what the prompt does to you. The PRD arrives as a finished artifact. You move into editor mode — tightening sentences, swapping a word, reordering a bullet. Editor mode is a different posture from author mode. Editors smooth; authors decide. A spec is a sequence of decisions written down. If the decisions are the model’s and the smoothing is yours, the spec belongs to nobody.

The fix isn’t to throw AI out of the PRD process. It’s to change the unit of work. Don’t prompt for the whole document. Prompt for the outline you wrote, one section at a time, with the context the model needs to make that one section good. The result reads like yours because it is.

The PRD anatomy you can reuse

Before any AI enters the picture, it helps to have a stable outline you carry from spec to spec. The shape of a good PRD doesn’t change much across products or teams. Here’s a nine-section anatomy that travels well; copy it once, tweak the order if your team prefers, then stop renegotiating it every Monday.

PRD — standard outline (v1) 1. Problem — the friction, who feels it, evidence, why now. 2. Users — cohort, segment, real account or ticket anchors. 3. Goals & Non-goals — what success means; what is explicitly out. 4. Scope — what ships in this PRD vs what comes later. 5. UX shape — flow at a high level; not a pixel design. 6. Acceptance criteria— testable, observable conditions for “done”. 7. Risks & assumptions— what could break; what we’re betting on. 8. Metrics — how we’ll know in production it worked. 9. Open questions — what we don’t know yet, and who owns finding out.

Nine sections, one line each. Print it. Tape it next to your monitor. The outline isn’t precious — if your team uses different headings, swap them in. What matters is that you write the headings yourself before you involve any AI tool. The headings are the through-line. The model fills under them. You don’t fill under the model.

Think of it like a kitchen mise en place. Before the cooking starts, you lay out the bowls, the cutting board, the salt, the oil. The actual cooking happens fast, but only because the layout already decided what was going where. A PRD outline is the mise en place. The AI helps with the cooking. The layout is yours.

The outline-first workflow

The workflow is short enough to fit on an index card. Five steps; nothing fancy.

  1. Write the nine headings yourself, in order, on a blank page.
  2. Under each heading, type one sentence describing what that section will say. This is your stake in the ground. It can be rough.
  3. Open your AI tool of choice and paste the outline plus a context pack (more on that in a minute).
  4. Ask the AI to expand one section at a time, in your structure, against your one-sentence stake. Not a full PRD — one section.
  5. Read what comes back. Rewrite the two or three sentences that carry your actual product judgment in your own voice. Move on.

The whole loop takes about an hour for a sprint-sized PRD — longer for a strategic bet, shorter for a tactical brief. The wall-clock time isn’t much different from a full-PRD-prompt approach. The artifact is dramatically different. You’ll feel the difference the first time someone asks “why this scope, not the broader one?” and you have a sentence ready that you actually wrote.

The one-sentence stake under each heading is the single highest-leverage habit in this whole workflow. It takes thirty seconds per section. It tells the AI what you actually want said, instead of leaving it to guess. Almost every “the AI gave me generic output” complaint dissolves when stakes are present.

From discovery doc to PRD: what carries forward

Chapter 4 ended with a small promise: a discovery session, done well, hands the PRD three things — a chosen candidate, the central assumption that candidate rests on, and the cheap experiment that justifies committing. Now we use them. They’re the seeds of the PRD’s most important sections, and getting the handoff right is what keeps the spec connected to the discovery instead of restarting from scratch.

The chosen candidate becomes the heart of Scope and UX shape. It’s the answer to “what are we building?” If the discovery picked the inline-upgrade flow, the Scope section names that flow specifically; the UX-shape section sketches it at a high level. The rejected options don’t disappear — they become a one-line “considered and deferred” note, often in Open Questions or Non-goals, so reviewers know the team didn’t skip the alternatives.

The central assumption becomes the heart of Goals and Risks. “We’re betting that the trip to the pricing page is the friction, not the price” is the assumption; the Goals section then names the metric that would prove the bet right (conversion lift on dashboard-origin traffic), and the Risks section names what would prove it wrong (no lift, or lift offset by a refund-rate spike). Without the assumption explicit in the spec, the Goals and Risks sections drift into platitudes; with it, both sections sharpen on contact.

The cheap experiment becomes the heart of Metrics and the kill-switch language inside Risks. “Three-arm A/B; sample size 4,000 per arm; two weeks; primary metric is second-session rate” turns into the Metrics section’s primary measurement plan. The kill switches in Risks fall out naturally from the same shape: if the experiment’s primary metric goes the wrong way, the spec already names what happens.

The trick to a clean handoff is to feed the discovery doc itself into the AI’s context pack, not just paraphrase from it. Models trained on lots of PRDs will happily invent generic versions of any of the three pieces if you don’t hand them the real one. Give the AI the real discovery doc, and the PRD’s Goals, Risks, and Metrics sections write themselves with much less friction.

Attaching context, and why it changes everything

The single biggest determinant of PRD-section quality is what the model knows when it starts writing. A model with no context will give you generic prose. A model with a focused context pack will give you something that sounds like your team wrote it.

Five pieces of context cover most situations. None of them are long; the whole pack fits on one page if you’re honest about what matters.

  • Product overview. One paragraph: what the product is, who pays for it, the core flow, current scale.
  • Customer segments and glossary. Five to ten lines: the cohorts your team talks about by name, the internal terms that have specific meanings (“workspace”, “board”, “owner”).
  • The discovery doc. The chosen candidate, the central assumption, the rejected options — everything chapter 4 produced.
  • Prior decisions. Three to five “what we don’t do and why” lines from past quarters. Saves the AI from suggesting things you already ruled out.
  • One similar prior PRD. A spec from your team that you’d be happy to imitate in shape and tone. The AI learns your house style faster from one good example than from any prompt.

Different tools accept context differently and the surface keeps changing — but the principle is constant. Claude takes project knowledge as attached files. ChatGPT’s custom GPTs and uploaded files do the same job. Gemini Gems work similarly. Copilot and Windsurf can read your workspace files directly when you’re working in an editor. The trick is the same in all of them: feed the model the five pieces, then prompt for the section.

Pasting these five at the top of a fresh chat takes about ninety seconds the first time, and is essentially free after you’ve saved the pack as a reusable artifact. Chapter 11 covers how to build that pack once and use it across every spec; for now, the move is to paste the five pieces in once, and notice the difference in section quality. You will notice.

Notice what just happened. The AI didn’t write the PRD. The PM wrote the outline and the stake under section 6. The AI drafted six AC against that stake. The PM caught a discovery-doc detail the AI didn’t internalize (the dismissal rule), added a real edge case the AI missed (admin-not-owner), and held the model from running ahead into other sections. Total time: maybe seven minutes. The PM is still the author.

Two small habits made the section solid. One: the stake sentence told the model what good looked like, so the AC were already aimed at the right shape. Two: the PM stopped the model from sprinting ahead. “Just this section” is one of the most useful constraints you can put on an AI in a PRD context. The model will happily fill all nine sections in one shot if you ask. Don’t.

A second dialogue: filling the Risks section

Different section, same loop. The PM has finished AC and is back at the prompt for section 7 (Risks & assumptions). The stake under that heading reads: “Risks of the inline-upgrade approach — payments, perceived pushiness, and surprise charge events — with mitigations or kill switches for each.” The PM is using Gemini this time; the cadence is the same.

Same outline and context pack as before. Expand section 7 only. Stake says: risks of the inline-upgrade approach (payments, perceived pushiness, surprise charges) with mitigations or kill switches for each. List four to six risks. For each, name the failure mode in plain language, the mitigation, and the kill-switch condition if one exists.
Five risks drafted. (R1) Perceived pushiness — users feel cornered into upgrading. Mitigation: dismissible prompt, no dark patterns, clear pricing visible before payment confirmation. Notice the moves again. The PM caught a wrong assumption (R3 framing), forced a split that the model wouldn’t have made on its own (R5 into R5a/R5b with different owners), and ended the dialogue before letting the model run on into Metrics. The judgment sentences — “we are accepting risk X because Y, and the kill switch is Z” — are the next thing the PM will write, by hand, in their own voice. The model gave good scaffolding; the calls are still the PM’s.

One detail worth noting: when the dialogue across two sections (AC and Risks) revealed that the AI hadn’t carried the “dismissed for the session” rule across, the PM didn’t blame the model — they re-anchored. AI tools have wobble; the human-in-the-loop catches it. That’s the loop working as designed.

The two or three sentences that have to be yours

Even with a perfect outline and a great context pack, every PRD has a small number of sentences that the AI cannot write — not because it’s incapable, but because those sentences encode your judgment about your trade-offs. They’re the sentences a stranger could not have produced, even with full context. The Goals section usually has one. The Non-goals has one. The Scope cut has one. The Risk-acceptance line in Risks has one.

Here’s a typical AI draft and the rewrite that turns it into a real Goals section.

GOALS This PRD aims to improve free-to-paid conversion by streamlining the upgrade experience for users who reach plan limits. We want to deliver a seamless, frictionless flow that drives engagement and increases revenue while maintaining a delightful user experience for both free and paid users.
GOALS Lift free-to-paid conversion among users who hit the project-count limit from the current 1.8% (dashboard-origin traffic) toward the 4.1% benchmark we see from marketing-origin traffic, by removing the trip to the pricing page. We are accepting a small risk that inline upgrade feels pushy to a subset of users; we’ll watch NPS-by-cohort and the dismissal rate weekly for the first month. If dismissal exceeds 60% in week one, we hold and rethink.

The before paragraph is fluent and says approximately nothing. The after paragraph names a number, a baseline, a benchmark, the trade-off the team is consciously taking, the signal that would prove the trade-off was wrong, and a pre-committed response. A reviewer can disagree with the after paragraph; nobody can disagree with the before, because there’s nothing concrete to disagree with. Disagreeable specifics are the proof that judgment was applied.

The rewrite isn’t hard or long. It’s often three or four sentences across the whole PRD. But those sentences are the ones that carry the spec’s weight. If you outsource them to the AI, the spec will quietly soften everywhere they appear, and the team will feel the softness without being able to point at it.

Iterative refinement: pass-by-pass, not all-at-once

A second small habit that compounds: refine in passes. A finished PRD section usually goes through three quick rounds, not one big one.

Pass 1 — skeleton. The AI fills the section against your stake. You’re looking for shape: are the right ideas in the right places? Don’t worry about wording yet.

Pass 2 — sharpen. Push back on anything generic. “Replace ‘robust’ with the specific failure mode it’s defending against.” “The Risks section lists three risks; the discovery doc named a fourth — add it.” This is where the AI earns its keep, because it’s genuinely good at sharpening on direction.

Pass 3 — sand. Read the section out loud. Cut sentences that don’t carry weight. Rewrite the two or three judgment sentences in your voice. Done.

Running all three passes inside one prompt — “write me a sharpened, polished AC section now” — produces a draft that looks finished but wasn’t pressure-tested at any of the three stages. The shape might be wrong; you won’t catch it because the prose is too smooth to hear the structural problem underneath. Pass-by-pass is slower for the first PRD and faster for every PRD after, because each pass teaches you what to look for next time.

Versioning discipline: the cheap, boring habit that saves you

Specs change. They get reviewed, get pushed back on, get edited in meetings, get rewritten at midnight before a launch. The PRD that ships isn’t the PRD that started, and the team often can’t reconstruct what changed or why. That’s where versioning discipline earns its place.

The minimum viable habit is a one-line decision history at the top of the spec, plus a saved snapshot before each big edit. That’s it. Five minutes a week.

DECISION HISTORY v0.1 — Initial draft from discovery candidate “inline upgrade”. v0.2 — Added admin-on-other-workspace AC after eng review. v0.3 — Cut analytics rework from scope; pushed to Q3. (E. asked.) v0.4 — Tightened Goals to name dismissal-rate kill switch. v0.5 — Replaced “seamless” language throughout. Final for review.

Each line takes ten seconds to write. The accumulated value over a quarter is enormous. A reviewer who joins late can read five lines and know what the PRD has been through. A future you, six weeks from now, can answer the “why did we cut analytics?” question in the team retro without rummaging through chat logs.

The snapshots matter for a different reason: they let you back out of an iteration that turned out to weaken the spec. Sometimes a feedback round produces edits that look good in the moment but quietly break the through-line. With versions saved, you can look at v0.3 and v0.5 side by side and notice the drift. Without versions, the original shape is gone.

Yours to decide: which sentences in the PRD carry your actual judgment, and which were just plausible filler. The AI can draft language all day; only you know which call you’re making, which trade-off you’re consciously taking, and which signal would change your mind. Those sentences must be in your voice, with your specifics, and you must be prepared to defend them in a review. The PRD has your name on it, not the model’s.

A useful smell test for finished sections

Read each PRD section and ask: if I removed this sentence, would the PRD be meaningfully less informative? Sentences that survive the test carry weight. Sentences that don’t are usually AI hedging or politeness padding — harmless individually, but cumulatively they smother the spec. A high-leverage PRD typically has 30–40% fewer sentences than its first draft, and reads sharper for it.

Tools differ; the principle stays

The shape of how you feed context and request a section varies a little tool to tool. The principle — outline first, context pack, one section at a time, three passes — is identical everywhere.

In Claude, project knowledge accepts your context pack as files; you start a new conversation per PRD and reference the project. In ChatGPT, a custom GPT or uploaded files plays the same role; you can also paste the pack into the system message of a long conversation. In Gemini, a Gem holds the pack; you call it for each new section. In Copilot or Windsurf, you’re typically inside an editor with the spec doc open and other reference docs nearby; the tool reads them as part of its context, and you ask for one section at a time using inline prompts. Same five pieces, same one-section discipline, different surfaces.

The thing to watch for, regardless of tool, is the temptation to lean on whichever surface is fastest at the moment and let it shape the workflow. The workflow should shape the tool, not the other way around. If your tool of the week makes “give me a full PRD” the path of least resistance, the right move is to write your outline somewhere else first and bring it in.

A second mini-example: the billing-notification setting

Quick second pass on a different scenario, to show the technique generalizes. The PM is speccing a small feature: letting workspace owners control which billing-related notifications go to which admins. Sprint-sized work, not a strategic bet.

The PM writes the nine headings, types one stake under each (e.g. under Scope: “ships behind a feature flag, owners-only UI, no API changes this milestone”), and pastes the context pack plus the discovery doc into a fresh ChatGPT conversation. They ask for section 5 (UX shape) only.

The model returns a competent UX description with a settings panel, three notification categories, and a per-admin toggle. The PM reads it, notices the model invented a category called “dunning notifications” that doesn’t exist in the product’s glossary, and asks for a rewrite using only the three real categories. The model complies. The PM then rewrites two sentences in their own voice: one specifying that the panel must be reachable from the existing billing settings page (no new top-level nav), one specifying that toggles default to “on” for backwards compatibility.

Total time on section 5: about eight minutes. The PM moves on to Risks. Same loop. Same three passes. By lunch, six of nine sections are filled. By end of day, the PRD is in v0.4 and ready for an engineering pre-review.

The mini-example is deliberately undramatic. That’s the point. Outline-first PRD work is supposed to be steady, low-tension, and reliably producing artifacts that read like the PM wrote them — because the PM did.

Disclosure and trust: a small habit that pays

One question that comes up the first time you take an AI-augmented PRD into a review: should the team know which sections AI helped write? The honest answer is yes, and the practical answer is that the disclosure can be tiny. A one-line note at the top of the spec is enough.

NOTES Drafted with AI assistance (Claude). Section authorship and judgment are the PM’s. Discovery doc and prior-PRD context attached. Decision history below.

That single line accomplishes three things at once. It tells engineers the spec was assisted, so the soft questions (“wait, did you actually verify this?”) come up explicitly rather than as suspicion. It signals to reviewers that you’re not hiding the workflow, which is the fastest way to build trust with a skeptical audience. And it gives future you a record of which tools you used on which spec, which becomes useful six months later when you’re refining your own kit.

The disclosure isn’t an apology. It’s a workflow signature. The same way a paper might note “analysis performed with R” or a design might note “mockups in Figma”, a PRD can note that AI helped draft. Tool-honesty is not the same as tool-blame. The team you’re working with will appreciate the honesty more than they would the silence; the silence usually leaks anyway, and leaks badly.

Chapter 9 goes deeper into how to mark confirmed-vs-assumed lines inside the spec itself, so engineering knows where the load-bearing assumptions are. The note at the top is the small version of the same idea. Both habits compound, and both make AI-augmented spec work feel like normal collaboration rather than something done in secret.

What about the parts where AI is genuinely strong?

Outline-first isn’t a way of holding the AI back — it’s a way of pointing it at the work it’s good at. The model is unusually strong at three jobs inside a PRD, and you should lean into all three.

Filling Risks and Open Questions. Asking “what could go wrong with the inline-upgrade approach if the user has multiple payment methods on file?” will produce a list that catches at least one risk you didn’t think of. The model is fluent at adversarial enumeration; this is exactly the use case.

Translating between audiences. A Goals section written for engineering reviews can be re-voiced for an exec one-pager in thirty seconds, and the AI is good at preserving substance while shifting register. The pattern shows up again in chapter 10 when we cover stakeholder-specific reframing.

Sharpening verbs. Vague verbs — “improve”, “optimize”, “streamline” — are easy for the AI to spot in your draft if you ask it to. “Find every vague verb in this section and propose a specific behavior it could be replaced with.” You will get a useful list, often shorter than you expected.

Cross-checking consistency. A long PRD often has small drift between sections — the Goals section names a metric the Metrics section doesn’t track, or the Risks section assumes a constraint the Scope section relaxed two revisions ago. Asking the AI “list every internal inconsistency between sections of this PRD” takes ten seconds and frequently catches one or two issues that would have surfaced embarrassingly in review. This is exactly the kind of dull, careful work models are unusually good at.

None of these jobs require you to surrender authorship. They require you to point the model at the part of the work where it adds the most value, and keep authoring the rest.

When to close the chat and write by hand

For all the technique above, there are moments inside a PRD where the right move is to close the AI tab entirely and write the section yourself. Three signals tell you you’re in one of those moments.

Signal 1: the section is small and the call is yours. Goals and Non-goals are usually short. They’re mostly the judgment sentences. Asking the AI to draft them and then rewriting most of what comes back is more friction than value. For these sections, often the right move is to skip the AI entirely on the first pass — write your three to five sentences, then ask the AI to pressure-test them rather than draft them. Inversion of the usual loop.

Signal 2: the AI is producing fluent prose for something that has no real answer yet. If you don’t actually know what the metric should be, asking the AI for a metric will produce a confident-sounding made-up one. The right move is to leave the line empty in the spec, mark it as an Open Question with an owner, and resolve it before review. AI can’t help you decide something you haven’t decided yourself.

Signal 3: the section is about people, not features. Stakeholder management notes, escalation paths, who owns what after launch — these are deeply contextual to your team. The model has no idea who Maria is or why she has strong opinions about billing copy. Write these sections by hand. They’re short anyway.

None of this contradicts the rest of the chapter. Outline-first PRD work isn’t “use AI everywhere”; it’s “use AI on the parts where it adds value, by hand on the parts where it doesn’t.” The outline tells you which is which.

What “owning it” actually means

Owning a PRD doesn’t mean writing every sentence by hand. It means three things, and they’re worth saying plainly.

One: you can defend any sentence in the spec without saying “the AI suggested it”. If a reviewer asks why a particular AC is shaped the way it is, you have an answer that connects it to the discovery, the cohort, the constraint, or the trade-off you’re taking.

Two: you can name the two or three sentences that carry the spec’s judgment, and you wrote those sentences in your own voice. The rest of the PRD can be AI-assisted prose; the load-bearing sentences cannot.

Three: you can describe what would change your mind. A spec without a falsification condition is a wish; a spec with one is a commitment. The condition belongs to you, not the model.

If those three are true, the PRD is yours, even if a model helped draft 80% of the words. If any of the three is false, the PRD is the model’s, even if you typed every word yourself. The percentage of AI prose isn’t the test. Authorship is the test, and authorship is decided by which calls you made, not by which keys you pressed.

Common stumbles and how to walk through them

Stumble 1: the AI keeps drifting back into generic language. Re-anchor with the context pack. If the section keeps returning “robust” and “seamless”, the model has lost the thread; paste the discovery doc and the prior PRD again, and re-ask for that one section. Most models will sharpen on the next pass when the anchor is fresh.

Stumble 2: the outline is changing every time you sit down. If you’re renegotiating the nine headings each PRD, you’re burning energy on form instead of content. Lock the outline as a personal default. Tweak the order if a particular spec needs it; don’t reinvent the structure weekly.

Stumble 3: the spec keeps growing instead of converging. If your PRD is at v0.7 and longer than v0.4 was, ask the AI for the cut list: “identify five sentences in this PRD that could be removed without losing meaning.” You’ll usually find them. Length signals confidence; conciseness signals care.

Stumble 4: you’re editing AI prose instead of authoring sections. The tell is that you spend more time fixing the model’s sentences than writing your own. Step back. Re-read your one-sentence stakes under each heading. If they’re thin, the AI fills the void with generic prose. Strengthen the stakes; the editing burden drops.

Stumble 5: the AI keeps inventing details that aren’t in your product. Made-up screen names, made-up settings, plausible-sounding feature names that don’t exist. The cause is almost always that the context pack didn’t include the glossary, or the glossary is too thin. Add the glossary with the names of the screens, settings, and features the spec is going to touch. The hallucinations usually stop on the next pass.

Stumble 6: the spec sounds great in isolation and weak next to a real PRD from your team. Read the new draft alongside one prior PRD that the team was happy with. If the new one feels less specific, less concrete, less “this team’s voice” — the context pack is missing the prior PRD. Add it; the gap usually closes.

Scaling the technique to the size of the bet

Not every PRD needs nine fully filled sections. The depth of the workflow should scale with the cost of getting the spec wrong. Three rough modes cover most situations.

Tactical brief. Two-day fixes, copy changes, small UX adjustments. The full nine-section template is overkill. Use a compressed version: Problem, Scope, AC, one risk, one metric. The outline-first principle still applies — write the four headings, type one stake under each, fill them with AI assistance. Total time: thirty minutes. Don’t turn a small fix into a bureaucratic exercise; the cost of the spec shouldn’t exceed the cost of the work.

Sprint-sized feature. The canonical version of this chapter’s workflow. Nine sections. Three-pass refinement on each. Maybe an hour or two of focused work, often spread across half a day. This is where outline-first earns its strongest leverage; the technique is sized for this kind of spec.

Quarter-sized initiative. A spec that will absorb engineering for weeks and shape multiple teams. The same nine sections, but each one likely needs its own pre-PRD discovery, its own data pull, its own reviewer pass. Don’t try to fill these PRDs in a single AI session. Spread the outline-first loop across days. Add a Risks section that’s genuinely two pages long, because the risks at this scale matter. Add a stakeholder-by-stakeholder review log because you’ll need it for the post-launch retrospective.

The mistake to avoid: applying tactical-brief depth to a quarter-sized initiative. A strategic bet sharpened in thirty minutes will produce a PRD that papers over the real disagreement. The opposite mistake — full nine-section ceremony for a one-line copy change — is rarer but real, and equally annoying for engineering. Match the depth to the cost. When in doubt, slightly under-invest in tactical and slightly over-invest in strategic.

Co-writing the PRD with a teammate

The outline-first workflow adapts naturally to a pair session with one engineer or one designer. A small adjustment to the loop is all it takes: the AI becomes the third participant in the room rather than your private drafting partner.

The pattern that works: share a screen with your AI tool open. The PM types the outline and the one-sentence stakes while the teammate watches and reacts. The teammate’s first contribution is usually to push on a stake — “your stake under Scope says ‘ships behind a flag’, but you should also say which flag and who turns it on.” Stake gets sharpened. Then the AI fills the section against the sharpened stake. Then the teammate reads and reacts again. Then the PM rewrites the judgment sentences in their own voice while the teammate watches and confirms.

Two specific moments are worth naming. First, when the engineer in the room flags a cost or feasibility concern that the AI couldn’t have known about (“that’s actually a six-week change because of the way our auth service is wired”), the workflow updates immediately — the stake under Scope or under Risks gets revised, and the AI re-fills with the new constraint visible. Second, when the AI proposes something that looks reasonable but contradicts a constraint the engineer knows about silently (“we don’t do per-region toggles in this codebase”), the engineer’s pushback is the thing that catches it — not the model.

The PRD that comes out of a 45-minute pair session like this is often better than the one that comes out of three days of solo iteration. The teammate’s domain knowledge is in the spec from the start, not bolted on at review time. The AI accelerates the typing; the human in the room provides the constraints; the PM owns the calls. Three roles, one document, one short session.

The thing to watch: don’t let the AI become a tie-breaker. If you and the engineer disagree about a scope cut, the answer isn’t to ask the model who’s right. The model has no stake in the outcome and will produce a polite middle path that satisfies neither of you. Have the disagreement directly. The PRD is better for it, and so is the working relationship.

Try it this week

The Outline-First PRD Drill. Pick a real ticket in your backlog — small enough to fit in a sprint, important enough to be worth a real PRD.

  1. On a blank page, write the nine headings from the PRD anatomy in this chapter. Don’t open AI yet.
  2. Under each heading, type one sentence describing what that section will say. Rough is fine. Thirty seconds per heading.
  3. Paste the outline, your one-sentence stakes, and a small context pack (product overview, glossary, the relevant discovery notes if you have them, one prior PRD for tone) into the AI tool you use most — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, or Windsurf.
  4. Ask the AI to expand exactly two of the nine sections. Do not let it expand the others. Do three passes on each: skeleton, sharpen, sand.
  5. For one of those two sections, find the two or three sentences that carry your actual product judgment and rewrite them in your own voice. Use specifics. Name a trade-off.
  6. Save it as v0.1. Add a one-line decision history at the top.

Bonus credit: take the same outline into a second tool and run the same workflow on the other two sections. Compare what comes back. The shape will be similar; the texture will differ. Notice which tool’s default voice is closest to your team’s house style — that’s a useful thing to know for next time.

The shape of the artifact you should end with

By the time the chapter’s technique becomes a habit, your PRDs should share a few traits. They open with a problem statement specific enough that a reviewer can disagree with it. They name the cohort by something concrete — an account, a ticket pattern, a usage slice — rather than a generic persona. The Goals section names a baseline number, a target number, and a kill switch. The Non-goals section is honest enough to feel slightly uncomfortable. The Scope cut explains what was deferred and why. The AC are testable in the literal sense that an engineer could write a check for each one. The Risks are specific enough to be wrong. The Metrics tie back to the Goals, line by line. The Open Questions section names an owner and a deadline for each unknown. And at the top, the decision-history log shows the spec’s journey in five lines.

None of those traits require you to write every word yourself. All of them require you to author the spec, in the sense that the calls in it are calls you can defend. The AI is a co-author at best, and a competent typist at worst. You stay in the seat.

One last reframe

The mental shift this chapter asks for is small but real. A PRD isn’t a piece of prose to be generated; it’s a sequence of decisions to be recorded. The prose is the wrapping. The decisions are the thing. AI is excellent at the wrapping and incapable of the decisions. The outline-first workflow lines those two truths up so neither is fighting the other — the model gets to do what it’s best at, and you get to do what only you can.

The reader who walks away from this chapter with one habit — write the headings yourself, fill one section at a time, rewrite the judgment sentences in your own voice — has the move that turns AI from a tempting shortcut into a genuine accelerant. Everything else in the course builds on that one move.
