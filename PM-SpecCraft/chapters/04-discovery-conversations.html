Chapter 4

Discovery Conversations with AI

Widening the lens without losing customer truth — how to brainstorm in breadth, generate alternatives, and recognize where the chat ends and the field starts.

From sharp problem to wide route

It’s Tuesday morning. The sharpened problem is sitting at the top of your draft doc. Your tech lead messaged at 9:04: “Got the problem statement, looks solid. What are we actually building?” You smile, because that’s the right question, and because you don’t have an answer yet. You shouldn’t. The next hour is the one where you find out.

You closed chapter 3 with a four-field problem statement and a sense of relief. The fuzzy brief is now a real thing — a specific cohort, a specific friction, a number you can point at, a success line you could defend in a review. That’s the destination. This chapter is about the route.

Discovery is the part of product work where you’re no longer asking what is the problem? but what shapes could the answer take? Three different solutions could address the same sharpened problem. Five could. Twelve could. Most of the time, the first solution that comes to mind is the obvious one — not the best one. The job of discovery is to walk past the obvious answer and see whether something better is hiding two doors down.

AI is unusually good at this kind of widening. It will happily list twelve angles when you ask for twelve. It doesn’t get tired. It doesn’t play office politics around which option is fashionable. It also, however, doesn’t know your customers, your data, your constraints, or the small accumulated wisdom your team built last quarter. Discovery with AI is a back-and-forth: the model widens, you ground; the model lists, you choose; the model proposes, you go and check.

What discovery does — and what it doesn’t

It’s easy to confuse discovery with research. They’re cousins, not twins. Discovery generates and tests candidate answers; research generates and tests real ones. AI is great at the first job and only partly useful for the second.

Use AI to widen the candidate set: more user segments, more job framings, more solution shapes, more risks, more analogies from adjacent products. Use real customers to narrow the set: which of these candidates is actually true for the people you’re building for? The mistake is to skip the second move and ship the AI’s widest list as if it were research output. The mistake in the other direction is to skip the first move and only ever consider the three solutions your team already brought to the meeting. Discovery is both moves, in order.

A small habit that works: write your discovery prompt with a target number in it. “Give me 12 angles.” “List 10 segments.” “Suggest 8 alternatives.” The number forces the AI to push past the obvious three. You’ll prune later — the goal up front is breadth.

Ask for breadth first, narrow second

The single biggest discovery skill with AI is the breadth-first prompt. It sounds simple. It changes everything downstream.

A narrow prompt — “what should we do about the noisy boards problem?” — produces a tidy three-paragraph answer with a recommendation already baked in. You then spend the rest of the conversation editing the AI’s first idea, instead of choosing among real alternatives. A breadth-first prompt — “list 12 different shapes a fix for the noisy boards problem could take, with no preference among them; one line each” — produces a menu. You then pick which three are worth thinking harder about.

The 12 won’t all be good. Maybe four are obvious, four are interesting, two are wild, and two are bad. That’s the right ratio. The job of the prompt is to show you the space; the job of the human is to walk through it.

This works in any major tool — Copilot, Windsurf, Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini. Some models will give you twelve genuinely distinct ideas; others will give you ten variations of three. If you’re getting variations, push back: “those are too similar to each other; give me five that sit further apart on the design space.” The model will almost always oblige.

Generating user segments — and the trap of accepting them

Discovery often starts with people, not solutions. Who is this for? Whose week gets meaningfully better if we ship something here? AI is fluent at sketching segments — ask Gemini for “eight user segments who would benefit from a fix to noisy notifications” and you’ll get eight crisp paragraphs, each with a label, a job, a pain.

They will sound right. They will be plausible. Some of them will also be invented. The model is generating segments from patterns it’s seen across thousands of products, not from your customer base. A segment like “the cross-functional design lead juggling six projects” might describe nobody on your platform — or it might describe your most engaged user. The AI can’t tell you which.

The move that high-leverage PMs make: take the AI’s list, then ground each segment against something concrete. A real account name. A support-ticket pattern. A usage-data slice. A persona document your team already validated. Segments that survive grounding are real for your product. Segments that don’t are starter prompts for the next interview, not facts.

If you can’t name a single real account, ticket, or usage pattern that matches the AI’s segment, treat it as a hypothesis, not a finding. Plausible segments and observed segments are different things, and the spec should make the difference visible.

Jobs-To-Be-Done candidates

A close cousin of segments is jobs-to-be-done — the small framing that asks “what is the user trying to accomplish, in the moment, when they reach for the product?” AI will list jobs as freely as it lists segments. “When I open a noisy board, I want to know what changed since yesterday so I can decide if I need to act.” “When I get pinged on weekends, I want to triage in five seconds without opening the app.” Useful. Plausible. Possibly real.

The trick is the same: the AI generates candidate jobs; real interviews tell you which jobs your users actually have. You don’t need a 30-person research project for this — even three customer calls will quickly confirm or kill an AI-suggested job. The cost is low; the leverage is high; the move can’t be skipped.

One pattern that works: paste an AI-generated list of jobs into your discovery doc, and put a tiny tag next to each — (observed), (plausible, untested), or (speculative). The doc immediately becomes more honest. Reviewers know which jobs are anchored and which are still hypotheses.

A second pattern, useful when the AI’s job framings sound too much alike: ask explicitly for jobs that contradict each other. “Give me three candidate jobs that would lead to different solutions, where solving for one job hurts the others.” That single move forces the model to articulate trade-offs, which is closer to how real users actually feel about the product than a list of harmonious jobs.

From fuzzy assumption to testable hypothesis

Most discovery conversations contain at least one assumption hiding inside a sentence. “Users will tune notifications if we give them better controls.” That sounds like a plan. It’s actually three claims taped together: that users want to tune (rather than mute), that better controls would shift their behavior, and that the behavior shift would solve the underlying problem. Each is a separate hypothesis, each is testable, and each could be wrong.

AI is helpful here. Paste a fuzzy paragraph of strategy and ask: “extract every implicit assumption in this paragraph and rewrite each as a testable hypothesis with a clear failure condition.” ChatGPT will give you back a list. Some hypotheses will be obviously checkable in your data. Others will need a customer call. A few will be untestable as written, which is itself useful information — it means you’re carrying a belief you can’t confirm, and that belief deserves a label.

The shape of a clean hypothesis is small but specific: a subject (the cohort), a claim (the behavior or outcome), and a falsification condition (what data would prove this wrong). “Free-tier users who hit the project-count limit will convert to paid at >15% within 14 days” passes; “users will appreciate the upgrade flow” doesn’t. Asking the AI to rewrite vague hypotheses into this shape costs almost nothing and produces immediately better discovery output.

The alternative-solution table

Once you have a sharpened problem, a real cohort, a few candidate jobs, and a small set of hypotheses to track, the next move is to lay out the candidate solutions side by side. A simple table works wonders. Five rows, three columns: solution shape, rough cost, biggest risk. Fit-for-purpose can be a fourth column when you’ve grounded it.

ALTERNATIVES — noisy-board cohort, draft 1 Shape | Cost | Biggest risk -------------------------------|--------|----------------------------- 1. Per-board mute toggle | S | Users mute everything; we | | lose all signal from cohort. 2. Smart digest (1/day rollup) | M | Latency — urgent items | | feel slow; new failure mode. 3. Auto-follow only owned | M | Hard to define “owned” in | | shared workflows. 4. Notification quality score, | L | Heavy investment for the suppress low-score items | | wrong cohort if we’re wrong. 5. Onboarding nudge: review | S | Doesn’t fix existing pain; defaults at day 3 | | only helps new joiners.

The table is deliberately rough — one line per cell, no fancy formatting, no heroics. Its job is to make the choices comparable. Once you can see five candidates next to each other, the conversation shifts from “what should we build?” to “why this one and not that one?” — and that’s the conversation that produces a good spec.

AI can draft this table in a minute if you describe the cohort and ask for five solution shapes with cost and risk columns. What it cannot do is grade your team’s real cost of each option, which depends on your stack, your roadmap, and your engineers. So treat the AI’s draft as scaffolding. Replace the cost estimates with your own. Sharpen the risks against what your engineers and designers actually worry about.

A worked discovery dialogue

Here’s the technique on a different example, so you can see it generalize. The PM is working on onboarding drop-off — new sign-ups who don’t reach their second session — and is using Claude. The same conversation works in Gemini, ChatGPT, or Copilot; the cadence is in the questions, not the tool.

Sharpened problem: new sign-ups in our self-serve tier reach the dashboard but not the “create your first project” step. 38% bounce after the welcome screen. Don’t propose a solution yet. List 12 different shapes a fix could take — no preference, one line each, no two ideas the same flavor.
(1) Move the “create project” CTA above the fold on the welcome screen. (2) Replace the welcome screen with the project-creation flow itself. (3) Pre-create a sample project so the dashboard isn’t empty. (4) Add a 30-second guided tour after the welcome screen. (5) Send a follow-up email at +24h with a one-click resume link. (6) Run a single-question intent survey on the welcome screen and tailor the next step. (7) Add a chat widget that proactively offers help if the user idles > 20s. (8) Replace generic dashboard with a role-specific starter view (designer, PM, engineer). (9) Add a “skip the dashboard” option that takes them straight to project setup. (10) Soft-pause the signup until the user has a project name in mind, gating the welcome behind a one-question form. (11) Show three example projects to clone. (12) Drop the welcome screen entirely.
Good list. Group these into three families — same underlying bet inside each family.
Family A — Reduce friction to the first project: 1, 2, 9, 12. Family B — Increase activation by reducing the empty-state: 3, 8, 11. Family C — Re-engage users who didn’t finish: 4, 5, 6, 7, 10.
Useful. From our funnel data: 71% of bouncers leave within 90 seconds, before any nudge could fire. Family C looks weaker than I thought. Stay in A and B. For each family, name the strongest version, the weakest version, and the assumption each rests on.
Family A — strongest: option 12 (drop the welcome entirely). Weakest: option 1 (move CTA), small impact. Assumption: the welcome screen is the friction, not the project flow itself. Family B — strongest: option 3 (pre-created sample). Weakest: option 11 (clone examples), high cost for unclear lift. Assumption: emptiness, not friction, is what causes users to bounce.
The two families have different root assumptions about why users bounce. Before we pick, what’s the cheapest test that would distinguish them?
A two-week A/B with three arms: control (welcome screen), Family A treatment (drop welcome — option 12), Family B treatment (sample project — option 3). Sample size to detect a 5-point shift in second-session rate is roughly 4,000 users per arm at your traffic levels. Cost: low engineering, two weeks. Outcome: tells you which assumption is closer to true; the loser exits the discovery; the winner becomes the spec.

Notice the cadence. Round 1 is breadth: twelve candidates with no opinion. Round 2 is structure: group them into families with shared assumptions. Round 3 is grounding: real funnel data kills a whole family in one move. Round 4 is sharpening: the strongest and weakest version of each surviving family. Round 5 is the test: how would we tell which assumption is closer to true? Total time, maybe fifteen minutes. The output is two clear options and a plan to choose between them.

The AI listed, grouped, and proposed. The PM brought funnel data, killed a family, and chose the test. That’s the division of labor.

A second mini-example: pricing-page conversion

The same loop, applied to a very different problem, in shorthand. A growth PM is staring at a flat pricing-page conversion rate. The sharpened problem from chapter 3’s loop reads: “Visitors who reach the pricing page from the ‘Compare plans’ CTA in the dashboard are converting at 1.8% to paid, vs 4.1% for visitors arriving from the marketing site — a gap of about 2,200 missed conversions per quarter. The cohort is heavy on existing free-tier accounts who have used the product for >30 days.” Open Gemini.

Round 1 — breadth. “List 12 different shapes a fix for this conversion gap could take. No preference. One line each.” The model proposes ideas ranging from “swap the plan-comparison table for a usage-based recommender” to “trigger a one-click upgrade prompt when the user hits a specific in-product limit” to “remove the pricing page entirely for in-app traffic and inline the upgrade flow into the dashboard.” Twelve genuinely different shapes; not three rephrased four times.

Round 2 — families. The PM groups the candidates: (A) reframe the page, (B) move the upgrade decision out of the page entirely, (C) personalize the page based on the user’s usage. Three families, three different bets.

Round 3 — grounding. The PM checks billing data: 73% of free-tier users converting to paid did so within seven days of hitting the project-count limit. Family B looks suddenly stronger — the moment of conversion isn’t actually on the pricing page; it’s in the product, when a limit bites. The pricing page is just a polite stop on the way.

Round 4 — sharpen. “Strongest version of family B: an inline upgrade flow at the moment the user hits the project-count limit, with the pricing page becoming a fallback rather than the primary path. Assumption: the friction is the trip to the pricing page itself, not the price or the plan structure.”

Total time: about twelve minutes. The original instinct — “redesign the pricing page” — would have produced a quarter of design and engineering work. The discovery loop revealed that the page might not be the right place to fix the problem at all. The cost saved is significant; the leverage was in the breadth-first prompt that kept families A, B, and C visible long enough to compare them honestly.

Why “just brainstorm” is a quiet trap

Discovery looks deceptively similar to brainstorming. Both produce candidate ideas. Both feel generative. The trap is treating discovery like brainstorming with bigger output — producing 12 ideas and stopping there. That’s half the loop.

The other half is structure: grouping candidates into families, naming the assumption each family rests on, designing the cheap test that would distinguish them. Without structure, breadth becomes noise — twelve options and no way to decide. With structure, breadth becomes leverage — twelve options collapse into three meaningful bets, and the bet you choose has a falsifiable assumption attached to it.

If you only do one of the two halves, the second half is the high-leverage one. A meeting that started with six pre-existing candidates but ended with families and a cheap test will produce a better spec than a meeting that generated 30 candidates and chose by vote. Structure is what turns discovery output into discovery insight.

A useful prompt to keep handy: “Take this list of options and group them into families based on the underlying belief about why the problem exists. Name each belief in one sentence.” That single move turns a flat list into a decision-quality artifact in about ninety seconds.

AI-flavored generic outputs

If you do enough discovery sessions, you’ll start spotting a small dialect. “We could leverage user behavior to deliver a robust, seamless experience that delights customers and drives engagement.” Every word in that sentence is technically valid; the sentence as a whole says approximately nothing. AI tools produce this dialect by default when the prompt was vague, the topic was unfamiliar, or the model is hedging.

The fix is rarely to throw the output away. The fix is to interrogate it word by word. “Leverage how, exactly?” “Robust against what failure mode?” “Seamless with which adjacent flow?” “Engagement on which observable signal?” Each question forces the AI to commit to specifics — specifics it could have produced the first time if you’d asked for them. Most tools, asked this way, will sharpen on the next pass. The ones that don’t are usually a sign you’re past what AI can do on this topic; that’s a cue to bring in a human.

If you can swap out your product’s name and drop the same paragraph into a competitor’s deck, the discovery output is too generic. Real discovery is unmistakably about your users, your data, your trade-offs. The proper noun count should be high.

Borrowing from adjacent products, on purpose

One discovery move worth naming explicitly: AI is unusually good at surfacing how other products solved similar-shaped problems. Ask Copilot for “five products outside our category that solved a comparable cohort problem to ours, and what the shape of their fix was” and you’ll get a useful set of analogies. Some will be obvious neighbors. Some will be surprising — an in-product moment from a video game, a pattern from an e-commerce checkout, a notification model from a meditation app. Surprising analogies are where good discovery often hides.

The thing to watch for: the AI may invent product features that don’t exist, or describe a feature that existed three years ago and has since changed. Treat each cited example as a hypothesis to verify with a screenshot or a brief check, not as a fact. The pattern you borrow is real even if the AI’s description isn’t — you just need to confirm the pattern before you cite it in a doc.

Borrowed patterns also need to be re-grounded for your context. A checkout flow that works for an e-commerce site selling shoes may not transfer to a B2B SaaS upgrade flow, even if the underlying psychology is the same. The borrowing is inspiration, not blueprint. The translation back to your product is your job.

The hypothesis log: a small artifact that compounds

One artifact that pays compound interest across multiple discovery sessions: a running hypothesis log. A simple list, kept in your notes, of every assumption you’ve generated about the cohort or problem area, with three columns: hypothesis, status (open / confirmed / killed), and how we’d know.

AI helps you populate it quickly. Paste the discovery doc and ask Claude to extract every implicit hypothesis. You’ll get back a cleaner list than you’d have written by hand. As discovery sessions accumulate, the log grows. As you run experiments and interviews, you flip statuses. Six months in, you have an institutional memory of what your team has tested and learned about a cohort — and new PMs who join can read the log instead of re-litigating the same questions.

The hypothesis log doubles as a defense against subtle AI drift. If you notice the model is suggesting the same idea you already killed two months ago, the log catches it. The discovery conversation doesn’t have to start fresh every time; the team’s accumulated wisdom can come along for the ride.

A small detail worth getting right: keep the log in a place every PM on the team can read and update. A shared doc, a wiki page, a project-management board — the venue matters less than the access. Discovery memory locked in one person’s notes is institutional knowledge with a single point of failure. The log’s value compounds the moment it’s shared.

If you can’t name three hypotheses your team has actively confirmed or killed in the last quarter, you may be running discovery without a memory. Each session generates ideas; few of them feed the next session. A hypothesis log fixes that without much overhead — ten minutes of upkeep a week, dramatic compounding over a quarter.

What AI can’t replace in discovery

Most of this chapter has been about widening with AI. Here’s the part the AI can’t do for you, ever, in any tool, no matter how good the prompt: it can’t hear a real customer’s tone of voice. It can’t read the pause in a sales call when a prospect describes their workflow. It can’t feel the texture of a support thread that’s gone fourteen messages deep. It can’t notice that the user keeps rephrasing the question because the original question wasn’t what they actually meant.

Those signals are the difference between a product that fits and a product that’s plausible. AI can scaffold around them — help you prepare interview questions, summarize transcripts, surface patterns across many calls. It cannot generate them. The customer voice has to come in from outside the chat.

Yours to decide: when a candidate solution is real enough to spec. AI can list, group, prioritize, and pressure-test. It cannot tell you that the customer you talked to last Thursday lit up when you described option 3, or that the support team has been quietly logging the same complaint for six months. Discovery is grounded by humans — sales calls, customer calls, usage data, support patterns, your own product instincts. The AI accelerates the thinking; it does not replace the field work.

Feeding the model context before you ask for breadth

The single biggest lever for the quality of an AI-driven discovery session is what you put into the chat before the breadth-first prompt. A model with no context will give you twelve generic candidates — the same twelve any product team in your category might receive. A model with rich context will give you twelve candidates that actually fit your product, your stack, and your constraints.

Three pieces of context are usually enough to transform the output. First, a one-paragraph description of the product and its current shape (“we’re a mid-market SaaS for X, our core flow is Y, our constraints are Z”). Second, the sharpened problem statement from chapter 3. Third, a short list of options your team has already tried — not so the AI avoids them, but so it knows which adjacent shapes are worth proposing.

Pasting these three at the top of the chat takes about ninety seconds. The improvement in candidate quality is dramatic. The 12 angles stop including ideas you ruled out three quarters ago, and start including angles that genuinely fit your situation. This is also why a reusable context pack — covered properly in chapter 11 — is one of the highest-leverage investments a PM can make once they’re running discovery sessions weekly.

Hurried vs done-well: the same prompt, two ways

Round 1 of a discovery conversation sets the tone for everything after it. The hurried version closes doors before you knew they were open. The done-well version keeps every door visible until you choose which to walk through. The difference is one extra line in the prompt.

Hurried version: “What should we do about onboarding drop-off after the welcome screen?”
I’d recommend a guided tour after the welcome screen, paired with a sample project so users land on something rather than nothing. This addresses the empty-state problem most onboarding flows hit and is well-precedented across SaaS products.
Done-well version: “Don’t propose a solution. Given that new sign-ups bounce after the welcome screen, list 12 distinct shapes a fix could take — no preference, one line each. Then group them into 3 families that share an underlying bet about why users are bouncing.”
(1…12, with families A/B/C as in the earlier dialogue.) Note: I have no opinion among them — the right family depends on whether your bouncers leave because the welcome is friction, because the dashboard feels empty, or because the moment isn’t right for them. Funnel data should pick one.

The first answer is a finished plan you have to argue against. The second is a map you can walk into. The cost of the second prompt is one extra sentence; the upside is twenty minutes of a useful conversation instead of twenty minutes of editing somebody else’s premature recommendation.

The pattern repeats across every discovery move you make with AI. The hurried version of any prompt closes a door — it gets you to a recommendation faster but leaves you with one option to defend rather than several to choose between. The done-well version costs a little more time up front and pays it back many times over in the conversation that follows.

Discovery at different scales

Not every discovery conversation needs twelve angles and three families. The depth of the loop should scale with the size of the bet. Knowing which mode you’re in saves you from over-investing on small things and under-investing on big ones.

Tactical brief (a small fix, a one-day change). A 5-angle prompt is plenty. You’re mostly checking that the obvious answer really is the best one. Total time: five minutes. The cost of more discovery exceeds the cost of the work itself.

Sprint-sized feature. The canonical version of the loop in this chapter: 12 angles, 3 families, 1 cheap test. Total time: 15–20 minutes of focused work, often spread across a morning while you do other things. This is the mode most discovery conversations live in.

Quarter-long initiative or strategic bet. 20+ angles across multiple sessions, customer interviews running in parallel, a discovery doc that lives for weeks before becoming a spec. The same cadence applies; the wall-clock duration is days or weeks. Skipping the breadth-first move on a strategic bet is how organizations end up shipping the third-best option as if it were the only one.

The mistake to avoid: running a tactical-brief depth on a strategic-bet problem. A quarter-shaping decision sharpened in fifteen minutes will produce a confident-sounding output that papers over the real disagreement in your org. The depth of the loop scales with how seriously you take the question.

The opposite mistake is rarer but real: running a strategic-bet depth on a tactical brief. If a one-day fix gets the full twelve-angles treatment, you’ll spend an hour deciding what to spend two hours building. Match the cost of the discovery to the cost of the work. When in doubt, slightly under-invest in tactical and slightly over-invest in strategic — the asymmetry of regret is in your favor.

When the discovery stalls

The loop is simple but it doesn’t always glide. A few stall patterns are worth knowing — not as failures, just as moments where you have to consciously change something to keep moving.

Stall 1: every angle feels equally good. You finish the breadth prompt and you can’t pick. The 12 candidates all look fine, no clear winner. This usually means you don’t yet have enough data to discriminate — the next move isn’t another AI prompt, it’s the smallest piece of customer signal that would rule out at least one family. Pull a support ticket sample. Watch three session recordings. Ask sales which two complaints they hear most this quarter. New signal, then back into the loop.

Stall 2: the AI keeps giving you variations of the same idea. Twelve options, but really only three different bets dressed up twelve ways. Push back: “those are too similar; give me five candidates that sit further apart on the design space.” If that doesn’t work, change tools. Different models have different default flavors; sometimes a switch from ChatGPT to Claude (or back) is enough to break the rut.

Stall 3: the discovery is finding a different problem than the one you sharpened. The 12 candidates keep solving something adjacent to your sharpened problem statement. This is rare and useful when it happens. The right move isn’t to override the candidates — it’s to look back at the problem statement. The loop may be telling you the problem is mis-stated. Take ten minutes back in chapter 3’s territory before continuing.

Stalls aren’t a sign you’re using the technique wrong. They’re usually a sign you’ve hit the edge of what AI alone can do, and the next move is human — a data pull, a customer call, a conversation with a teammate. The honest version of the craft includes those moves as part of the loop.

Sharing the discovery output

Once the discovery conversation has produced something useful — an alternatives table, a family grouping, a list of grounded segments — what you do with it next determines whether it survives contact with the team. Three small habits make discovery output stick.

Capture the rejected options, not just the chosen one. The 9 candidates you didn’t pick aren’t waste. They’re the answer to next quarter’s “did we consider X?” question, and they’re a paper trail showing the team that the chosen option won on merit, not on convenience. A short “Considered and deferred” section under your alternatives table costs nothing to maintain and earns trust over time.

Tag every claim with its grounding. When you write up the discovery output, mark each line with one of three tags: (observed) for things you can point at in real data, (plausible, untested) for AI-suggested or team-suggested ideas you haven’t yet validated, and (speculative) for genuine guesses. Reviewers immediately know what to push on. Engineers know which assumptions are load-bearing. Future you, six weeks from now, knows which beliefs to revisit.

Walk it through with one engineer or designer before going wider. Five minutes with a teammate who knows your stack will catch the cost-estimate that’s wildly off, the option that depends on a service you don’t own, or the family that’s already been tried and didn’t work. That’s the cheapest pressure-test you can run, and it happens before the full review meeting where the cost of being wrong is much higher.

How do you know you’ve explored enough?

Three quiet signals tell you a discovery conversation is done well enough to move on from.

You can name a candidate you almost picked but didn’t. If your alternatives feel like “the obvious one and a few weak straw-men,” you haven’t explored enough. Real discovery produces at least two options that were close calls. If you can’t name one, run another breadth-first prompt with a higher target number.

You can articulate the central assumption your chosen option rests on. Not “we think users will like this.” Something falsifiable: “we’re betting that the empty-state, not the welcome-screen friction, is what causes 38% of users to bounce.” If you can’t name the assumption, the spec hasn’t earned its commitment yet.

You can describe one cheap experiment that would falsify your choice. If nothing in the next two weeks could prove your option wrong, you may be choosing on taste rather than evidence. That’s sometimes legitimate — some bets are taste calls. But name it as such and own it; don’t pretend evidence drove a decision that taste actually drove.

None of these are arbitrary tests. Each one is a sign that one piece of the discovery loop reached a usable level of specificity. When the three are yes, you’re ready to commit a candidate to spec.

When to leave the chat

A good discovery session has a natural ending. Three small signals tell you the chat has done its job and the next move is human.

Signal 1: the AI is paraphrasing itself. You’re three rounds in and each new answer rearranges words from earlier ones. New context isn’t arriving. This usually means the model has run out of the context you fed it, and what it needs now is a real customer signal you don’t have yet. Time to schedule a call.

Signal 2: the next question is “what would the user think?” If the conversation has reached the point where you’re trying to predict actual user behavior, the chat can guess but it can’t know. Open the calendar; book the interview. Forty-five minutes with three users will produce more signal than four more rounds with the model.

Signal 3: you’re using the AI to win an internal argument. If you find yourself asking the model to endorse the option you already prefer, you’ve left discovery and entered self-justification. Close the tab and have the disagreement directly with the person you’re trying to convince.

From discovery to spec: what carries forward

A discovery session has done its job when it produces three things you can carry into the PRD draft: a chosen candidate, the central assumption that candidate rests on, and the cheap experiment (or the named taste call) that justifies committing. Anything less than three and the spec will start with a question instead of an answer.

Anything more than three — ten alternatives, four families, a sixteen-page discovery doc — is artifact for its own sake. The PRD doesn’t need to relive the discovery; it needs to commit. The discovery doc lives in your notes as a reference. The spec moves on with the chosen option clearly marked, the rejected options noted briefly, and the assumption made explicit.

That handoff between “discovery output” and “PRD input” is where many specs quietly lose energy — the discovery is rich, but only the recommendation makes it into the spec, and the assumption disappears. A well-handled handoff names the assumption in the spec itself so reviewers can pressure-test it. The next chapter will pick that thread up directly.

A small reusable prompt kit for discovery

If you’d like a starting kit, these five prompts cover the cadence above. They work in any major tool with at most a small tweak. Save them once; use them often.

  • Breadth: “Given this sharpened problem [paste], list 12 different shapes a fix could take. No two ideas the same flavor. One line each. No preference among them.”
  • Family grouping: “Group these candidates into 3 families, where each family shares an underlying bet about why this problem exists.”
  • Strongest and weakest: “For each family, name the strongest version, the weakest version, and the central assumption it rests on.”
  • Cheapest test: “Given two surviving families with different assumptions, propose the cheapest test that would distinguish them. Include sample size and timeline.”
  • Generic-language interrogation: “Pick the three vaguest phrases in the answer above and rewrite each as a specific, observable claim about our product.”

You don’t need to use them in this exact wording — the cadence is the goal, not the script. After a few weeks the prompts will adapt themselves to your voice, your product, and your team’s vocabulary.

Try it this week

The Twelve Angles Drill. Pick one feature idea on your roadmap or backlog — ideally one where your team has already settled on a solution shape and stopped exploring.

  1. Open the AI tool of your choice (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot, Windsurf — whichever you reach for most).
  2. Paste your sharpened problem statement (or write one on the spot using the chapter 3 template).
  3. Ask for 12 alternative solution shapes, no preference among them, one line each. Do not hint at the option your team already picked.
  4. Group the 12 into 3 families with shared assumptions. Mark which assumption you can already confirm or deny with data you have.
  5. Compare the strongest version in each family to the option your team had picked. Which 3 of the 12 would you actually validate with users this week?
  6. If one of those three isn’t the option your team had settled on — congratulations. That’s the discovery conversation paying off.

Bonus credit: run the same exercise in a second tool and compare. The 12 candidates will overlap a lot; the framing of the families often won’t. The gap is informative.

Discovery in a room vs discovery solo

Most of this chapter has framed discovery as a solo activity at your desk. The same loop adapts well to a room of two or three people — with one small adjustment: the AI becomes a third participant rather than a private brainstorming partner.

A useful pattern: someone shares the screen with the AI tool open. The PM types the breadth-first prompt while the team watches; the candidates arrive on the screen as a starting menu. The team then takes over — reading aloud, reacting, killing options, sharpening others, calling out which assumption a candidate rests on, naming which engineer or designer would object to which option. The AI is a generator of starting material; the room is what turns the material into a decision.

The thing to avoid: letting the AI’s output become the meeting’s default direction by anchoring effect. If the first candidate the AI writes is option 1 in the list, the room will spend disproportionate time on it. A small habit that helps: ask the AI to randomize the list order before you read it, or have someone re-shuffle the candidates by hand. The framing of which option is “number 1” should not be the model’s call.

Tools matter less than facilitation here. Whether the room is using Windsurf, Claude, or ChatGPT, the principle is the same: AI generates breadth, the team narrows with judgment. The PM’s job in the room is to make sure both halves of the loop happen — that the team doesn’t fall in love with the AI’s first list, and that the team also doesn’t reject every AI candidate just to feel ownership over the outcome. The right answer often includes ideas from both sources.

One last reframe

Discovery with AI looks deceptively similar to brainstorming alone with a notebook. The difference is leverage. A solo brainstorm typically produces three or four candidates before fatigue sets in; an AI-assisted one reaches twelve in two minutes. A solo brainstorm rarely groups its own output into families with shared assumptions; the AI will do that on request. A solo brainstorm has a hard time pressure-testing its own ideas; the AI will gladly play the skeptic if you ask it to.

What stays unchanged is the part that matters most. You’re still the one who decides which family to bet on, which assumption to test first, which customer to call, which option to spec. AI doesn’t shrink that responsibility — it sharpens the conversation that leads up to it. The reader who walks away from this chapter with a habit of asking for 12, grounding against real data, and leaving the chat at the right moment has the move that makes discovery worth doing in the first place.
