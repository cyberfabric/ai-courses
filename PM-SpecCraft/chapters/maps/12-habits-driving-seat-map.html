Chapter 12

Habits That Keep You in Control

Memory Map
🚗

The Big Idea

Tools change every quarter. Habits don’t. The PMs who feel most in control three years into the AI era are the ones whose practice survived a tool switch — small daily and weekly rituals that keep judgment sharp while AI takes care of the mechanical work.

🛠️ The Five Habits

📝
Daily decision log
5 minutes, end of day, three lines max: what I decided, why, what I’ll watch for. Don’t outsource it to AI.
🔍
Weekly spec review
Friday afternoon, 15 minutes. Re-read shipped specs, mark passages as yours vs. AI-augmented. Notice the ratio.
🧹
Monthly prompt audit
30 minutes, last Friday. Prune unused, add new wins, refresh the context pack. Keeps the kit honest.
🤝
Engineering cadence
15 min/week with one engineer, rotated quarterly. Pure spec calibration — no planning, no estimates.
🔇
Knowing when to set AI aside
Customer calls, post-incident reviews, scope-cutting calls, 1:1s about your work. Presence is the work.
⚖️
If you only do one
Start with the daily log. It’s the smallest commitment and gives you the most honest mirror of your work.

🚗 Tomorrow Morning → Year Two

  1. Tomorrow

    Open a fresh markdown file. Write three lines before closing the laptop. Day 1 of the decision log.

  2. This week

    Block 15 minutes Friday for the spec review. Mark yours vs. AI-augmented. Don’t change anything yet.

  3. This month

    Schedule the recurring monthly prompt audit. Last Friday, 30 minutes, on the calendar.

  4. This quarter

    Set up the weekly 15-minute engineering calibration. Rotate engineers monthly.

  5. This year

    Find a peer-PM partner. Once a quarter, swap a recent spec, mark each other’s yours-vs-AI passages.

⚖️ Consistency > Intensity

HabitBeats this…
5 min/day on the log for a quarter One frantic 3-hour session a year
15 min every Friday for spec review 4-hour deep audit on the company retreat
30 min last Friday of each month Yearly “rebuild the kit” sprint
Small habit that lasts 2 years Ambitious habit that lasts 2 months

📈 The Fluency Curve

CheckpointWhat clicksWhat changes
3 months The mechanics. Sharpening a fuzzy brief takes a third of the time. The assistant becomes a tool, not an event. You stop noticing it most of the time.
6 months The judgment. Smell tests run automatically; you spot issues before reviewers do. You start trusting your gut when AI output feels wrong.
12 months The role shifts. Less time on prose; more on customer truth, written argument, taste. The mechanical layer takes 1/4 of the calendar it used to. Judgment fills the space.

🚫 When AI Doesn’t Belong in the Room

🎤
Customer interviews
The half-spoken pause is the insight. AI suggestions in real time pull you out of the room.
🚒
Post-incident reviews
The room is already nervous. Take notes by hand. Let the conversation breathe.
✂️
Scope-cutting calls
Several humans’ trade-offs in one head — not a model’s.
📨
First hour after escalation
Read the angry emails yourself, in their original tone. AI can’t tell you which one ends in churn.

🧪 Quick Quiz

Test yourself

How long does the daily decision log take, and what’s in it?

Five minutes, three lines max: what I decided, why I decided it, what I’ll watch for tomorrow. Written before closing the laptop, in your own voice — not the assistant’s.

Why is consistency more important than intensity for these habits?

Five minutes a day for a quarter beats one frantic three-hour session a year. The habits are designed small specifically so they survive bad weeks, busy weeks, and quiet weeks. A small habit that lasts two years beats an ambitious habit that lasts two months.

If two autopilot signals fire at once, what’s the recovery?

Smaller than you’d expect. One Friday afternoon, set the assistant aside, write one section of the next spec entirely by hand. Three or four hand-written sections across two weeks usually resets calibration. Earlier detection makes recovery faster.

What three things won’t these habits fix?

(1) Solving the wrong problem at the company level. (2) Stakeholders who don’t value clear specs. (3) Burnout or low motivation. The habits keep the driver sharp; they don’t guarantee the road is going somewhere good.

If you can only adopt one habit, which one and why?

The daily log. Smallest commitment, most honest mirror, and most of the other habits get easier once it’s running because the log is what makes drift visible.

📖 Glossary

Decision log
An append-only file with three lines per day on the calls you made and why.
Marking (in spec review)
Tagging passages in your shipped specs as either “clearly yours” or “AI-augmented” to calibrate where judgment is showing through.
Prompt audit
Monthly pass over your kit: prune unused entries, add new wins, refresh the context pack.
Spec calibration
A 15-minute weekly slot with one engineer to flag what’s ambiguous, what’s landing well, what would have been written differently.
Autopilot signals
Six small tells that judgment is fading: AI tone in your writing, fewer questions, no “hmm” moments, lost framing, sameness across specs, quiet log.
Fluency curve
The 3 / 6 / 12 month arc — mechanics click, judgment shows up, role shifts toward customer time and written argument.
Partner version
An optional peer-PM pairing for swapping spec passages, stress-testing prompts, and mutual smell-testing once a quarter.

If You Remember Nothing Else

  • Tools change every quarter. Habits don’t. Build for the durable layer.
  • Daily 5-min log + weekly 15-min review + monthly 30-min audit = the core stack.
  • Don’t outsource the decision log to AI. The trace of your reasoning is the value.
  • If autopilot signals fire, recovery is small: write one section by hand on Friday.
  • Some moments don’t belong to the assistant: customer calls, incidents, scope cuts, 1:1s.
  • If you only do one habit, do the daily log. It’s the foundation everything else rests on.
  • AI accelerates the mechanical layer; you stay in the driver’s seat for the judgment layer.
  • You’ve done the reading. Tomorrow morning the work starts. Drive well.
