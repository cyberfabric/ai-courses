Chapter 3

From Fuzzy Idea to Sharp Problem

Memory Map
🔪

The Big Idea

Sharp specs come from sharp problems — and sharp problems come from rounds, not one-shot prompts. AI adds polish to whatever sharpness you bring it. Bring fuzz, you get polished fuzz. Bring four sharp fields, you get a spec the team can actually ship.

🎯 The Four-Field Destination

❓
PROBLEM
What is going wrong, in 1–2 specific sentences. Not the symptom. Not the solution. The thing itself.
👤
USERS
Who specifically experiences this — a cohort, role, or moment-in-the-product concrete enough to sit with tomorrow.
📊
EVIDENCE
How you know it’s real. Tickets, sessions, calls, surveys, usage data — with at least one number or named source.
🏁
SUCCESS
One observable signal you could watch on a dashboard. Not “users will be happier.”

Roughly four sentences total. No solution anywhere — just the shape of the gap.

🔁 The 5-Whys Loop, Adapted for AI

  1. Restate & interpret

    Hand the AI the brief. Ask for a restatement plus three plausible interpretations. Test what the model heard.

  2. Redirect to user moment

    “Those are too generic. For each interpretation, name a specific user, doing a specific thing, hitting a specific friction.”

  3. Ask for evidence

    “If interpretation X is correct, how would I know? What would I check first — tickets, sessions, calls, usage data?”

  4. Sharpen the gap

    “Given what we now know [paste evidence], restate the problem in two sentences. No solutions. No softening words.”

  5. Counter-frame (optional)

    “Make the strongest case that this isn’t the real problem at all.” Honest counter-arguments expose blind spots.

Five rounds is a maximum, not a minimum. Many problems collapse cleanly in three.

🔍 Three Patterns AI Quietly Adds

Pattern What it sounds like Redirect
1. Skipping to the solution “We should add a settings page with granular controls.” (problem half + premature fix half) “Stay on the problem. No solutions yet. Just the shape of the gap.”
2. Inventing plausible context “In most SaaS products, about 30% of users disable notifications within their first week.” “Is that from my product, or an industry estimate? If estimate, mark it as such.”
3. Reframing too neatly Two sentences, perfect parallel structure, no rough edges — reads like a press release. “That’s too clean. Where is this problem messy in real life? What contradiction would a user feel?”

🚧 When the Loop Stalls

🤷
Stall 1: Every reading feels equal
After round 1, you can’t pick. Next move isn’t another prompt — it’s 15 minutes with whoever wrote the brief.
🔁
Stall 2: Same answer, new words
AI has run out of context. Paste in tickets, usage data, glossary — new context unlocks new sharpening.
🚪
Stall 3: Sharpened the wrong problem
Bring both versions back to the requester: “You said X. The most defensible version is Y. Which do you want me to scope?”

📐 Before & After: One Frame

Field Before (fuzzy) After (sharp)
Problem “Notifications are bad.” ICs on shared boards get pinged for cards they don’t own — mute notifications globally rather than tune them.
Users None named Individual contributors joining shared boards with >5 active cards. Not owners, not admins.
Evidence None 58 distinct accounts in Q2 tickets. 41% disable rate vs. 12% baseline.
Success “Reduce complaints.” Disable rate drops 41% → <20% in two release cycles, no drop in board activity.
Result Engineer asks: “okay, what do you want me to build?” Real conversation about 3–4 design options, each clearly fit-for-purpose.

✅ Three Signals You’ve Sharpened Enough

👥 You can name the user as a person
📈 Success line has a number in it
Ready for solution conversation
🎬 You can describe what changes for the user
🪞 Can’t swap product names without breaking it

🚨 Smell Tests

🪤
“We should” tell
If the AI’s problem statement contains “we should,” “we need to,” or “the solution is” — you’re reading half-problem + half-proposal.
🎲
Plausible ≠ true
Unfamiliar percentages, customer names, or feature names? Treat as placeholders until confirmed.
✨
Press-release prose
Restatement reads too smooth? Real problems have edges. Slow down and ask where it’s messy.
🔄
Product-swap test
Could you swap in a different product’s name without changing anything else? Then it’s not yet sharp.

📒 Key Terms

Amplification effect
AI takes whatever sharpness you bring and adds polish — including polishing fuzz so it’s harder to see.
Four-field statement
Problem / Users / Evidence / Success — the shape of every sharpened brief. Anchor of the whole spec.
Rounds, not one shot
Sharpening through 3–5 prompts that each narrow the next. The cadence matters more than the count.
Restate & interpret
Round 1’s twin moves: mechanical playback of what the AI heard, plus generative offering of multiple readings.
Counter-frame
Asking AI to argue that this isn’t the real problem. If the counter doesn’t move you, your framing was solid.
Solution-creep
The AI’s default to slip a fix into a problem statement. Redirect: “No solutions yet. Just the shape of the gap.”
Inherited brief
A fuzzy brief that came from leadership. Loop still applies — but the goal is making it legible, not relitigating it.
Considered and deferred
Short note under the problem statement listing the readings you didn’t pick — cheap to keep, earns trust later.

🤝 Sharing the Sharpened Problem

  1. Top of the spec, not appendix

    The four-field statement is the spec’s anchor — first thing every reviewer reads.

  2. Share before writing the rest

    Send the four fields to your tech lead and designer. “Does this feel real, before I scope a solution?”

  3. Keep rejected interpretations visible

    A short “Considered and deferred” section — the candidates for next sprint, and the answer for week-six questions.

❓ Quick Quiz

What are the four fields of a sharpened problem statement?

Problem (what’s wrong, no solution attached), Users (a specific cohort or moment), Evidence (a number or named source), Success (one observable signal). Roughly four sentences total.

Why does asking AI for “a PRD on notifications” quietly cost you a sprint?

The AI doesn’t flag underspecification — it produces a confident, polished PRD on top of assumptions you never made. Six weeks later, engineering ships exactly what you wrote, and the same VP says: “users still say notifications are bad.”

What does Round 4 do that earlier rounds don’t?

Round 4 collapses everything you’ve learned into two sentences with no solutions and no softening words like “improve” or “optimize.” It’s the moment the gap gets locked into language you can ship against.

The AI keeps rephrasing the same answer. What’s the move?

It’s out of context, not out of capability. Paste in two recent support tickets, last quarter’s usage summary, or the relevant glossary section. New context unlocks new sharpening.

Three signs you’ve sharpened enough?

(1) You can name the user as a person, not “our users.” (2) The success line is one sentence with a number in it. (3) You can describe what changes for that user’s day in plain language.

Won’t twelve minutes of sharpening slow you down?

It moves the time upstream. The PRD is 30% faster to write because the foundation is solid, the review is shorter, the engineering kickoff cleaner. Twelve minutes of sharpening shows up as hours saved — in a different column of the ledger.

🧪 Try It: The Sharpening Drill

  1. Pick one fuzzy brief on your desk

    A Slack ask, backlog item, stakeholder request — anything under-defined.

  2. Write at the top: “NO SOLUTIONS YET”

    The instruction is partly for the model. It’s mostly for you.

  3. Run all four rounds

    Even if you think you’re done after round 2. Restate & interpret → user moment → evidence → sharpen.

  4. Pull the actual evidence

    Don’t accept the AI’s estimates as numbers. Check tickets, sessions, or whatever’s closest.

  5. Note which smell tripped you

    Solution-creep, invented context, or too-neat reframing? That’s your tool’s default. Knowing it makes the next loop faster.

Key Takeaways

  • AI doesn’t flag fuzzy input — it polishes whatever you bring. Sharpness is on you, in rounds.
  • The destination is four fields: Problem / Users / Evidence / Success — no solution anywhere.
  • The loop: restate → user moment → evidence → sharpen → counter-frame. Each round narrows the next.
  • Three patterns to redirect: solution-creep, invented context, too-neat reframing.
  • Stalls are signals, not failures — the next move is human (a conversation, a data pull, a sanity check).
  • Round 1 done well separates restating (mechanical) from interpreting (generative). Same prompt, both jobs.
  • Tiny / medium / large briefs scale with depth: 1 round / 3–4 rounds / 5+ rounds across days.
  • Twelve minutes upstream = hours saved downstream. The leverage compounds across the whole spec.
