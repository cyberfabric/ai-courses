Chapter 10

Iterating After First Feedback

Memory Map
🌀

The Big Idea

Slow down for ten minutes before speeding up. Iteration goes wrong when you respond comment-by-comment, edit-by-edit, with the AI patching in between — each change reasonable, the spec quietly losing its through-line. The fix is a five-step sequence: capture → classify → draft → edit → log. AI accelerates triage, drafts alternatives, and translates for new audiences. You stay the decision-maker on what counts as must-fix, when to hold the line, and whether the through-line drifted.

🔁 The Iteration Loop

📥 Capture
5 min, all comments verbatim
→
🗂️ Classify
3 buckets, AI sanity-check
→
✍️ Draft
diff prompts, options + trade-offs
→
🛠️ Edit
your call, your voice
→
📒 Log
one dated line per change

The first time you run it, it feels slow. By the third or fourth time, the steps compress — capture becomes ten minutes, classification is muscle memory, diff prompts are reflex. Iteration is a craft you practice in calm weather so it works in storms.

🗂️ The Three Buckets

🚨
Must-Fix
Blocks shipping. Factual error, contradicting AC, impossible scope, unknown constraint. Work these first, in one batch. One log line, the change, move on.
⚖️
Should-Consider
Real point, answer depends on context the commenter doesn’t see. Diff prompt with two alternatives + trade-offs. Sometimes change, sometimes a respectful no.
📦
Out-of-Scope
Interesting but belongs to a different spec/release/problem. Acknowledge, log as follow-up, explicitly place outside this spec.

Classification is fast — about a minute per comment. Don’t agonize. The bucket can change as you learn more, but having a label on every comment turns an overwhelming pile into a manageable list. Run the AI sanity check (“classify these 14 with one-line reasons”) and override anything you disagree with. The AI is fast at triage; the call stays yours.

✂️ The Diff Prompt — The Small Thing That Compounds

The diff prompt template “Here’s the current section. Here’s the comment. Show me the proposed change as a before/after diff, so I can see exactly what’s shifting. For each substantive change, give me a one-line rationale.” Two outputs from one pass: 1. The diff itself — visible, evaluable change 2. The rationales — first drafts of decision-log lines

A “rewrite this section” prompt produces new prose you compare slowly side-by-side. The diff prompt forces the change to be visible. The lazy version of iteration accepts the new prose because it sounds reasonable. The rigorous version reads the diff and asks “is this single change worth the trade?”

📐 30%-Rule: Patch or Rewrite?

🩹 Patch in place (< 30% changes) 📝 Rewrite the section (> 30% changes)
Faster; preserves the original voice when most sentences stay Slower; resets the section to a clean state
Risk: the section reads as two voices stitched together Risk: takes longer; needs a re-voice pass to sound like you
Patches accumulate — round 3 patches read as assembled, not written Single authorial voice across all sentences after the rewrite
Use when the original framing still holds and only details change Use when feedback shifts the section’s shape, not just its words

The 30% threshold is rough but useful. Above it, patches start to read worse than rewrites. The other reason to rewrite past the threshold: patches accumulate — by round three, a heavily patched section shows its seams. A clean rewrite, drafted in one pass and re-voiced, reads as a single authorial voice even if it took longer.

🎤 Same Facts, Three Audiences

⚙️ Engineering
precision — behavior, constraints, edge cases
📊 Leadership
outcome — problem, call, risk, metric
📄 The spec
(one source of truth)
💼 Sales
narrative — who, when, one-line pitch
🔄 AI as translator
each frame, never each fact

Don’t write three specs. Write one spec well; use AI as a translator when you need to brief a different audience. Translations live in slack messages or emails; the spec stays the spec. Always re-read against the source — AI translation has a small but consistent failure mode of dropping qualifiers, rounding numbers, or softening constraints. “Under 15%” ↔ “fewer than two in ten” is a translation; “close to 10%” is a fact change in disguise.

🌡️ Reading Your Own Reaction Before You Reply

  1. 🟢 Comment lands cleanly

    You read it, agree, make the change, no internal weather. Just process it through the loop.

  2. 🔴 Comment lands sharply

    The wording felt dismissive, the criticism felt unfair, the senior reviewer skipped context. This is where iteration most often goes wrong.

  3. ⚠️ Two failure modes

    Over-correction (make a change you don’t agree with, just to make discomfort go away) or defensiveness (long reply explaining why the comment is wrong). Both are reactions to the feeling, not the substance.

  4. ⏸️ The fix is mechanical

    Leave the comment alone for an hour. Move to a different bucket. Make a coffee. Come back and re-read it as if a teammate you trusted had raised the same point in a tone you liked. Decide on the merits, not the temperature.

This is exactly the kind of judgment AI can’t do for you. The assistant has no read on whether the comment landed sharply, and no stake in how you reply. You’re the one with the relationship to maintain and the spec to defend.

🧰 Where AI Earns Its Keep — and Where It Doesn’t

✅ Lean on AI 🚫 Keep both hands on the wheel
Triaging large comment batches — first-pass classification with one-line reasons Deciding which bucket a comment belongs in — the call is yours, the assistant suggests
Drafting diff-style alternatives — two options, named trade-offs, your pick Writing comment-thread replies — the polish gives you away in two threads
Translating for a new audience — engineering → leadership → sales, same facts Rewriting the problem statement on the fly — drift risk, every single time
Late-round sanity checks — “identify drift from the original problem statement” Feeding the whole batch + spec, asking for a “revised spec” — produces invisible drift

📅 Trickle Pattern: The Daily Iteration Window

The daily iteration window pattern While the spec is open for review: · Comments accumulate during the day — don’t respond · 20–30 min late-afternoon window: run the mini sequence capture → classify → must-fix urgent → queue rest → log · Then close the laptop until tomorrow Exception: must-fix comments that are actively misleading the conversation get fixed inside an hour, with a one-line slack heads-up to the people who saw the original. Why it works: · Prevents comment-by-comment edit drift · Reviewers know the spec changes daily, not in real time · No ghost edits appearing while they’re still typing

🔄 Round 1, 2, 3 — What Changes

  1. Round 1 — the loud feedback

    Load-bearing corrections, framing shifts, scope negotiations. AI earns its keep on triage and drafting alternatives. Run the full capture-classify-draft-edit-log sequence.

  2. Round 2 — the comments narrow

    Tighter, more specific, often about wording rather than substance. The bar for “must-fix” rises — what was must-fix in round one is should-consider now. Move the bar deliberately.

  3. Round 3 — mostly polishing

    Diff prompts come back with options that all read as “equivalent, slightly differently phrased.” That’s the signal you’ve hit diminishing returns. AI shifts from co-author to proofreader: “identify any drift from the original problem statement” or “list inline annotations that have become stale.”

  4. Knowing when to stop

    Self-check before each new round: “What specifically would have to change for this round to be worth doing?” If the answer is “not much, but I want one more pass,” declare the spec frozen, push the version, let engineering start.

Specs don’t need to be perfect; they need to be good enough to build against. Knowing when to stop iterating is part of staying in the driver’s seat. The assistant will happily keep refining until you tell it to stop. You’re the one who knows the marginal value of round four is now lower than the cost of the delay.

🛑 The “Stop the World” Comment

🧱
Tactical comment
Lives inside the spec’s frame — sharper AC, missed edge case, clearer phrasing. Patches into the iteration loop normally.
🌋
Structural comment
Challenges the frame itself — the problem statement, the user, whether this work should happen at all. Argues upstream of what the spec is doing.
⏸️
The right response
Stop iteration. Don’t patch. Reply: “This is bigger than an iteration. Can we find 30 minutes to talk through the implications?” Have the conversation. Decide together.
📢
Update the log loudly
“v1.0 paused 2026-05-02 after K.’s structural comment on permissions. v2.0 underway with revised scope.” Other reviewers see the pause — not silence.

AI can’t spot stop-the-world comments for you — they require domain knowledge or organizational context the assistant doesn’t have. Recognizing one when it arrives, and respecting it instead of patching past it, is part of the craft. Patching a structural comment with diff prompts produces a spec that’s patched on top of a broken foundation — the patches read fine in isolation; the document collapses on close inspection.

🔒 Versioning & the Freeze Signal

Status line at the top of the spec Status: open for review through 2026-05-03 EOD; freezes for sprint planning Tuesday 2026-05-05. Three pieces of info: · It’s open · Here’s the deadline · Here’s what happens after Pair with version labels in the decision log: v1.0 — frozen for sprint planning v1.1 — correction to AC 3 (logged with rationale) v1.2 — deferred decision moved to log Frozen ≠ immutable. Frozen = engineering can build against it; further changes go through a different process, not open-comment iteration.

📒 The Decision Log Earns Its Keep

Decision log — after iteration round 1 2026-05-01: Updated AC 3 to support workspace-level expiry override (default 7d, max 30d). Reason: K.’s comment about enterprise quarterly batches; security cap kept tight. Open question added: SOC 2 upper-bound review. 2026-05-01: Reframed problem statement for clarity. No fact change — same metrics, same scope, tighter prose. Reason: M.’s comment. 2026-04-30: Removed “recursive permission inheritance” edge case — didn’t match actual behavior. 2026-04-30: Added [ASSUMPTION] marker to spam-attribution claim. 2026-04-30: Corrected permissions model description. 2026-04-26: Initial draft. AI-assisted; sections marked [DRAFTED-WITH-AI] reviewed and edited by me. Six lines tell the story of two days. Skipping the log is the single largest cause of through-line drift.

👃 The Smell Test for Iteration

The tell. You re-read the spec after a round of edits and feel a small disorientation — the words are different, the AC are different, but you can’t quite point to what shifted. The decision log has three entries; the spec feels like it has six entries’ worth of changes.

The check. Open the original problem statement and the current one in two windows. Read them aloud. Do they describe the same problem? Same user? Is the success criterion still the metric you’d defend at a quarterly review? If any answer wobbles, the through-line moved while you weren’t looking.

The fix. Reconcile out loud, in the log. Either: “through-line updated — new problem statement reflects the workflow K. surfaced; old framing was too narrow,” and live with it. Or: “reverted problem-statement edits made during iteration round 1 — the original framing still holds,” and restore it. Both are valid. Silent drift is what isn’t.

What’s yours: the through-line. Comments will move things around. AC will get sharper; scope will narrow or stretch; metrics will get re-cut; phrases will tighten. What does not move — what cannot move without you saying so explicitly — is the original problem you set out to solve, the user you said this was for, and the call you made about the shape of the answer. Those three are the spec’s through-line, and they’re yours. If a round of feedback ends with one of them subtly shifted and no decision log entry recording the shift, stop and reconcile. Either the through-line should have changed (log it loudly) or it shouldn’t have (revert the drift). Iteration is supposed to sharpen the spec around its center, not redraw the center.

📚 Key Terms

Capture-classify-draft-edit-log
The five-step iteration sequence. Slow down for ten minutes (capture + classify) before speeding up (draft + edit + log).
The three buckets
Must-fix (blocks shipping), should-consider (real point, weighed against the spec), out-of-scope (different spec). Every comment lands in exactly one.
Diff prompt
“Here’s the section, here’s the comment, propose two alternative edits as a before/after diff with trade-offs.” Forces change to be visible and evaluable.
30%-rule
Below 30% changes → patch in place. Above 30% → rewrite the section. Above the threshold, patches start reading worse than rewrites.
Through-line
The original problem, the original user, the call you made about the shape of the answer. The spec’s center. Yours, immovable without explicit logging.
Decision log
One dated line per change at the top of the spec, in chronological order. The receipt that prevents through-line drift.
Daily iteration window
20–30 minutes late afternoon while the spec is open. Comments accumulate during the day; you process the batch in the window. Prevents comment-by-comment drift.
Stop-the-world comment
A structural comment that challenges the frame itself — problem, user, whether this work should happen. Don’t patch; pause and have a conversation.
Voice drift
Across three rounds of accepting AI’s “cleanest” phrasing, the spec tilts toward the assistant’s register and away from yours. Voice pass: rewrite two paragraphs in your own words to anchor the rest.
Two-sentence rule (for replies)
Sentence 1: acknowledge the specific point. Sentence 2: state your call. AI-generated essay-replies signal you didn’t actually engage. Longer answer needed? — schedule a 15-minute call.
Frozen
Engineering can build against it; further changes go through a different process. Not immutable — just no longer in open-comment iteration. Signal explicitly with a status line.
Deferred decisions
Open questions that didn’t resolve before freeze, captured in a section near the top with default behavior + a date. Engineering can build; security/legal can review on their own timeline; nobody is blocked.
Silent drift
The through-line shifted across edits but no decision log entry recorded the shift. The single largest failure mode of iteration.

❓ Quick Quiz

What’s the five-step iteration sequence?

Capture → classify → draft → edit → log. Capture every comment verbatim before changing anything (5 min). Classify into must-fix / should-consider / out-of-scope. Draft alternatives with diff prompts. Edit in your own voice. Log each change. The order matters — capturing before classifying surfaces contradictions; classifying before drafting prevents comment-by-comment drift; logging keeps the through-line auditable.

What are the three buckets, and how does each get handled?

Must-fix (blocks shipping — factual error, contradicting AC) → work in one batch first; one log line + the change. Should-consider (real point, depends on context the commenter doesn’t see) → diff prompt with two alternatives + trade-offs; sometimes change, sometimes a respectful no. Out-of-scope (different spec/release/problem) → acknowledge, log as follow-up, explicitly place outside this spec.

Why is the diff prompt better than “rewrite this section”?

Because it forces the change to be visible. A rewrite produces new prose you compare slowly, side-by-side. The diff prompt highlights exactly what’s shifting, which is what you actually need to evaluate. The lazy version of iteration accepts new prose because it sounds reasonable; the rigorous version reads the diff and asks “is this single change worth the trade?” Bonus: when you ask for one-line rationales per changed sentence, those become first drafts of the decision log.

What’s the 30%-rule, and why does the threshold exist?

If less than ~30% of a section needs to change, patch in place — faster, preserves voice. If more than ~30%, rewrite the section — cleaner, single voice. The threshold matters because patches accumulate: by round three, a heavily patched section has had three rounds of additions stitched in, and the prose starts to show its seams (your voice + assistant’s voice + assistant’s second voice). A clean rewrite resets it.

What does AI do well in iteration, and what should stay yours?

AI lean-in: triaging large comment batches (first-pass classification with reasons), drafting diff-style alternatives, translating for new audiences, late-round sanity checks. Stays yours: deciding which bucket a comment belongs in, writing comment-thread replies (the polish gives you away), rewriting the problem statement on the fly (drift risk), and the “feed the whole batch + spec, ask for a revised spec” anti-pattern (invisible drift).

What’s the smell test for iteration?

The tell: you re-read the spec after a round of edits and feel a small disorientation — the words are different, but you can’t point to what shifted; the log has three entries but the spec feels like six. The check: open the original problem statement and the current one in two windows; read them aloud. Same problem? Same user? Same defendable success criterion? If any answer wobbles, the through-line moved while you weren’t looking. The fix: reconcile out loud in the log — either accept the new through-line and log it, or revert the drift.

How do you tell a stop-the-world comment from a tactical one?

A tactical comment lives inside the spec’s frame — AC, edge cases, scope, metrics. A structural / stop-the-world comment argues upstream of what the spec is doing — it challenges the problem statement, the user, or whether this work should happen at all. The right response: stop the iteration loop. Reply “this is bigger than an iteration; can we find 30 minutes to talk?” Have the conversation. Decide whether the spec rewrites, re-scopes, or stands. Update the log loudly so other reviewers see the pause.

How do you handle comments when they trickle in over a week?

Agree with yourself on a daily iteration window — 20–30 minutes, late afternoon, every day the spec is open. Comments accumulate during the day; you don’t respond when they land. At the window, run the mini sequence on whatever arrived since yesterday. Exception: must-fix comments that are actively misleading the conversation get fixed inside an hour with a one-line slack heads-up. Everything else waits for the daily window. The discipline costs nothing; the trust margin is real (reviewers see a predictable cadence, not ghost edits).

How do you serve engineering, leadership, and sales without writing three specs?

Write one spec well, then use AI as a translator for briefings. Engineering wants precision; leadership wants outcome; sales wants narrative. Same facts, different framing. Translations live in slack messages or emails; the spec stays the source of truth. Always re-read against the source — AI translation has a small but consistent failure mode of dropping qualifiers, rounding numbers, or softening constraints. “Under 15%” ↔ “fewer than two in ten” is a translation; “close to 10%” is a fact change in disguise.

What’s the signal that you’ve gone one iteration round too far?

The diff prompts come back with options that all read as “equivalent, slightly differently phrased.” The assistant isn’t producing alternatives because there aren’t meaningfully different alternatives left. Self-check before each new round: “What specifically would have to change in the spec for this round to be worth doing?” If the answer is “not much, but I want one more pass,” the round isn’t worth doing. Declare the spec frozen, log the freeze decision, push the version, let engineering start. You can always iterate again after engineering surfaces something concrete.

Key Takeaways

  • Slow down for ten minutes before speeding up. Capture all comments verbatim before changing anything — surfaces the full surface area and any contradictions.
  • The five-step loop: capture → classify → draft → edit → log. The order matters; skipping steps is what produces drift.
  • Three buckets — must-fix, should-consider, out-of-scope. Every comment lands in exactly one. Classification is fast (~1 minute per comment); don’t agonize.
  • Work the must-fix bucket end-to-end first, in one batch — corrections to facts, not negotiations of taste. Then the should-considers sit on a clean baseline.
  • The diff prompt: “here’s the section, here’s the comment, propose two alternatives as a before/after diff with trade-offs.” Two outputs, one pass.
  • 30%-rule: under 30% changes → patch in place; over 30% → rewrite. Patches accumulate; rewrites reset the section to a clean state.
  • Same facts, three audiences. Engineering wants precision; leadership wants outcome; sales wants narrative. One spec; AI as translator. Translations live in messages, not the spec.
  • Always re-read the translation against the source — AI translation has a quiet failure mode of dropping qualifiers, rounding numbers, softening constraints.
  • Reading your own reaction matters. Comments that land sharply trigger over-correction or defensiveness. Leave them alone for an hour; come back and decide on the merits.
  • Trickle pattern: a daily 20–30 minute iteration window prevents comment-by-comment drift. Reviewers get a predictable cadence; you keep the through-line.
  • Round 1 = loud feedback (triage + draft alternatives). Round 2 = comments narrow (the must-fix bar rises). Round 3 = mostly polishing (AI shifts to proofreader).
  • Knowing when to stop: “What specifically would have to change for this round to be worth doing?” If the answer is “not much,” freeze and ship.
  • Stop-the-world comments challenge the frame itself — problem, user, whether the work should happen. Don’t patch; pause and have a conversation. Update the log loudly.
  • The two-sentence rule for replies: acknowledge the point, state your call. AI-generated essay-replies signal you didn’t engage. Longer answer needed? Schedule a call.
  • Multi-stakeholder contradictions: don’t pick a side — surface the contradiction explicitly so the right people can resolve it. Then log + reply.
  • Voice drift sneaks in across rounds. Voice pass before freeze: rewrite two paragraphs in your own words. Two paragraphs of authorial voice anchor the rest.
  • Decision log = the receipt. One dated line per change. Skipping it is the single largest cause of through-line drift.
  • Versioning: a status line at the top of the spec (open through, freezes when) plus version labels in the log. Frozen ≠ immutable; just no longer in open-comment iteration.
  • Open-questions block evolves: every answered question moves to a one-line decision log entry. Unanswered questions go to a deferred-decisions section with default behavior.
  • AI lean-in: triage, diff drafts, translation, late-round sanity checks. Hands on the wheel: bucket calls, comment replies, problem-statement edits, “rewrite the whole spec” prompts.
  • The smell test: silent drift — the words are different, the through-line wobbled, no log entry. Reconcile out loud or revert.
