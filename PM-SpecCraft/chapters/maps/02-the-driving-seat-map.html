Chapter 2

The Driving Seat: 5 Decisions Only You Make

Memory Map
🪪

The Big Idea

Before you open any AI tool, name the call you’re about to make. AI is brilliant at producing the route — you name the destination. Five decisions show up in every spec, and none of them belong to the model.

🚦 The Five Decisions

1. Worth solving?
→
2. Who is it for?
→
3. What’s out?
→
4. Success = ?
→
5. Ship when?

A two-sentence pass on each beats a thirty-minute pass on one and a blank on the other four.

🧭 What AI Can Do vs. What’s Yours

Decision AI is great at… Yours to decide
1. Worth solving? Listing 10 plausible candidates; comparing on stated criteria Which one earns a quarter of your team’s time
2. Who is the user? Generating clean, plausible segments by the dozen Which segments are real in your data, with your customers
3. What’s out? Listing adjacent features all day (often expanding scope) Saying no — the part that costs you something
4. Success = ? Enumerating a dozen sensible metric candidates The one or two metrics you’ll sign your name to
5. Ship when? Listing risks and unmade decisions Weighing inputs that don’t live in any document

🃏 The Five-Decision Card

🎯
1. Worth solving?
Why this problem, why now, over the others on your backlog. The AI lists; only you carry the consequences.
👤
2. Who is the user?
A specific cohort — not “users.” Collapse AI segments against real tickets, calls, and usage data.
✂️
3. What’s in & out?
Non-goals are load-bearing. Write them before letting AI fill anything in.
📈
4. Success = ?
The metric you’ll defend in every review. AI enumerates; you read the room and pick.
🚢
5. Ship when?
Risk × urgency × capacity × the human variables AI can’t see. The release call is yours, every time.
🪜
The spine
Every later chapter sits on top of these five. The decisions are the chassis; techniques tune the rest of the car.

🎚️ Three Patterns: AI as Sounding Board

⚖️ Steel-man both sides
🔍 List what you’re missing
AI returns material; you keep the call
🥊 Strongest counter-argument
❌ Never “what should we do?”

“Make the case for and against, then I’ll decide” — not “what do you think we should do?”

🚨 Tells of a Soft Decision

📝
Paragraph, not sentence
You can describe the problem in a paragraph but not in one sentence.
💼
Job title, not person
Your “user” is a role, not someone you’ve spoken to in 60 days.
📋
No non-goals
Your scope description has only goals — nothing explicitly out.
😶
Vague metric
“Users will be happier” with no number, baseline, or window.
📅
No ship date
You can’t name when this would ship if it had to ship today.
🎰
Asking AI for “the answer”
You’re prompting for a verdict, not material to think with.

🔁 The Healthy Rhythm

  1. Name the decision before prompting

    One sentence: “I’m working Decision 4 — success metric for in-product guides.”

  2. Ask AI for material, not verdicts

    Steel-man both sides. List what you’re missing. Argue against your call.

  3. Push back when the AI sounds too neat

    If it offers a draft conclusion, ask for the strongest counter-case.

  4. Make the call in your own voice

    Rewrite the AI’s phrasing in language you’d defend in review.

  5. Write the decision down

    Even one sentence per decision, on paper, so the AI fills detail around real anchors.

📒 Key Terms

The five decisions
Worth solving / who’s the user / what’s out / success metric / ship date. The irreducible PM calls.
Sounding board
Using AI to surface considerations, alternatives, and counter-arguments — without delegating the call itself.
Non-goals
Explicit list of what this release will not do. Each one is a future argument settled in advance.
Steel-man
Asking AI to make the strongest possible case for an option, on the option’s own terms.
Gap-find
Prompting: “what would a senior PM ask me about this that I haven’t addressed?” Surfaces 5–8 candidates, 1–2 will sharpen.
Counter-argue
Asking AI to argue sharply against a call you’ve made. If the counter doesn’t move you, your decision was solid.
Inherited decision
A call already made by leadership or commitment. Your job shifts from making the decision to making it legible.
Local metric
The spec-level version of an org-level metric — the next mile-marker, not the final destination.

📐 Two Versions of Decision 1

Aspect Soft (description with a solution attached) Sharp (real decision)
User “New users” Second-invited users (largest dropping cohort)
Evidence None named ~14 tickets/wk, 2 named deals/month, funnel data
Alternative considered None Navigation rework — ruled out for now (a quarter vs. 2 weeks)
Confirmed with Implicit (the AI?) Support lead, sales lead, data team
Result Spec fights you in every section Spec almost writes itself once drafting starts

❓ Quick Quiz

What are the five decisions only you make?

(1) Is this problem worth solving? (2) Who is the user? (3) What’s in scope and what’s out? (4) What does success look like? (5) When is it good enough to ship?

Why can’t the AI make Decision 5 for you?

Almost none of the inputs live anywhere the AI can read — team morale this week, two engineers out Monday, the customer threatening to churn, the stuck legal review, the competitor who launched yesterday. Ship calls weigh variables that aren’t in any document.

What’s the difference between “what do you think we should do?” and “make the case for and against”?

The first prompt invites the model to recommend; the second invites it to assist your judgment. Same model, very different output — and very different ownership of the decision.

Why are non-goals load-bearing?

They tell engineers what to build and what to leave alone, tell stakeholders what won’t ship, and they’re what you point to in week six when someone says “wait, doesn’t this also need imports?” Each non-goal is a future argument settled in advance.

Do the five decisions still apply when leadership has already made some of them?

Yes — and they apply harder. With inherited decisions, your job shifts from making them to making them legible: who decided, when, on what evidence, and what would change the call. AI can help draft the “decision context” paragraph.

What’s the “decisions before prompts” principle?

Before opening any AI tool to draft, expand, or summarize, name in one sentence what call you’re about to make. AI amplifies whatever you bring it — sharp decisions in, sharp spec out; soft decisions in, polished spec nobody can ship from.

🧪 Try It: The Five-Decision Drill

  1. Pick one product question on your plate

    A feature, a bug investigation, a small initiative — anything spec-shaped this week.

  2. Write one sentence per decision — on paper

    Before opening any AI tool: problem, user, scope/non-goals, metric, ship date.

  3. Pick the softest of the five

    The one you feel least solid on. That’s where AI’s leverage is highest.

  4. Stress-test with AI

    Steel-man, gap-find, or counter-argue. Use Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — whichever you have.

  5. Update your sentence in your own voice

    Compare with your usual flow. Were you the author of the spec, or the editor of the AI’s spec?

Key Takeaways

  • Five decisions stay yours: worth solving / who’s the user / what’s out / success metric / ship date.
  • Name the decision before opening the AI — you’re asking for directions, you’d better know your destination.
  • AI amplifies whatever you bring it: sharp decisions in, sharp spec out; soft decisions in, polished spec nobody can ship from.
  • Three sounding-board patterns: steel-man both sides, list what you’re missing, write the strongest counter-argument.
  • Non-goals are load-bearing — write them first, before letting AI fill anything in.
  • A two-sentence pass on each of the five beats a thirty-minute pass on one.
  • When decisions are inherited, your job is to make them legible, not to remake them.
  • If you can’t name which decision you’re working on, the AI’s draft is decoration. Close the chat. Name it. Go back.
