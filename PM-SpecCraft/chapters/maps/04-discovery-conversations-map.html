Chapter 4

Discovery Conversations with AI

Memory Map
🛣️

The Big Idea

Discovery with AI is two moves, in order: the model widens, you ground. Ask for twelve angles, group them into three families with shared assumptions, then bring in one piece of customer signal to kill a family. AI generates breadth in two minutes; humans bring the truth that makes breadth useful.

🔁 The Discovery Loop — Five Rounds

  1. Breadth

    “List 12 different shapes a fix could take. No two ideas the same flavor. One line each. No preference among them.”

  2. Families

    “Group these into 3 families — each family shares an underlying bet about why the problem exists. Name each bet in one sentence.”

  3. Grounding

    Bring real signal — funnel data, ticket patterns, a usage slice. One number can kill a whole family in one move.

  4. Sharpen

    “For each surviving family, name the strongest version, the weakest version, and the central assumption it rests on.”

  5. Cheapest test

    “Given two surviving families with different assumptions, what’s the cheapest test that distinguishes them? Sample size, timeline.”

Total time: ~15 minutes. Output: one chosen candidate, one assumption, one cheap test.

🪜 The Two Halves — Both Required

🌐 Widen
AI generates 12 candidates
Cheap, fast, generative
→
🎯 Narrow
Humans ground & choose
Customer signal, judgment

Half-1 alone = noise. Half-2 alone = the same three options your team always brings.

📐 Hurried vs Done-Well Prompts

Aspect Hurried Done-well
Prompt shape “What should we do about X?” “Don’t propose. List 12 distinct shapes. No preference.”
Output One recommendation to argue against A menu you can walk through
Conversation that follows 20 minutes editing AI’s premature pick 20 minutes choosing among real alternatives
What you gain Speed to a plausible answer The chance to find a better one two doors down

👥 What AI Can & Cannot Do

Move AI is great at AI cannot do
Segments Sketch 8 plausible cohorts in 30 seconds Tell you which match real accounts on your platform
Jobs-to-be-done List candidate jobs across the product Confirm which jobs your users actually have
Hypotheses Rewrite fuzzy claims as testable ones Tell you which hypothesis is the load-bearing one
Alternative shapes Draft a 5-row table of solutions/cost/risk Estimate your team’s real cost or roadmap fit
Customer voice Prepare interview questions, summarize transcripts Hear the pause in a sales call. Ever.

📊 The Alternative-Solution Table

🪧
Shape
One line per candidate — concrete enough to picture.
💰
Cost (S/M/L)
AI drafts; you replace with real estimates from your stack and team.
⚠️
Biggest risk
The one thing that could break this option. Sharpened against what engineers actually worry about.
🎯
Fit (optional)
Add only when you’ve grounded each option against real cohort data.

🧪 Hypothesis Hygiene

Vague (fails) Testable (passes)
Form “Users will appreciate the upgrade flow.” “Free-tier users who hit the project-count limit will convert to paid at >15% within 14 days.”
Subject “Users” (which ones?) Free-tier users who hit a specific limit
Claim “Appreciate” (unobservable) Convert to paid (countable)
Falsification None <15% conversion in 14 days

→
Strategic bet

Match the depth of the loop to the cost of the work. Tactical depth on a strategic bet is the more dangerous mistake.

🚧 When the Discovery Stalls

🤷
Stall 1: Every angle feels equal
Not a prompt problem — a signal problem. Pull tickets, watch sessions, ask sales for top complaints. Then back to the loop.
🔁
Stall 2: Variations of the same idea
“Those are too similar. Give me 5 candidates that sit further apart on the design space.” Or switch tools — different defaults break ruts.
🎯
Stall 3: Wrong problem found
Candidates keep solving something adjacent. Don’t override — go back to chapter 3 territory. The loop is telling you the problem is mis-stated.

🚪 When to Leave the Chat

🪞 AI is paraphrasing itself → schedule a call
❓ Next question is “what would the user think?” → book interviews
Close the tab. The next move is human.
🥊 You’re asking AI to endorse your pre-pick → have the disagreement directly
🌀 New context isn’t arriving → go gather some

✅ Three Signals You’ve Explored Enough

  1. You can name a candidate you almost picked

    Real discovery produces close calls. If your alternatives feel like “the obvious one and weak straw-men,” widen again.

  2. You can articulate the central assumption

    Not “users will like this.” Something falsifiable: “we’re betting empty-state, not friction, causes the bounce.”

  3. You can name one cheap experiment that would falsify it

    If nothing in the next 2 weeks could prove your option wrong, you may be choosing on taste — own that, don’t hide it.

🚨 Smell Tests

🪞
Product-swap test
If you can drop the same paragraph into a competitor’s deck, the discovery output is too generic. Real discovery has high proper-noun count.
👤
Real account check
Can’t name a single account, ticket, or usage pattern that matches the segment? Hypothesis, not finding. Tag it.
📒
Hypothesis log gap
Can’t name 3 hypotheses confirmed/killed in the last quarter? You’re running discovery without a memory.
🧠
Generic-dialect check
“Leverage,” “robust,” “seamless,” “delight” — interrogate each: leverage how, robust against what, seamless with which flow?

📦 Three Pieces of Context Before Breadth

🏢
Product shape
One paragraph: who you serve, the core flow, your constraints. Frames every candidate.
🔪
Sharpened problem
The four-field statement from chapter 3. Anchors the breadth-first prompt.
🚫
What we’ve already tried
A short list of options that didn’t work — not to avoid them, but to know which adjacent shapes are interesting.

~90 seconds to paste. Dramatic improvement in candidate quality.

🤝 Sharing Discovery Output

  1. Capture the rejected, not just the chosen

    The 9 you didn’t pick = next quarter’s “did we consider X?” answer + a paper trail showing the chosen option won on merit.

  2. Tag every claim with grounding

    (observed) · (plausible, untested) · (speculative). Reviewers immediately know what to push on.

  3. Walk it past one engineer or designer

    5 minutes catches the wild cost estimate, the dependency you don’t own, the family already tried. Cheapest pressure-test there is.

📒 Key Terms

Breadth-first prompt
A prompt with a target number (“list 12”) that forces the AI past the obvious three. The single biggest discovery skill.
Family
A group of candidates that share an underlying bet about why the problem exists. Three families typically map a problem space.
Grounding
The move of bringing real customer signal — tickets, sessions, calls, data — against AI candidates to kill the ones that don’t apply.
Hypothesis log
A running list of assumptions with status (open / confirmed / killed) and how-we’d-know. Compounds across discovery sessions; defends against drift.
Alternative-solution table
5 rows × 3 columns: shape / cost / biggest risk. Makes choices comparable. Shifts the conversation from “what?” to “why this not that?”
Generic dialect
The AI’s default language when a prompt is vague or the topic is unfamiliar — words that sound valid but commit to nothing.
Borrowed pattern
A solution shape from an adjacent product that fits your problem’s shape. Inspiration, not blueprint — translation back to your context is yours.
Discovery handoff
What the spec carries forward from discovery: chosen candidate + central assumption + cheap experiment. Anything less, the spec starts with a question.

❓ Quick Quiz

What does a breadth-first prompt do that a narrow prompt doesn’t?

It produces a menu instead of a recommendation. A narrow prompt gives you the AI’s favorite idea wrapped in three paragraphs, and you spend the conversation editing it. A breadth-first prompt gives you 12 distinct shapes; you pick which 3 are worth thinking harder about.

Why isn’t generating 12 candidates and stopping there enough?

That’s only half the loop. The high-leverage half is structure — grouping candidates into families, naming the assumption each family rests on, designing the cheap test that would distinguish them. Without structure, breadth becomes noise.

What three things must a discovery session produce to feed the PRD?

(1) A chosen candidate. (2) The central assumption the candidate rests on. (3) The cheap experiment (or named taste call) that justifies committing. Anything less, and the spec opens with a question instead of an answer.

The AI keeps giving you variations of the same three ideas. What now?

Push back: “those are too similar; give me five candidates that sit further apart on the design space.” If that doesn’t work, switch tools — different models have different default flavors, and a switch from ChatGPT to Claude (or back) often breaks the rut.

Three signals it’s time to leave the chat?

(1) The AI is paraphrasing itself — new context isn’t arriving. (2) Your next question is “what would the user think?” — book the interview. (3) You’re asking the AI to endorse the option you already prefer — have the disagreement directly.

Why is “the AI cannot replace customer voice” the chapter’s anchor?

AI can sketch segments, list jobs, draft alternatives, pressure-test ideas. It cannot hear the pause in a sales call, feel the texture of a 14-message support thread, or notice that the user keeps rephrasing because the original question wasn’t what they meant. Those signals are the difference between fits and plausible.

Key Takeaways

  • Discovery = AI widens, you ground. Both halves are required; either alone is broken.
  • Breadth-first prompts (“list 12”) produce a menu; narrow prompts produce a recommendation you have to argue against.
  • The canonical loop: breadth → families → grounding → sharpen → cheapest test. ~15 minutes; produces a candidate, an assumption, a test.
  • Three pieces of context before breadth (product shape, sharpened problem, what we’ve tried) dramatically improve candidate quality.
  • Tag every discovery claim: (observed) · (plausible, untested) · (speculative). Honesty compounds.
  • Match depth to the bet: tactical (5 angles), sprint (12 / 3 families / 1 test), strategic (multi-session). Tactical depth on strategic bets is the worse mistake.
  • Stalls are signals. The next move is usually human — a data pull, a customer call, a teammate conversation.
  • Three signals to leave the chat: AI paraphrasing itself, “what would the user think?”, asking AI to endorse a pre-pick.
  • A hypothesis log turns each discovery session into compounding institutional memory — ten minutes a week, dramatic payoff over a quarter.
  • What carries to the spec: chosen candidate + central assumption + cheap experiment. Nothing less.
