Chapter 7

Pressure-Testing Your Spec

Memory Map
🛡️

The Big Idea

Pressure-testing is the cheapest review pass you’ll ever run — private, fast, no calendars, no ego. You wear six different reviewer hats with AI’s help, find the holes while the spec is still cheap to change, and walk into engineering review with the holes you already found, the ones you decided to leave open, and the reasons. That meeting runs short.

🎭 What Pressure-Testing Is — and Isn’t

Activity Asks Use it for
✅ Pressure-testing “Will this, as written, survive contact with team and user?” Finding holes, naming assumptions, listing risks
❌ Polishing “Is the prose tight?” Different pass, different prompt — later
❌ Scope review “Is this the right thing to build?” Already answered before pressure-testing starts

Frame it explicitly: “Assume the decision to build this has been made. Don’t question scope. Find what’s missing, risky, or underspecified about the spec as written.”

🎩 The Cast of Six Reviewer Hats

🛠️
Senior engineer
State machines, race conditions, missing error handling, scope hidden behind innocent verbs (“sync”, “refresh”, “just”).
🎨
Designer
Empty states, error states, mid-flow interruptions, mobile vs. desktop, accessibility gaps.
⚖️
Legal & compliance
Personal data flows, cross-tenant leakage, consent prompts, retention rules, ToS implications.
🎧
Customer support
Unrecoverable states, who picks up the phone, what the macro will say, how to undo a wrong action.
🔐
Security
Token expiry, permission escalation, replay risk, who sees what, what happens when an account is compromised.
💰
Finance & ops
Billing edges, refund paths, seat-count drift, currency rounding, what shows up on the invoice.

Pick the three or four hats that map to your domain — not all six. Each role is a lens, not a person; even if your team has no legal reviewer, the lens still finds things, because it’s a different question shape.

🧪 The Four-Part Red-Team Prompt

🎭 Clear role
“You’re a senior backend engineer at a B2B SaaS…”
→
🎯 Clear job
Not “review this” — specific verbs & constraints
→
📄 The spec
Pasted or attached, with context paragraph
→
📋 Output shape
3 lists: MISSING / RISKY / UNDERSPECIFIED. Quote the line.
Prompt Output shape Result
❌ “Review this spec and tell me what’s wrong” Free-form prose Polite, generic, blends “add metrics” with “consider perf”. Not actionable.
✅ Role + job + 3-list output + “quote the line” Three labelled lists, line-traceable Specific findings tied to specific lines. Convert to edits almost mechanically.

The single instruction that earns its keep: “Quote the line you’re reacting to.” Turns vague feedback into traceable feedback — and catches hallucinated findings (no line = probably invented).

🎬 The Worked Sequence — ~25 Minutes, 4 Hats

  1. Engineer hat

    Find missing rate limits, double-invite behavior, what happens for new sign-ups via invite, undefined revoke semantics.

  2. Legal & compliance hat

    Find missing consent, retention policy, disclosure-of-affiliation risk in misdirected invites, vague audit logs.

  3. Customer support hat

    Find “I never got the email” story, sole-admin lockout, single-use link friction, “removed member” UX.

  4. Security hat

    Find token entropy gap, anomalous-IP behavior, phishing-payload shape, mailbox-takeover risk, audit of failed attempts.

  5. Combine & rank

    “Dedupe across the four passes. Top eight findings ranked by how badly it hurts if we ship without addressing it.” That ranked list is the next pass’s input.

🔍 The Other Three Power Prompts

🕵️
Implicit-assumption extractor
“List every assumption this spec is making, stated or unstated. Number them. Say which are explicit vs inferred.” AI is unusually good at this — it has no shared context with you, so it has to make every assumption explicit just to read the spec.
🧓
“Senior PM in your job”
“You’re a senior PM with ten years in this domain. Five questions you’d ask me in a 1:1 before signing off.” Surprises in two ways: questions probe motivation, scope, second-order effects — and 2-3 are ones you’ve been quietly avoiding.
🗺️
Gap matrix
Scope rows × reviewer columns. Checkmark or one-word note in each cell. Empty cells = unaddressed concerns. Great artifact to share with engineering: “here’s what we covered, here’s what we explicitly chose not to.”

📊 The Risk Register — Four Columns, Five Rows

📝 Assumption / Risk
📈 Likelihood (Low / Med / High)
Risk Register
💥 Impact (Low / Med / High)
🩹 Mitigation (path forward)

Five rows is plenty. The discipline isn’t completeness — it’s naming the few risks that, if they materialize, you want a paper trail showing you saw them coming. Mitigation column moves the eng review from “is this a risk?” to “is this the right mitigation?”

🚦 Triage: Three Buckets for Every Finding

🛠️ Act on this release
Edit the spec now, in the same session
→
📌 Defer with open-question note
Add to the open-questions list at the top
→
🙅 Decline (with reason)
One sentence in your notes — the proof triage happened

What’s yours: triage, not generation. AI will produce findings forever — it will keep playing reviewer hats until you stop asking. None of that is the work. The work is deciding which findings warrant a spec edit this release. That call is grounded in things AI doesn’t have: your release date, your team’s capacity, the conversation you had with sales last Tuesday. A reviewer pass that produces fifty findings and zero decisions hasn’t pressure-tested anything — it’s just a longer to-do list.

👃 Four Common Stumbles — Named Without Blame

📏
Uniform-confidence findings
All twelve items in the same authoritative tone, blocker and typo alike. Fix: ask for ranking by impact, and for which findings the model is least sure about.
👻
Invented reviewer concerns
A designer flag about a screen the spec never mentioned. Fix: the quote-the-line rule catches most. If the model can’t cite a line, drop the finding.
🌀
Reviewer drift
By the third hat, the engineer is giving legal-flavored answers. Fix: fresh thread per hat, or re-anchor the role at the top of each new prompt.
📜
Length-as-effort
Eighteen findings looks more thorough than four — usually isn’t. Fix: cap at six per hat, “only ones you’d defend in a review”. Average quality climbs.

🎚️ Calibrating Pickiness

When Calibration line Expect
Early in a draft “Be the pickiest version of this reviewer. Flag anything you’d push back on, even small things.” ~2× findings; you’ll drop most. Goal: don’t miss a category.
Late in a draft, near deadline “Only flag issues that would change the build, the timeline, or the risk profile. Skip stylistic and minor issues entirely.” 4–6 findings worth reading at 11pm.

🤔 When the Spec Passes Too Easily

✅ Good news: earlier passes (ch3 problem, ch5 PRD, ch6 AC) plugged the obvious holes
A clean pass means one of two things
⚠️ Other version: prompt too soft, model being polite, spec too short to grip

Diagnostic: paste the spec in a fresh thread and ask the model to describe it back to you in its own words. If the description has gaps or invents details, those gaps are the findings the original review didn’t surface.

⏱️ The Five-Minute Version

  1. Pick the one hat that maps to the biggest unknown

    Touches money → finance. Touches data → legal. State transitions → engineering.

  2. Run the standard four-part prompt

    Same shape as the long version — role, job, spec, three-list output.

  3. Add one calibration line

    “Limit yourself to the three findings most likely to embarrass us if we ship without addressing them.”

  4. Triage and edit. Done.

    Three findings, one hat, five minutes. Use as a default, not a corner-cut — small specs hide assumption-shaped bugs.

📒 Key Terms

Pressure-testing
The cheapest review pass — you adversarially review your own spec with AI’s help before sharing it with the team. Private; mistakes don’t cost trust here.
Reviewer hat
A specific role you ask the AI to play (engineer, designer, legal, support, security, finance). Each is a lens, not a person; the value is the question shape.
Four-part red-team prompt
Clear role + clear job + the spec + structured output shape (MISSING / RISKY / UNDERSPECIFIED, quote the line). Same shape across all hats.
Quote-the-line rule
“Quote the line you’re reacting to.” Turns vague feedback into traceable feedback — and catches hallucinated findings (no line cited = probably invented).
Implicit-assumption extractor
Prompt that lists every assumption the spec is making, stated or unstated. Reveals the gaps your own context filled in silently.
Risk register
Four-column table at the bottom of the spec: assumption/risk, likelihood, impact, mitigation. Five rows is plenty. Names the risks you saw coming.
Gap matrix
Scope rows × reviewer columns. Empty cells = unaddressed concerns — either fill them or consciously decline. Great handoff artifact.
Triage (act / defer / decline)
What you do with each finding. Triage is the work; generation is the AI’s job. A pass with no decisions isn’t pressure-testing.
Calibration line
One sentence at the top of the prompt that dials pickiness up (early in a draft) or down (near a deadline). Same template; different ratio of findings.
Reviewer drift
By hat 3-4, the model starts blending lenses — engineer answers sound legal, support answers sound design-y. Fix with fresh threads or role re-anchoring.
The five-minute version
One hat, three findings, “most likely to embarrass us” calibration. Use as the default for small specs, not a shortcut for big ones.

👃 Five Smell-Test Questions Before Sharing

  • Can you name the riskiest assumption in the spec out loud, in one sentence?
  • Did each reviewer hat produce at least one finding you ended up acting on?
  • Does the spec now name what’s explicitly out of scope, not just what’s in?
  • If a stranger read the open-questions list, would they understand what you’re still figuring out?
  • Could you point an engineer at a specific paragraph and say “this is the part I’m least sure about” without hesitating?

If any answer is “not yet”, you’ve found the next twenty minutes of work. None of the questions are about the spec being perfect; all of them are about you knowing where the spec stands.

❓ Quick Quiz

Why is pressure-testing “the cheapest review pass you’ll ever run”?

Because it’s entirely yours — no calendars to coordinate, no apologies for taking people’s time, no ego on the line because no one else has read the draft yet. Mistakes are private. You find them, you fix them, and the version that lands in your team’s inbox is already a draft or two better than what you’d have sent.

What are the four ingredients of a working red-team prompt?

(1) Clear role — “you’re a senior backend engineer at a B2B SaaS”; (2) clear job — not “review this”, but specific verbs and constraints; (3) the spec itself as input, with a paragraph of product context; (4) output shape — three labelled lists (MISSING, RISKY, UNDERSPECIFIED) with line quotes. Same four-part shape works in any tool.

What does the “quote the line” rule do for you?

Two things. It turns vague feedback into traceable feedback — converting findings into edits becomes almost mechanical. And it catches hallucinations — if the model can’t cite a specific line, the finding is probably invented and you can drop it without ceremony.

What’s the difference between pressure-testing and polishing?

Pressure-testing asks “will this, as written, survive contact with the team and the user?” Polishing asks “is the prose tight?” Asking a polish prompt to do a pressure-test gives you a beautifully worded spec with the same holes it had before. Different pass, different prompt — do polish later, separately.

Why are implicit-assumption prompts unusually effective?

Because the AI has no shared context with you — it has to make every assumption explicit just to read the spec. Most spec problems are assumptions in disguise: the line reads cleanly because you and the reader supplied the same unstated context, until someone supplies different context and the spec breaks. AI surfaces those silent assumptions for you.

What three buckets do you triage findings into?

Act on this release (edit the spec now, in the same session), defer with an open-question note (add to the list at the top of the spec), or decline with a one-sentence reason (in your notes — the proof triage happened). Triage is the work; AI generates findings forever.

How do you avoid “reviewer drift” across multiple hats?

By hat three or four, the model starts blending lenses — engineer answers come out legal-flavored, support answers sound like design. Fix with a fresh thread per hat, or re-anchor by repeating the role at the top of each new prompt. Costs nothing; preserves the lens.

What does a clean “passes too easily” result actually mean?

One of two things. Good version: earlier passes (sharp problem, careful PRD, triaged AC) plugged the obvious holes — this is real. Other version: prompt too soft, model being polite, spec too short to grip. Diagnostic: paste in a fresh thread and ask the model to describe the spec back to you in its own words. Gaps in the description are the findings.

What’s the five-minute version, and when do you use it?

One reviewer hat (the one mapping to the biggest unknown) + the standard four-part prompt + one calibration line: “limit yourself to the three findings most likely to embarrass us if we ship without addressing them.” Use as the default for small specs, not a shortcut for big ones — small specs hide more assumption-shaped bugs, not fewer.

Key Takeaways

  • Pressure-testing is private, fast, and yours — the cheapest review pass before engineering sees the spec.
  • It’s not polishing and not scope review. Frame it explicitly: “assume the build decision is made; find what’s missing/risky/underspecified about the spec as written.”
  • Six reviewer hats: engineer, designer, legal, support, security, finance. Pick the 3-4 that map to your domain.
  • Four-part red-team prompt: clear role + clear job + the spec + 3-list output (MISSING/RISKY/UNDERSPECIFIED). Same shape, swap the role.
  • Insist on the quote-the-line rule — converts vague feedback to traceable feedback, and catches hallucinations.
  • Run an implicit-assumption extractor — AI is unusually good at this because it has no shared context with you.
  • Add a 4-column risk register: assumption, likelihood, impact, mitigation. Five rows is plenty.
  • Run a “senior PM in your job” prompt — the questions probe motivation and second-order effects, and 2-3 are usually ones you’ve been quietly avoiding.
  • Use a gap matrix (scope rows × reviewer columns). Empty cells = unaddressed concerns — great handoff artifact.
  • Triage is the work: act now / defer with open question / decline with one-sentence reason. AI produces findings forever; the call is yours.
  • Watch for: uniform-confidence findings, invented concerns, reviewer drift, length-as-effort. Each has a one-line fix.
  • Calibrate pickiness up early in a draft, down near a deadline. Same template, one extra sentence.
  • Convert findings to edits in the same session — don’t close the laptop with twelve loose findings. Half won’t make it back in.
  • If the spec passes too easily, ask the model to describe it back in its own words. Gaps in the description are the real findings.
  • The five-minute version (one hat, “three most embarrassing”) is the right default for small specs, not a corner-cut for big ones.
  • Re-run the pass once after edits to catch new wrinkles. Two re-runs is the ceiling — past that, real human reviewers add more value.
