Chapter 8

Validating AI Output: 7 Smell Tests

Memory Map
👃

The Big Idea

Confidence is not correctness. AI prose reads with the same calm authority whether it’s grounded in your repo and your tickets or invented because the conversation seemed to want a sentence. Tone is not a signal of truth. Seven quick smell tests train your eye to spot the difference — in seconds, before the paragraph reaches engineering. The smells are yours to catch; the model can’t reliably catch its own.

👃 The Seven Smell Tests at a Glance

🪄
1. Generic-language tells
Leverage, robust, seamless, scalable, delight, streamline. Words that sound substantive and evaporate the moment you ask what they mean. Check: could two engineers build two different things from this sentence?
🎭
2. Fabricated specifics
A screen name, version, customer, table, or feature flag you don’t recognize from your own product or context. The shape of authority without the source. Check: grep your repo / Jira / CRM. If absent, ask the model which it invented.
🎯
3. Suspiciously round numbers
30% improvement. 50% of users. 10x faster. Numbers ending in 0 or 5, no source, no method, no baseline. Check: where would I look this afternoon to verify this? If “nowhere”, it’s a guess in evidence’s clothing.
🫥
4. Missing context
The paragraph would survive being pasted into a competitor’s spec with only the product name swapped. No platform, no team norm, no real constraint. Check: remove the product name — could a stranger tell who wrote this?
📉
5. Hallucinated metrics
“Engagement uplift.” “Activation velocity.” Sounds like a metric; isn’t one you can observe today. Check: which event, query, or dashboard produces today’s value? If “we’d have to instrument it”, that’s scope — or the metric must change.
🌫️
6. Vague verbs
Improve, optimize, ensure, enhance, streamline, support. Direction without destination. Check: can a QA engineer write a one-line test for this AC? If not, the verb is doing too much heavy lifting.
🪞
7. Uncanny consensus
The AI agrees with you immediately, in language that mirrors your own. Mirror, not sounding board. Check: ask for the strongest counter-case — “the three best reasons I’m wrong, persuasively.” If it’s as strong as the original, you weren’t as sure as you felt.
🪶
+1. The “describe back” prompt
When a section feels off but no specific tell fires: paste it back in a fresh thread — “describe what this commits to in your own words; list anything vague, missing a source, or open to two readings.” Catches what the seven miss.

After the first week, the tells light up automatically. You don’t open a checklist; you read a paragraph, an eye catches a tell, you re-prompt, you keep moving. The cost drops from minutes to seconds.

🚦 Patch vs. Delete-and-Restart — The Two-Criterion Rule

Situation What to do Why
1 or 2 tells fire, structurally sound paragraph 🩹 Patch — re-prompt for sharper language, swap the bad bits, keep the bones The structure is fine; the language was just lazy
3+ tells fire, OR any single tell is “the underlying claim is invented” ♻️ Delete and restart with more context attached and a tighter prompt Patching a generic template leaves the generic structure intact — restarting is faster every time
Tie-breaker: count the sentences you’d need to rewrite 2 of 5 → patch. 4 of 5 → restart. Spares you the most common time-sink: rewriting a generic paragraph one sentence at a time

🎬 The Loop in Real Time — Two Tells, One Re-prompt

  1. AI returns “Goals” section

    “Empower workspace admins to seamlessly onboard… drive a 30% increase in seat utilization.” — smell tests 1 (generic) and 3 (round number, no source) fire.

  2. PM names the tells, asks for honesty

    “Goals 1 and 2 are mostly marketing words. Goal 3 has a 30% number with no source. Where did that come from?”

  3. AI self-discloses

    “Goals 1 and 2 are templates, not derived from your context. The 30% is an estimate; I have no source. I should have said so.” That self-disclosure is the actual win.

  4. PM re-attaches real context, re-prompts

    “Today, only 40% of invited members complete sign-up because of spam/expiry. Rewrite the goals using only behaviors we can observe in our analytics, drop unsourced numbers.”

  5. AI returns measurable, grounded goals

    Goals tied to the invite-funnel report; instrumentation gaps flagged explicitly (“Goal 2 requires new instrumentation”). Total time: under three minutes.

🎚️ Calibrating Tests to Spec Stage

📝 Early draft:
tests 4 & 7
(missing context, uncanny consensus)
Test 2
(fabricated specifics)
matters at every stage
📤 Near-final:
tests 1, 3, 5, 6
(generic words, round numbers, hallucinated metrics, vague verbs)

When drafting, run the tests softly; placeholders are fine while you’re still exploring shape. When polishing for share, run them hard. The reflex stays the same; the tolerance changes. A fabricated specific is a landmine no matter when it ships — that one never gets a free pass.

🧰 The Smell-Test Reminder — Paste at the Top of Long Sessions

Self-prompt for the model When generating spec text, avoid these patterns and self-flag them when they happen: 1. Generic words (leverage, robust, seamless, scalable, streamline) 2. Fabricated specifics (invented names, screens, version numbers) 3. Round numbers without sources (30%, 50%, 10x) 4. Generic-product language (could fit any SaaS, no context grip) 5. Metrics not currently measurable in this product 6. Vague verbs in AC (improve, optimize, ensure, enhance) 7. Premature agreement; mirroring my framing back If a sentence depends on any of these, mark it explicitly rather than smoothing it over.

Works in Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Copilot Chat, Windsurf’s Cascade. Doesn’t eliminate the patterns — makes the model self-disclose them faster. That self-disclosure cuts your re-prompting in half.

🎯 What the Tests Catch — And What They Don’t

Catches Doesn’t catch
Confidently generic prose entering the spec without a fight A wrong product decision dressed up in clean prose
Hallucinated metrics that look measurable A misread of the customer
Round numbers with no source A perfectly measurable metric tracking the wrong thing
Vague verbs that aren’t testable Scope decisions made for the wrong reason
Generic templates dressed up to look bespoke Whether the spec is solving the right problem at all

The tests are about language, not judgment. The bigger questions live in chapters 2 (deciding), 3 (sharpening), and 7 (pressure-testing). What the tests do catch — sloppy language — costs trust with engineering fastest, so the small reflex pays off big.

What’s yours: the smell tests are your job, not the AI’s. The model can’t reliably catch its own generic language, can’t tell you which numbers it invented, and can’t notice that it’s describing a generic SaaS rather than your product. The same statistical reflex that produces the prose is what produces the smells. You’re the only one in the conversation who knows your product, your users, and what counts as a real number in your dashboard. When a sentence ships in your spec with a hallucinated metric in it, the byline is yours, not the model’s.

📒 Key Terms

Smell test
A 5-second reflex check on AI output. Each one targets one predictable way spec prose slides — vague where it should be concrete, invented where it sounds specific.
The tell
The visible pattern in the output that triggers the test. “Leverage”, “30%”, “robust” — the words and shapes that should make your eye catch.
The check
A quick mental test you can run on the sentence in seconds — e.g., “could two engineers build two different things from this?” or “can a QA engineer write a one-line test for it?”
The re-prompt
What you actually paste back to the AI to fix the issue — usually a sharpening request with explicit permission to admit it doesn’t know.
Patch vs. delete-and-restart
1–2 tells = patch. 3+ tells, or any invented underlying claim = restart with more context. Restarting is almost always faster than rewriting a generic paragraph one sentence at a time.
Fabricated specifics
Names, version numbers, screens, customers that sound grounded but came from the model’s defaults rather than from your context. The most dangerous tell because it mimics authority.
Hallucinated metric
A success metric the AI suggests that you cannot, today, point at a dashboard, query, or event for. Either the metric needs new instrumentation (and that becomes part of the scope) or the metric must change.
Uncanny consensus
The AI agreeing with you immediately, in your own framing. The mirror tells you very little. Run smell test 7 by asking for the strongest counter-case.
The “describe back” prompt
An eighth, off-list test: paste a section in a fresh thread, ask the model to describe what it commits to and list anything vague or missing. Surfaces gaps the seven tests miss because they’re about silence, not about a tell in existing prose.
Smell-test reminder
A short kit-style prompt pasted at the top of long sessions. Lists the seven patterns and asks the model to self-flag rather than smooth them over. Reduces re-prompting overhead.
Calibration to stage
Soft testing during early drafts (placeholders OK), hard testing before share. Same reflex, different tolerance — except for fabricated specifics, which never get a free pass.

🏷️ Before vs. After — A Single AC Paragraph

“Before” tells “After” commitment
“Leverage modern web standards” (test 1) Specific UI: workspace name, inviter’s name, “Accept and Sign In” button
“Seamless and robust onboarding” (test 1) Link works mobile + desktop, valid for 7 days, logged in audit trail
“Drive a 50% improvement in activation” (tests 3, 5) Sign-up share: baseline 60%, target 80% (source: invite-funnel report)
“Reduce support load by streamlining” (test 6) “Invite link expired” tickets: baseline 12/wk, target under 3/wk (tag: invite-expiry)

The “after” is longer, less elegant, and considerably more useful. Engineering can build from it. QA can test it. Support recognizes their own ticket category in it. Nothing is decorative.

👃 What the Smells Have in Common

None of the seven tests are about catching the AI being “wrong.” They’re about catching language that doesn’t commit.

  • Generic verbs don’t commit to behavior.
  • Round numbers don’t commit to a source.
  • Hallucinated metrics don’t commit to a measurement.
  • Missing context doesn’t commit to a product.
  • Uncanny consensus doesn’t commit to an opinion.

You’re not trying to debunk the model — you’re asking each sentence what it’s prepared to commit to. Sentences that commit get to stay. Sentences that don’t either get a re-prompt or get cut.

❓ Quick Quiz

Why is “confidence not correctness” the framing for this whole chapter?

Because AI output reads with the same calm authority regardless of where the words came from — a sentence grounded in your repo and a sentence the model invented look identical. If you treat tone as a signal of correctness, you will sometimes be right and sometimes be embarrassed, and the second one costs more. The seven tests replace tone-reading with reflex-checking.

What three parts does every smell test have?

The tell (what you notice), the check (the 5-second test you run on it), and the re-prompt (what you ask the AI to do about it). The shape is identical across all seven, which is what lets the reflex generalize.

What’s the rule for patch vs. delete-and-restart?

1 or 2 tells — patch. The structure is sound; the language was just lazy. 3+ tells, or any single “underlying claim is invented” — delete and restart with more context and a tighter prompt. Tie-breaker: count the sentences you’d need to rewrite. Two of five = patch. Four of five = restart, every time.

Why is fabricated specifics (test 2) sneakier than generic-language tells (test 1)?

Test 1 sounds vague; test 2 sounds extremely concrete. A specific screen name, customer name, version number, or feature flag has the shape of authority. To the model, real specifics and invented ones read as equally plausible. You can’t tell from tone — you have to grep your repo, Jira, or CRM, or just ask the model plainly which it took from context vs. invented.

What’s the test for a hallucinated metric?

For each metric in the spec, ask: “which event in our analytics, which row in our database, or which report would tell me the current value of this metric, today, before the build starts?” If the answer is “we’d need to instrument it”, that instrumentation is part of the scope — or the metric needs to change. Shipping a metric that can’t actually be observed makes the success section fiction.

Why is smell test 7 (uncanny consensus) the most consequential?

Because the other six catch language; this one catches a tilt in the conversation that quietly steers your judgment. The output feels great in the moment because the model agrees with you and mirrors your framing — but it tells you very little. The fix: “pretend you’re a senior PM who disagrees with my last decision; three best reasons I’m wrong, persuasively.” If you only ever run one test, run this one.

What does the “describe back” prompt do that the seven tests can’t?

Catches silences — what the section doesn’t say. Pasted into a fresh thread with “describe what this commits to in your own words; list anything vague or missing”, the model is unusually good at critiquing prose it didn’t generate in the same conversation. Treat it as the eighth test that doesn’t fit on the list. Used together, the seven plus “describe back” find almost everything that matters before the spec leaves your screen.

How does calibration to spec stage work?

Early drafts, while you’re still exploring shape: tests 4 and 7 matter most (missing context, uncanny consensus — they catch sections not yet grounded). Near-final drafts: tests 1, 3, 5, 6 matter most (generic words, round numbers, hallucinated metrics, vague verbs — they catch language that reads as careless to engineering). Test 2 (fabricated specifics) matters at every stage equally — it’s a landmine whenever it ships.

What do all seven tests have in common at the deepest level?

None of them are about catching the AI being wrong. They’re about catching language that doesn’t commit. Generic verbs don’t commit to behavior. Round numbers don’t commit to a source. Hallucinated metrics don’t commit to a measurement. You’re asking each sentence what it’s prepared to commit to — sentences that commit stay; sentences that don’t either get a re-prompt or get cut.

Key Takeaways

  • Confidence is not correctness — AI prose reads the same whether grounded or invented. Tone is not a signal of truth.
  • The seven tests: generic-language tells, fabricated specifics, suspicious round numbers, missing context, hallucinated metrics, vague verbs, uncanny consensus.
  • Each test has the same three parts: the tell, the check, the re-prompt. The shape generalizes.
  • Fabricated specifics (test 2) is the sneakiest — it sounds more concrete, not less, because the model treats real and invented specifics as equally plausible.
  • For metrics: ask “what event/query/dashboard tells me today’s value?” If the answer is “none”, instrumentation becomes scope or the metric changes.
  • Vague verbs fail because you can’t write a one-line test for them. AC must be observable, or it isn’t AC.
  • Smell test 7 is the most consequential — if you only ever run one, run this one. Ask for the strongest counter-case.
  • Two-criterion rule: 1–2 tells = patch. 3+ tells or any invented claim = delete and restart with more context. Restarting beats sentence-by-sentence rewriting every time.
  • Tie-breaker: count sentences you’d rewrite. Two of five = patch. Four of five = restart.
  • The model can self-disclose smells when asked — honest answers to “which specifics did you take from my context vs. invent?” surface fabrications fast.
  • Paste the smell-test reminder at the top of long sessions — the model produces visibly more conservative output and self-flags as it goes.
  • When something feels off but no specific tell fires: use the “describe back” prompt in a fresh thread. It catches gaps about silence, not about prose.
  • Calibrate to stage: tests 4 & 7 dominate early drafts, tests 1/3/5/6 dominate near-final, test 2 matters at every stage equally.
  • All seven tests share one purpose: catching language that doesn’t commit. Sentences that commit stay; sentences that don’t get re-prompted or cut.
  • The smells are your job, not the AI’s — the same statistical reflex that produces the prose produces the smells. The byline is yours.
  • After a week or two of practice, your own first drafts contain fewer tells — the seven patterns become fingerprints you can’t un-see, in any prose.
