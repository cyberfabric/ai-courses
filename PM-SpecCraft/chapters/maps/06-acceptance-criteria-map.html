Chapter 6

Acceptance Criteria & Edge Cases

Memory Map
✅

The Big Idea

AC are the spec’s testable surface — the place where ambiguity stops being a writing problem and starts being a build problem. AI can generate 50 edge cases in 30 seconds. You decide which 5 ship, which 5 defer, and which 40 you politely ignore. The triage is the work.

🎯 Three Properties of Good AC

🧪
Testable
A person or script can run a check and get an unambiguous yes/no. “Loads quickly” ✗ — “Renders within 500ms p95” ✓.
👁️
Observable
The signal lives somewhere visible — a UI state, a log line, an analytics event, a database row. If it only lives in someone’s head, it’s not a criterion.
🎯
Unambiguous
Two reasonable engineers reading it build the same thing. If it could justify three implementations, it isn’t done.

Most vague AC fail two of the three at once. Specifics are the proof that judgment was applied.

📐 Three AC Formats — All Valid, Pick by Fit

Format Shape Strong fit for
Given / When / Then Precondition → trigger → outcome User flows; sequences of states
Checklist Flat list, all must be true Settings, configs, audit logs (presence, not sequence)
Behavior list Plain present-tense statements Small features; high-level summary atop a longer list

Mix formats inside one PRD. Forcing twenty Given/When/Thens because you started with one is what’s messy.

🔁 The Sharpening Loop — One Round, Four Minutes

  1. Paste the vague AC list

    The first-draft bullets: “loads fast”, “handled gracefully”, “works on mobile”.

  2. Ask for a rewrite plus flags

    “Rewrite each AC as testable. For any criterion you can’t make testable without more from me, don’t guess — flag it and tell me what you’d need to know.”

  3. AI returns rewrite + flagged questions

    Latency budget? Empty-state Figma frame? Error event schema? Free list of decisions still needed.

  4. Answer the questions in one turn

    500ms p95. Frame F-12. `search.error` with {code, query, latency_ms}. Network drop = separate state.

  5. AI sharpens, you stop

    Clean AC out the other end. Total: ~4 minutes.

The flag-don’t-guess instruction is the difference between sharp AC and sharp-looking AC that quietly invented numbers you never agreed to. Use it every time.

🪣 The Eight Edge-Case Buckets

🪹
1. Empty states
First-use, no data, zero results, recently deleted, recently archived.
⚠️
2. Error states
Server errors, validation, timeouts, partial failures, retry behavior.
📡
3. Slow / no network
Slow connections, offline, intermittent drops, timeouts mid-flow.
♿
4. Accessibility
Keyboard-only, screen-reader announcements, focus, contrast, motion-reduced.
🔐
5. Permissions
Different roles, plan tiers, expired sessions, revoked access mid-session.
🌐
6. Multi-language & RTL
Long translated strings, right-to-left layout, locale number/date formats.
🕐
7. Time zones & DST
Cross-timezone scheduling, DST transitions, near-midnight, leap days.
📚
8. Large data & pagination
Thousand-row lists, deep pagination, very long strings, very large numbers.

One prompt → 30–60 candidates. Most you’ll defer or ignore. A few will change your release plan — and you wouldn’t have remembered the eight categories at 4 PM on a Friday.

✂️ The Pruning Filter — Three Questions

📊 How often?
Frequency × reach
0.1% weekly vs 30% per session
→
💥 How bad?
Impact
Data loss / revenue ↑
Cosmetic drift ↓
→
💰 How cheap?
What does it touch?
Code + design + copy + analytics + QA + rollout
Score Action Example
Often, bad, cheap SHIP as AC No-brainer.
Rare, mild, expensive DEFER as follow-up ticket Easy call.
Rare but catastrophic SHIP even if expensive Data corruption affecting 0.1% of users.
Common but mild Often DEFER Slight layout drift on a niche browser; keep AC list short.

“Cheap” isn’t lines of code. It’s what the change touches. A two-line back-end change that requires new design + copy + analytics + QA isn’t cheap — it’s expensive in disguise.

🏁 Definition of Done — The Standing Bar

📋 AC = what this feature does
🏗️ DoD = what every feature does
Two different artifacts
📌 Agree DoD once a quarter with eng + design
🔗 Reference from every PRD: “Standard team DoD applies; see [link]”

Negotiating DoD per ticket leads to ten different DoDs and a team that quietly stops trusting any of them. One-off additions are fine and visible (“In addition to standard DoD: security review”).

👃 Two Smell Tests for Your AC List

🧑‍🔬
The QA-sentence test
For each AC: “Could I describe in one sentence how QA would test this?” If “not really”, or it needs “basically” / “ideally” — not testable yet. Push back into the sharpening loop.
⚖️
The uniform-confidence test
Do all AC sound equally confident? Often a signal the easy ones got the same treatment as the hard ones — and the hard ones are hiding edge cases. Hard AC should look harder.

🩹 Five Patterns to Watch For

🌫️
Untestable verbs
“Intuitive”, “clear”, “fast”, “robust”, “seamless”. Replace each with an observable.
🌈
Happy-path-only AC
Reads like the feature working perfectly. No errors, no empty states. Run the eight-bucket prompt before declaring done.
🪤
AC that smuggle in solution
“The user sees a modal” commits to a modal. Specify behavior (“clear confirmation before charges”); leave modal-vs-inline to design.
📈
AC that grow in review
6 → 19 across three rounds. Add a one-line note next to each new AC: who asked for it and why. Catches scope expansion.
🐘
The single mega-AC
Six lines, four conditions joined by “and”. Almost always four AC pretending to be one. Split it.

👥 Writing AC Live With One Engineer

  1. Open shared screen: PRD + AI tool side by side

    PM types first-draft AC; engineer reads each one and reacts.

  2. Engineer flags “we can’t observe that”

    Rewrite on the spot — ask AI for three observable alternatives, pick what eng can implement cheaply.

  3. Engineer surfaces a hidden cost

    “This implies recomputing permissions on every render.” Either rewrite the AC or accept the cost in Risks — never silently.

  4. Engineer flags an analytics gap

    Required event doesn’t exist yet. Either change the assertion or add a “new analytics event required” line.

30 minutes live > 3 days solo. The AI is a typist and a sharpener, not a tie-breaker. If you and the engineer disagree, have the disagreement directly — the model produces a polite middle path that satisfies neither.

🌫️ AC for Fuzzy Outcomes

📊 Aggregate signals (“clicks top-3 in ≥30% of sessions”)
🚧 Bounds & guardrails (“p95 latency does not regress > 5%”)
Translate “feature works”
🚫 Negative cases — “these failure shapes must not occur”
🪜 AC = floor; Metrics = goal — same number, different framing

Negative cases are your part to write. Engineering doesn’t always know which failures would embarrass your specific customer base. You do.

📜 AC and the Spec’s Change History

  1. AC drift more than any other section

    Added in review, sharpened in pair sessions, deferred when sprint runs hot. Drift is healthy — only if visible.

  2. One-line note per change at the top

    “v0.6: added AC 7 (admin-on-other-workspace) after eng review.” Ten seconds; legible six weeks later.

  3. Ask AI to summarize the diff

    After a pair session: “summarize the diff between this AC list and the previous one in three bullets for the decision history.” Paste straight in.

❓ Quick Quiz

What three properties make an AC useful?

Testable (a person or script can run a check and get yes/no), observable (the signal lives somewhere visible — UI state, log, event, DB row), unambiguous (two reasonable engineers build the same thing). Most vague AC fail two of the three at once.

Why is the “flag-don’t-guess” instruction so important?

Without it, AI will fill in plausible numbers, frame names, and event schemas it invented — you get sharp-looking AC that quietly committed the team to a 1s budget you never agreed to and a fallback rule that doesn’t exist. With it, you get sharp AC plus a free list of decisions still to make.

What are the eight edge-case buckets?

Empty states, error states, slow/no network, accessibility, permissions, multi-language & RTL, time zones & DST, large data & pagination. Asking explicitly for each catches most missed bugs up front — you wouldn’t have remembered all eight at 4 PM on a Friday.

What does “cheap to handle” really mean?

Not lines of code. What the change touches: code paths, design, copy, analytics, QA, rollout. A two-line back-end change that requires new design + copy review + new analytics event + new QA pass is expensive in disguise. Ask the engineer.

What’s the difference between AC and Definition of Done?

AC describe what this feature does. DoD describes what every feature does — the team’s standing bar. DoD belongs in a single referenced document, agreed once per quarter, not renegotiated per ticket. One-off additions are fine and explicit (“In addition to standard DoD…”).

Why is uniform polish across an AC list a smell?

Because the easy AC and the hard AC required different levels of work, and if they all sound equally confident, the hard ones probably got the easy treatment — with edge cases hiding inside. Hard AC should look harder: more conditions, more specifics, more flagged ambiguity.

How long should a sprint-feature AC list be?

Roughly 5–12 criteria. Below 5: probably missing edge cases — run the eight-bucket prompt. Above 12: probably too big a feature for one PRD, or contains DoD-flavored items that should be referenced from the standing bar instead.

What role does AI play in a live AC session with one engineer?

Typist and sharpener — not tie-breaker. If you and the engineer disagree about whether an edge case ships, asking the model to break the tie produces a polite middle path that satisfies neither and ages badly. PM owns the call; engineer owns the cost estimate; AI helps express what you both already decided.

How do AC for fuzzy outcomes (ML, ranking, summaries) differ?

Instead of single-state pass/fail, assert on aggregate signals (“clicks top-3 in ≥30% of sessions”), bounds (“p95 latency does not regress > 5%”), and negative cases (“summary never references information not in the source”). The negative half is the part you’re best positioned to write, because you know which failures would embarrass your customers.

Key Takeaways

  • AC are the spec’s testable surface — where ambiguity stops being a writing problem and starts being a build problem.
  • Three properties: testable, observable, unambiguous. Most vague AC fail two at once.
  • Three formats — Given/When/Then, checklist, behavior list — mix freely; pick the shape that fits the criterion.
  • Sharpen with the flag-don’t-guess prompt: AI returns a rewrite plus a free list of decisions still to make.
  • Ask the AI to walk the eight edge-case buckets explicitly — you won’t remember them at 4 PM Friday, and most missed bugs come from those eight corners.
  • Prune with three questions: how often, how bad, how cheap? Cheap = what the change touches, not lines of code.
  • DoD is the team standing bar: agree once a quarter, reference from every PRD. Don’t renegotiate per ticket.
  • Two smell tests: QA-sentence (can you describe a test in one sentence?) and uniform-confidence (do hard AC look hard enough?).
  • Watch for: untestable verbs, happy-path-only AC, AC that smuggle in implementation, AC that grow in review, the single mega-AC.
  • A 30-minute pair session with one engineer beats 3 days of solo iteration. AI is the typist, not the tie-breaker.
  • Aim for 5–12 AC per sprint feature. Above 12: pull out DoD-flavored items; bundle variations; defer rare ones.
  • Fuzzy outcomes need aggregate signals, bounds, and explicit negative cases. The negative cases are yours to write.
  • Log every AC change as a one-line decision history. AC drift is healthy only if visible.
  • Read the AC section aloud once before sharing. The ear catches vague sentences the eye lets slide.
