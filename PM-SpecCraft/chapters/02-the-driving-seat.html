Chapter 2

The Driving Seat: 5 Decisions Only You Make

Problem, user, scope, success, ship — the irreducible PM decisions, and how to use AI as a sounding board for each without quietly handing the wheel over.

The polished spec that decides nothing

Picture this. It's a Wednesday afternoon. You have forty-five minutes before your next meeting, and a half-formed feature idea in your head: "we should let teams export their dashboards." You open Claude in a fresh tab, type a one-line prompt, and ask for a draft PRD.

Eight seconds later you have something genuinely impressive. Headings, user stories, acceptance criteria, even a small risks section. It looks like a spec. You feel productive. You almost paste it into the ticket.

Then you stop, because you notice something. The draft assumes a user persona you never picked. It assumes an export format you never decided on. It assumes success means "users can export" — not "support tickets about manual screenshots drop by half." None of those calls were yours. The draft looks like a spec, but the actual decisions are still missing.

That gap — between something that reads like a spec and something that is a spec — is the entire subject of this chapter. We're going to name the five decisions that make a spec real. Not as a checklist to memorize, but as the thing you steer the AI around.

Decisions before prompts

A useful habit: before you open any AI tool to draft, expand, or summarize anything spec-shaped, you can say in one sentence what call you're about to make. Not what you're going to write — what you're going to decide.

Think of it like asking someone for directions. If you don't know your destination, no one — human or AI — can help you get there. They'll just produce plausible-sounding routes to nowhere in particular. The AI is brilliant at producing the route. You name the destination.

The five decisions below show up in every spec, in every product, in every team. AI tools can help you think about each of them. None of them can take any of these decisions for you — not because the technology isn't good enough, but because of what these decisions are. They commit you to something. They create accountability. That accountability is the thing you signed up for when you took the role.

A small reframing helps when the deadline is breathing on your neck. The five decisions don't all need long answers — they need any answer. A two-sentence pass on each beats a thirty-minute pass on one and a blank on the other four. You're not writing a treatise; you're putting a stake in the ground for each decision so the AI has something to build around. Deeper work happens later, in chapters 3 through 7.

Decision 1: Is this problem worth solving?

Of all the problems your users have, of all the things your competitors are doing, of all the ideas in your backlog — which one earns a slice of your team's quarter? That's the decision.

AI tools are excellent at listing problems. Ask ChatGPT for ten things that might be wrong with your onboarding flow, and you'll get ten plausible candidates in seconds. Most of them will sound reasonable. A few will be sharp. One or two will be irrelevant to your actual product reality. The list is not the decision. Picking which one your team will sweat over for the next six weeks — that's the decision.

What helps: bring the AI candidates, not consequences. Treat the list as raw material. Then ask the AI to stress-test the candidate you're already leaning toward. That conversation is useful. Asking the AI to "pick the most important one" is not, because the AI doesn't know your roadmap, your CEO's anxieties, your team's last incident, or your ARR concentration.

I'm choosing between three problems for next quarter: (1) admins can't bulk-invite users, (2) reports take 30+ seconds to load on large workspaces, (3) we have no audit log for compliance customers. Which one would you push back on me investing in, and why?
Happy to push back. The bulk-invite problem feels small — admins likely solve it once during onboarding, then never think about it. The report-load problem affects daily usage but you've already shipped two perf improvements this year and may be hitting diminishing returns. The audit-log gap is the one I'd push you to interrogate hardest: it's binary (you either have it or you don't), it unblocks specific deals, and the cost is mostly known. So my pushback is on (1). What's making you consider it?
Honestly, recency bias — three customers complained last week. But you're right, we'd build it once and they'd forget about it. Audit log it is. Help me write the problem statement for that one.

Notice what the AI did and didn't do. It compared, it pushed back, it asked a follow-up. It did not say "go build the audit log." The PM made that call. The AI was a sounding board for a decision the PM was already orbiting.

Yours to decide: which of the candidates is worth a quarter of your team's time. AI can rank them on stated criteria. Only you carry the weight of the consequences.

Decision 2: Who is the user?

You can't write a useful spec for "users." You can write one for the on-call engineer who needs to silence a noisy alert at 3am. You can write one for the finance lead at a 200-person company doing month-end close in a hurry. The specificity is what makes the rest of the spec land.

AI can generate plausible user segments by the dozen. Ask Gemini for five personas for a "team admin tool" and you'll get five clean, vaguely-realistic personas. They will all sound right. Some of them will not exist in your actual customer base. A few may be amalgams of personas you already use, with the names changed.

What works: take the AI's segment list as a starting hypothesis, then collapse it against reality. One sales call, one support-ticket scan, one look at your usage data is usually enough to know which two of the five are real and which three are theory. The AI cannot do that step. You can, in an hour.

One quick example. A PM working on a "team admin" feature asked Copilot for likely user segments. The AI returned six: IT admin, ops manager, security lead, team lead, executive sponsor, and external auditor. The PM then spent thirty minutes reading recent support tickets and discovered that in their product, "IT admin" and "security lead" were almost always the same person, "ops manager" was a label nobody self-identified with, and "external auditor" had never opened the product. Six segments collapsed into two real ones: the IT/security person, and the team lead. The spec got dramatically simpler.

If your AI-generated segments all read like LinkedIn job titles and none of them feel like a person you've actually spoken to, that's the cue to put the chat aside for thirty minutes and read recent customer conversations.
Yours to decide: which segments are real in your product, with your data. AI can name plausible users. You confirm them with humans.

Decision 3: What's in scope, and what's out?

Scope is the place where most specs quietly fail. Not because something gets included that shouldn't — but because the things that won't be included this round are never named, and so they keep haunting reviews, planning, and standups for weeks.

The non-goals are load-bearing. They tell engineers what to build and what to leave alone. They tell stakeholders what won't ship in the first cut. They are the thing you point to when someone six weeks into the project says "wait, doesn't this also need to handle imports?"

AI tools, when prompted broadly, tend to expand scope. Ask Windsurf for a full PRD for "edit checkout flow" and you'll often get an outline that quietly assumes you also want to add saved payment methods, a wishlist, and a guest checkout. Each one is a reasonable adjacency. None of them belong in your release unless you say so. This is a pattern, not a problem — once you see it, it's easy to redirect.

What works: write the non-goals first, before you let the AI fill anything in. A short list of "we are explicitly not doing X this release" cuts the AI's scope-creep tendency in half.

## Goals - Customers can edit shipping address before order ships - Confirmation email reflects the edit within 5 minutes - Edits respect the existing 30-min order-cancel window ## Non-goals (this release) - Editing payment method - Editing line items or quantities - Editing orders that have already shipped - A new "edit history" view for customer service - Bulk edits for B2B accounts

Notice that the non-goals section is longer than the goals. That's healthy, not a sign of trouble. Each non-goal is a future argument you've already settled in advance.

Yours to decide: what is not in this release. AI can list adjacent features all day. Saying no is the part that costs you something — and that's exactly why it has to be your call.

Decision 4: What does success look like?

Every spec ships with an implicit promise: "if this works, we'll know because X." That X is the success metric. It's the thing your manager will ask about in a quarterly review. It's the thing you'll defend if the launch underwhelms. It's the thing that, six months later, tells you whether to invest more in this area or wind it down.

AI is great at generating metric candidates. Ask ChatGPT for ten ways to measure success for a redesigned onboarding flow and you'll get the usual suspects: activation rate, time-to-first-value, drop-off rate, day-7 retention, and several variants of each. They're mostly fine metrics. They are not yet your decision.

The decision is which one or two metrics you will be judged on. The AI cannot make that call because it requires reading the room: what your leadership currently cares about, what your team has historically been able to move, what your data infrastructure can actually measure cleanly, what the last three launches were judged on. None of that lives in a model.

One PM working on an onboarding revamp got ten metric candidates from the AI. They picked one: "percent of new accounts that complete a 'first useful action' within their first session." Then they wrote three sentences in their own voice about why that one and not the others. Those three sentences became the line they defended in every review for the rest of the quarter. The AI helped enumerate. The PM owned the call.

Yours to decide: the one or two metrics you'll sign your name to. AI can suggest a dozen. You pick the one your career and your team's quarter will be measured against.

Decision 5: When is it good enough to ship?

This is the hardest of the five. Risk, urgency, team capacity, customer relationships, regulatory windows, the state of last week's incident, what the CTO said in standup, what your biggest customer is asking for — the ship call weighs all of them at once.

AI tools cannot do this weighing. Not because they're missing some feature, but because almost none of the inputs live anywhere the AI can read. Your team's morale this week. The fact that two engineers are out next Monday. The customer who's threatening to churn. The legal review that's stuck. The competitor who launched something similar yesterday. These are the variables, and most of them aren't in any document, anywhere.

What AI can do, very usefully, is help you list the open risks and unmade decisions before you decide. "Here's what I'm planning to ship; what would worry you most about this going out next Tuesday?" is a good prompt. The AI will produce a list. You'll recognize three of them as real and two as paranoid. You'll make the call.

Yours to decide: when to ship, what to cut to ship sooner, what to delay to ship cleaner. AI can list the risks. The release call is yours, every time.

The five-decision card

Here they are in one place. If you only remember one thing from this chapter, remember this card.

1. Worth solving?
→
2. Who is it for?
→
3. What's out?
→
4. Success = ?
→
5. Ship when?

Every later chapter in this course sits on top of these five. When we sharpen a fuzzy problem in chapter 3, we're working Decision 1. When we write the PRD in chapter 5, we're locking in Decisions 2 and 3. When we draft acceptance criteria in chapter 6, we're tightening the boundary between Decision 3 and Decision 4. When we pressure-test in chapter 7, we're checking we haven't quietly let any of the five drift. The decisions are the spine. The techniques in the rest of the book are how you keep that spine straight while moving fast with AI.

All five, on one feature

Decisions are easier to spot in the abstract than to make under deadline. Here's a single, realistic example: a small B2B SaaS team considering an "in-product guides" feature — little tooltip-style walkthroughs that appear inside the app to help new users learn key flows. The PM has two weeks to firm up the spec. Watch how the five decisions map onto a real artifact.

Decision 1 — worth solving? Three signals support it: support gets repeat tickets on the same three flows, sales calls keep ending in "we'll need to train our team on this," and the activation funnel shows a 40% drop after account creation. The PM asks ChatGPT to push back: "is in-product guidance the right intervention, or are we papering over a navigation problem?" The AI surfaces a real alternative (rework navigation first) and a counter (guides ship in two weeks, navigation rework is a quarter). The PM stays with guides for now and notes navigation as a later candidate.

Decision 2 — who is the user? The AI proposes "new users" as the segment. The PM tightens that: not all new users, but specifically the second user invited into a workspace by an admin. They're the largest cohort and the one who drops off most. "First admin" is a different segment with a different problem (they were sold to; they already had context). Naming the segment more precisely changes which flows the guides cover.

Decision 3 — in scope, out of scope? The PM lists what's in: three flagship flows, dismissable tooltips, basic show-once logic. They list what's out: branching tutorials, video, custom tour authoring by customers, A/B testing of guide content, analytics dashboards on guide engagement. The non-goals get explicit. Two of them came directly from AI suggestions the PM declined.

Decision 4 — what does success look like? The AI offers seven metrics. The PM picks one and writes it themselves: "second-invited users complete at least two of the three flagship flows within their first three sessions, up from a 35% baseline to 55% within six weeks of launch." Specific, observable, defensible. The number isn't magic — it's what the team thinks they can move with this intervention.

Decision 5 — ship when? Two weeks of build, one week of internal soak, three weeks of measured rollout. The PM lists what would force a delay: a known Q3 incident replaying, a tooling change that breaks the analytics pipeline, or a sales-led ask to white-label the guides for a top customer. None of those would be visible to an AI. All of them are real ship-call inputs.

By the time the PM opens their AI tool to draft the actual PRD — the headings, the user stories, the AC — the five decisions are already on paper. The draft that comes back is genuinely useful, because the AI is filling in detail around real decisions instead of inventing decisions to fill in detail around. The PRD takes thirty minutes instead of two days, and the PM is the author of it, not the editor.

A second dialogue: working a metric in your own words

Decision 4 trips up more PMs than any of the others, because the AI's metric suggestions all sound competent. Here's how to keep the call yours, even when you accept the AI's nominee.

I'm picking the success metric for the in-product guides feature. Give me five candidates with the trade-offs each one carries.
(1) Activation rate — clean and well-known, but slow to move and confounded by other changes you ship in the same window. (2) Guide-completion rate — easy to measure, but it measures the feature using itself, not the outcome. (3) Time-to-second-session — a strong leading indicator, but noisy at small N. (4) Multi-flow adoption among second-invited users — closest to the underlying job-to-be-done, harder to instrument. (5) Day-7 retention of cohorts exposed to guides vs. control — the gold-standard answer, but requires an A/B setup you may not have built.
Walk me through the case for (4) specifically. Why might that be the wrong choice?
It's lagging by a few weeks (you need three sessions to count), so your feedback loop is slower than for completion rate. It depends on event instrumentation that may not exist yet for two of the three flows — you'd be paying an instrumentation cost up front. And if the guides don't move it, you can't easily tell whether the guides failed or whether multi-flow adoption is bottlenecked elsewhere.
That instrumentation cost is real but worth paying — we'll want it for other features anyway. I'm going with (4). Help me write a one-paragraph defense in plain language for the PRD.
Draft: "We will measure success as the percentage of second-invited users who complete at least two of the three flagship flows within their first three sessions. We chose this over completion rate (which measures whether people use the guides, not whether they get value) and activation rate (too lagging and confounded by other Q3 launches) because it's the closest available proxy for the actual outcome we care about: new collaborators getting productive without a support touch."
Good shape. Let me rewrite that in my own voice.

The PM accepted the AI's metric nominee — option (4) — but the surrounding decision-making was theirs. They asked for trade-offs, they pushed on the weakest case, they extracted the AI's draft as a starting point, and then they rewrote it in their own voice. None of that takes more than fifteen minutes. The result is a metric the PM will defend in every review for the rest of the quarter, in language that sounds like them.

Common shapes of a soft decision

If you're not sure whether a decision is firm in your head or still soft, here are the common tells. Recognize one of these patterns and you've found the place to apply the next chapter's techniques first.

  • You can describe the problem in a paragraph, but not in one sentence.
  • Your "user" is a job title, not a person you've spoken to in the last 60 days.
  • Your scope description has no non-goals — only goals.
  • Your success metric is "users will be happier" or "engagement will increase" without a number, a baseline, or a window.
  • You can't name the date the feature would ship if it had to ship today.
  • Two senior people in your org would describe the goal of this work in noticeably different language.
  • You catch yourself prompting the AI for "the answer" rather than for material to think with.

None of these are failures. They are exactly the moments where AI is most useful as a sounding board — and exactly the moments where it's most tempting to let the AI decide for you. The trick is to notice the softness, name it, and then deliberately use the AI to harden it.

AI as a sounding board, not a decision-maker

So how do you actually use AI on these decisions without quietly letting it take over? Three patterns work especially well, and they all share one trait: the AI returns material, but the call stays with you.

Pattern 1: Steel-man both sides. When you're between two options, ask the AI to make the strongest possible case for each. "Argue for option A as if you've been working on this for a year. Then argue for option B with the same energy." You read both. You decide. The AI never recommends — it just makes both choices as legible as possible.

Pattern 2: List what you might be missing. Once you have a draft of one of the five decisions, paste it back into the chat and ask: "what would a senior PM ask me about this that I haven't addressed?" You'll typically get five to eight questions. Two of them will already be answered in your head. Two will be irrelevant. One or two will genuinely sharpen your thinking.

Pattern 3: Write the strongest counter-argument. Once you've made a call, ask the AI to argue against it. Not gently — sharply. "I'm planning to ship this in two weeks with the audit-log feature scoped to read-only. Tell me the strongest case for delaying or expanding scope." If the counter-argument doesn't move you, your decision was solid. If it does move you, you found something before engineering did.

A small phrasing shift makes a big difference. Instead of "what do you think we should do?" try "make the case for and against, then I'll decide." The first prompt invites the model to recommend; the second invites it to assist your judgment. Same model, very different output. Try this in Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini back-to-back — the pattern holds across all three.

The full rhythm in one conversation

Here's a slightly longer dialogue showing all three patterns in a single thread, working Decision 1 in depth. The PM is choosing among three problems and uses Windsurf as the sounding board. Notice how the PM keeps redirecting whenever the AI starts to sound like it's making the call.

I'm choosing between three problems for next quarter. (1) audit log for compliance, (2) better workspace search, (3) team-level analytics. Steel-man each one as if it were the obvious right choice.
For audit log: it unblocks specific enterprise deals, the cost is well-understood, and shipping it removes a recurring objection in sales calls. For workspace search: it's the most-touched feature you have, even small improvements compound across millions of sessions, and search quality is a felt-quality signal customers cite. For team-level analytics: you currently have no surface for managers, and that's the buyer for your top tier — this could shift the buying conversation. All three are credible.
Good. Now what would a senior PM ask me about each that I haven't said?
On audit log: how many deals have you actually lost to this in the last two quarters — with names? On search: what's your current baseline, and what does a "win" look like in numbers, not vibes? On analytics: have you talked to the manager-tier buyers in the last 90 days about what they'd want to see — or are you guessing?
That's useful. The analytics one I'm guessing on, which is a real signal. I'm leaning audit log. Argue against it — sharply.
Audit log is a feature you ship to close a deal that may already be lost; compliance buyers tend to ask for it as a checklist item even when they wouldn't actually use it. Building it commits you to maintaining a security-sensitive surface forever. The deals you'd unblock may be smaller than the engineering cost over a two-year horizon. And while you're building it, your competitor ships the workspace-search improvement that affects every customer you have.
That's a real argument. The maintenance-cost-forever point is the one that lands. I'll still pick audit log, because the deals are named and quantified — but I'll scope it tighter than I was planning. Read-only first, write later.

Look at what the PM did across that conversation. They asked for material three different ways: steel-man, gap-find, counter-argue. They got useful material each time. They never asked the AI to decide. They made the call, and they refined it based on what came back. That's the rhythm.

If at any moment in a planning chat you notice yourself thinking "I'll just go with what it suggested," pause. The AI's suggestion is fine as a starting point. The decision is still yours, and ten minutes of extra thought now will save you weeks of confusion later.

One short test for the chapter

If you can't say in one sentence which of the five decisions you're currently working on, the AI's draft is decoration. Close the chat, name the decision, then go back. Two minutes saved upstream is two weeks saved downstream.

A few common worries

"My team won't tolerate me writing non-goals — they'll see it as me restricting them." The opposite usually plays out. Engineers tend to feel grateful when scope is bounded; ambiguity costs them more than constraint does. Frame non-goals as "what I'm explicitly not asking you to figure out this round," and the energy shifts. AI tools can help here too: ask Gemini or Copilot to draft the non-goals section based on your goals, and then trim ruthlessly. The non-goals are an act of clarity, not an act of refusal.

"My leadership has already picked the success metric. Do I still own Decision 4?" Yes — in a slightly different shape. You own the local version of the metric: what it means in this spec, how it'll be measured here, what numbers count as a success or a miss for this piece of work. The org-level metric is the destination; your spec-level metric is the next mile-marker. Use the AI to translate between the two.

"What if I'm genuinely not sure on Decision 1, and I want the AI to help me decide?" That's healthy — but the technique is "use the AI to surface considerations you haven't weighed" rather than "ask the AI which one to pick." Steel-man, gap-find, counter-argue. After three rounds you'll know which one you're orbiting. Make the call. If you're still genuinely unsure after that, the right next move is a customer call or a data pull, not another prompt.

"What about decisions inside a single sprint — do they all need the five?" No. The five decisions scale with the size of the work. A two-day fix may compress all five into two sentences. A quarter-long initiative may need a paragraph on each. The rule isn't "always do all five at full depth." The rule is "be able to name where each one stands before you start prompting." Even a quick mental pass counts.

"My PM peers don't work this way. Will I look slow?" The opposite, in practice. People who've internalized the five decisions tend to draft faster, not slower, because they're not re-litigating the basics in every review. The upfront fifteen minutes pays back in hours of back-and-forth that never happens. The pattern is invisible to people who don't see your prep, but very visible in the quality of what you ship.

"Sometimes I'm handed a feature with the decisions already made — by leadership, by a customer commitment, by something we promised in a board deck. Do the five still apply?" They do, and they apply harder, not less. When decisions are inherited, your job shifts from making them to making them legible. Write each of the five down explicitly: who decided this, when, on what evidence, and what would change the call. That documentation protects the team six weeks later when someone says "wait, why are we even doing this?" and the answer needs to be more than "leadership said so." AI is genuinely useful here too — ask Claude or ChatGPT to draft the "decision context" paragraph for each of the five based on what you already know. Edit, sign, move on.

Why this matters for everything else

These five decisions are not a phase you do at the start of a project and then forget. They're the questions you keep asking, in lighter and heavier forms, all the way through. When a stakeholder pushes back in week three, the answer is usually a re-statement of one of the five. When engineering asks "do we need to handle this case?", the answer comes from Decision 3. When a launch underperforms, the post-mortem usually surfaces a wobble on Decision 1 or Decision 4 — not on the implementation.

The reason to make these decisions explicit, in writing, before you start prompting is that AI tools amplify whatever you bring them. If you bring sharp decisions, you get a sharp spec faster than ever. If you bring soft decisions, the AI fills them with plausible-sounding text, and you end up with a polished spec that nobody can quite ship from. Same tool, very different outcomes. The difference is upstream.

Two paths, same feature

Here's the same Decision 1 statement — the problem-worth-solving call — written two ways for the same in-product guides feature. Notice how the second version does work the first version cannot. Both took roughly the same amount of time. The second was anchored on real decisions before the AI got involved.

Problem: New users are confused when they first open the product and don't know how to get started. We should help them learn the key features more easily, which will improve activation and reduce support load. AI suggests in-product guides as a solution.
Problem: Second-invited users (the largest cohort dropping out of activation) complete fewer than two of our three flagship flows in their first three sessions, and a meaningful share of them never return. Support sees ~14 tickets/week traceable to this gap; sales loses two named deals/month at this stage. Why this and not navigation rework: guides ship in two weeks, nav rework is a quarter; we'll do nav next if guides under-deliver. Decision owner: PM (me). Confirmed with: support lead (ticket review), sales lead (Q2 deal log), data team (funnel pull).

The first version is not wrong — it just isn't a decision yet. It's a description with a solution attached. The second version commits to a specific cohort, names the alternative considered and why it lost, and points to the evidence that any reviewer can re-check. The AI helped draft the second version, but the call about what to commit to happened in the PM's head first.

This is the pattern that scales. A spec built on top of decisions like the second one almost writes itself once you start drafting. A spec built on top of soft language like the first one fights you in every section, because there's no anchor to write toward.

What changes after a few weeks of this

People who internalize the five decisions usually report a similar set of shifts. Their drafts get faster, because the AI is doing detail-work on top of stable inputs instead of inventing the inputs from scratch. Their reviews get shorter, because stakeholders aren't litigating the basics anymore — the basics are written down. Their feedback rounds become tighter, because comments tend to land on craft and edge cases instead of "wait, who is this for?"

The tooling fades into the background, which is the goal. You stop noticing whether you're prompting Claude or Gemini or Copilot, because the work isn't in the prompt — it's in the decisions you bring to the prompt. Different tools, same craft. That's the durable skill.

You'll also notice a smaller, quieter change: you stop dreading the blank page. The blank page was scary because it represented all five decisions stacked on top of each other, undifferentiated. Once you can name the five and tackle them one at a time, the blank page becomes five short questions instead. AI is excellent at five short questions in a row.

One more shift worth naming: the way you respond to "what do you think?" from your team changes. Instead of an instinctive "let me get back to you," you start replying with which of the five decisions the question touches and where you currently stand on it. Engineers and designers feel the difference within a couple of weeks. The conversation gets faster because both sides know what frame they're inside.

Try it this week

The Five-Decision Drill. Pick one product question on your plate this week — a feature you're scoping, a bug investigation, a small initiative, anything spec-shaped. Before you open any AI tool, do this:

  1. On paper or in a plain doc, write one sentence for each of the five decisions:
    • Is this problem worth solving? (Why this one, why now?)
    • Who is the user? (Be specific — a person, not a job title.)
    • What's in scope and what's not? (Write the non-goals.)
    • What does success look like? (One metric you'd defend.)
    • When is it good enough to ship? (Earliest credible date and what would force you to delay it.)
  2. Now open Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or whatever your team uses. Pick the one decision you feel softest on. Ask the AI to stress-test it — steel-man, gap-find, or counter-argue.
  3. Update your one-sentence answer in your own words. Notice what changed.
  4. Compare that experience with your usual flow of "open chat, ask for a draft, edit." Which one made you the author of the spec, and which one made you the editor of the AI's spec?

You don't have to do this for every spec forever — but doing it once a week for a month builds the muscle.
