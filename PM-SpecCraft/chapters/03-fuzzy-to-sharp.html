Chapter 3

From Fuzzy Idea to Sharp Problem

How to use AI in rounds, not in one shot, to turn a vague brief like “notifications are bad” into something specific enough to ship against.

The brief is one line

It’s a Monday morning. Your VP drops a Slack message: “Users keep saying our notifications are bad. Can you scope a fix for next sprint?”

You read it twice. “Bad” is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Bad how? For which users? Too many notifications? Too few? Wrong channels? Wrong content? Wrong timing? “Bad” could be any of these — or all of them at once for different people.

You have the urge to open Claude or Copilot and ask for “a PRD for improving notifications.” Eight seconds later you’d have three pages of plausible-sounding spec. It would have headings. It would have AC. It would propose a settings page with quiet hours and per-channel toggles. It would feel like progress.

It would also be solving a problem you haven’t actually defined yet. The spec would be a confident answer to a question you never asked. Six weeks later, after engineering ships exactly what you wrote, the same VP would say: “Users still say our notifications are bad.”

This chapter is about the half-hour between the fuzzy brief and the first prompt — the part where you and the AI work together to find out what the actual problem is. Done well, it changes everything downstream. Done in a hurry, it quietly costs you a sprint.

Fuzzy in, fuzzy out (only worse)

Here’s an uncomfortable truth about modern AI tools: they don’t politely tell you when your input is too vague. They produce confident, well-formatted output regardless. Ask Gemini for a PRD on “notifications are bad” and you will not get back “I’m sorry, that’s underspecified, please clarify.” You’ll get a PRD. It will look like a PRD. It will read like a PRD. It will be drafted on top of assumptions you never made.

This is the amplification effect. AI takes whatever sharpness you bring and adds polish. If you bring sharp problem definition, it adds beautiful prose around your judgment. If you bring fuzz, it adds beautiful prose around the fuzz, and the prose itself disguises the fuzz so well that it’s hard to spot the gap from the outside. Reviewers nod. Engineers start building. The cracks show up six weeks later.

The trick isn’t to use AI less. The trick is to use it specifically for the sharpening — in rounds, not in one shot. The first prompt is never the last prompt. Each round adds one more turn of the screw.

Think of sharpening a problem the way a chef sharpens a knife: it takes several passes against the stone, each at a slightly different angle. One pass produces a knife that’s sharper than before but still dull. Four or five passes produce a knife you can use. The same is true here. One prompt produces a problem statement that’s sharper than the brief but still mushy. Four prompts produce something you can ship against.

The destination: a four-field problem statement

Before we walk into the loop, let’s name the destination. The shape you’re sharpening toward isn’t a paragraph — it’s four small fields. If you can fill them in clear language, you’re done. If you can’t, you have another round to do.

PROBLEM — what is going wrong, in one or two specific sentences. Not the symptom. Not the solution. The thing itself. USERS — who specifically experiences this. A cohort, a role, a moment-in-the-product — concrete enough that you could sit down with one of them tomorrow. EVIDENCE — how do you know this is real. Tickets, sessions, calls, survey lines, usage data — with at least one number or named source. SUCCESS — what would change if this were fixed. One observable signal. Not “users will be happier.” Something you can watch on a dashboard.

Four fields. Roughly four sentences total. No solution anywhere in there — just the shape of the gap. Notice that the template doesn’t ask for “goals” or “requirements” or “UX” — those come later. The job here is to get the gap into focus.

This is the version of the problem statement you’ll feed every later step in the spec. Chapter 5’s PRD outline sits on top of these four fields. Chapter 6’s acceptance criteria are derived from the success line. Chapter 7’s pressure-tests will pull on each of the four. Get this right and the rest of the spec is mostly populating. Get this wrong and the rest of the spec is rework.

The 5-whys loop, adapted for AI

You’ve probably seen “5 whys” in a workshop somewhere — a technique that asks “why?” five times in a row to get from a symptom to a root cause. The same instinct works beautifully with AI, with one twist: you’re doing the asking, the AI is doing the answering, and you’re also doing the rejecting.

A useful rhythm:

  • Round 1 — restate. Hand the AI the brief and ask for a restatement, plus three plausible interpretations. You’re testing what the model heard.
  • Round 2 — redirect to user. Push back: “those are too generic; who specifically is feeling this, and at what moment?”
  • Round 3 — ask for evidence. “How would I know this is real? What would I check first?”
  • Round 4 — sharpen the gap. “Given what we now know, restate the problem in two sentences. No solutions. No softening words.”
  • Round 5 (optional) — counter-frame. “Make the strongest case that this isn’t the real problem at all.”

Five rounds is a maximum, not a minimum. Many problems collapse cleanly in three. Some need a sixth pass. The cadence is more important than the count: each round narrows what the next round can drift into.

A worked dialogue: notifications, four rounds

Here’s the technique on a real-feeling example. The PM is using ChatGPT today, but the same dialogue works in Claude, Gemini, Copilot, or Windsurf — the principle is in the questions, not the tool.

Brief from my VP: “Users say our notifications are bad. Scope a fix for next sprint.” Don’t propose a solution yet. Restate the brief and give me three different interpretations of what “bad” could mean.
Restated: users are dissatisfied with the notifications they receive from your product, but the source of the dissatisfaction is unspecified. Three interpretations: (1) Volume — too many, leading to fatigue and muting. (2) Relevance — notifications fire for events users don’t care about. (3) Timing/channel — notifications arrive at the wrong moment or in the wrong place (email vs. in-app vs. push).
Good. Those are too generic to act on. For each interpretation, name a specific user moment — who is the user, what are they doing, what notification arrives that’s wrong?
(1) Volume: a team admin who invited 30 people in one batch receives 30 separate “X joined the workspace” emails over the next hour. (2) Relevance: an individual contributor on a project gets pinged for every comment on every card on every board, even ones they don’t own. (3) Timing/channel: a manager gets a critical mention as an email at 2am Sunday, but never sees the in-app banner because they’re not logged in.
Interpretation (2) lines up with what I’ve been hearing in support tickets. Stay on that one. How would I know it’s real? What would I check first?
I’d suggest three checks. (a) Search support tickets for the past 90 days for “too many,” “noisy,” “turn off,” “unsubscribe,” and “mute” — count distinct accounts that filed one. (b) Pull the rate at which users disable email notifications in the first 30 days after joining. (c) Sample 20 notifications sent yesterday and ask: would I, as the recipient, want this?
I checked (a) yesterday: 58 distinct accounts, mostly individual contributors on shared boards. Restate the problem now in two sentences. No solutions. No softening words.
Individual contributors on shared boards receive notifications for activity on cards they don’t own or follow, producing fatigue that leads them to mute notifications globally rather than tune them. In the last quarter, 58 distinct accounts opened support tickets about this, and the typical pattern is to disable all notifications within two weeks of joining a noisy board.
That’s the problem. One last thing — argue that this isn’t the real problem.
The real problem may be a board hygiene issue: shared boards accumulate participants who shouldn’t be on them, and the notification volume is a downstream symptom. Fixing notifications fine-tunes the symptom; fixing membership management would address the cause. Also possible: the “notifications are bad” complaint is a proxy for “I don’t trust your product to respect my attention,” which any per-event tuning won’t fully solve.

That’s four rounds plus an optional fifth. Total time: maybe twelve minutes. The PM started with “notifications are bad” and ended with a problem statement that names a specific cohort, a specific behavior, a specific piece of evidence, and an honest counter-argument. None of the rounds asked for a solution. None of them pushed for prose. Each one made the gap a little smaller.

Notice what the AI did and didn’t do across the conversation. It enumerated possibilities the PM might have missed. It produced specific user moments when asked. It suggested where to look for evidence. It restated the problem when asked, in the words the PM steered it toward. It even argued against itself when invited. What it did not do: pick the interpretation, find the actual evidence, decide which counter-argument matters. Those calls were the PM’s.

Before and after, in one frame

Here’s the same problem written before the loop and after. Both took roughly the same wall-clock time to write. The difference isn’t in the prose — it’s in what the prose commits you to.

Problem: Users say our notifications are bad. We should improve the notification experience to reduce complaints and increase engagement. AI suggests adding a settings page with quiet hours, per-channel toggles, and a digest mode.
PROBLEM: Individual contributors on shared boards receive notifications for activity on cards they don’t own or follow, producing fatigue that leads them to mute notifications globally rather than tune them. USERS: Individual contributors who join an existing shared board with >5 active cards. Not board owners, not admins. EVIDENCE: 58 distinct accounts opened support tickets in Q2 matching “too many / noisy / turn off / mute.” Email-disable rate in the first 30 days for this cohort is 41% vs. 12% for single-board users. SUCCESS: Email-disable rate for new individual contributors on shared boards drops from 41% to under 20% within two release cycles, with no drop in board activity for the cohort.

The first version is a description of a feeling with a solution stapled on. The second version is a problem you can build around. It tells you who, it tells you what, it tells you how you know, and it tells you what changes if you’re right. It deliberately doesn’t tell you how to fix it — that’s the next step, and you’ll do it better because you got this step right.

You can imagine handing each version to an engineer. The first one produces a polite “okay, what do you want me to build?” The second one produces a real conversation about three or four design options, each clearly fit-for-purpose. That conversation is the payoff for the twelve minutes of sharpening you did upstream.

Three patterns AI quietly adds (and how to redirect)

If you do this loop a few times, you’ll start to notice the same three patterns showing up across tools. They aren’t failures — they’re defaults. Once you can name them, you can redirect in one sentence.

1. Skipping straight to the solution

The most common pattern: you ask for a restatement and the AI sneaks in a fix. “Users find notifications overwhelming — we should add a settings page with granular controls.” The first half is the problem; the second half is a solution you didn’t ask for. The model is trying to be helpful. Helpful here means premature.

Redirect: “Stay on the problem. No solutions yet. Just the shape of the gap.” Repeat as needed. Most tools settle into it after one or two corrections.

If the AI’s problem statement contains the words “we should,” “we need to,” or “the solution is,” you’re reading a half-statement of the problem and a half-proposal of the solution. That’s the cue to ask for the problem alone.

2. Inventing plausible context

Ask Windsurf or ChatGPT for evidence — “how would we know this is real?” — and you may get a confident reply with specific-sounding numbers: “In most SaaS products, about 30% of users disable notifications within their first week.” That number may be roughly true, made up, or wildly wrong — the AI doesn’t flag the difference. It just states it.

Redirect: when the AI offers a number, ask “is that from my product, or is that an industry estimate? If estimate, mark it as such.” The good models will then label their own claims appropriately. Either way, you replace the AI’s estimate with your real number before the problem statement gets locked.

If the AI gives you a percentage or a customer-name or a feature-name you don’t recognize from your own product, treat it as a placeholder until you can confirm. Plausible isn’t the same as true.

3. Reframing too neatly

Sometimes the AI’s restatement is suspiciously crisp. Two sentences, perfect parallel structure, no rough edges. It feels great to read. It also covers up the actual messiness of the problem, which usually has rough edges.

Redirect: “That’s too clean. Where is this problem messy in real life? What contradiction or trade-off would a user feel?” The honest answer almost always introduces a tension you’ll need to live with in the design.

If the AI’s restatement reads like a quote from a press release, slow down. Real problems have edges. A problem statement that resolves all the tensions on the page often resolved them by ignoring them.

Separating problem from solution

The single most common reason a sharpening loop stalls: you’re secretly working on the solution while pretending to define the problem. Symptoms include reaching for “we should add…” in your own typing, or evaluating each interpretation by “could we build that?” rather than “is that what’s actually wrong?”

A small habit that helps: when you sit down to sharpen, write “NO SOLUTIONS YET” at the top of the chat. It feels silly, and it works anyway. The instruction is partly for the model. It’s mostly for you.

Once you have a sharp problem statement, the solution conversation gets dramatically easier — because now there are clear criteria for what counts as a fix. Three different solution shapes become comparable on shared ground. That conversation is chapter 4’s territory.

Yours to decide: which sharpened problem is the one you commit to. Round 4 may give you two or three credible problem statements, each true in its own way. The AI can polish all three. Only you can pick the one your team will spend a sprint on — and stand behind in review, planning, and post-mortem.

A second mini-example: “the dashboard feels cluttered”

Same loop, different brief, in much less detail to show that the technique generalizes. A founder mentions in passing: “the dashboard feels cluttered.” You open Claude.

Round 1 — restate & interpret. The AI offers three readings: too many widgets on screen, too many competing visual styles, or wrong information density for the task users actually do there. You ask which is most consistent with recent feedback.

Round 2 — user moment. You push for the moment-in-the-product. The AI proposes: a returning user opens the dashboard at the start of their day to find out what changed since yesterday, but spends 40 seconds scanning before they find the “recent activity” section.

Round 3 — evidence. You check session recordings — the 40-second number is roughly right (your sample shows 30–60 seconds). You note that the “recent activity” section is buried below the fold on standard laptop resolutions.

Round 4 — sharpen. “Returning daily users currently spend 30–60 seconds locating the ‘what changed’ section of the dashboard each morning, because it’s buried below the fold and visually similar to the static configuration panels around it.”

Total time: about ten minutes. The brief “dashboard feels cluttered” would have produced a redesign. The sharpened problem will produce a much smaller, much more targeted change. The cost saved is a quarter of design and engineering time. The leverage is upstream, in the half-hour you spent narrowing the problem.

When the loop stalls

The loop is simple but it doesn’t always glide. A few stall patterns are worth recognizing — not as failures, just as common moments where you have to consciously change something to keep moving.

Stall 1: every interpretation feels equally true. You finish round 1 and you can’t pick. All three readings of the brief feel like they could be right. This usually means the brief is genuinely ambiguous and the next move isn’t another AI prompt — it’s a fifteen-minute conversation with whoever wrote the brief. “Hey, when you said ‘notifications are bad,’ were you thinking volume, relevance, or timing? What made you raise it now?” That conversation will collapse three readings into one, fast.

Stall 2: the AI keeps giving you the same answer in different words. You’re on round 4 and the model has stopped sharpening — each restatement is just a paraphrase of the last one. This usually means the AI has run out of context to draw on, and you need to feed it more before continuing. Paste in two recent support tickets. Drop in last quarter’s usage data summary. Add the relevant section of the product glossary. New context unlocks new sharpening.

Stall 3: you’ve sharpened a problem nobody asked you to solve. You finish round 4 with a beautiful problem statement — and realize the sharpened version is meaningfully different from what your VP actually wants. This is rare but useful when it happens. The right move is not to quietly substitute your sharpened problem for theirs. The right move is to bring both back to the VP and say: “You said X. When I sharpened it, the most defensible problem looked more like Y. Which one do you want me to scope?” Two sentences, ten minutes of their time, and you’ve avoided a quarter of the wrong work.

Stalls aren’t a sign you’re using the technique wrong. They’re a sign the technique is working — you’ve hit the edge of what AI alone can do, and the next move is human (a conversation, a data pull, a sanity check). The honest version of this craft includes those moves; the autopilot version pretends they aren’t needed.

Sharing the sharpened problem

Once you have a sharpened problem statement, what you do with it matters almost as much as the sharpening itself. Three small habits make the work pay off downstream.

Put it at the top of the spec, before anything else. Not in an appendix. Not buried under “Goals.” The four-field statement is the spec’s anchor; it should be the first thing every reviewer reads. When someone five weeks later asks “wait, why are we doing this?” the answer is one scroll away.

Share it before you write the rest. Send the four fields to your tech lead and your designer. Ask: “Does this problem feel real to you, before I scope a solution?” You’ll learn one of two things in five minutes — either they nod, in which case you proceed with confidence, or they raise something you missed, in which case you do another half-round before you’ve burned the rest of the spec on the wrong target.

Keep the rejected interpretations in your notes. The two readings of the brief you didn’t pick aren’t waste — they’re the candidates for next sprint, or the explanation you owe someone six weeks from now when they ask “did we consider X?” A short “Considered and deferred” section under your problem statement, with one line each, costs nothing to maintain and earns enormous trust over time.

None of these three habits requires AI. They’re what you do with the AI’s output once it’s sharpened. The sharpening is the leverage; the sharing is what compounds it across your team.

Reusable prompts for the loop

If you’d like a starting kit, these five prompts cover the cadence above. They work in any major tool — Copilot, Windsurf, Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — with at most a small tweak. Save them in a notes file; you’ll use them often.

  • Round 1: “Here’s the brief: [one line]. Don’t propose a solution. Restate it and give me three different interpretations of what could be going wrong.”
  • Round 2: “Those interpretations are too generic. For each, name a specific user, doing a specific thing in our product, hitting a specific friction.”
  • Round 3: “If interpretation X is correct, how would I know? What would I check first — tickets, sessions, calls, usage data?”
  • Round 4: “Given what we now know [paste evidence], restate the problem in two sentences. No solutions. No softening words like ‘improve’ or ‘optimize.’”
  • Round 5 (optional): “Make the strongest case that this isn’t the real problem at all. What might we be missing?”

You don’t need to use them in this exact order or this exact wording — the goal is the cadence, not the script. After a few weeks the prompts will adapt themselves to your voice and your product.

What round 1 looks like done well vs. done in a hurry

Round 1 sets the angle for everything that follows. A round 1 done in a hurry produces a chat thread that’s hard to recover — you spend the next three rounds undoing the framing the AI locked in too early. A round 1 done well opens the space the rest of the loop will explore.

The hurried version usually looks like this: you paste the brief, ask “what do you think we should do?”, and the AI returns a tidy three-paragraph answer with a problem framing and a recommended approach baked together. It looks complete. It is also a closed door — everything you ask after that point is now a tweak of the AI’s first proposal, not an exploration of the actual problem space. You can recover, but it’s harder than starting again.

The done-well version separates two things: restate and interpret. Restating is mechanical — the AI plays back the brief in its own words to confirm what it heard. Interpreting is generative — it produces multiple readings of what the brief might mean. Asking for both, in the same prompt, with explicit “no solutions yet,” keeps round 1 wide open.

Here’s a short comparison of the same brief, run two different ways. Same tool (Claude), same starting line, very different round-2 conversations.

Hurried version: “Users say notifications are bad. What should we do?”
I’d suggest a notification settings overhaul: add quiet hours, per-channel toggles, a digest mode, and a smart-default that learns from user behavior. This addresses the most common notification complaints and is well-precedented across SaaS products.
Done-well version: “Users say notifications are bad. Don’t propose a solution. Restate the brief in your own words and give me three different readings of what ‘bad’ could mean — with no preference among them.”
Restated: there is unspecified user dissatisfaction with notifications, surfaced via informal feedback. Three readings: (1) volume — too many, leading to fatigue. (2) relevance — the notifications fire for events users don’t care about. (3) timing — notifications arrive at the wrong moment or in the wrong channel.

Look at the second exchange compared to the first. The hurried version handed you a finished proposal you have to talk the AI out of. The done-well version handed you three doors and let you choose which one to open. The whole rest of the loop is easier from the second starting point.

Sharpening at different scales

A small tactical fix and a quarter-long initiative both benefit from sharpening — but the depth of the loop scales with the work. Knowing which mode you’re in saves you from over-investing on small things and under-investing on big ones.

Tiny brief (a one-day fix or a small bug investigation). One round is usually enough. Restate the brief, name the user moment, confirm the evidence in one number. The four-field statement might be three sentences total. Don’t spend twenty minutes on something that will cost two hours to build — the math doesn’t work.

Medium brief (a sprint-sized feature). Three to four rounds. This is the canonical version of the loop the chapter has been describing. The investment is twelve to twenty minutes; the saved cost is days of misaligned work.

Large brief (a quarter-long initiative or a strategic bet). Five or more rounds, often spread across multiple sessions and conversations with stakeholders. You’ll likely run the loop, get a sharpened problem, share it with leadership and engineering, get pushback, and run another loop on the parts that moved. The cadence is the same; the wall-clock duration is days, not minutes.

The mistake to avoid: running a tiny-brief depth on a large-brief problem. A vague strategic bet sharpened in twelve minutes will produce a confident-sounding statement that papers over the actual disagreement in your org. The leverage of this technique scales with how seriously you take the rounds.

How do you know you’ve sharpened enough?

Three quiet signals tell you a problem is sharp enough to move on from.

You can name the user as a person. Not “our users” or “the customer.” Someone you could describe in two sentences — what role, what moment, what they were trying to do. If your user is still “people who use the product,” you have another round to do.

You can write the success line in one sentence with a number in it. “Email-disable rate for this cohort drops from 41% to under 20% within two release cycles.” If your success line is “users will be happier,” the problem is still soft.

You can describe what would change for the user. If a friend asked “what will their day look like differently after this ships?” you can answer in plain language. If you can’t, the problem is still abstract.

None of these are arbitrary tests — they’re each a sign that one of the four fields (problem, users, evidence, success) has reached a usable level of specificity. When all three are yes, you’re ready to move into solution conversations.

If you re-read your sharpened problem statement and could swap in a different product’s name without changing anything else, it’s not yet sharp. Real problem statements are unmistakably about your product, your users, your data.

What about briefs that came from leadership?

Sometimes the fuzzy brief isn’t yours to question — it came from the CEO, the board, a major customer, a contractual commitment. The sharpening loop still applies; it just runs in a different mode. Instead of finding the problem, you’re making the inherited problem legible.

The four fields still work. PROBLEM gets framed as “leadership has identified X as the gap” with the same precision. USERS still needs naming — even an inherited brief has a real cohort behind it. EVIDENCE may be partially “leadership conviction based on Y conversation,” which is honest and useful. SUCCESS is what you and leadership agree counts as “done.” AI can help you draft each of these, especially the framing of leadership conviction in language that doesn’t sound like blame or skepticism.

The point isn’t to relitigate the inherited brief. It’s to write it down clearly enough that you, your engineers, and your future-self all know what was decided and why. That documentation is what protects the team six weeks later when the brief inevitably mutates in someone’s memory.

A short worry: “won’t this slow me down?”

Twelve minutes feels like a lot when your VP is waiting and your sprint planning is tomorrow. The honest answer: yes, twelve minutes is more than zero minutes. It is also dramatically less than the cost of building the wrong thing for two weeks and having to rescope.

Most of the time, this loop runs in the background of your day — you start it, do something else for an hour, come back, run round 3 with the evidence you pulled, finish the loop. The wall-clock cost is twelve minutes of attention spread across a morning. Compared to the cost of a poorly-defined sprint, the trade is wildly in your favor.

And once the loop becomes habit, you’ll start running a one-round version on briefs that don’t even need a full pass — just enough to catch the obviously-fuzzy ones before they hit a ticket. That’s the real compounding benefit: the technique gets cheaper the more you use it.

One more reframing that helps: the twelve minutes you spend sharpening isn’t time taken away from spec-writing — it’s time moved upstream. The PRD you’ll draft tomorrow afternoon will be 30% faster to write because the foundation is solid. The review meeting next week will be shorter because reviewers won’t need to re-litigate the basics. The engineering kickoff will be cleaner because the problem statement answers most of the questions before they’re asked. The twelve minutes show up later as hours saved, just in a different column of the ledger.

Try it this week

The Sharpening Drill. Pick one fuzzy brief on your desk this week — a Slack ask, a backlog item, a stakeholder request, anything that arrived as a sentence or two and feels under-defined.

  1. Open the AI tool of your choice (Copilot, Windsurf, Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — whichever you use most).
  2. Write at the top of the chat: “NO SOLUTIONS YET. We’re sharpening the problem.”
  3. Run the four-round loop using the prompt patterns above. Do all four, even if you think you’re done after round 2.
  4. For round 3, actually pull the evidence — check tickets, sessions, or whatever’s closest. Don’t accept the AI’s estimates as real numbers.
  5. At the end, write your sharpened problem statement in the four-field shape (Problem / Users / Evidence / Success).
  6. Compare it to the original brief. Note which of the three smell tests — solution-creep, invented context, or too-neat reframing — you had to redirect the AI on. That’s your tool’s default; knowing it makes the next loop faster.

Bonus credit: try the same brief in two different tools (Claude and Gemini, say) and compare. The sharpened problem statement should be roughly the same; the path to it will look different. That gap is your craft, not your tool.

One last look in the rear-view mirror

The whole chapter has been about a single technique: rounds, not one shot. Most of the leverage of AI in spec craft hides in this loop — not because the technique is sophisticated, but because the alternative (one prompt, full PRD, paste, ship) is so close at hand and so quietly costly.

Sharpening problems in rounds is the first habit that separates “I use AI to write specs” from “I write specs with AI as a co-pilot.” Both look similar from the outside. They produce wildly different artifacts. The four-field statement at the top of the chapter is the smallest, most portable artifact of this craft — once you have one in hand, every later step gets easier and every later chapter of this course has something concrete to work on.
