Chapter 12

Habits That Keep You in Control

The kit you built last chapter is the artefact. The habits in this chapter are what keep the artefact sharp and keep you in the driver’s seat as the months stack up.

Six months in

Imagine yourself on a Tuesday morning, six months from now. You sit down with coffee, open your AI tool of the moment, and paste in the context pack you refreshed last quarter. The first response back is sharp because the assistant already knows the product. You spend twenty minutes drafting the AC for a small in-flight spec; you would have spent an hour on it last year. By 10am the morning’s mechanical work is done. The kit has earned its place. The habits, mostly, are still doing their quiet work in the background.

Now imagine the same Tuesday playing out a different way. The kit has settled. The prompt library is at its soft cap. The context pack got refreshed last quarter when your team launched a new segment. You’ve switched AI tools once already — from one chat assistant to another — and the migration took an afternoon because everything was plain markdown. By any measurable standard, you’re faster at spec work than you were on day one of this course.

And yet the question that matters most isn’t whether you’re faster. It’s whether the specs you’re shipping six months in still sound like you. Whether the calls you’re making are still calls you’d defend in front of a senior eng review, an angry customer, or a worried CFO. Whether you’re still asking the awkward “hmm” questions in discovery, or whether you’ve quietly stopped because the assistant’s first answer sounded plausible enough.

That’s the trade-off this final chapter is about. AI use compounds; speed compounds; consistency compounds. So does drift, if nothing pulls against it. The pull comes from habits — small, daily, almost-boring practices that keep your judgment muscle warm even on the days the kit could carry the spec by itself.

Five habits, walked through in this chapter. None of them is dramatic. None of them takes long. Their power is entirely in the repetition. The PMs who feel most in command three years into the AI era are the ones who built and held these habits. The PMs who feel most carried-along by the assistant are the ones who built the kit and skipped the habits. Same tools; very different relationships to the work.

Why habits over tools

Tools change every quarter. Last year’s favourite assistant is this year’s second choice. The features that mattered to you in March get matched by a competitor in June. By the end of any twelve-month stretch, the tools you reach for daily look meaningfully different from the ones you reached for at the start.

Habits don’t care. The five-minute end-of-day log works the same in Claude as it does in ChatGPT, Copilot, Windsurf, or Gemini. The weekly spec review doesn’t depend on which tool wrote which paragraph. The cadence with engineering is a calendar discipline, not a software feature. When the tool changes, the habit stays.

Why this matters. The PMs who feel most in control three years into the AI era aren’t the ones who picked the right tool early. They’re the ones who built habits that survived a tool switch. The habit is the durable layer; the tool is the replaceable layer.

This chapter walks through five habits, in order from daily to less frequent. None of them takes long. The whole stack costs maybe twenty minutes a day on a heavy day, and the compounding payoff is that your sense of where you stand on every spec stays clear. You’re never surprised by your own work three weeks after shipping it, because you’ve been keeping notes the whole time.

Habit 1: The “what did I decide today” log

Five minutes at the end of the day. One file, append-only, three lines or fewer. The line shape is brutally simple: what I decided, why I decided it, what I’d watch for tomorrow. Some days you’ll have one line; some days three; some days you’ll skip because nothing real got decided. All of those are fine.

Here’s a week’s worth of entries from a working PM’s log. Names changed; shape preserved.

Decision log — week of 2026-04-27 Mon: Cut the second admin role from v1 of the invite-flow spec. Why: the use case is real but only for ~3 enterprise accounts; not worth the AC sprawl in a 2-week scope. Watch: re-open if a 4th account asks in Q3. Tue: (no decision worth logging — mostly review and discovery) Wed: Held the 7-day invite expiry against design’s push for 30 days. Why: the data shows 95% of accepted invites land in 4 days; longer windows mostly grow stale-link incidents. Watch: re-check after the SSO-routing fix lands; that may shift the distribution. Thu: Asked eng to defer the “bulk invite” surface to v2. Why: spec is already at the edge of the team’s capacity; I’d rather ship the core well than three things half-done. Watch: customer success may push back; have the v2 timing ready before the next CS sync. Fri: Approved the AI-augmented edit pass on the spec’s open-questions section — rewrote the intro paragraph in my own voice afterwards. Why: the AI version was clearer but lost the “why we care” framing. Wanted to be sure the framing carried through. Watch: see if reviewers comment on the intro; if not, the rewrite worked.

Five entries in a week, three to five lines each. The whole file fits on a single page after a year. The value isn’t in any single entry — it’s in the cumulative record. After three months, the log is the most honest answer you have to the question “what did I actually do this quarter?”

Two practical notes. First, write the log before closing your laptop, not after the fact in the morning — the call you made an hour ago is much sharper in your head than one you made yesterday. Second, do not let an AI tool write the log for you. The whole point is the act of articulating your own decisions in your own voice. If you outsource that to Claude or ChatGPT, the log loses what makes it useful: the trace of your reasoning.

What does the log unlock after a quarter of doing it? Three things, mostly. You start spotting patterns in your own decisions — the kinds of trade-offs you keep making the same way, which is useful when you want to test whether you’re actually weighing them or whether they’ve become reflex. You build a record you can pull up before any review or perf conversation, with one-line answers to “what did you ship and why,” ready to defend. And you have honest evidence, three months later, of whether the “watch for” lines came true — which is the closest thing to a feedback loop your own judgment ever gets.

Habit 2: The weekly spec review

Once a week, ideally Friday afternoon when the brain is already winding down, re-read every spec you shipped, edited, or seriously updated that week. Twenty minutes max. You’re not editing — the specs are out the door. You’re reading to mark.

Two marks, scribbled in the margin or in a private comment thread. The first mark goes on passages that are clearly yours: the framing of the problem, the call on scope, the paragraph where you pushed back on a reviewer. The second mark goes on passages that are clearly AI-augmented: the AC table the assistant generated and you mostly kept, the risk register that came out of a prompt template, the translated-for-leadership intro you tweaked but didn’t rewrite.

The point of the marks is calibration. Over a few weeks of doing this, you build an honest internal map of which parts of your spec work are genuinely yours and which parts are riding on the assistant. If the AI-augmented marks start outweighing the “clearly yours” marks — especially in places where judgment matters — that’s a signal to pull back, not a verdict on your competence. Just a signal.

The opposite signal is also worth catching: weeks where the “clearly yours” marks dominate everywhere, including in the mechanical sections. That usually means you stopped using the kit and reverted to freelancing. Slower output for no extra judgment. The Friday review surfaces that just as quickly as it surfaces over-reliance.

One small rule that makes the review honest: don’t use AI to do the marking. The whole point is your own reading. If you ask an assistant to flag which paragraphs sound AI-generated, you’ll get a confident answer that’s often wrong — assistants are bad at recognising their own voice once it’s been edited. Do this one with your eyes only. It takes fifteen minutes; it’s the only fifteen minutes a week where you read your own work the way a reader will.

Habit 3: The monthly prompt audit

Once a month, sit with the prompt library and the context pack for thirty minutes. This is the same review you saw at the end of chapter 11 — prune, add, refresh — only this time we’re going to walk it as a habit, not as a one-off.

The audit is the part where it’s easiest to slip into autopilot, ironically, because the assistant is genuinely useful for it. Done well, it’s a real conversation. Here’s the shape:

[Pasted: full current prompt library, all 14 entries.]

Walk this library as a critical reviewer. For each prompt, tell me: (a) is the “when to use” line still accurate, or has the actual use shifted? (b) is the prompt itself longer than it needs to be? (c) does it overlap with another prompt in the library? Don’t suggest deletions yet — just flag what’s drifted.
Walking the 14 prompts. Five flags worth your attention:

sharpen-problem-statement (v2): the “when to use” says “early discovery,” but the input shape now lists three artefacts that imply you’re past discovery. Either the line is stale or the input shape is.

generate-edge-cases (v3) and pressure-test-spec (v1): 60% overlap in the prompt body. Both ask the assistant to find what’s missing; the difference between “edge cases” and “pressure test” isn’t crisp in either prompt.

draft-handoff-summary (v2): the prompt grew from 4 lines to 11 over its versions. Most of the additions are hedges. The original v1 (which you archived) was sharper.

translate-for-audience (v2): still good, no flags.

summarize-decision-log (v1): hasn’t been used in your last 8 weeks of spec sessions, based on the recency notes you pasted.
Useful. On the sharpen-problem-statement drift — that’s real, I’ve been using it later in the spec life cycle. Suggest two ways to fix: split into two prompts, or rewrite the “when to use” to match the actual usage. Don’t pick for me; I want to weigh the trade-off.
Two options, with trade-offs:

Option A — split into two prompts. Keep the original sharpen-problem-statement for early discovery; create sharpen-spec-section for later refinement. Pro: each prompt is tighter and produces sharper output. Con: library grows by one entry, costing you against the soft cap; you have to remember which to use when.

Option B — rewrite the “when to use” line. Update it to: “Anytime a paragraph is fuzzier than its readers can act on — discovery or refinement.” Pro: library stays at 14 entries. Con: the prompt body still skews toward discovery; outputs at the refinement end may stay slightly off.

Option A is sharper but costs library headroom. Option B is cheaper but accepts mediocrity at one end.
Going with B for now — the cost of a 15th entry is real and I’m not yet sure the refinement use case is durable. Re-check in three months.

Notice the shape of this conversation. The PM brings the artefacts (the library), names the kind of review they want (drift, length, overlap), and explicitly holds the assistant back from making the call (“don’t suggest deletions yet”). The assistant flags; the PM weighs. When trade-offs come up, the PM asks for trade-offs framed clearly, then makes the choice and logs it. That’s the prompt audit working as a habit, not as outsourcing.

Habit 4: The weekly cadence with engineering

Fifteen minutes, once a week, with one engineer from the team. Not a stand-up. Not a planning meeting. A standing slot whose only purpose is spec calibration: how is the current spec landing, what’s ambiguous, what’s landing well, what would you have written differently.

The reason this habit works is selection. Over a quarter, you cycle through four or five engineers from the same team. Each one gives you a different read on the same kind of spec. The one who fixes infra bugs sees scope risk you don’t. The one who builds UI sees AC gaps that show up on the screen. The one who’s newest to the team sees the parts that assume too much shared context. None of them is wrong; all of them sharpen the spec craft faster than any solo review with an AI assistant could.

The fifteen-minute time box matters. Longer and the meeting drifts into project planning. Shorter and you don’t get past pleasantries. Fifteen minutes is enough to walk through one spec and surface one or two things; that’s the right unit of work for a weekly slot.

Here’s the shape that tends to work. The same shape every week, so neither of you spends time figuring out what kind of meeting this is.

Standing 15-min spec calibration — weekly 00:00–02:00  Pick the spec. (The one closest to estimation, or the one that’s causing the most ambiguity in stand-ups.) 02:00–06:00  Engineer reads two or three sections out loud, slowly. Whatever sounds wrong or vague gets flagged in the moment. 06:00–11:00  Conversation on the flags. PM asks: “what would you have written instead?” Engineer answers in plain words, not edits. PM takes notes by hand. 11:00–14:00  PM names one change to make in the spec this week, out loud. (Saying it commits it.) Engineer either agrees or pushes back — both are useful. 14:00–15:00  Pick whose turn next week. (Rotate through the team.) Done.

Notice what this slot is and isn’t. It isn’t a place to negotiate scope, debate estimates, or surface blockers — those have their own meetings. It’s a place to calibrate spec quality with one human at a time, in a room where neither of you has anything else to defend. After two months of these, you’ll notice your specs landing cleaner the first time engineers read them. The compounding is real and quiet.

Habit 5: Knowing when to set AI aside

Some moments don’t belong to the assistant. Naming them up front, before they happen, is what keeps the boundary clean.

When AI doesn’t belong in the room.

  • Customer interviews. The point of the interview is to hear unfiltered customer voice. Recording, sure. Live transcription, fine. Letting an assistant suggest follow-up questions in real time? You’ll miss the half-spoken pause that contains the actual insight.
  • Post-incident reviews. The room is already nervous. An AI summary of who said what makes it more nervous. Take notes by hand; let the conversation breathe.
  • Scope-cutting calls with engineering or design. The call requires holding several humans’ trade-offs in one head — not a model’s trade-offs.
  • One-on-ones with your manager about your work. Whatever the assistant would say about your last quarter, it’s your account of it that matters. Don’t outsource the framing.
  • The first hour after a customer escalation. The assistant cannot tell you which of the three angry emails is the one that ends in churn. Read them yourself, in their original tone.

Notice that none of these moments are about the assistant being technically incapable. The assistant could write a competent customer-interview question or a serviceable post-incident summary. The point is that the act of being in the moment, listening with your full attention, is what makes the next decision a good one. Habit five is the discipline of being fully present when presence is the work.

A small example of how this plays out. A PM is on a customer call with an enterprise admin who’s frustrated with the new invite flow. The temptation is to have ChatGPT or Gemini running in a side window, summarising in real time, suggesting follow-ups. The PM tries it for the first ten minutes. It’s technically working. But the PM notices something: they’re reading the assistant’s suggested follow-up while the customer is mid-sentence, and the customer’s tone has just shifted in a way that wasn’t in the words. The PM closes the side window. Twenty minutes later they realise the thing the customer most needed wasn’t named in any sentence — it was in a pause. The pause wouldn’t have made it through the assistant. That’s the whole reason habit five exists.

When habits stall: a short troubleshooting guide

None of these habits is hard in any single instance. Holding them for months is the part that takes work. Here are three failure modes and the small fixes that bring the habit back.

The log goes blank for a week. First check: was the week actually decision-light, or did you just forget the log existed? If decision-light, ignore it; some weeks are. If you forgot, move the log to a place you can’t miss — a sticky tab in your browser, the first item in your daily notes app, a calendar reminder at 5:30pm. The habit isn’t broken; the trigger is.

The Friday spec review keeps getting pushed. The slot is real but you haven’t done it in three weeks. Usually this means the slot is too long or too late in the day. Try fifteen minutes instead of twenty, at 3pm instead of 4:30. The work isn’t bigger; the timing is fighting you.

The monthly prompt audit feels like a chore. Almost certainly because the kit has grown past where you can audit it in thirty minutes. Cap the audit at thirty minutes; whatever you don’t reach, leave for next month. Or shrink the kit until thirty minutes is enough. A small kit you audit beats a big kit you avoid.

Signals you’re slipping into autopilot

This is the smell test for the whole stack. None of these signals is a verdict; each one is a friendly nudge to look up from the screen and check whether the driver is still driving.

Six signals worth catching early.

  1. AI tone in your own writing. You re-read a slack message you sent and it has “leverage” or “robust” or “seamless” in it — words you don’t actually use in conversation. The assistant’s voice has crept into your hands.
  2. Fewer questions in reviews. Last quarter you used to push back on engineering’s estimates and design’s framing. This quarter you’ve mostly nodded along. Not because they got better; because asking feels like extra work.
  3. No “hmm” moments. A week goes by without the small pause where something an interviewer or engineer or customer said made you stop and think. The assistant has been smoothing the rough patches that used to make you stop.
  4. You can’t name your own framing. Someone asks why you scoped the spec the way you did, and your first instinct is to scroll back through the doc to find the answer. The framing came from somewhere; you’re no longer sure where.
  5. Specs reading more alike than they used to. The last three specs you shipped have the same paragraph rhythm, the same headings, the same shape of risk register. The kit is doing too much; your voice is doing too little.
  6. Decisions log going quiet. The end-of-day log has had blank days for a stretch. Either the days really are decision-light (possible, but rare) or the decisions are happening and you’re not noticing them. The second case is the autopilot tell.

The check. If two or more of these are showing up at the same time, treat it as a friendly heads-up, not a failure report. Take a single Friday afternoon, set the assistant aside, and write one section of the next spec entirely by hand. Notice what comes back.

The framing matters here. None of those signals means you’ve done anything wrong. They mean the conditions for autopilot are ripe and a small course-correction will keep you in the driver’s seat. The reason the signals exist is that AI use compounds in two directions at once — toward leverage, and toward atrophy — and the only person in a position to notice the second direction is you.

What does the recovery look like, when the signals are firing? Smaller than you’d expect. You don’t need to throw the kit out, swear off AI for a month, or rebuild your prompt library. You take one Friday afternoon, set the assistant aside, and write one section of the next spec entirely by hand. Maybe the problem statement; maybe the open-questions list. The slow re-engagement of the muscle that’s been resting is the whole intervention. Often, three or four hand-written sections across two weeks is enough to reset the calibration. Then you go back to using AI as a co-pilot, with your own voice fresh in your ears again.

One more thing worth noting about the recovery: it’s easier the earlier you catch it. A PM who notices two of the autopilot signals in week three of the slip and does a single hand-written section recovers in a week. A PM who notices the same signals six months in, after the kit has done most of the writing for that whole stretch, has a longer climb back — not impossible, but harder. The Friday spec review is what makes early detection routine. Without it, the signals tend to surface only when something external pushes them up: a confused customer call, a senior reviewer’s sharp comment, a perf conversation where you struggle to name what was actually yours. Better to catch them on a Friday afternoon, when the only stake is the next week.

The fluency curve

The course you’ve been reading covers the first thirty days of the practice. The interesting question is what happens after that. Three checkpoints are worth knowing.

3 mo
The mechanics click

The kit is solid. Prompts feel automatic. Sharpening a fuzzy brief takes a third of the time it did on day one. You stop noticing the assistant most of the time; it’s become a tool, not an event.

6 mo
The judgment shows up

You start trusting your gut when something the assistant produced feels wrong. The smell tests run automatically; you don’t consciously walk the seven from chapter 8 anymore. Reviews get faster because you spot the issues before the reviewer does.

12 mo
The role shifts

You spend less time on prose and more on the parts of the job AI can’t touch: customer time, written argument, taste calls, escalation judgment. The mechanical layer takes a quarter of the calendar it used to. The judgment layer expands to fill the space.

The curve isn’t guaranteed. PMs who skip the habits tend to plateau around month three: faster at the mechanical layer, no sharper at the judgment layer. PMs who run the habits consistently hit the twelve-month shape and stay there. Same kit, same tools, very different careers.

How the role grows from here

The PM job in 2026 has a different centre of gravity than the PM job in 2020. The mechanical work — drafting, summarising, reformatting, translating, generating — has gotten dramatically faster. The judgment work — what to build, who for, when to ship, what to cut — hasn’t. The job is shifting toward the judgment work, because that’s where the leverage now sits.

Three things are worth investing in for the next stretch.

Customer time. Not user testing of finished work; raw interviews with the people who pay you. Two hours a week, minimum. The assistant cannot replace this and will not get any closer to replacing it. It’s the durable source of taste.

Written argument. The ability to write a clear three-paragraph argument for or against a product call — in your own voice, defensible to a senior reviewer, ready to be wrong — is the skill the next decade rewards most. The assistant can help draft; only you can hold the position.

Cross-discipline literacy. Enough engineering to pressure-test estimates. Enough design to recognise good interaction shapes. Enough finance to defend a metric. The assistant can supplement any of these on demand, but it can’t replace your ability to recognise when an answer is wrong — and that recognition only comes from carrying enough of the discipline yourself.

Notice that none of those three are tool skills. None of them depend on which AI assistant you’re using this year. They’re the muscles that compound regardless of the tooling layer, and the more time the kit and the habits free up for them, the more your role grows in the direction the era rewards.

A useful frame: imagine the calendar of a PM twelve months into this practice. The mechanical layer — drafting AC, summarising threads, reformatting for audiences, generating edge cases — takes maybe four hours a week. The judgment layer — customer interviews, written argument, scope-cutting calls, escalation conversations — takes the rest. A year earlier, that ratio was inverted. The work didn’t shrink; the centre of gravity moved. That movement is what the kit and the habits were for all along.

How this looks across different PM roles

The five habits are written for a generic PM, but PMs aren’t generic. The shape adapts depending on what kind of work fills your week. Three quick adaptations worth naming.

If you’re a consumer PM — high-volume specs, fast feedback loops, lots of A/B tests — the daily log is doing more work than you’d expect. You’re making three or four scope or experiment-design calls a day, most of them small, and without a log they blur into one indistinguishable stream. The weekly spec review sometimes shrinks to a glance; the monthly prompt audit sometimes grows because you accumulate prompts faster. Hold the daily log especially carefully.

If you’re a B2B / enterprise PM — longer specs, slower feedback, account-specific edge cases — the engineering cadence is the high-leverage habit. Your specs land on engineers who need to understand industry constraints, contractual obligations, customer-specific exceptions. Fifteen minutes a week with one engineer is where you find out which assumptions in your spec haven’t survived contact with the implementation. The log and the audit still matter; the engineering slot is the one that protects shipping quality most directly.

If you’re a senior or staff PM — multiple teams, less direct spec authorship, more reviewing — the weekly spec review changes shape. You’re re-reading specs from your reports as much as your own. The marking exercise becomes “is the human voice still showing through here, or has the kit absorbed it?” The signal you’re looking for shifts from your own drift to your team’s. The habit doesn’t go away; it points outward instead of inward.

None of these adaptations changes the core. Daily log, weekly review, monthly audit, weekly engineering cadence, knowing when to set AI aside. The shape stays; the proportions adjust to your actual week.

Habits with a partner

All five habits work solo. Some of them get sharper with a partner — not a manager, not a report, but a peer-PM, ideally on a different team, who’s also trying to keep their own driving-seat practice alive. The partnership doesn’t need to be formal. Two people, one short standing chat, a shared interest in the craft.

What works well in pairs: the weekly spec review (you each pick one shipped paragraph, swap, mark each other’s yours-vs-AI-augmented passages, compare); the monthly prompt audit (you each bring two prompts you’re unsure about, the other person stress-tests them); the autopilot smell test (you describe a recent week, the other person flags any of the six signals showing up). What doesn’t work well in pairs: the daily log. That one’s yours alone, and showing it to anyone risks turning it into something performative rather than honest.

The partner system has one specific virtue worth naming: a peer notices things about your work that you’re too close to notice yourself. The third-paragraph hedge that’s become reflex; the AI tone in the slack message you wrote; the customer segment you’ve stopped naming because you assume everyone knows. A good partner asks “why this phrasing?” once a week and that question alone is often worth the partnership.

If you can’t find a peer, an asynchronous version works almost as well: write a short Friday note describing one judgment call you made that week, send it to a trusted colleague, and ask only that they read it. You don’t need a reply. The act of articulating the call to a real human reader (not the assistant, who will just agree) sharpens the call itself. After a few months, the colleague will start replying anyway, and you’ll have built something close to the partner version by accident.

If you only do one of these

Five habits is a lot to start at once. If the five feel like too much, here’s the rank order, by how much each one protects your driving-seat practice in isolation.

Start with the daily log. Five minutes, three lines, end of day. It’s the smallest possible commitment and it gives you the most honest mirror of your work. Most of the other habits get easier once the log is running, because the log is what makes drift visible.

Add the weekly spec review next. Fifteen minutes, Friday afternoon, mark your shipped work. Pairs naturally with the log: at the end of the week, you have both a record of the calls you made and a read on whether they’re still showing up in your written work.

Then the monthly audit. Thirty minutes, last Friday of the month. The kit you built in chapter 11 will start drifting in the second month; this is what catches the drift before it costs you a session.

The engineering cadence and knowing when to set AI aside both compound the others, but neither is foundational the way the first three are. Hold the bottom of the stack first. Add the rest as the practice settles.

A note on consistency over intensity

One framing worth carrying with you: these habits reward consistency far more than intensity. Five minutes of the decision log every weekday for a quarter beats one frantic three-hour session of writing down everything you remember from the past month. Fifteen minutes of spec review every Friday beats a four-hour deep audit you do once a year on the company retreat. The habits are designed to be small specifically so you can hold them through bad weeks, busy weeks, and the quiet weeks between projects. An ambitious habit that survives two months is worth less than a small habit that survives two years.

The implication for getting started: don’t over-design. The first decision-log entry doesn’t need to be in the perfect file format. The first weekly spec review doesn’t need a checklist. The first prompt audit doesn’t need a polished template. The habit’s value comes from doing it; the format settles into something workable after about three or four iterations. Better to start rough on Monday than to wait until you’ve picked the perfect note-taking app to begin.

What habits won’t fix

Worth naming honestly: there are real failure modes in spec work that no amount of personal habit will catch. The habits keep you in the driving seat; they don’t guarantee the road is going somewhere good.

If your team is solving the wrong problem at the company level, your daily log will document a quarter of well-reasoned decisions about a thing that shouldn’t have been built. The log will be honest; the work will still have been wasted. That kind of misalignment shows up in roadmap reviews and customer truth, not in personal-habit territory.

If your stakeholders don’t value clear specs — if pressure-tested AC and visible open-questions sections get you punished rather than rewarded — the habits will still keep your judgment sharp, but the specs themselves will start to feel like effort thrown into a hole. That’s an organisational problem, not a personal one. The fix is harder and slower and lives in conversations with leadership, not in tweaks to your prompt library.

And if your relationship with your own work is strained — burnout, low motivation, the kind of disengagement that keeps you on the screen but not in the work — the habits will feel performative. The log will be empty; the Friday review won’t happen; the audit will get pushed. The habits aren’t the fix for that; rest is. Notice it, name it, take care of it, and the habits will be there when you come back. They are patient.

The honest framing is that the habits sharpen you for work that’s worth doing. They don’t make work worth doing on their own. That’s a different problem and it lives outside this course. Knowing the limit makes the habits more useful, not less — you stop expecting them to fix things they were never built to fix, and you turn the right questions toward the right places.

Year two and beyond: where the practice goes

The course covered the first thirty days. The fluency curve named months three, six, and twelve. What about the years after that?

Year two tends to be quiet, in a good way. The kit barely changes; the habits run almost without thought; the calendar has shifted decisively toward judgment work. The interesting question stops being “how do I integrate AI into my spec craft?” and becomes something more useful: “what should I be doing with the time the practice has freed up?”

The answers are personal. Some PMs go deeper into customer truth — doubling their interview cadence, learning to run analysis sessions on raw transcripts, building a stronger sense of taste from raw input. Some go deeper into written argument — longer-form pieces, clearer position-taking, more time on the few documents per quarter that actually shape strategy. Some go cross-discipline — learning enough engineering to estimate alongside their team, enough design to recognise good interaction shapes, enough finance to defend metrics under pressure.

None of those second-year directions are about AI tooling. They’re about what you do with the calendar the tooling gave back. The kit and the habits were the bridge; the bridge held; the question now is what’s on the other side of the bridge for you specifically. That’s a question this course can’t answer, and the answer is more interesting because of it.

One pattern worth flagging, though, because it shows up in more PMs than you’d expect. Some PMs in year two notice that the parts of the work they enjoy most have shifted — that the customer interview, or the written argument, or the scope-cutting call now feels like the centre of the job, where it used to feel like the cost of getting to the “real” work of authoring specs. That shift is usually a sign that the practice is working as intended. The mechanical layer has receded; the judgment layer has taken centre stage; the parts of the role that compound over a career are now what fills your time. If that’s where you find yourself, follow it. The tooling will keep getting better; the part of the job you’ve uncovered is the part that’ll still be yours in twenty years.

Try It tonight: day one of the log

Before you close your laptop, open a fresh markdown file. Name it decision-log.md. Write three lines, one each, in this shape:

  1. What I decided today. Pick the most product-relevant call you made — even a small one. (“Held the deadline against design’s push to add a settings toggle.” “Cut a section from the spec on review.” “Said no to the surprise meeting.”)
  2. Why I decided it. One sentence. Not a justification — an honest reason. (“Because the toggle adds two AC and we’re already at scope-edge.”)
  3. What I’ll watch for tomorrow. One thing that would tell you the call was wrong. (“If design surfaces a use case I missed in the next sync, I’ll re-open.”)

That’s the entry. Five minutes, three lines, day one of a habit that runs as long as you’re writing specs. Tomorrow night, do it again. By Friday you’ll have your first week.
