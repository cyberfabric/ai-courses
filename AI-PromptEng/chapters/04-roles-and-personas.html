Chapter 4

Role Play and Personas

Make the AI wear different hats — how personas shape tone, expertise, and output quality

The Power of "You Are..."

In Chapter 3, you learned how to structure your thinking with zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought. Now we'll change who the AI thinks it is — and watch the output transform completely.

Two words can dramatically change an AI's response: "You are..."

Tell it "You are a senior Android developer," and it writes production-grade Kotlin with proper error handling. Tell it "You are a QA engineer," and it immediately thinks about edge cases and failure modes. Tell it "You are a tech lead reviewing a junior's PR," and the tone shifts to constructive, mentor-like feedback.

This is persona prompting, and it's one of the highest-impact techniques in your toolkit.

Why Personas Work

AI models are trained on text written by millions of people in different roles. When you say "You are a security researcher," you're not casting a magic spell — you're activating the patterns associated with how security researchers write, think, and prioritize.

The analogy: Think of a persona like tuning a radio. All the stations are always broadcasting, but the dial selects which one you hear. "You are a..." is you turning the dial to the station that plays the kind of output you want.

Without a persona, the AI defaults to a generic, middle-of-the-road response — like hearing all stations at once. It's technically correct but lacks depth, focus, and the right tone for your situation.

Without Persona

What should I consider when adding push notifications to my Android app?
When adding push notifications, consider the following:
- Choose a service (FCM, OneSignal, etc.)
- Handle permissions
- Design notification content
- Test on different devices
- Consider user preferences

(Generic list that could be from any blog post)

With Persona

You are a senior Android developer with 8 years of experience shipping production apps. You've implemented push notifications in multiple apps and dealt with the edge cases that trip people up.

What should I consider when adding push notifications to my Android app?
Here's what actually matters from having shipped this multiple times:

The things that will bite you:
- FCM tokens refresh silently. If you store them once and never update, 20% of your users will stop getting notifs within 3 months. Subscribe to onNewToken() and update your backend.
- Android 13+ requires POST_NOTIFICATIONS runtime permission. Many teams forget this and wonder why notifs don't work on new devices.
- Don't rely on data messages arriving when the app is killed. Samsung and Xiaomi battery optimization kills your service. Use notification messages as a fallback.
- Notification channels are mandatory since API 26. Create them in Application.onCreate(), not lazily...

(Goes deep into production-tested advice you won't find in tutorials)

Same question. Wildly different value. The persona made the AI draw from its "senior developer who's been burned by edge cases" knowledge, not its "introductory tutorial" knowledge.

Five Basic Roles You Should Know

You can assign any role, but these five archetypes are useful across almost every situation:

Expert
"You are an expert in..." — Deep, technical, authoritative answers. Best for knowledge-heavy tasks.
Critic
"You are a harsh code reviewer..." — Finds flaws, challenges assumptions. Best for review and QA tasks.
Teacher
"You are a patient instructor..." — Explains clearly, uses analogies. Best for learning and onboarding.
Interviewer
"You are an interviewer for a senior role..." — Asks probing questions. Best for prep and self-testing.
Translator
"You are a technical-to-business translator..." — Converts jargon to plain language. Best for cross-team communication.
Pick the role based on what you need, not what sounds impressive. If you want brutal honesty about your code, use the Critic. If you want to learn, use the Teacher. If you want to communicate to stakeholders, use the Translator. The role steers the output.

The Specificity Spectrum

Not all personas are created equal. There's a spectrum from vague to hyper-specific, and where you land on it matters.

From Generic to Laser-Focused

  1. Level 1 (Generic): "You are an expert." — Slightly better than nothing, but barely.
  2. Level 2 (Role): "You are a senior Android developer." — Good. Activates Android + experience patterns.
  3. Level 3 (Role + Context): "You are a senior Android developer at a fintech company." — Better. Now it considers compliance, security, and financial UX.
  4. Level 4 (Full Persona): "You are a senior Android developer at a fintech company with 10 years of experience in Kotlin. You've worked on banking apps that handle millions of transactions daily. You're known for writing clean, testable code and catching performance issues before they reach production." — Best. Hyper-focused output.

You don't always need Level 4. For a quick question, Level 2 is fine. But for important tasks — architecture decisions, code reviews, technical documentation — the extra specificity pays off massively.

Level 4 persona in action:

You are a senior iOS developer with 12 years of experience, specializing in SwiftUI and Combine. You've worked on apps with millions of users. You have strong opinions about architecture and prefer MVVM with coordinators. You're meticulous about memory management and performance.

Review this SwiftUI view for potential issues. Focus on performance, memory leaks, and architectural concerns.

The AI will now review code through the lens of someone who cares about these specific things. It will flag retain cycles, unnecessary view recomputations, and architectural concerns that a generic review would miss.

Role Stacking: Combining Personas

Sometimes you need more than one perspective. Role stacking combines multiple areas of expertise in a single persona.

Single Role

You are a senior Android developer. Review this API integration code.

Good — but only catches developer-facing issues.

Stacked Role

You are a senior Android developer who also has 5 years of backend experience and has worked closely with QA teams. You understand both sides of the API contract and think like a tester when reviewing client code.

Now the AI considers: does the client handle all backend error codes? Are there race conditions the QA team would catch? Is the retry logic solid?

Don't go overboard. Two or three stacked roles work great. Five stacked roles creates a contradictory mess. The AI can't simultaneously be a "risk-averse security auditor" and a "move-fast-and-break-things startup CTO."

Ready-Made Personas for Your Team

Here are battle-tested personas for different roles on a mobile development team. Copy, adapt, and use them.

The "Explain It To..." Technique

You can also set the persona of the audience instead of the AI itself. This adjusts the complexity level of the output.

Adjust Complexity by Changing the Audience

Instead of "You are an expert" (which makes the AI talk as an expert), try "Explain this to a..." (which makes the AI explain for a specific audience). The output adapts to the audience's level.

"Explain to a junior dev"

Explain what coroutines are in Kotlin. Assume I'm a junior developer who understands basic Kotlin but has never used coroutines.

Result: Simple, step-by-step explanation with analogies and basic examples. No assumed knowledge.

"Explain to a tech lead"

Explain what coroutines are in Kotlin. Assume I'm a tech lead who knows threading and RxJava but wants to understand how coroutines compare.

Result: Comparison with RxJava, discussion of structured concurrency, performance characteristics, and migration strategy.

Same topic. Completely different depth and approach. The audience persona is one of the most underused techniques in prompt engineering.

Where to Put Your Personas

In Chapter 2, we discussed the instruction hierarchy. Personas can live at different levels, and where you put them matters.

System-Level Personas

  • Where: CLAUDE.md, .cursorrules, ChatGPT custom instructions, API system messages
  • When: For persistent personas you want across all interactions
  • Example: "You are a coding assistant for an Android fintech app. Always consider security, performance, and accessibility."
  • Benefit: Set it once, it applies to every conversation

User-Level Personas

  • Where: In the chat message itself
  • When: For one-off tasks where you need a specific perspective
  • Example: "Pretend you're a QA engineer and try to break this feature."
  • Benefit: Flexible, changes per prompt, no configuration needed
Power move for teams: Set up a shared CLAUDE.md or .cursorrules with your team's default persona — the tech stack, code conventions, and quality expectations. Then individual developers can add task-specific personas on top. System-level persona + user-level persona = hyper-relevant output.

Making Personas Persistent

Typing a persona into every prompt gets old fast. Here's how to make them stick:

✓ Claude Projects: Add your persona to the project instructions. It applies to every conversation in that project.
✓ Claude Code: Add the persona to your CLAUDE.md file. It becomes part of every interaction.
✓ ChatGPT: Go to Settings → Personalization → Custom Instructions. Paste your persona there.
✓ Cursor: Add your persona to the .cursorrules file in your project root.
✓ API usage: Put the persona in the system message parameter.

Persona Anti-Patterns

Anti-Pattern 1: The Over-Specified Persona

"You are a 37-year-old Bulgarian developer named Stefan who lives in Sofia, enjoys hiking, has two cats named Bit and Byte, graduated from Sofia University in 2011, and prefers dark-roast coffee."

None of those details help the AI write better code. Irrelevant specifics waste tokens and can confuse the model. Stick to role, expertise, experience, and priorities.

Anti-Pattern 2: Contradictory Personas

"You are a cautious, risk-averse security expert AND a move-fast startup engineer who values shipping speed over everything."

The AI can't optimize for both risk-avoidance and speed-at-all-costs. Pick one priority, or clearly state the trade-off: "Lean toward security, but flag where we could accept more risk to ship faster."

Anti-Pattern 3: The Flattery Persona

"You are the world's greatest programmer who has never written a bug and knows everything about every language."

This doesn't help. It's like hiring someone because they claim to be "the best" — it doesn't tell the AI what to focus on. "You are a senior Kotlin developer focused on clean architecture and testability" is far more useful.

When NOT to Use Personas

Personas aren't always the answer. Sometimes they add overhead without value:

Don't bother with a persona for:

  • Simple factual questions: "What's the syntax for a Kotlin data class?" — The AI knows this. No persona needed.
  • Basic code completion: "Finish this function" — The code context is enough.
  • Quick translations or conversions: "Convert this JSON to YAML" — It's a mechanical task.
  • One-word answers: "What HTTP status code means 'Not Found'?" — 404. Done.

Personas shine when:

  • You need depth and nuance: Architecture decisions, code reviews, design discussions
  • The "perspective" matters: Security review vs. feature review vs. performance review
  • You're generating content for a specific audience: Docs for juniors, reports for management
  • You want the AI to push back: The Critic persona challenges your assumptions

Advanced: Multi-Persona Conversations

Here's a powerful technique: ask the AI to simulate a conversation between different personas. This is like having a mini team meeting inside your AI session.

Multi-persona debate:

I'm considering migrating our Android app from XML views to Jetpack Compose. Simulate a brief discussion between these three people:

1. The Senior Developer who values code quality and developer experience
2. The QA Lead who worries about regression testing and stability
3. The Engineering Manager who needs to balance timeline and team capacity

Each person should give their top concern and one recommendation. Then summarize the consensus.
Senior Developer: "I'm for it. Compose eliminates boilerplate, makes UI testable, and our developers are already learning it. My concern: we need a gradual migration strategy, not a big-bang rewrite. Start with new features in Compose, wrap existing screens with ComposeView."

QA Lead: "My concern is regression risk. We have 200+ UI test cases based on Espresso. Migrating to Compose means rewriting those tests with ComposeTestRule. Recommendation: keep existing screens in XML until we need to modify them. Only migrate screens that are already changing."

Engineering Manager: "My concern is timeline. We have Q3 feature commitments. Recommendation: allocate 20% of sprint capacity to migration. Target one screen per sprint. Don't let migration block feature work."

Consensus: Gradual migration. New features in Compose. Existing screens migrate only when being modified. 20% sprint allocation. QA adapts tests per-screen as they migrate.

You just got three expert perspectives and a team decision in one prompt. This is incredibly useful for solo developers who don't have a full team to bounce ideas off of.

Hands-On: Build Your Persona Toolkit

Exercise: Write Three Personas and Test Them

Create three personas tailored to your daily work:

  1. Your Expert Persona: Write a persona for someone with deep expertise in your primary technology. Be specific about experience, tech stack, and what they care about.
  2. Your Critic Persona: Write a persona whose job is to find problems in your work. Focus them on the types of issues that matter most (security? performance? UX?).
  3. Your Translator Persona: Write a persona who can explain your technical work to a non-technical audience (your PM, your VP, your client).

The test: Pick something you worked on this week — a feature, a bug fix, a design decision. Ask all three personas the same question about it:

  • Expert: "What could be improved?"
  • Critic: "What's wrong with this?"
  • Translator: "Explain this to a product manager in 3 sentences."

Compare the three responses. You'll be amazed at how different they are — and how useful each perspective is.

Bonus: Save your best personas somewhere you can reuse them — a snippet, a note, or your project's CLAUDE.md file.
