Chapter 9

Prompting for Everyone: Writing, Analysis, and Planning

Beyond code — emails, reports, data analysis, meeting prep, and decision frameworks

Not Just for Code Anymore

If you've been following along, you might think prompt engineering is mostly about code reviews, test cases, and debugging. But here's the thing: you spend most of your workday doing things that aren't coding.

You write emails. You prepare for meetings. You analyze data. You make decisions. You write status reports. You plan projects. You prepare interview questions. You review documents.

Every single one of these tasks gets dramatically better with good prompts. This chapter is for everyone on the team — developers, QAs, managers, designers, and anyone who types words into an AI tool.

The 80/20 Rule of AI at Work: Most people use AI for coding and writing. The biggest untapped productivity gains are in analysis, planning, and decision-making — tasks where AI can think through options you'd never have time to consider.

Email Drafting: From Blank Screen to Send

We've all stared at a blank email, especially the tricky ones. The "we need to push back the deadline" email. The "your team's API keeps breaking ours" email. The "I disagree with this technical decision" email.

AI won't send the email for you, but it can get you 80% there in seconds.

Vague Prompt

Write an email about the deadline.

Structured Prompt

Write a professional email to Sarah (engineering manager, partner team) about pushing our API integration deadline from April 20 to May 5.

Tone: professional but collaborative, not apologetic
Key points:
- We discovered a data migration issue that affects 3 endpoints
- We've already fixed 2 of 3, the third needs her team's input
- Propose a sync meeting this week to align

Keep it under 150 words. End with a specific ask (meeting time).

AI Output

Subject: API Integration Timeline Update — Proposing May 5

Hi Sarah,

Quick update on the API integration timeline. During our data migration work, we found an issue affecting three endpoints. We've already resolved two, but the third — the user-preferences sync — requires input from your team on the expected payload format.

Given this dependency, I'd like to propose moving our integration deadline from April 20 to May 5. This gives both teams breathing room to get it right rather than ship something fragile.

Could we set up a 30-minute sync this week to walk through the remaining endpoint together? I'm flexible Wednesday or Thursday afternoon.

Thanks,
[Your name]

The Email Formula

Recipient + relationship (who are they, what's the dynamic) + topic + tone (formal? friendly? firm?) + key points (what to cover) + constraints (word limit, specific ask at the end).

The tone instruction is the most important part. "Professional but collaborative" produces vastly different output than "direct and urgent."

Pro tip: For sensitive emails, ask the AI for 2-3 variations at different tones ("one version that's diplomatic, one that's more direct"). Pick the one that matches the situation, or mix and match paragraphs.

Meeting Preparation: Stop Winging It

How many hours per week do you spend in meetings? Now, how many of those meetings have a clear agenda? AI can turn "we should meet about the thing" into a structured, productive session in 30 seconds.

Prompt 
Generate an agenda for a sprint retrospective meeting.

Duration: 45 minutes
Attendees: 6 developers, 1 QA engineer, 1 engineering manager
Context: We just finished a sprint where we shipped the payment feature
  but had 3 production incidents in the process.

Goals:
- Identify what went well (especially the cross-team collaboration)
- Dig into the root causes of the production incidents
- Agree on 2-3 specific action items for next sprint

Format the agenda with time allocations for each section.
Include 2-3 discussion questions per section.
AI Output 
Sprint Retrospective — Payment Feature
Duration: 45 minutes | Facilitator: [EM]

1. Check-in (5 min)
   One word to describe this sprint. Go around the room.

2. What Went Well (10 min)
   - What made the cross-team collaboration work this sprint?
   - Which practices should we keep doing?
   - What would you tell other teams to try?

3. Production Incidents Deep Dive (15 min)
   - For each incident: what was the trigger, and where
     did our process fail to catch it earlier?
   - Were there warning signs we ignored or missed?
   - Did our monitoring/alerting help or fail us?

4. Action Items (10 min)
   - What's ONE thing we can change next sprint to prevent
     similar incidents?
   - Who owns each action item? By when?
   - How will we know if it worked?

5. Wrap-up (5 min)
   - Confirm action items and owners
   - Rate this retro: 1-5 (quick show of hands)

In 30 seconds you have a meeting that would normally take 10 minutes to plan — and it's better structured than most agendas people create from scratch.

Document Review: A Second Pair of Eyes

Whether it's a design doc, a project proposal, or a technical RFC, AI makes an excellent first reviewer. It won't replace human judgment, but it catches things humans miss when they're too close to the content.

Prompt 
Review this project proposal for clarity and completeness.

Evaluate:
1. Is the problem statement clear to someone outside the team?
2. Are there gaps in the technical approach?
3. Are success criteria measurable and specific?
4. Is the timeline realistic given the scope?
5. What questions would a skeptical VP ask?

For each issue, rate severity (Critical / Important / Minor)
and suggest a specific fix.

[paste document here]
The "skeptical VP" trick: Asking "what questions would a skeptical [role] ask?" is one of the most powerful review prompts. It forces the AI to think adversarially — exactly the perspective you need before presenting to stakeholders.

Data Analysis: Making Sense of Numbers

You don't need to be a data scientist to analyze data with AI. Whether it's crash reports, user metrics, or survey results, AI can spot patterns you'd spend hours hunting for.

Prompt 
Analyze this crash report data and identify patterns.

Data (last 7 days):
- Monday: 45 crashes, 80% on Android 12, 60% during checkout
- Tuesday: 52 crashes, 75% on Android 12, 55% during checkout
- Wednesday: 120 crashes, 90% on Android 12, 85% during checkout
- Thursday: 115 crashes, 88% on Android 12, 82% during checkout
- Friday: 30 crashes, 40% on Android 12, 20% during checkout

Questions:
1. What changed between Tuesday and Wednesday?
2. Why did crashes drop on Friday?
3. What's the most likely root cause?
4. What should we investigate first?

Present findings as: Pattern → Evidence → Hypothesis → Next Step
AI Output

Pattern 1: Spike correlated with Android 12 + checkout
Evidence: Wed-Thu crashes jumped 2.3x, with Android 12 share rising from 75-80% to 88-90% and checkout from 55-60% to 82-85%.
Hypothesis: A deployment on Tuesday evening introduced a regression in the checkout flow that primarily affects Android 12.
Next step: Check deployment logs for Tuesday evening. Look for changes to checkout-related code, especially payment or WebView components that behave differently on Android 12.

Pattern 2: Friday drop suggests a partial fix or external factor
Evidence: Crashes dropped to 30 (lowest all week), Android 12 share dropped to 40%, checkout dropped to 20%.
Hypothesis: Either a hotfix was deployed Thursday evening, or the affected user cohort stopped retrying.
Next step: Check if a release went out Thursday. Check if crash-free user rate recovered or if the same users simply stopped using the feature.

The Analysis Framework

Always tell AI what to look for and how to present findings. The magic formula:

Data (paste it) + Specific questions (what do you want to know?) + Output structure (Pattern → Evidence → Hypothesis → Next Step).

Without specific questions, you get generic observations. With questions, you get actionable insights.

Decision Frameworks: Think It Through

Some of the hardest moments at work aren't coding problems — they're decisions. Should we rewrite this service or patch it? Should we hire a contractor or train someone internally? Should we adopt this new framework?

AI won't decide for you, but it's exceptional at structuring the decision so nothing gets overlooked.

Prompt 
Help me decide between two approaches for our notification system.

Option A: Build custom notification service in-house
Option B: Use Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) with a thin wrapper

Context:
- Android app with 200K MAU
- Need push, in-app, and email notifications
- Team of 4 Android devs, no backend specialists
- Timeline: must ship in 6 weeks
- Budget: limited, prefer low ongoing cost

My priorities (ranked):
1. Ship within 6 weeks
2. Low maintenance burden long-term
3. Flexibility to add channels later
4. Cost

Present as a pros/cons matrix, then give a clear recommendation
with reasoning. Flag any risks I might be underestimating.
AI Output (abbreviated) 
| Criteria              | Option A: Custom    | Option B: FCM Wrapper |
|-----------------------|---------------------|-----------------------|
| Ship in 6 weeks       | ⚠️ Risky — 8-10 wks | ✅ Achievable          |
| Maintenance burden     | ❌ High — you own it | ✅ Low — Google owns infra |
| Flexibility            | ✅ Full control      | ⚠️ FCM-shaped choices  |
| Cost                   | ❌ Server + DevOps   | ✅ Free tier covers 200K |

Recommendation: Option B (FCM Wrapper)
Given your team composition (no backend specialists) and
6-week deadline, building custom infrastructure is high-risk.
FCM handles push + in-app natively, and you can add email
via a lightweight Cloud Function later.

Risk you may be underestimating:
Option A's "8-10 weeks" assumes no surprises. Notification
infrastructure has hidden complexity: retry logic, rate
limiting, delivery tracking, device token management. With
no backend experience on the team, this could easily become
a 12-16 week project.

Notice how the AI flagged a risk the person didn't ask about. That's the value of giving enough context — the AI can reason about things you haven't considered yet.

Decision-making power moves:
  • "What am I not considering?" — Forces the AI to think beyond your framing
  • "Argue against my preferred option" — Gets you a devil's advocate for free
  • "If this decision fails, what's the most likely reason?" — Pre-mortem analysis

Status Reports: From Scattered Notes to Polished Updates

Nobody loves writing status reports. But everyone loves receiving clear, structured ones. AI bridges that gap — you provide the raw notes, it produces the polished update.

Your Raw Notes

payment feature done, 3 bugs fixed, perf improved, john working on notifications, blocked on API from backend team, demo friday, need to update docs

Structured Prompt

Turn these rough notes into a weekly status report for my engineering manager.

Notes: payment feature done, 3 bugs fixed, perf improved, john working on notifications, blocked on API from backend team, demo friday, need to update docs

Format: Completed / In Progress / Blocked / Next Week
Tone: concise and professional
Add severity/priority indicators where relevant.

AI Output 
Weekly Status — Mobile Team (Week of April 14)

✅ Completed
- Payment feature — shipped to production (P0)
- Fixed 3 bugs: checkout crash on Android 12, cart total
  rounding error, duplicate order on double-tap
- Performance optimization — app cold start improved

🔄 In Progress
- Notification system — John is implementing (ETA: next week)
- Documentation updates — pending completion of notification work

🚫 Blocked
- Backend API for user preferences — waiting on backend team
  (contact: [backend lead]). Blocking notification feature.
  ⚠️ Risk: if not resolved by Wednesday, notification
  timeline slips.

📋 Next Week
- Friday: demo of payment feature to stakeholders
- Complete notification system (dependency: backend API)
- Update developer documentation

Two minutes of messy notes became a status report that makes you look organized and on top of things. Your manager will love you.

Presentation Outlines: Structure Before Slides

The worst way to build a presentation is to open PowerPoint and start adding slides. The best way is to outline your story first. AI excels at this.

Prompt 
Create a 10-slide outline for a presentation about our AI
adoption results.

Audience: engineering leadership (VP and directors)
Duration: 20 minutes
Goal: get approval to expand AI tooling to all teams

Key data points:
- 3-month pilot with 2 teams (Android, QA)
- 30% faster code review turnaround
- Test case generation time dropped from 2 hours to 20 minutes
- Developer satisfaction: 4.2/5.0
- $0 additional cost (used existing Claude Team subscription)

For each slide, provide:
- Slide title
- 2-3 bullet points of content
- Speaker notes (1-2 sentences of what to SAY)

End with a clear ask and next steps slide.

This gives you a complete presentation skeleton in under a minute. You just need to add the visuals and your personal touches.

Interview Preparation: Both Sides of the Table

Whether you're hiring or being hired, AI is your prep coach.

If You're the Interviewer

Prompt 
Generate 8 interview questions for a mid-level Android developer.

Must assess:
- Kotlin proficiency (2 questions)
- Architecture knowledge — MVVM, Clean Architecture (2 questions)
- Problem-solving approach (2 questions)
- Team collaboration and communication (2 questions)

For each question provide:
- The question itself
- What a GOOD answer includes (green flags)
- What a WEAK answer looks like (yellow flags)
- A follow-up question to go deeper

Difficulty: mid-level (3-5 years experience).
Avoid: trick questions, whiteboard algorithm puzzles.

If You're the Candidate

Prompt 
I have an interview for a senior Android developer position
at a fintech company. Help me prepare.

My background: 5 years Android, strong in Kotlin and Compose,
limited experience with payment systems.

Generate:
1. 10 likely technical questions and strong answers
2. 5 behavioral questions (STAR format answers)
3. 5 smart questions I should ask the interviewer
4. Key fintech/payment concepts I should know

For technical answers, include what separates a "good" answer
from a "great" answer.
Mock interview mode: After generating questions, try this follow-up: "Now act as the interviewer. Ask me these questions one at a time. After each answer, give me feedback on what was strong and what I could improve. Be honest, not encouraging." This turns AI into a surprisingly effective practice partner.

Learning and Research: AI as Your Tutor

AI is one of the best learning tools ever created — if you prompt it correctly. The key is to tell it your current level and ask it to build from there.

Generic Learning Prompt

Explain Kotlin coroutines.

Targeted Learning Prompt

Explain Kotlin coroutines to someone who understands threads and callbacks but has never used coroutines.

Structure:
1. The problem coroutines solve (why callbacks are painful)
2. The mental model (what's actually happening under the hood)
3. The 3 most important concepts (scope, dispatcher, suspend)
4. A practical example: fetching user data from an API
5. Common mistakes beginners make
6. 5 quiz questions to test my understanding

Use analogies from everyday life. Keep explanations under 3 paragraphs each.

The Learning Ladder

Great learning prompts have three components:

  1. Where I am: "I understand X but not Y"
  2. Where I want to be: "I need to understand Z well enough to [practical goal]"
  3. How to check: "Give me quiz questions to verify I got it"

The quiz questions are the secret weapon. They force the AI to teach at the right depth, and they give you a way to confirm you actually learned something.

Delegation and Task Breakdown

Whether you're a tech lead splitting a feature across your team or a developer breaking down your own work, AI helps you think through the decomposition.

Prompt 
Break down this feature into tasks for a team of 4.

Feature: Add "Favorites" functionality to our e-commerce app
- Users can favorite/unfavorite products
- Favorites persist across devices (synced to backend)
- Favorites page with grid view, sorting, and search
- Push notification when a favorited item goes on sale

Team:
- Alex: senior Android dev, strong in architecture
- Maria: mid-level Android dev, great with UI/Compose
- James: junior Android dev, learning backend integration
- Priya: QA engineer

For each task provide:
- Task name and description
- Assigned team member (and why they're the best fit)
- Dependencies (what must be done first)
- Estimated effort (in story points: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8)
- Acceptance criteria (how do we know it's done)

Order tasks to maximize parallel work.
Flag any risks or potential blockers.

This prompt does what a good tech lead does: it considers skills, dependencies, parallelism, and risks. The AI's output won't be perfect — you know your team better — but it gives you a solid starting point that would take 30+ minutes to draft from scratch.

The Manager's Toolkit

Managers spend most of their day communicating, planning, and making decisions — exactly the tasks where AI shines brightest. Here are prompts designed for common management scenarios.

1:1 Meeting Prep

Prompt 
I'm preparing for a 1:1 with a developer who has been
missing deadlines for the past 3 sprints. They're technically
strong but seem disengaged.

Help me prepare:
1. 3 open-ended questions to understand what's going on
   (not accusatory, genuinely curious)
2. How to frame the deadline concern constructively
3. 3 possible root causes I should consider
4. A suggested structure for a 30-minute conversation
5. How to end the conversation with a clear next step

Tone guidance: supportive but honest. I want to help them
succeed, not just point out the problem.

Performance Review Drafting

Prompt 
Help me draft performance review feedback for a developer.

Strengths observed:
- Shipped payment feature on time with minimal bugs
- Mentored 2 junior developers on architecture patterns
- Proactively improved CI pipeline, saving 15 min per build

Areas for growth:
- Tends to work in isolation, doesn't communicate blockers early
- Code reviews sometimes lack depth (approves too quickly)
- Could take on more technical leadership in design discussions

For each area (strengths and growth), write:
- A specific, evidence-based observation
- The impact of this behavior on the team
- For growth areas: a concrete suggestion for improvement

Tone: balanced, specific, growth-oriented. Avoid corporate fluff.
No sandwiching — put strengths first, then growth areas honestly.
Privacy warning: Never paste employee names, personal details, or sensitive HR information into public AI tools. Use your company's approved AI tools with enterprise data protection, or anonymize the details before prompting.

Team Planning

Prompt 
I need to plan next quarter's roadmap for my mobile team (6 people).

Committed work:
- Payment v2 (large, 4 weeks)
- Notification system (medium, 2 weeks)
- 2 security patches (small, 3 days each)

Requested but not committed:
- Dark mode redesign
- Offline mode
- Analytics dashboard
- Performance optimization sprint

Constraints:
- 1 developer will be on vacation for 3 weeks
- We need to keep 20% capacity for production support
- Q3 goal is 99.5% crash-free rate

Help me prioritize the "requested" items. For each, provide:
- Recommendation: Do / Defer / Descope
- Reasoning (in 1-2 sentences)
- Risk of deferring

Then suggest a rough quarter timeline showing when each
item starts and finishes, accounting for the constraints.

The Universal Prompting Pattern

By now you've seen prompts for emails, meetings, analysis, decisions, reports, presentations, interviews, learning, delegation, and management. Notice something? They all follow the same structure.

The pattern that works for everything:

1. Task — What do you want? (Write / Analyze / Decide / Plan / Generate)
2. Context — Who's involved? What's the situation?
3. Specifics — What details, data, or constraints matter?
4. Format — How should the output look?
5. Quality bar — Tone, depth, length, what to avoid

This is Chapter 2's framework applied to non-code tasks. The building blocks work everywhere.

Common Mistakes with Non-Code Prompts

Mistake: No Audience

"Write a summary of this document."

For whom? A summary for your CEO looks very different from one for your tech lead.

Fix: Specify Audience

"Summarize this technical design doc for our VP of Engineering. Focus on business impact, timeline, and risks. Skip implementation details."

Mistake: No Tone

"Write a message to the team about the outage."

AI defaults to corporate-speak. You get "We sincerely apologize for the inconvenience..."

Fix: Set the Tone

"Write a Slack message to the engineering team about last night's outage. Tone: direct and transparent, no corporate speak. Include: what happened, what we did, what we're changing. Keep it under 200 words."

Mistake: Accepting First Draft

Taking the AI's first output and using it as-is. AI output is a first draft, not a final product.

Fix: Iterate

"This is good but too formal. Make it warmer and add a specific example from last sprint." or "The analysis is thorough but too long. Cut it to the 3 most impactful findings."

Exercise: Your Non-Coding Prompt Library

Pick 3 non-coding tasks from your last work week. For each one:

  1. Write an optimized prompt using the Universal Prompting Pattern (Task + Context + Specifics + Format + Quality bar)
  2. Run it through your AI tool of choice
  3. Evaluate: how much time would this have saved you? What would you adjust in the prompt?

Suggested tasks to try:

  • A tricky email you spent too long writing
  • A meeting that could have used a better agenda
  • A decision you made that could have been more structured
  • A status update or report
  • A document you reviewed or needed to review
  • An interview you conducted or prepared for

Bonus challenge: Create a reusable prompt template for one recurring non-coding task (weekly status report, sprint planning, 1:1 prep). Save it somewhere your team can use it.
