Reviewing Your Colleagues’ AI-Assisted PRs

💡 The Big Idea

📝

Editing a Ghostwritten Book

41% of merged code is AI-assisted (mid-2026). AI is the ghostwriter — your job as reviewer is to make sure the "author" actually understands the text they're putting their name on. Frame questions as curiosity, not suspicion.

🧩 Core Concepts

📣
AI Disclosure
Every PR should state: which AI tool, which sections, how much manual editing. MicroPython and Drupal already mandate this. It's a trust mechanism, not bureaucracy.
👥
Pair Reviewing
Author walks through PR, reviewer asks questions in real-time. Use for auth, payments, migrations, or heavy AI usage. 30 min now saves hours of debugging later.
🤝
Curiosity, Not Suspicion
"Interesting approach — can you walk me through why?" beats "Did AI write this?" Frame comprehension checks as your need to understand, not their need to prove competence.
📚
Knowledge Transfer
Code review is the primary mechanism for team learning. When AI writes and nobody reviews deeply, knowledge transfer breaks down. Ask "why" questions, share TILs.
📜
Team Agreement
Written agreements remove awkwardness from enforcement. Cover: AI disclosure, review SLAs, PR size limits, comprehension standard, and escalation paths.
🔏
The Merge Fingerprint
When you approve a PR, you co-sign it. AI can't be held accountable at 2 AM. You can. No matter how much AI contributed, a human must take responsibility.

💬 Constructive vs. Accusatory Comments

Aspect ✅ Constructive ❌ Accusatory
Opening "Interesting approach — can you walk me through..." "Did AI write this? This looks like a typical AI pattern..."
Framing Your need to understand Their need to prove competence
Effect Author engages, knowledge transfers Author gets defensive, learning dies
Pattern Praise first, then probe Suspicion first, damage later

🗣️ The "Can You Explain?" Toolkit

  1. "I want to make sure I understand..."

    Frames it as your need. Invites a walkthrough without implying incompetence.

  2. "Could you add a comment explaining the reasoning?"

    Asking for documentation = natural request that doubles as a comprehension check.

  3. "What did you consider before going with this approach?"

    If they considered alternatives, they understand it. If not, that's the signal.

  4. "What happens if the input is null?"

    Specific edge-case questions are the gentlest comprehension test. The author can check — the important thing is that they can.

🚫 When to Send a PR Back

🤷
Can't Explain Core Logic
If they can't explain it now, they can't debug it at 2 AM.
📏
Over 500 Lines, No Guide
A 1,000-line AI PR shouldn't be reviewed — it should be split.
🧪
Tests Mirror Implementation
Tests should verify behavior, not repeat the code structure.
🌊
AI Scope Creep
Unrelated files modified. AI "helpfully" refactored nearby code. Revert the extras.
❓
No Intent Statement
Code without stated intent is code without accountability.
🔒
Security-Critical, No Review
Auth, payments, secrets = mandatory human security pass.

👤 Solo Review 👥 Pair Review

🔑 Key Terms

AI Disclosure
PR section stating which AI tool was used, which sections it generated, and how much manual editing followed.
PR Contract Framework
Every PR includes: Intent, Proof, Risk + AI disclosure, Focus areas. A forcing function for author accountability.
Pair Reviewing
Author and reviewer evaluate code together in real-time. More expensive than solo review but builds shared understanding.
Merge Fingerprint
When you approve a PR, you co-sign it. AI can't be paged at 2 AM — you can.
Review SLA
Team agreement on review turnaround: 24h standard, 4h security-critical, immediate for hotfixes (Google's benchmark).
Prompt-Style Commits
Commit messages that describe what AI was told to do rather than what the code actually does. A tell for low-comprehension AI usage.

❓ Quick Quiz

Test Your Understanding

What's the "ghostwritten book" analogy about?

When you edit someone's own writing, they can explain what they meant. When you edit a ghostwritten manuscript, the credited author might not know either. AI is the ghostwriter — your job is to make sure the "author" understands the text they're putting their name on.

How do you ask "can you explain this?" without being a jerk?

Frame it as your need to understand: "I want to make sure I understand this correctly — can you walk me through the flow?" Lead with praise, ask about tradeoffs and alternatives, use specific edge-case questions as gentle comprehension tests.

What are the four parts of the PR Contract Framework?

1) Intent (what & why), 2) Proof (tests, screenshots, logs), 3) Risk + AI disclosure (risk tier and which sections AI generated), 4) Focus areas (1-2 sections needing human judgment).

When should you pair-review instead of solo-review?

For security-critical code, architectural changes, PRs with heavy AI generation and minimal manual editing, and when the author is new to the area of the codebase.

What's the difference between prompt-style and human-style commit messages?

Prompt-style: "Add retry logic with exponential backoff" (describes what AI was told). Human-style: "payment: retry on gateway timeout (max 3, backoff 1-8s)" (describes what the code does and why). Prompt-style messages suggest the author accepted output without deep engagement.

📌 Key Takeaways

If You Remember Nothing Else

  • 41% of merged code is AI-assisted — you can't tell from the diff which parts a human wrote.
  • Require AI disclosure in every PR: which tool, which sections, how much manual editing.
  • Frame questions as curiosity, not suspicion — "interesting approach, can you walk me through it?"
  • Code review is the primary mechanism for team knowledge transfer. Don't let it become rubber-stamping.
  • Send PRs back when: author can't explain core logic, over 500 lines without a guide, or security-critical with no review.
  • Pair-review complex AI-heavy PRs — 30 min now saves hours of debugging later.
  • Write a team review agreement. Written norms remove awkwardness from enforcement.
  • The merge button is your fingerprint. AI can't be paged at 2 AM. You can.
