Chapter 9

Reviewing Your Colleagues’ AI-Assisted PRs

Team dynamics, AI disclosure, and building a review culture that values understanding over velocity

The New Reality

Chapters 7 and 8 were about you — your skills, your comprehension workflow. But code review is a team sport. And here’s the new reality: by mid-2026, roughly 41% of merged code is AI-assisted. Your colleagues are using Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code, and Windsurf just like you are. Their PRs land in your review queue, and you have no idea which parts a human wrote and which parts an AI generated.

This changes the game. When you reviewed a colleague’s code before AI, you could assume they understood every line — they wrote it, after all. Now? The author might have generated 80% of the PR in a single AI session and spent five minutes skimming it before requesting review. They might understand every line. They might not. You can’t tell from the diff alone.

Think of it like editing a ghostwritten book. When you edit someone’s own writing, you can ask “what did you mean here?” and get a real answer. When you edit a ghostwritten manuscript, the credited author might not know what the ghostwriter meant either. AI is the ghostwriter. Your job as reviewer is to make sure the “author” actually understands the text they’re putting their name on.

Requiring AI Disclosure in PRs

The first step is knowing what you’re reviewing. In February 2026, the MicroPython project added AI disclosure to their Contributor Guidelines, requiring every contributor to pick one option: “I did not use Generative AI tools” or “I used them, but a human has checked the code.” The Drupal project followed with mandatory “AI Usage Disclosure” sections in every PR template. Failure to disclose can result in PR closure.

These aren’t bureaucratic obstacles — they’re trust mechanisms. When a colleague discloses that parts of their PR are AI-generated, it changes how you review. You spend less time on the human-written parts (where the author likely has deep understanding) and more time on the AI-generated sections (where comprehension gaps are probable).

A Simple AI Disclosure Template

Add this to your PR template. It takes 30 seconds to fill out and saves your reviewer significant time:

## AI Disclosure
- [ ] No AI tools were used in this PR
- [ ] AI tools were used (specify below)

**AI tool(s) used:** (e.g., Claude Code, Copilot, Cursor)
**AI-generated sections:** (e.g., "retry logic in payment.ts",
    "all test files", "initial scaffolding, then manually edited")
**Author verification:** I have reviewed and understand all
    AI-generated code in this PR

The key line is the last one: “I have reviewed and understand all AI-generated code.” This isn’t a checkbox to rush past. It’s a commitment. If the author checks it and can’t explain the code during review, that’s a conversation worth having.

The PR Contract Framework for Team Reviews

Risk tier &

Osmani calls this framework “a forcing function for author accountability.” If an author can’t complete this template, they likely don’t understand their own change well enough to ask someone else to approve it. The principle is simple: insist on proof, not promises.

For AI-generated PRs, the “Risk + AI Disclosure” field is where transparency lives. The author should state which tool generated the code, which sections, and how much manual editing happened afterward. This isn’t about gatekeeping AI — it’s about giving you, the reviewer, the context to review effectively.

The Accountability Principle

“No matter how much AI contributed, a human must take responsibility.” The merge button is a developer fingerprint — when you approve a PR, you’re co-signing that this code is safe to ship. AI can’t be held accountable at 2 AM. You can.

How to Review Without Being a Jerk

Here’s the tension: you need to verify that your colleague understands their AI-generated code, but you don’t want to imply they’re incompetent. The line between “thorough review” and “interrogation” is real, and crossing it damages team trust.

The secret: frame questions as curiosity, not suspicion.

Constructive Review Comments
"Interesting approach with the circuit breaker pattern here — I haven't seen it used in this part of the codebase before. Can you walk me through why this was the right choice over simple retry logic?"
You’re genuinely asking about the decision. The author can explain their reasoning (or discover they can’t). Either way, knowledge transfers.
Accusatory Review Comments
"Did AI write this? This looks like a typical AI pattern — over-engineered circuit breaker when a simple retry would work."
You’ve made it personal. The author will get defensive instead of engaging with the technical substance. Even if you’re right, you’ve killed the learning moment.

The “Can You Explain This?” Conversation

Sometimes you need to ask directly whether the author understands a section of code. This is the most delicate part of reviewing AI-assisted work. Here’s how to do it without implying incompetence:

  • “I want to make sure I understand this correctly — can you walk me through the flow?” Framing it as your need to understand, not their need to prove competence.
  • “This is a non-obvious pattern. Could you add a comment explaining the reasoning?” Asking for documentation is a natural request that doubles as a comprehension check.
  • “I’m curious about the tradeoff here — what did you consider before going with this approach?” If they considered alternatives, they understand it. If they didn’t, that’s the signal.
  • “What happens in this function if the input is null?” A specific edge-case question is the gentlest comprehension test. If the author knows, great. If they have to check, that’s fine too — the important thing is that they can check.

The “Praise First” Pattern

When reviewing AI-assisted code, lead with something genuinely positive before asking probing questions. “Nice coverage on the edge cases in the tests — one question about the retry logic in line 42...” builds collaboration instead of conflict. This isn’t about being soft — it’s about maintaining the trust that makes honest technical discussion possible.

Pair Reviewing

For complex or risky AI-generated PRs, the best approach is pair reviewing: two humans reviewing the code together, typically the author and the reviewer. Unlike pair programming (where you write code together), pair reviewing happens after the code is written — you evaluate it together.

Here’s how it works:

  1. The author shares their screen and walks through the PR, section by section
  2. The reviewer asks questions in real-time — no waiting for async comment threads
  3. Both identify AI-generated sections and discuss whether the approach is right
  4. Decisions are made on the spot: approve, request changes, or split the PR

Pair reviewing is more expensive than solo review. Use it selectively: for PRs touching auth, payments, or data migrations; for new team members; for any PR where the author discloses heavy AI usage and you want to verify comprehension. The 30 minutes you invest in a pair review saves hours of debugging later.

One underappreciated benefit: pair reviews build team trust faster than any other practice. When you work through a tricky AI-generated section together, both participants walk away with shared understanding. That shared understanding is what makes on-call handoffs smooth and incident response fast.

When to Pair Review vs. Solo Review

Solo review works for: standard features, UI changes, well-tested utility functions, and PRs under 200 lines where the author has high credibility on the topic. Pair review is worth the investment for: security-critical code, architectural changes, any PR where the AI disclosure indicates heavy generation with minimal manual editing, and onboarding situations where the author is new to the area of the codebase.

Knowledge Transfer Through Reviews

Here’s something most teams miss: code review is the primary mechanism for knowledge transfer in a development team. It’s not documentation. It’s not meetings. It’s the daily practice of reviewing each other’s code and asking questions.

When AI generates most of the code, this knowledge transfer mechanism breaks down. The author didn’t build deep understanding while writing — they generated and reviewed. The reviewer skims the diff and approves. Nobody learned anything. Two people signed off on code that neither fully owns.

To keep knowledge transfer alive:

  • Ask “why” questions in reviews: Not “what does this do?” but “why this approach?” The answer teaches both of you.
  • Share what you learned: When you discover something interesting during review, comment it. “TIL: the payment gateway returns 202 for async processing, which is why we need this polling loop.”
  • Request inline comments for non-obvious code: “Can you add a comment explaining why we retry on 429 but not on 503?” Future readers will thank you.
  • Treat review discussions as learning moments: The back-and-forth in review comments is where institutional knowledge gets created and preserved.

When Nobody Learns From Reviews

If your team’s review process has devolved into “LGTM” rubber stamps, you’re not doing code review — you’re doing code acknowledgment. In 2026, with AI generating 41% of merged code, this means a growing percentage of your codebase is code that nobody on the team deeply understands. That’s not a future problem. That’s the problem that wakes you up during the next incident.

When to Send a PR Back

Sometimes the right review outcome is rejection. Not as punishment — as quality control. Here are clear criteria for sending an AI-generated PR back:

Send It Back When... Why
The author can’t explain the core logic If they can’t explain it now, they can’t debug it at 2 AM
The PR is over 500 lines without clear breakdown A 1,000-line AI PR shouldn’t be reviewed — it should be split
No tests, or tests that mirror the implementation Tests should verify behavior, not repeat the code structure
AI scope creep: unrelated files were modified AI loves to “helpfully” refactor nearby code. Revert the extras.
No intent statement (no ticket, no description) Code without stated intent is code without accountability
Security-critical code with no security review Auth, payments, secrets = mandatory human security pass
Duplicates existing functionality AI doesn’t know your codebase already has a retry utility

Sending a PR back isn’t adversarial. Frame it constructively: “This looks like solid work — can we split it into two smaller PRs so I can review the payment logic separately from the UI changes?” Most authors appreciate the guidance, especially when they’re still calibrating how much AI-generated code to include per PR.

When Velocity Pressure Conflicts With Thoroughness

Let’s be honest: every team feels pressure to ship fast. AI amplifies this pressure — if code can be generated in minutes, why should review take hours? Managers see the ticket throughput go up and expect the pipeline to flow.

Here’s how to push back without sounding like you’re blocking progress:

  • Frame review as risk reduction, not slowdown. “A 30-minute review now prevents a 6-hour incident next week.” Connect review quality to on-call quality — that resonates with managers.
  • Set a 24-hour review SLA. Google uses this benchmark. It’s fast enough to maintain flow and slow enough to be thorough. Make it a team norm, not a suggestion.
  • Use AI review tools for the first pass. Let CodeRabbit or Claude Code catch the obvious issues. That frees your time for the high-judgment work — architecture, security, and business logic — where humans add real value.
  • Track review quality, not just review speed. Bugs caught in review vs. bugs that escaped to production is the metric that matters. Fast reviews that miss bugs aren’t fast — they’re deferred work.

The goal isn’t to slow AI-assisted development down. It’s to make the review step efficient enough that it doesn’t become the bottleneck — without sacrificing the thoroughness that keeps production stable.

Building Team Review Agreements

Individual habits aren’t enough. You need explicit team agreements that everyone commits to. Here’s what a solid team review agreement covers:

## Team Code Review Agreement — [Your Team]

### AI Disclosure
- All PRs MUST include the AI disclosure section
- Specify which tool, which sections, and level of manual editing
- "Author verification" checkbox is mandatory

### Review SLA
- Standard PRs: reviewed within 24 hours
- Security-critical PRs: reviewed within 4 hours
- Hotfixes: pair-reviewed immediately

### PR Size Limits
- Target: under 400 lines per PR
- Over 500 lines: must include a reviewer guide explaining the sections
- Over 1,000 lines: split into smaller PRs before requesting review

### Comprehension Standard
- Authors must be able to explain any section of their PR
- Reviewers may ask "walk me through this" on any AI-generated section
- If the author can't explain a section, the PR needs rework

### Escalation
- Disagreements go to the team lead, not to passive-aggressive comments
- Security concerns escalate immediately — don't approve and file a ticket later
- If review is blocked for >48 hours, pair-review to unblock

Print this (or adapt it) and get your team to sign off. A written agreement removes the awkwardness from enforcement — you’re not being difficult, you’re following the process everyone agreed to.

PRs Where Commit Messages Describe the Prompt, Not the Change

A telltale sign of low-comprehension AI usage in your team’s PRs:

git log --oneline
a3f2c1d Add retry logic with exponential backoff
b8e4a7f Implement circuit breaker pattern for payment gateway
c2d9f3e Add comprehensive error handling and logging
d7a1b5c Write unit tests for payment service

Every commit message sounds like a prompt. Compare with a human-authored sequence:

git log --oneline
a3f2c1d payment: retry on gateway timeout (max 3, backoff 1-8s)
b8e4a7f payment: circuit breaker to stop cascading failures (#347)
c2d9f3e payment: log charge attempts for billing audit trail
d7a1b5c payment: test double-charge prevention edge case

The first set describes what the AI was told to do. The second describes what the code actually does and why. When you see prompt-style commit messages in a colleague’s PR, it suggests they accepted the AI’s output without enough engagement to write their own commit messages.

A Colleague Who Can’t Explain Their Own Code

Scenario: You’re reviewing a colleague’s PR that adds a new caching layer. You leave a comment: “Why did you use LRU eviction instead of TTL-based? Our access patterns seem more time-sensitive.” The response comes back: “Let me check — I think the AI chose that approach.”

This is the red flag. Not that they used AI — that they don’t know why the code makes the decisions it makes. If the caching strategy is wrong for your use case, nobody will notice until the cache starts evicting hot data and response times spike.

What to do: Don’t embarrass them. Say: “Let’s pair on this for 15 minutes — I want to make sure the eviction strategy matches our access patterns.” You’ve turned a potential conflict into a learning session. If the pattern repeats, have a private conversation about AI usage hygiene.

Reviewing a Colleague’s AI-Assisted PR

  • Check the AI disclosure section — which tool, which sections, how much manual editing?
  • Read the intent statement — does the change match the ticket or stated goal?
  • Verify proof of function — tests, screenshots, or logs demonstrating it works
  • Check PR size — over 500 lines? Request a split or a reviewer guide
  • Ask at least one “why this approach?” question on an AI-generated section
  • Check for AI scope creep — were unrelated files modified?
  • Check for duplication — does this recreate existing utilities?
  • Verify commit messages describe the change, not the prompt
  • For security-critical sections: apply the “high-speed intern” standard
  • Leave at least one constructive learning comment (“TIL...” or “nice approach because...”)

Draft Your Team’s Review Agreement

Do this now — it takes 15 minutes and gives you something concrete to propose at your next team meeting.

  1. Copy the team review agreement template from this chapter into a document
  2. Customize it for your team:
    • What’s your realistic review SLA? (24 hours is a good default)
    • What’s your max PR size? (400–500 lines is reasonable)
    • Who handles security escalations?
  3. Add your team’s AI disclosure template to your PR template (GitHub, GitLab, or Bitbucket)
  4. Share the draft with one teammate and ask for feedback before proposing it to the whole team
  5. Schedule 15 minutes in your next team meeting to discuss and ratify it

The hardest part isn’t writing the agreement — it’s getting buy-in. Start by sharing the stats: 41% of code is AI-assisted, and teams without review agreements accumulate comprehension debt silently. The agreement isn’t about slowing down — it’s about making sure your velocity is sustainable.

The Constructive Review Challenge

For the next week, practice the constructive review techniques from this chapter:

  1. In every PR review you do this week, leave at least one “why this approach?” question on a non-trivial section. Track the responses.
  2. Lead with one positive observation before asking any probing question. Note how the author responds differently compared to when you lead with a concern.
  3. If you find AI-generated code that the author can’t explain, handle it as described in this chapter — pair on it, don’t embarrass.
  4. At the end of the week, review your PR comments. Count:
    • How many “why” questions did you ask?
    • How many led to actual knowledge transfer?
    • Did any uncover a real comprehension gap?

This exercise builds the habit of treating reviews as learning opportunities rather than gatekeeping checkpoints. The metric that matters isn’t how many issues you found — it’s how much your team learned from the review process.
