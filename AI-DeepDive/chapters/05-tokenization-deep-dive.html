Chapter 5

Tokenization Deep Dive

How text becomes numbers — the BPE algorithm traced step by step

Companion: This expands on AI-Intro Ch 4: Tokens. There you learned that AI reads tokens, not words. Now you’ll build a tokenizer from scratch.

In Chapters 1–4, we built an entire neural network that takes numbers in and pushes numbers out. But language models work with text. So how does “The cat sat on the mat” become a list of numbers a network can process?

The answer is tokenization — and the dominant algorithm behind it is called Byte Pair Encoding (BPE). In this chapter, you’ll trace every step of BPE with real text, build a vocabulary from scratch, and understand why some words are trickier than they look.

The Problem: Text In, Numbers Out

A neural network can’t read the string "hello". It needs numbers. So we need a system that:

  1. Breaks text into pieces (tokens)
  2. Maps each piece to a number (token ID)
  3. Does this consistently — the same text always produces the same token IDs

The question is: what should those pieces be?

Three Approaches to Splitting Text

Approach Splits “unhappiness” as Vocab Size Problem
Character-level u, n, h, a, p, p, i, n, e, s, s ~256 11 tokens for one word — sequences get very long
Word-level unhappiness ~500,000+ Rare words missing; “unhappier” is unknown
Subword (BPE) un, happiness ~32,000–100,000 Best of both — compact AND handles rare words

Why subwords win

Character-level is too granular — the model has to figure out that c, a, t means “cat” from scratch. Word-level is too rigid — any word not in the dictionary is a dead end. Subwords hit the sweet spot: common words stay whole (the, cat), while rare words get split into recognizable pieces (un + happiness).

BPE: The Algorithm That Builds a Vocabulary

Byte Pair Encoding (BPE)

Start with individual characters. Count how often each pair of adjacent characters appears. Merge the most frequent pair into a new token. Repeat until you reach your target vocabulary size.

That’s it. The entire algorithm is: count pairs → merge the winner → repeat.

Let’s trace this with real text. We’ll use a tiny corpus:

low  low  low  low  low
lower  lower
newest  newest  newest
widest  widest

That gives us these word frequencies:

The _ marks the end of a word (it’s an actual token — BPE needs to know where words end). Our starting vocabulary is every unique character: {l, o, w, _, e, r, n, s, t, i, d} — 11 tokens.

What just happened?

After just 5 merges, the most common word (“low”) became a single token, and the common suffix “-est” was also captured. That’s exactly the goal: frequent patterns get their own tokens, while rare patterns stay split into smaller, still-recognizable pieces. If we kept going, low e r _ would eventually merge into lower_ too.

Checkpoint

After merge 5, the word “newest” is represented as n e w est _ — four tokens. If we did a 6th merge and est _ won, what would “newest” look like?

Answer: n e w est_ — three tokens. Each merge compresses the representation.

The Merge Table: Your Tokenizer’s Recipe

The output of BPE training isn’t a program — it’s a list of merge rules in priority order:

PriorityMerge RuleNew Token
1l + olo
2lo + wlow
3e + ses
4es + test
5low + _low_

This table is stored alongside the model. When you want to tokenize new text, you apply these merges in order.

Encoding New Text

Let’s tokenize the word "lowest" using our 5-rule merge table. This word was not in our training corpus.

Encoding “lowest” step by step

Start: Split into characters: l o w e s t _

Apply rule 1 (l + o → lo):

lo w e s t _

Apply rule 2 (lo + w → low):

low e s t _

Apply rule 3 (e + s → es):

low es t _

Apply rule 4 (es + t → est):

low est _

Apply rule 5 (low + _): Can’t apply — low and _ aren’t adjacent.

Result: [low, est, _] — 3 tokens.

The power of subwords

The tokenizer never saw “lowest” during training, but it handled it perfectly by combining low (a common word) with est (a common suffix). This is why subword tokenization is so powerful — it can encode any text, even words it has never seen, by composing known pieces.

Real models work the same way, just with much larger vocabularies. When you type "hello" into ChatGPT, it might become token [15339]. The word "indescribable" might split into [ind, esc, rib, able] → [521, 3978, 822, 481].

Special Tokens: The Control Signals

Beyond regular text tokens, every model uses special tokens that act as control signals:

TokenNamePurpose
<BOS>Beginning of SequenceMarks the start of a text. The model knows “this is where the input begins.”
<EOS>End of SequenceMarks the end. When the model generates this token, it stops.
<PAD>PaddingFills empty space when batching sequences of different lengths.
<UNK>UnknownPlaceholder for tokens not in the vocabulary (rare with BPE).

When you chat with Claude, your actual input might look like this internally:

<BOS> [system prompt tokens] [user message tokens] <EOS>

The model generates tokens until it produces <EOS>, which signals “I’m done talking.”

Why “Strawberry” Is Hard

You may have heard that language models struggle to count the letters in “strawberry.” Now you can understand why.

A real tokenizer might split “strawberry” like this:

"strawberry" → ["str", "aw", "berry"]

The model sees 3 tokens, not 10 letters. It has no direct access to the individual characters s, t, r, a, w, b, e, r, r, y. When you ask “how many r’s are in strawberry?”, the model would need to:

  1. Know that token "str" contains the letters s, t, r
  2. Know that "aw" contains a, w
  3. Know that "berry" contains b, e, r, r, y
  4. Count: 1 + 0 + 2 = 3 r’s

But the model was trained on tokens, not characters. It never explicitly learned the character composition of each token. It’s like asking you to count the number of times the letter “a” appears in a paragraph — while only being allowed to look at it one word at a time, through frosted glass.

The character blindness trap

This isn’t a bug in any particular model — it’s a fundamental consequence of subword tokenization. Models reason about tokens, not characters. Tasks that require character-level awareness (letter counting, anagrams, rhyming) are inherently harder because the model has to reverse-engineer information that was discarded during tokenization.

Vocabulary Size: The Tradeoff

How many tokens should a vocabulary have? It’s a balancing act:

Vocab SizeTokens per TextEmbedding TableWho Uses This
Small (~8K) More tokens (text is split finely) Small (8K × embed_dim) Early models
Medium (~32K) Balanced Moderate GPT-2, many open-source models
Large (~100K+) Fewer tokens (common words are single tokens) Large (100K × embed_dim) GPT-4, Claude

Why does this matter? Two reasons:

  1. Speed: Fewer tokens = fewer forward passes during generation. If “artificial intelligence” is 1 token instead of 4, generation is 4× faster for that phrase.
  2. Context window: A 128K-token context window fits more actual text if the vocabulary is large (each token carries more meaning).

But a larger vocabulary means a bigger embedding table. With 100K tokens and 4096-dimensional embeddings, that’s 100,000 × 4,096 = 409 million parameters just for the embedding layer.

Checkpoint

A model with a 50K vocabulary and 2048-dimensional embeddings has how many parameters in its embedding table?

Answer: 50,000 × 2,048 = 102,400,000 (about 102 million parameters).

The Multilingual Tax

BPE vocabularies are built from training data. If most of that data is English, English text gets very efficient tokenization — common words like “the,” “and,” and “is” are single tokens.

But what about Japanese, Korean, or Arabic? These languages had fewer examples in the training data, so their characters got merged less. The result:

Tokenization efficiency by language

The same meaning expressed in different languages:

LanguageTextApproximate Tokens
English“Artificial intelligence is changing the world”~6 tokens
Spanish“La inteligencia artificial está cambiando el mundo”~9 tokens
Japanese“人工知能は世界を変えている”~15 tokens
Korean“인공지능이 세상을 바꾸고 있다”~14 tokens

What this means in practice

If Japanese takes 2.5× more tokens than English for the same meaning, then a Japanese user’s 128K context window effectively holds only about 51K tokens worth of meaning compared to English. It’s also 2.5× slower to generate and 2.5× more expensive per API call (since APIs charge per token). This is the multilingual tax — and it’s why newer models invest heavily in multilingual training data to improve tokenization efficiency for non-English languages.

Real-World Tokenizers

Modern models use variations of BPE, each with slight twists:

All of them share the same core idea: learn which byte/character sequences appear most often, merge them, and build a vocabulary from the bottom up.

Byte-level BPE: Modern tokenizers start with 256 bytes (not characters) as the base vocabulary. This means they can handle any input — including emojis, code, and binary data — without ever producing an <UNK> token. Every possible byte sequence can be represented.

The BPE Shorthand Analogy

Imagine you’re a medieval scribe copying books by hand. Writing “artificial intelligence” a thousand times is exhausting, so you invent the shorthand “AI.” Then you notice you write “the” constantly, so you create a squiggle for it. Over time, you develop hundreds of abbreviations — the most common phrases get the shortest symbols.

BPE does exactly this, but automatically. It looks at billions of words and creates shorthand for the most common patterns. The difference is that a BPE tokenizer does this in minutes instead of a scribe’s lifetime, and its “shorthand symbols” become the vocabulary the model uses forever after.

Tokenization Gotchas

Understanding tokenization helps explain several quirks of language models:

Why leading spaces matter

" hello" (with a space) and "hello" (without) may be different tokens. Many tokenizers encode the space as part of the token. This is why prompt formatting matters — invisible whitespace differences can change the token sequence.

Why code is expensive

Code has lots of unusual character patterns: ===, </div>, __init__. These may not merge well, producing many small tokens. A 100-line Python function might use more tokens than a 100-line English paragraph.

Why token limits feel inconsistent

A “4,096-token limit” doesn’t mean 4,096 words. Common English averages about 1.3 tokens per word, but technical text, code, or non-English languages can be 2–4 tokens per word. That 4K limit might be 3,000 English words or 1,200 Japanese words.

Exercise: BPE Encoding by Hand

Your turn: encode “newer”

Using our 5-rule merge table from earlier, tokenize the word "newer". The word ends with _.

Merge rules:

  1. l + o → lo
  2. lo + w → low
  3. e + s → es
  4. es + t → est
  5. low + _ → low_

Steps:

  1. Start with characters: n e w e r _
  2. Check rule 1 (l + o): no l o pair → skip
  3. Check rule 2: skip (no lo)
  4. Check rule 3 (e + s): no e s pair → skip
  5. Check rules 4 and 5: skip
  6. Result: [n, e, w, e, r, _] — 6 tokens. None of our merge rules applied!

This shows a key insight: a word that’s rare in the training data stays split into small pieces. "newer" didn’t appear in our corpus, and its character pairs didn’t match any learned merges. With a larger vocabulary (more merges), er would likely become a token, reducing this to [n, e, w, er, _] — 5 tokens.

Bonus exercise: what would a 6th merge do?

Look at the state after merge 5. What pair is most likely to be merged next, and how would it change the tokenization of "newest"?

Hint: After merge 5, the remaining top pairs include est _ (count: 3 + 2 = 5). If merged, “newest” would go from [n, e, w, est, _] to [n, e, w, est_] — just 4 tokens.

Key Takeaways

  • Tokenization converts text to numbers. The model never sees raw text — only token IDs.
  • BPE builds a vocabulary by repeatedly merging the most frequent character pairs. Count → merge → repeat.
  • Subword tokens are the sweet spot: common words stay whole, rare words split into known pieces.
  • The merge table is the tokenizer’s recipe — apply merges in order to encode any text.
  • Character blindness: Models reason about tokens, not letters. That’s why counting letters in “strawberry” is surprisingly hard.
  • Vocabulary size is a tradeoff between efficiency (fewer tokens) and embedding table size.
  • Multilingual tax: Languages underrepresented in training data get less efficient tokenization.
