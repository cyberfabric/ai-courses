Chapter 11

Embeddings — From Words to Geometry

Train a tiny embedding, compute cosine similarity step by step, and search a vector space

Companion: This expands on AI-Intro Ch 9: Embeddings. There you learned that embeddings place words in a space where similar things are close together. Now you’ll compute the actual numbers, train a tiny embedding from scratch, and build a semantic search engine by hand.

Every chapter in this course has relied on embeddings without fully explaining them. In Chapter 5, tokens got IDs. In Chapter 6, those IDs became vectors before entering the attention mechanism. In Chapter 7, positional encoding was added to them. But where did those vectors come from? And why do they capture meaning?

That’s what this chapter is about. By the end, you’ll understand how words become numbers, why “king - man + woman ≈ queen” actually works, and how to build semantic search with nothing more than dot products.

The Core Problem: Numbers Need Meaning

A neural network only understands numbers. Token ID 4821 for “cat” and 4822 for “dog” look like they’re right next to each other, but “cat” and “dog” are no more similar than “cat” and “quantum.” Token IDs are arbitrary labels — they carry no meaning.

What an Embedding Does

An embedding is a lookup table that maps each token ID to a vector of numbers. Each token gets its own unique vector, and the vectors are learned during training so that tokens used in similar contexts end up with similar vectors.

If the vocabulary has 50,257 tokens and the embedding dimension is 768, the embedding table is a 50,257 × 768 matrix. That’s 38.6 million learnable parameters, and they’re all just for converting token IDs to meaningful vectors.

Embedding Lookup: The Simplest Operation in AI

Tiny example: vocabulary of 5 tokens, embedding dimension of 3.

The embedding table (learned during training):

TokenIDEmbedding Vector
cat0[0.8, 0.2, -0.1]
dog1[0.7, 0.3, -0.2]
car2[-0.5, 0.9, 0.4]
truck3[-0.4, 0.8, 0.5]
happy4[0.1, -0.6, 0.9]

To embed the word “dog”: look up row 1 → [0.7, 0.3, -0.2].

That’s it. No computation. Just a table lookup.

Notice the Patterns

Look at the vectors above. “Cat” [0.8, 0.2, -0.1] and “dog” [0.7, 0.3, -0.2] are close — similar numbers in each position. “Car” [-0.5, 0.9, 0.4] and “truck” [-0.4, 0.8, 0.5] are also close to each other, but far from the animals. The network learned this from seeing millions of sentences where “cat” and “dog” appear in similar contexts (“The ___ sat on the mat”) while “car” and “truck” appear in different ones (“She drove the ___ to work”).

How Embeddings Are Trained: The Skip-Gram Idea

The most famous embedding training method is Word2Vec’s skip-gram. The idea is almost absurdly simple:

Skip-Gram Training

Given a center word, predict which words appear nearby. The network has one task: for each word in a sentence, predict the words within a window (say, 2 positions left and right).

The “weights” of this prediction network are the embeddings. They get adjusted during training so that words with similar contexts develop similar vectors.

Skip-Gram: One Training Step

Sentence: “The cat sat on the mat”

Window size: 2 (look 2 words left and right)

Training pairs (center → context):

Center WordContext Words
Thecat, sat
catThe, sat, on
satThe, cat, on, the
oncat, sat, the, mat
thesat, on, mat
maton, the

What the network learns:

  • “Cat” predicts “sat” — so “cat” and “sat” embeddings shift closer
  • “Dog” (in other sentences) also predicts “sat” — so “dog” moves toward “sat” too
  • Over millions of sentences, “cat” and “dog” both move toward the same neighborhood because they predict similar context words

The Key Insight: You Are the Company You Keep

The model never sees a definition of “cat” or “dog.” It only sees which words appear near them. But that’s enough! Words that appear in similar linguistic contexts share meaning. This is called the distributional hypothesis: “You shall know a word by the company it keeps.”

Quick Check

If “Paris” and “Tokyo” frequently appear near words like “capital,” “city,” and “population,” what happens to their embeddings during training?

Answer: They move closer together in the embedding space, because they predict similar context words. The model has no idea these are real cities — it just sees the co-occurrence patterns.

Cosine Similarity: Measuring Closeness

Now that we have vectors for words, we need a way to measure how “similar” two vectors are. The most common measure is cosine similarity.

Cosine Similarity

Cosine similarity measures the angle between two vectors, ignoring their length. The formula:

cos(A, B) = (A · B) / (|A| × |B|)

Where A · B is the dot product and |A| is the magnitude (length) of A.

  • 1.0 = identical direction (maximally similar)
  • 0.0 = perpendicular (unrelated)
  • -1.0 = opposite direction (maximally different)

Cosine Similarity Step by Step

Let’s compute the similarity between “cat” and “dog”:

A = [0.8, 0.2, -0.1] (cat)

B = [0.7, 0.3, -0.2] (dog)

Step 1: Dot product (multiply element-wise, then sum)

PositionABA × B
10.80.70.56
20.20.30.06
3-0.1-0.20.02

A · B = 0.56 + 0.06 + 0.02 = 0.64

Step 2: Magnitude of A

|A| = sqrt(0.8² + 0.2² + (-0.1)²) = sqrt(0.64 + 0.04 + 0.01) = sqrt(0.69) = 0.831

Step 3: Magnitude of B

|B| = sqrt(0.7² + 0.3² + (-0.2)²) = sqrt(0.49 + 0.09 + 0.04) = sqrt(0.62) = 0.787

Step 4: Divide

cos(cat, dog) = 0.64 / (0.831 × 0.787) = 0.64 / 0.654 = 0.979

Reading the Result

0.979 is very close to 1.0 — these vectors point in nearly the same direction. Cat and dog are highly similar in embedding space. Now let’s check cat vs. car:

Cat vs. Car: A Dissimilar Pair

A = [0.8, 0.2, -0.1] (cat), C = [-0.5, 0.9, 0.4] (car)

Dot product: (0.8)(-0.5) + (0.2)(0.9) + (-0.1)(0.4) = -0.40 + 0.18 - 0.04 = -0.26

|A| = 0.831 (same as before)

|C| = sqrt(0.25 + 0.81 + 0.16) = sqrt(1.22) = 1.105

cos(cat, car) = -0.26 / (0.831 × 1.105) = -0.26 / 0.918 = -0.283

Result: -0.283 — negative! These vectors point in roughly opposite directions. Cat and car are dissimilar in embedding space.

The King − Man + Woman = Queen Trick

The most famous embedding result is vector arithmetic on words. Here’s the idea: if embeddings truly capture meaning, then relationships between concepts should be represented as directions in the vector space.

Vector Arithmetic with 4D Embeddings

Suppose our trained embeddings (simplified to 4 dimensions) look like:

WordEmbedding
king[0.9, 0.8, 0.3, 0.7]
queen[0.8, 0.9, 0.7, 0.6]
man[0.7, 0.5, 0.2, 0.6]
woman[0.6, 0.6, 0.6, 0.5]

Compute: king − man + woman

Positionking− man+ woman= Result
10.9-0.7+0.60.8
20.8-0.5+0.60.9
30.3-0.2+0.60.7
40.7-0.6+0.50.6

Result: [0.8, 0.9, 0.7, 0.6]

Queen: [0.8, 0.9, 0.7, 0.6]

Exact match! The “royalty” direction + the “female” direction = queen.

What Just Happened

The vector from “man” to “king” captures the concept of royalty. The vector from “man” to “woman” captures gender. By subtracting the male component and adding the female component to “king,” you arrive at “queen.” The embedding space has organized itself so that abstract relationships are linear directions.

In practice, the result won’t be an exact match — you find the nearest neighbor to the result vector. But the nearest neighbor is reliably “queen.”

Caveat: This works impressively for well-represented relationships in the training data. It doesn’t work for everything. “doctor - man + woman” might give “nurse” instead of “doctor” — reflecting biases in the training data, not reality. Embeddings capture the patterns in text, including the biases.

Cosine vs. Euclidean Distance

Why cosine similarity instead of regular distance? Because embeddings can vary in length (magnitude) for reasons unrelated to meaning.

Cosine Similarity

  • Measures the angle between vectors
  • Ignores vector length
  • [1, 2, 3] and [2, 4, 6] have cosine = 1.0 (identical direction)
  • Best for: meaning similarity, text search

Euclidean Distance

  • Measures the straight-line distance
  • Affected by vector length
  • [1, 2, 3] and [2, 4, 6] have distance = 3.74 (not close!)
  • Best for: spatial reasoning, clustering

In practice, most embedding applications use cosine similarity. Longer documents produce longer vectors (more accumulated meaning), but the direction — what the text is about — is what matters.

Embedding Dimensions: How Many Do You Need?

Our examples use 3 or 4 dimensions to keep the math friendly. Real embeddings use hundreds or thousands:

ModelDimensionsWhy
Word2Vec (original)300A good balance for word-level meaning
GPT-2768Richer representation for token-level context
GPT-312,288175B params need wide vectors for expressiveness
OpenAI text-embedding-3-small1,536Optimized for search and retrieval
OpenAI text-embedding-3-large3,072More nuance for complex tasks

More dimensions = more nuance, but also more memory and compute. 768 dimensions can distinguish “bank (river)” from “bank (financial)”. 3 dimensions can’t.

Quick Check

If a model uses 1,536-dimension embeddings and has a vocabulary of 100,000 tokens, how large is the embedding table?

Answer: 100,000 × 1,536 = 153,600,000 parameters. At float16 (2 bytes each), that’s about 307 MB just for the embedding table.

From Word Embeddings to Sentence Embeddings

Word2Vec gives you one vector per word. But for search and comparison, you often need one vector for an entire sentence, paragraph, or document.

The simplest approach: average all the word vectors.

Averaging Word Vectors

Sentence: “The cat sat” using our 3D embeddings:

WordEmbedding
The[0.1, 0.0, 0.3]
cat[0.8, 0.2, -0.1]
sat[0.3, -0.4, 0.6]

Average:

[(0.1+0.8+0.3)/3, (0.0+0.2-0.4)/3, (0.3-0.1+0.6)/3]

= [0.40, -0.07, 0.27]

This works reasonably well but loses word order (“dog bites man” = “man bites dog”). Modern sentence embedding models (like those behind OpenAI’s embedding API) use the full transformer to produce a single vector that captures meaning including word order and context.

Semantic Search: The Killer Application

The most practical use of embeddings is semantic search: find documents that match a query by meaning, not just keywords.

How Semantic Search Works

  1. Embed all documents — convert each to a vector
  2. Embed the query — convert the search query to a vector
  3. Compute similarity — cosine similarity between query and each document
  4. Rank — return the most similar documents

Semantic Search by Hand

Three documents, pre-embedded as 4D vectors:

DocumentEmbedding
Doc 1: “How to train a puppy”[0.8, 0.3, -0.1, 0.5]
Doc 2: “Car maintenance guide”[-0.4, 0.7, 0.6, 0.1]
Doc 3: “Teaching your dog tricks”[0.7, 0.4, -0.2, 0.6]

Query: “How do I teach my pet new commands?”

Query embedding: [0.6, 0.5, -0.3, 0.4]

Compute cosine similarity with each document:

Query vs. Doc 1:

Dot: (0.6)(0.8) + (0.5)(0.3) + (-0.3)(-0.1) + (0.4)(0.5) = 0.48 + 0.15 + 0.03 + 0.20 = 0.86

|Query| = sqrt(0.36+0.25+0.09+0.16) = sqrt(0.86) = 0.927

|Doc1| = sqrt(0.64+0.09+0.01+0.25) = sqrt(0.99) = 0.995

cos = 0.86 / (0.927 × 0.995) = 0.86 / 0.922 = 0.933

Query vs. Doc 2:

Dot: (0.6)(-0.4) + (0.5)(0.7) + (-0.3)(0.6) + (0.4)(0.1) = -0.24 + 0.35 - 0.18 + 0.04 = -0.03

cos = -0.03 / (0.927 × 0.990) = -0.033

Query vs. Doc 3:

Dot: (0.6)(0.7) + (0.5)(0.4) + (-0.3)(-0.2) + (0.4)(0.6) = 0.42 + 0.20 + 0.06 + 0.24 = 0.92

cos = 0.92 / (0.927 × 0.990) = 0.92 / 0.918 = 0.999 (rounding effect — extremely similar!)

Rankings:

RankDocumentSimilarity
1Doc 3: “Teaching your dog tricks”0.999
2Doc 1: “How to train a puppy”0.933
3Doc 2: “Car maintenance guide”-0.033

Why This Is Powerful

The query was “How do I teach my pet new commands?” Doc 3 is about “teaching your dog tricks” — different words, same meaning. Keyword search would miss this because the words don’t overlap. Semantic search finds it because the embeddings are close. That’s the whole point of embeddings: meaning becomes geometry.

Vector Databases: Search at Scale

Computing cosine similarity against every document works when you have 3 documents. When you have 10 million, you need something faster.

Vector databases (Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, pgvector) use clever data structures to find approximate nearest neighbors without checking every vector:

TechniqueHow It WorksTradeoff
IVF
(Inverted File Index)		 Cluster vectors into groups. Only search the nearest clusters. Fast, but may miss vectors in neighboring clusters
HNSW
(Hierarchical Navigable Small World)		 Build a multi-layer graph connecting nearby vectors. Navigate the graph to find neighbors. Very fast and accurate, but high memory usage
PQ
(Product Quantization)		 Compress vectors into short codes. Compare codes instead of full vectors. Huge memory savings, but approximate distances
This is the foundation of RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation). When ChatGPT or Claude searches your documents before answering: (1) your docs were embedded and stored in a vector database, (2) your query was embedded, (3) nearest neighbors were retrieved, (4) those documents were injected into the prompt as context. Everything you learned in this chapter powers step 1–3.

The Friend Groups Analogy

Training embeddings is like asking thousands of people “who do you hang out with?” and then placing people with similar friend groups near each other on a map.

  • “Cat” hangs out with “sat,” “mat,” “purr,” “kitten”
  • “Dog” hangs out with “sat,” “park,” “bark,” “puppy”
  • They share many friends → placed close together on the map
  • “Car” hangs out with “drive,” “road,” “engine” — different crowd → placed far away

The “map” is the embedding space. Your position on the map is your vector.
