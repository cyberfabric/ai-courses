Chapter 13

Model Architectures Compared

Encoder-only, decoder-only, MoE, diffusion, ViT — the landscape of model architectures explained

Companion: This expands on AI-Intro Ch 10: Models & Industry. There you surveyed the major model providers. Now you’ll understand the architecture behind each type — what makes BERT different from GPT, how MoE works mechanically, and why diffusion models create images from noise.

Throughout this course, we’ve focused on decoder-only transformers — the architecture behind GPT, Claude, and Llama. But that’s only one branch of a much larger family tree. Different tasks call for different architectures, and understanding why helps you pick the right tool for the job.

The vehicle analogy: Model architectures are like different types of vehicles. Encoder-only is a scanner (analyzes input). Decoder-only is a printer (generates output). Encoder-decoder is a translator (reads input, writes output). MoE is a team of specialists (only relevant experts work on each task). Diffusion is a sculptor (starts with a block of marble/noise and carves away).

The Transformer Family

The original 2017 “Attention Is All You Need” paper introduced both an encoder and a decoder. Since then, researchers have found that each half is powerful on its own. This created three branches:

ArchitectureSees InputProduces OutputKey ModelsBest For
Encoder-Only All tokens at once (bidirectional) Representations (not text) BERT, RoBERTa, DeBERTa Classification, embeddings, search
Decoder-Only Previous tokens only (causal) Text, token by token GPT, Claude, Llama, Mistral Chat, code, general generation
Encoder-Decoder All input tokens (encoder), then generates (decoder) Text, conditioned on encoded input T5, BART, original Transformer Translation, summarization

Encoder-Only: BERT and Understanding

BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) was a breakthrough in 2018. Its key innovation: bidirectional attention. Every token can attend to every other token, including tokens that come after it.

Masked Language Modeling (MLM)

BERT’s training task: randomly hide 15% of tokens in a sentence, then predict the hidden ones. Unlike GPT (which can only look left), BERT can look in both directions to figure out the masked word.

BERT’s Masked Prediction

Sentence: “The cat sat on the [MASK]”

What BERT sees: all surrounding tokens simultaneously

Because BERT sees “cat” and “sat on,” it strongly predicts “mat.” A left-to-right model could only see “The cat sat on the” — it can’t use context from the right.

Why Bidirectional Matters for Understanding

Consider: “The bank by the river was flooded.” To understand that “bank” means riverbank (not financial bank), you need the word “river” that comes after it. BERT sees both directions, making it much better at understanding meaning. But this same property means BERT can’t generate text — it would need to see words that don’t exist yet.

BERT is still widely used for classification (spam detection, sentiment analysis), semantic search (embedding queries and documents), and named entity recognition. It’s fast, efficient, and excels at tasks where you need to understand text rather than produce it.

Decoder-Only: GPT and Generation

You’ve spent 12 chapters studying this architecture. Here’s the recap in context of the full family:

The Decoder-Only Formula

Causal attention (each token only sees previous tokens) + autoregressive generation (predict one token, append, repeat). The causal mask from Chapter 7 is what makes this work — future tokens are hidden.

Why did decoder-only win the chatbot race?

  • Simpler architecture: One stack of transformer blocks instead of two (encoder + decoder). Easier to scale.
  • Universal interface: Generation handles everything — Q&A, code, translation, summarization. You don’t need a separate architecture per task.
  • Scaling behavior: Decoder-only models benefit more from scaling (more parameters, more data) than encoder-only models.
  • In-context learning: Large decoder models can learn new tasks from examples in the prompt, without retraining.

Quick Check

Can a decoder-only model do classification (like BERT)?

Answer: Yes! You can frame classification as generation: “Is this email spam? Answer yes or no.” The model generates “yes” or “no.” Less efficient than BERT for pure classification, but it works and requires no task-specific fine-tuning.

Encoder-Decoder: The Original Design

The original Transformer was encoder-decoder. The encoder processes the entire input (bidirectional, like BERT). The decoder generates the output one token at a time, attending to both its own previous output and the encoder’s representation of the input.

This is ideal for tasks with a clear input-output structure:

  • Translation: Read English sentence (encoder), generate French sentence (decoder)
  • Summarization: Read long document (encoder), generate short summary (decoder)

T5 (“Text-to-Text Transfer Transformer”) made this popular by framing every NLP task as text-to-text: classification becomes “classify: [input]” → “positive.”

Encoder-decoder is less common today because decoder-only models handle these tasks well enough and are simpler to train at scale. But for specialized applications like machine translation, encoder-decoder still holds its own.

Mixture of Experts (MoE): Selective Activation

What if you could have a massive model but only use a fraction of it for each token? That’s the idea behind Mixture of Experts.

How MoE Works

Replace the single FFN (feed-forward network) in each transformer block with N expert FFNs. A small router network decides which experts to activate for each token. Typically, only the top 2 experts (out of 8 or more) are used per token.

Result: the model has many more total parameters, but each token only activates a small subset. More knowledge, same compute per token.

MoE Routing: 8 Experts, Pick Top 2

A token arrives at an MoE layer. The router computes a score for each expert:

ExpertRouter ScoreSelected?
Expert 1 (grammar)0.05
Expert 2 (math)0.02
Expert 3 (code)0.45✔ Top 1
Expert 4 (science)0.08
Expert 5 (language)0.03
Expert 6 (reasoning)0.30✔ Top 2
Expert 7 (creative)0.04
Expert 8 (factual)0.03

After softmax on selected experts:

Expert 3 weight: 0.45 / (0.45 + 0.30) = 0.60

Expert 6 weight: 0.30 / (0.45 + 0.30) = 0.40

Final output: 0.60 × Expert3(token) + 0.40 × Expert6(token)

Six experts did zero work. The token only “paid for” 2 out of 8.

Why MoE Matters

Mixtral 8x7B has 47 billion total parameters but only activates ~13 billion per token (2 of 8 experts). It performs comparably to a dense 70B model while being much faster at inference. You get the knowledge of a larger model with the speed of a smaller one.

The catch with MoE: While compute per token is lower, you still need to store ALL the parameters in memory. A 47B MoE model needs roughly the same GPU memory as a 47B dense model, even though it only uses 13B at a time. MoE saves compute, not memory.

Diffusion Models: Creating Images from Noise

Diffusion models power Stable Diffusion, DALL-E, and Midjourney. They work on a completely different principle than transformers.

The Diffusion Principle

Two processes:

  1. Forward (training): Take a real image and gradually add random noise over T steps until it becomes pure noise
  2. Reverse (generation): Start with pure noise and gradually remove noise over T steps to create a clear image

The model learns to predict and remove the noise added at each step. At generation time, it applies this denoising to random noise, sculpting an image from nothing.

How Text Controls the Image

Text-to-image models combine a text encoder (often CLIP) with the diffusion model. The text prompt is encoded into a vector that guides each denoising step. The denoiser doesn’t just remove noise randomly — it removes noise in the direction of the text embedding. “A cat on a beach” guides the noise removal toward cat-like and beach-like patterns.

Typical diffusion models use 20–50 denoising steps. More steps = higher quality but slower generation. Techniques like DDIM and distillation can reduce this to 4–8 steps.

Vision Transformers (ViT): Images as Token Sequences

What if you could use the same transformer architecture for images? That’s the Vision Transformer (ViT) idea: split an image into patches and treat each patch as a token.

ViT: Image to Tokens

A 224×224 pixel image with 16×16 patches:

Step 1: Split into patches

224 / 16 = 14 patches per side → 14 × 14 = 196 patches

Step 2: Flatten each patch

Each 16×16 patch with 3 color channels = 16 × 16 × 3 = 768 values

Step 3: Project to embedding dimension

Each 768-value patch → linear projection → 768-dim embedding vector

Step 4: Add position embeddings + [CLS] token

Sequence: [CLS, patch_1, patch_2, …, patch_196] = 197 tokens

Step 5: Run through standard transformer blocks

Same attention mechanism, same layer norm, same FFN as in Chapters 6–7. The [CLS] token’s final embedding is used for classification.

ViT showed that transformers aren’t just for language — the attention mechanism works on any sequence of tokens, whether those tokens represent words or image patches.

Multimodal Models: Vision + Language

Modern models like GPT-4 Vision, Claude 3, and Gemini handle both text and images. How?

The Multimodal Recipe

  1. Process the image through a vision encoder (ViT or similar) to get image embeddings
  2. Project image embeddings into the same dimension as text token embeddings
  3. Concatenate: [image_tokens, text_tokens] — one unified sequence
  4. Feed through the standard decoder transformer

The transformer treats image and text tokens identically — it just attends to all of them. The magic is in the projection step that maps visual features into the same vector space as language.

This is why you can ask Claude “what’s in this image?” — the image is converted to tokens that live in the same space as text tokens, and the model attends across both modalities seamlessly.

State-Space Models: The Linear-Time Alternative

Attention is O(n²) — double the sequence, quadruple the compute (Chapter 10). State-space models (SSMs) like Mamba offer a fundamentally different approach: O(n) — linear time.

Instead of each token attending to all previous tokens, SSMs maintain a compressed state that summarizes the entire history. Each new token updates the state and reads from it. Think of it like a running summary instead of re-reading the full transcript.

Hybrid architectures like Jamba combine transformer layers with Mamba layers, getting the best of both: strong attention-based reasoning where needed and efficient linear-time processing for long contexts.

Quick Check

You’re building a spam classifier that needs to process 10 million emails per day. Would you choose BERT or GPT-4?

Answer: BERT (or a similar encoder-only model). It’s much smaller, faster, and specifically designed for classification. GPT-4 would work but costs 100× more per email with no quality benefit for this task.

Where the Field Is Heading

The landscape is evolving rapidly. Key trends to watch:

  • Hybrid architectures: Combining transformers with SSMs (Jamba) or convolutions to get the best properties of each
  • Efficiency improvements: Smaller models with better training (over-training + distillation) matching larger models
  • Multimodal by default: New models increasingly handle text, images, audio, and video natively
  • Longer context: From 2K to 1M+ tokens in 5 years, with MoE and SSMs pushing further
  • Inference-time compute: Models that “think longer” on hard problems (chain-of-thought, tree search) rather than just increasing training compute
  • On-device models: Efficient architectures (MoE, quantized models) making AI viable on phones and edge devices

Your Turn: Architecture Selection

For each use case, pick the best architecture and justify why. Choose from: Encoder-Only, Decoder-Only, Encoder-Decoder, MoE, Diffusion, ViT.

#Use Case
1A customer support chatbot that answers questions about your product
2A system that generates product images from text descriptions
3A search engine that finds relevant documents by meaning
4A model that needs deep knowledge across many domains but must be fast at inference
5A real-time machine translation service
Show Solution

1. Customer support chatbot: Decoder-only. Needs to generate natural language responses, handle diverse questions, and benefit from instruction tuning. GPT, Claude, or Llama-class models.

2. Image generation from text: Diffusion. Purpose-built for generating images from text descriptions. Stable Diffusion, DALL-E 3, or Midjourney.

3. Semantic search: Encoder-only. Needs to produce embeddings for documents and queries. BERT-family or specialized embedding models (text-embedding-3). Fast, efficient, optimized for similarity computation.

4. Broad knowledge, fast inference: MoE. More total parameters (broader knowledge) but fewer active per token (faster inference). Mixtral-style architecture.

5. Machine translation: Encoder-decoder or Decoder-only. Encoder-decoder (T5, NLLB) is the traditional choice with the clearest input/output mapping. But large decoder-only models also translate well with prompting.
