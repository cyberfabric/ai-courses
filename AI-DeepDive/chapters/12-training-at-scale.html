Chapter 12

How Models Are Actually Trained at Scale

Pre-training, fine-tuning, RLHF — the full pipeline from raw text to useful assistant

In Chapter 4, you traced one training iteration: forward pass, loss, backward pass, weight update. That was a single step for a tiny network. Now let’s zoom out to see the full picture: how do you go from a randomly initialized 175-billion-parameter model to something that can write code, answer questions, and follow instructions?

The answer is a three-stage pipeline, each stage fundamentally different in purpose, data, and cost.

The Three Stages

1
Pre-training

Learn language from trillions of tokens of internet text. “Read everything.”

↓
2
Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

Learn to follow instructions from curated prompt-response pairs. “Learn the job.”

↓
3
Alignment (RLHF / DPO)

Learn human preferences from comparison data. “Learn to be helpful, harmless, and honest.”

The education analogy: Pre-training is a general education (read everything, absorb knowledge). SFT is vocational training (learn a specific job — assistant). RLHF is an internship with feedback (a mentor corrects your behavior until you get it right).

Stage 1: Pre-training — Read the Internet

The Pre-training Task

The task is deceptively simple: predict the next token. Given a sequence of tokens, predict what comes next. That’s it. No labels, no annotations, no human feedback. Just next-token prediction on trillions of tokens.

The model processes text from books, websites, code repositories, Wikipedia, forums — everything. Each wrong prediction generates a loss, and the weights are adjusted to make the prediction better next time.

The Data Pipeline

You can’t just download the internet and press “train.” Raw web data is messy. Here’s what happens to it:

1
Web Crawl

Scrape billions of web pages (Common Crawl has ~250 billion pages)

↓
2
Deduplication

Remove exact and near-duplicate pages. The same article copied across 50 sites shouldn’t count 50 times.

↓
3
Quality Filtering

Remove spam, boilerplate, cookie banners, gibberish. Often uses a classifier trained to distinguish “Wikipedia-quality” text from junk.

↓
4
Safety Filtering

Remove toxic content, personal information, and copyrighted material (to the extent possible).

↓
5
Tokenization

Convert cleaned text to token sequences. Store on disk ready for training.

After filtering, a typical training dataset is 1–15 trillion tokens. Llama 3 was trained on 15 trillion tokens. That’s roughly equivalent to reading every book ever written — about 30 times.

The Compute Requirements

Pre-training is astronomically expensive. Let’s put some numbers to it.

How Long Would GPT-3 Training Take on Your Laptop?

GPT-3 training required approximately 3.14 × 10²³ floating-point operations (FLOPs).

Your laptop (M3 MacBook Pro): ~5 TFLOPS = 5 × 10¹² FLOPS/second

Time:

3.14 × 10²³ / 5 × 10¹² = 6.28 × 10¹⁰ seconds

= 1.99 × 10³ years

≈ 2,000 years

On 1,000 A100 GPUs (what was actually used):

Each A100: ~312 TFLOPS. Total: 312,000 × 10¹² FLOPS/second

3.14 × 10²³ / 3.12 × 10¹⁷ = 1.0 × 10⁶ seconds ≈ 12 days

(In practice, GPT-3 took about 34 days due to overhead, communication, and efficiency losses.)

Scale of the Problem

Pre-training is the most expensive stage by far — typically 99%+ of total compute. It’s also the stage that gives the model its knowledge. After pre-training, the model knows grammar, facts, code syntax, reasoning patterns, and much more. But it doesn’t know how to be useful yet — it just predicts next tokens.

Distributing Training Across GPUs

A 175B-parameter model doesn’t fit on a single GPU (80 GB of memory at best). You need to split the work. There are three main approaches:

StrategyWhat Gets SplitHow It Works
Data Parallelism The training data Each GPU has a full copy of the model. Different GPUs process different batches. Gradients are averaged across GPUs.
Model Parallelism
(Tensor)		 The model layers Each layer is split across GPUs. One matrix multiplication is divided into chunks computed on different GPUs.
Pipeline Parallelism The model depth Different GPUs handle different layers. Token flows from GPU 1 (layers 1–24) to GPU 2 (layers 25–48), etc.

Real training runs use all three simultaneously. Llama 3 405B was trained on 16,384 H100 GPUs using a combination of all three parallelism strategies.

Mixed Precision Training

A simple trick that nearly doubles training speed: use 16-bit floats (float16 or bfloat16) instead of 32-bit floats for most computations.

PrecisionBitsRangeSpeed
float3232High precision1× (baseline)
float1616Lower precision~2× faster
bfloat1616Wider range than fp16~2× faster

The key insight: gradients and activations don’t need full 32-bit precision. Most computations work fine in 16 bits. The master copy of weights is kept in float32 for the accumulation step (to avoid rounding errors compounding), but everything else runs in half precision.

Scaling Laws: Predictable Improvement

One of the most important discoveries in modern AI is that model performance improves predictably with scale. Double the compute, and the loss decreases by a consistent amount.

The Scaling Law

Loss follows a power law: L(C) = a × C^(-b), where C is the compute budget and a, b are constants. This means you can predict how good a model will be before training it by running smaller experiments and extrapolating.

Chinchilla Scaling: The Optimal Ratio

In 2022, DeepMind published the Chinchilla paper, showing that most models were trained on too little data for their size. The optimal ratio:

Optimal tokens ≈ 20 × parameters

ModelParametersChinchilla-Optimal TokensActual Training TokensStatus
GPT-3175B3.5T300BUnder-trained
Chinchilla70B1.4T1.4TOptimal
Llama 370B1.4T15TOver-trained (intentionally)

Why Llama 3 Over-Trains

Chinchilla says the optimal training ratio is 20×. But once trained, a smaller model is cheaper to run. Meta intentionally over-trains Llama 3 70B (15T tokens instead of the optimal 1.4T) because a better 70B model is cheaper to deploy than a compute-optimal 500B model. Training cost is paid once; inference cost is paid forever.

The Loss Curve: What Training Looks Like

During pre-training, the loss is logged at every step. A healthy training run looks like this:

Typical Loss Curve Milestones

Training ProgressLoss (log-perplexity)What’s Happening
Step 0~11.0Random predictions. Equivalent to guessing from 50K+ tokens.
First 1%~5.0Basic grammar and common words learned. Loss drops fast.
10%~3.5Decent text generation. Knows syntax and frequent patterns.
50%~2.8Strong language understanding. Facts and reasoning emerging.
100%~2.5Diminishing returns. Each step improves less. Training complete.

The loss drops rapidly at first (learning common patterns is easy) and then flattens. Each halving of the loss requires roughly 10× more compute. This is why frontier models cost so much — squeezing out the last few points of improvement is exponentially expensive.

Quick Check

If training loss keeps dropping but validation loss starts going up, what’s happening?

Answer: Overfitting. The model is memorizing the training data instead of learning generalizable patterns. This is less common in large-scale pre-training (the dataset is enormous) but common in fine-tuning on small datasets.

Stage 2: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

After pre-training, the model is a powerful text predictor but a terrible assistant. Ask it a question and it might continue with another question (because that’s what comes next in web text). It doesn’t understand the concept of “follow this instruction.”

What SFT Does

Take the pre-trained model and train it further on a curated dataset of (instruction, response) pairs. The model learns: when given text that looks like an instruction, produce text that looks like a helpful response.

The dataset is much smaller (10K–100K examples) but much higher quality — human-written or human-verified prompt-response pairs.

Before SFT (Base Model)

Prompt: What is the capital of France?
Output: What is the capital of Germany? What is the capital of Spain? These are common geography quiz questions that…

The model continues the pattern it sees — more quiz questions.

After SFT (Instruction-Tuned)

Prompt: What is the capital of France?
Output: The capital of France is Paris. It’s the largest city in France and serves as the country’s political, economic, and cultural center.

The model answers the question directly and helpfully.

SFT is fast and cheap compared to pre-training — often just a few hours on a handful of GPUs. But it has a profound effect on behavior.

Stage 3: Alignment — Learning Human Preferences

SFT teaches the model to follow instructions, but it doesn’t teach it which responses are better. Two grammatically correct responses can differ dramatically in quality. Alignment is about teaching the model to prefer the good response over the merely acceptable one.

RLHF: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback

The RLHF Pipeline

  1. Collect comparisons: Human raters see two responses to the same prompt and pick the better one
  2. Train a reward model: A separate model learns to predict which response humans would prefer
  3. Optimize with PPO: Use reinforcement learning to adjust the language model so it produces responses that score higher on the reward model

RLHF: A Simplified Walkthrough

Prompt: “Explain quantum computing simply.”

Response A: “Quantum computing uses qubits that can be 0, 1, or both simultaneously (superposition). This lets quantum computers explore many solutions at once, making them faster for certain problems like cryptography.”

Response B: “Quantum computing is a type of computation that uses quantum-mechanical phenomena such as superposition and entanglement to perform operations on data. It was theorized by Richard Feynman in 1982.”

Step 1: Human rater picks A (simpler, more directly helpful).

Step 2: Reward model trains to give A a score of 0.8 and B a score of 0.3.

Step 3: The language model is adjusted to produce more A-like responses. Specifically:

  • Generate a response to a new prompt
  • Reward model scores it: 0.6
  • PPO says: “adjust weights to make higher-reward responses more likely”
  • After adjustment, similar prompts produce responses scoring 0.7, then 0.75…
Reward hacking: A known risk with RLHF is that the model can learn to “game” the reward model instead of genuinely improving. For example, it might learn that longer responses score higher and start padding output unnecessarily. This is why the reward model and the training process need careful design.

DPO: The Simpler Alternative

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) achieves a similar result without training a separate reward model or using RL.

DPO in a Nutshell

Given pairs of (preferred response, rejected response), DPO directly adjusts the language model so that it assigns higher probability to the preferred response and lower probability to the rejected one. No reward model, no PPO — just a modified loss function.

Loss = -log(sigma(beta × (log_prob(preferred) - log_prob(rejected))))

The model simultaneously learns “more like this” and “less like that.”

AspectRLHFDPO
Reward modelRequired (separate model)Not needed
RL optimizerPPO (complex, unstable)Standard gradient descent
Training dataComparisons → rewards → RLComparisons directly
StabilityHarder to tuneMore stable
QualityState-of-the-artCompetitive, sometimes equal

DPO has become increasingly popular because it’s simpler to implement and more stable to train. Many recent models use DPO or a variant.

Constitutional AI (RLAIF)

What if you don’t have enough human feedback? Constitutional AI (used by Anthropic for Claude) replaces human raters with AI feedback:

  1. Write a set of principles (“the constitution”): be helpful, be harmless, be honest
  2. Have the AI generate responses, then critique its own responses against the principles
  3. Use the AI’s self-critiques as training signal (RLAIF = RL from AI Feedback)

This scales better than human feedback because you don’t need thousands of human raters. But the quality of the constitution and the critique model matter enormously.

Quick Check

Why is pre-training so much more expensive than fine-tuning and RLHF combined?

Answer: Pre-training processes trillions of tokens through the full model. SFT uses only 10K–100K examples, and RLHF generates a relatively small number of comparison pairs. The data volume in pre-training dwarfs everything else by orders of magnitude.

Putting It All Together

The Full Pipeline: GPT-4 Class Model (Estimated)

StageDataComputeDurationCost
Pre-training ~13T tokens from web, books, code ~25,000 A100 GPUs ~90 days ~$90M

Notice the ratio: pre-training is 99% of the cost but produces a model that can’t have a conversation. SFT + alignment is 1% of the cost but transforms it into a useful assistant.

The Education Analogy, Revisited

Pre-training is 20 years of general education: expensive, slow, and you can’t skip it. SFT is a 2-week job training course: cheap, fast, and transforms what you can do. RLHF is your first month on the job with a mentor: relatively quick, but crucial for professional behavior. Without the 20 years of education, the job training would be useless. Without the job training, all that education wouldn’t produce a useful employee.
