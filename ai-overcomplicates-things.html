Why AI Overcomplicates Your Context

AI's default is to add more. The research is clear: more context measurably degrades model performance. The fix isn't writing shorter prompts — it's owning what lives in the context window.

TL;DR

Ask Claude to improve your CLAUDE.md: it adds 40 lines. Ask for a skill on the same topic: it writes 150 more. Ask for a rule: a third version appears. Same guidance, three places, slightly different wording — and now every conversation carries the bloat.

This is not a quirk; it's a measurable failure mode of LLMs called context rot. Performance drops as context grows — on every frontier model, including Claude. GPT-4o falls from 99.3% accuracy at <1K tokens to 69.7% at 32K on the NoLiMa benchmark. Eleven of thirteen tested models drop below half their short-context score.

The fix is not "write less." It is you, doing the editorial work AI cannot do for itself: holding the global picture, deciding what is load-bearing, and removing what is not. AI is a writer. You are the editor — and asking AI to be its own editor produces a slightly different draft of the same length.

Across the courses on this site you will see CLAUDE.md described as the central context file, and you will also see chapters about skills, sub-agent prompts, output styles, hooks, and rules. You may have noticed that the same idea shows up in two or three of these places, sometimes worded slightly differently. That is not because the courses contradict themselves — it is because the underlying problem is real, and one of the most important things you can learn about working with AI tools is how to recognize and prune it.

This guide is about that pattern: why AI helpfully overcomplicates your context, what the published research actually says about the cost, and what to do instead.

The pattern you have probably already seen

Picture a totally normal week. On Monday you tell Claude: "our testing setup is a mess, please tidy up CLAUDE.md with clear instructions about how we run tests, what to mock, and what coverage we expect." Claude obliges and adds a confident, well-written 40-line section.

Wednesday rolls around. You read about skills and decide testing is exactly the kind of thing that should be one. You ask Claude to scaffold a run-tests skill. Claude writes a SKILL.md with a thoughtful description, several examples, and 150 lines of testing guidance. Some of it overlaps with Monday's CLAUDE.md additions. Some of it subtly contradicts them — Monday's version said "always run the integration suite locally," Wednesday's version said "skip integration tests unless `RUN_INTEGRATION=1` is set."

Friday you read about hooks and ask for a pre-commit hook that runs the test suite. Claude writes the hook plus a small note in CLAUDE.md explaining what the hook does — another 20 lines. By the end of the week, testing rules live in three places, two of them are advisory English text in your context window on every prompt, and at least one detail conflicts.

Why does this happen? Three reasons, and none of them are "the AI is bad."

  • The model only sees a slice. When you ask it to "improve CLAUDE.md," that is the file in front of it. It does not have an outside view of your skills directory, your hooks, or the prompts your sub-agents use. Helpfulness is locally optimised within whatever scope it can see.
  • "More text" is statistically the safer answer. In post-training, longer, more thorough explanations tend to score better than terse ones. The model has internalised that elaboration is a good default. Pruning is risky because it might cut something the user wanted.
  • The harness offers many surfaces. Claude Code lets you put instructions in CLAUDE.md, in skills, in sub-agent definitions, in output styles, in hooks. Each surface is good at something different. AI does not have a strong global model of which surface should hold what, so when you ask it to "add testing guidance to X," it adds testing guidance to X — even if a near-duplicate already exists in Y.

The result is context bloat: your default-loaded context grows linearly with every "helpful" addition, and the same instruction now exists in multiple, subtly inconsistent forms. This is not just untidy. It actively damages how well the model performs.

Why this is more dangerous than it looks

A bloated context is not just "the model has to read more." Three distinct mechanisms turn duplication into degraded output.

1. Conflict

When two instructions disagree, the model has to arbitrate. It does, but unpredictably — which one wins depends on phrasing, position in the prompt, recency in the conversation, and (in long contexts) where in the window the instruction sits. The Knowledge Conflicts for LLMs survey (ACL 2024) documents that even when instructed to follow a specific source, models silently blend conflicting facts and rarely flag the contradiction to the user.

A 2025 study on LLMs Get Lost in Multi-Turn Conversation showed something even more uncomfortable: across a wide set of frontier models, performance drops sharply when the same task is broken across multiple turns instead of stated cleanly once. Models lock onto early assumptions and fail to revise them when later instructions contradict. Your duplicated rules in CLAUDE.md and a skill act exactly like a multi-turn contradiction the model never resolves.

2. Dilution

You may have one critically important rule in CLAUDE.md — "never run database migrations without confirming the environment." That rule sits in a 600-line file alongside 40 ordinary preferences about formatting and naming. The model treats them all as equal-priority text. Important rules get drowned. Anthropic's own Best Practices for Claude Code page and HumanLayer's widely-cited Writing a good CLAUDE.md post both emphasise the same thing: prune ruthlessly, because important guidance buried in noise gets ignored.

3. Rot

Even if every line in your context is correct and consistent, the sheer length itself measurably hurts the model. This is the part most teams underestimate. It is the subject of the next section.

The science: context rot is real and measured

Three primary studies establish that long context degrades performance, on every model that has been tested. Each study attacks the question slightly differently, and together they leave little room for doubt.

Context Rot: How Increasing Input Tokens Impacts LLM Performance
Hong, Troynikov, Huber — Chroma Research, July 2025
All 18 frontier models tested degrade as input grows.

Chroma evaluated 18 state-of-the-art models — including GPT-4.1, Claude Opus 4, Gemini 2.5, and Qwen3 — on simple tasks like retrieval and text replication while varying input length. Every model got worse as the input grew, even on tasks they handled trivially with short context.

The study identifies three compounding mechanisms: lost-in-the-middle (information in the middle of the window is poorly attended to, with 30%+ accuracy drops in the worst case), attention dilution (transformer self-attention is quadratic, so every new token slightly weakens attention to every existing token), and distractor interference (semantically similar but irrelevant content actively misleads the model toward wrong answers).

Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts
Liu, Lin, Hewitt, Paranjape, Bevilacqua, Petroni, Liang — Stanford, TACL 2024 (arXiv July 2023)
Information in the middle of the window is found dramatically less reliably than at the edges.

The original empirical demonstration of the U-shaped curve. The authors plant a relevant fact at varying positions in a long input and measure retrieval accuracy. Performance is highest when the relevant information is at the beginning or the end of the context, and collapses when the model has to find information in the middle — even on models that nominally support 100K+ token windows.

The implication for your CLAUDE.md: a 600-line file is not just longer than a 60-line file, it has a middle, and rules that land in that middle can be effectively invisible to the model.

Read the TACL paper →
NoLiMa: Long-Context Evaluation Beyond Literal Matching
Modarressi et al. — Adobe Research, ICML 2025
GPT-4o: 99.3% → 69.7%. Eleven of thirteen models drop below 50% of their short-context baseline at 32K.

NoLiMa is a stricter version of the classic "needle in a haystack" benchmark. Older needle-in-haystack tests can be solved by literal lexical matching — the model just searches for a phrase. NoLiMa removes that shortcut: the question and the planted fact share no surface vocabulary, so the model has to reason about meaning. The drop-off is brutal.

On contexts under 1K tokens, the strongest models score near-perfect. By 32K tokens — well within what a "modern" 200K-token model is supposed to handle — most models have already lost more than half of their short-context performance. This is the most viscerally clear evidence that context length is not free.

What this means in practice

Every line you add to CLAUDE.md, every paragraph in an always-loaded skill, every preserved tool result — pushes you further along these degradation curves. The cost is invisible because there's no error message. The model just gets quietly worse: it follows fewer of your rules, retrieves less precisely, and starts to hallucinate or confidently choose the wrong option among similar candidates.

Anthropic's own engineering posts acknowledge this directly. The framing they use across Effective context engineering for AI agents and Effective harnesses for long-running agents is unambiguous:

“Context engineering is the art and science of curating what will go into the limited context window from that constantly evolving universe of possible information. Good context engineering means finding the smallest possible set of high-signal tokens that maximize the likelihood of some desired outcome.” — anthropic.com/engineering/effective-context-engineering-for-ai-agents

Read that sentence twice. The goal is not "comprehensive" context. It is the smallest set of high-signal tokens. AI's natural drift is in the opposite direction.

Why AI defaults to more

It is worth pausing on the why, because the failure mode is not random and it is not malice. Three forces conspire to make addition the default and pruning the exception, and once you see them you stop being surprised when AI confidently bloats your context.

The first is local optimisation. When you hand Claude a file and ask it to improve that file, the file is what it sees. It does not have an outside view of your other instruction surfaces, the prompts your sub-agents use, or the conversations you had last week. It is doing a careful, considered job within a frame that is, by construction, smaller than your project. The advice that comes back is good advice for that frame — and your frame is the whole project, which the model never saw.

The second is training pressure toward elaboration. Across post-training, longer and more thorough answers tend to score better than short ones. Models internalise that elaboration is the safer default; pruning is risky because cutting something might cut something the user actually wanted. Ask any frontier model to "tighten this up" and watch how often it returns a version that is somehow longer than the original. The model is not trying to bloat your file. It is trying not to lose anything, and addition is the easier way to be safe.

The third is no memory of what already exists. The model in front of you today does not remember that last Wednesday it added a testing section to the same file, or that on Friday it scaffolded a related skill. Every conversation begins with a slice and treats that slice as the world. Even within one long conversation, attention to earlier turns degrades as the conversation grows — the very phenomenon documented in the research above. So even if you remind it, the reminder competes with everything else in the window.

None of these are bugs. They are properties of how a helpful, single-shot, frame-bounded assistant works. But added together, they mean that "add a section about X" is something AI does well and "figure out whether X already lives somewhere and consolidate" is something AI does poorly. The asymmetry matters because the second operation is the one your context actually needs.

The recursive trap: why asking AI to fix this doesn't work

The natural reaction to everything above is to ask Claude to clean up the mess. "Audit my CLAUDE.md and the skills directory and consolidate the duplicates." It is a reasonable instinct. It also does not work, and watching why it does not work is one of the fastest ways to internalise the problem.

Claude on the consolidation task is the same Claude that wrote the duplicates. It has the same local view (only the files you handed it), the same training pressure toward elaboration, and no global model of what your team actually needs. What you tend to get back is a confident restructure that looks tidier — sections renamed, headings reorganised, a fresh "overview" paragraph added at the top. Length sometimes goes down a little. Often it goes up, because the consolidation comes with explanations of the new structure, a transition note about what was moved where, and a new section meta-describing the convention.

More uncomfortably, the consolidation can introduce new duplication. The model sees a testing rule in CLAUDE.md and a related rule in a skill, decides both are important, and writes a third version that "harmonises" them — without removing either of the originals, because deleting feels risky in the absence of a global view. Now you have three.

Deletion is the one operation the model cannot reliably do for you. It can add a paragraph that argues for deletion, it can describe what deletion would look like, it can produce a "before/after" with the after marked as "shorter." But the act of choosing what stays — the editorial judgment about which guidance is load-bearing for your team and which is decoration — is yours. Asking AI to do it for you is asking the writer to also be the editor of their own draft. They will try. The result will be a slightly different draft of the same length.

This is the recursive trap: every reasonable next move that involves AI doing the work tends to produce another version of the same problem. The escape is not a smarter prompt. The escape is a human, reading, deciding, and removing.

Why you have to own the context, not Claude

Everything above is about model behaviour. But there is a parallel problem on the human side: the longer you work with AI, the easier it becomes to outsource judgement to it. This includes outsourcing judgement about your own context engineering.

The research on this is uncomfortable. A widely-discussed Microsoft study published in 2025 found that, among knowledge workers using GenAI:

“Higher confidence in GenAI is associated with less critical thinking, while higher self-confidence is associated with more critical thinking. Workers who trusted the AI more applied less effort to verifying its output.” — Microsoft Research, summarised in coverage of the 2025 paper on cognitive forcing functions

A randomised controlled trial cited by recent coverage of AI coding assistants found that participants who used AI assistance scored 17% lower on a follow-up mastery quiz than participants who solved the same problems without it. The speed-up while working with AI hides a real slowdown on the next hard problem, when the scaffold is gone.

And in a 2025 analysis of "human in the loop" oversight as a safety mechanism: humans who were nominally in the loop caught AI errors only about half the time. Being technically present is not the same as being structurally engaged. In "vibe coding," the developer accepts AI output and ships it; they are in the loop in name only.

Anthropic itself published research on how AI assistance impacts the formation of coding skills, with results in the same direction: heavy reliance correlates with weaker independent skill development unless the reader actively engages with what the AI produces.

What this means for the courses on this site: do not implement the recommendations from any chapter without forming your own picture of the whole. If chapter 4 of one course tells you to put testing rules in CLAUDE.md and chapter 9 of another course tells you to put them in a skill, those are not contradictions — they are showing you the menu of options. You decide where each piece actually goes in your project, after reading both. AI cannot do this for you. AI is a writer; you are the editor.

The reader who skims, asks Claude "summarise the courses and apply them to my repo," and accepts the result will end up with exactly the bloated CLAUDE.md + duplicated skills + redundant rules pattern this guide is about. The reader who pauses, prunes, and consolidates will end up with a sharp, small context that performs measurably better.

Why the courses on this site look contradictory

If you have read more than two or three courses on this site, you have noticed something: each one treats its topic as central. The skills course argues that skills are the right home for most procedural guidance. The CLAUDE.md material argues that CLAUDE.md is the place for the things the model needs to know on every turn. The hooks course makes the case that anything deterministic should live in a hook. The sub-agents material argues that heavy, role-specific instructions belong in a sub-agent prompt. Read end-to-end, the courses can feel like they are quietly disagreeing.

They are not. Each course is honest within its frame, and each frame is deliberately deep, because the only way to teach a surface well is to spend a course on it. A chapter that gave skills two pages of attention so it could "fairly weigh skills against CLAUDE.md against hooks" would teach none of them properly. The depth is the point.

But that means the courses are a menu, not a configuration. If you read all of them and try to install everything each one recommends — or worse, if you ask Claude to read across them and do that installation for you — you will end up with the bloat pattern this guide opened with. Three places teaching three deep treatments of testing become three places where testing rules now live in your project, with subtle disagreements baked in because each course was written with a different emphasis.

Holding the global picture is your job, not the courses' job and not Claude's job. When you finish a course, the right question is not "what should I add to my project?" It is "of everything I now know is possible, what does my project actually need, and where — in one place — should it live?" The other options stay on the menu, available when their moment comes. They do not all need to be cooked at once.

This is also why the same idea sometimes appears in two courses at once. It is not redundancy in the writing — it is the same surface viewed from two different audiences' starting points. One course introduces skills as "shortcuts you can type." Another introduces skills as "context the model can fetch on demand." Same mechanism, two framings, neither wrong. Your job is to land on the mental model that fits how you work, and not to install both.

The tells: spotting the pattern in your own sessions

Once you know what the failure mode looks like, you start noticing it in real time. The signs are not subtle. They are just easy to miss when you are in the flow of accepting helpful suggestions one after another.

The most common tell is a small ask producing a polished new section. You said "the test command in this file is wrong, can you update it," and what came back is the corrected command plus a freshly written paragraph about your project's testing philosophy and a note suggesting a related convention. Nothing in the suggestion is bad in isolation. Nothing was asked for either. The model optimised locally for "leave this file better than I found it," and "better" reads as "more."

Another tell is a "policy" being proposed for a thing your project clearly already has a position on. If your repository has been Python-only for three years and Claude proposes adding "we use Python" to CLAUDE.md, the proposal is dead weight — the file extensions are already telling Claude that. The fact that the suggestion reads well can paper over the fact that it adds no constraint that did not exist already.

Watch for new files appearing when an existing file would have done. Watch for prose written for things that, once you stop and think, are structurally deterministic — the kind of thing a script could enforce instead of a paragraph the model has to remember. Watch for "examples" sections growing every time you ask a question, even when the original example was clear. Watch, especially, for additions that "harmonise" guidance you did not realise was in conflict — that often means the model invented a conflict to resolve, because resolving things is rewarded.

None of these symptoms are reasons to mistrust AI. They are reasons to read what AI proposes the same way you would read a junior teammate's pull request — with the assumption that some of it is good, some of it is filler, and the editorial judgment about which is which sits with you.

The single most important habit

If you take only one thing from this guide: when AI proposes adding to your context, your first reaction should be where can this be removed instead? — not where should this go?. Removal is almost always the right move. Keeping the context window small, sharp, and load-bearing is the single highest-leverage thing you can do for AI-assisted work.

The courses on this site are deliberately rich because they cover a lot of surfaces. They show you the full menu of options — CLAUDE.md, skills, sub-agents, hooks, output styles, rules. That richness is what you need to understand the system. It is not what you need to install. When you implement a course's recommendations in your own project, your job is to read across all the chapters, form a global picture, and put each piece of guidance in one place — the right place — with no duplicates.

AI cannot do that pruning for you. It does not have the global view. It defaults to addition, and the published research is unambiguous about the cost of that default. Be the editor.
