Chapter 5

Subdomains: Core, Supporting, Generic

Where to spend your best engineers — and what to outsource without guilt

You ended chapter 4 with a context map: boxes for contexts, arrows labeled with patterns. This chapter is about a different colored marker. We're going to color the boxes by strategic value: which contexts are your competitive moat, which are necessary plumbing, and which you should stop reinventing.

This is the conversation a CTO wishes their senior engineers were having. Most engineers default to "all the code is interesting and worth building." That's a great mindset for a junior. For a senior, it's how teams burn 18 months reimplementing OAuth.

The strategic question

Open the careers page of three of your competitors. Look at the job descriptions. Notice that almost all of them are hiring for the same things: backend engineers, mobile engineers, infra, data. The same stacks. The same technologies. The same buzzwords.

Now look at one specific question: where do they say their team uniquely shines?

You'll see one or two areas singled out. "Our matching algorithm." "Our underwriting models." "Our route optimization." That singled-out thing is their core domain. Everything else is, to them, just plumbing.

The DDD subdomain lens is a way to make this kind of investment thinking explicit, structured, and applicable to your codebase. It splits everything you build into three buckets:

🏆
Core — your competitive moat. Where you must outclass everyone.
🛠️
Supporting — needed, custom-shaped, but not a differentiator.
🔌
Generic — needed, but identical at every company. Buy it.

Three buckets. That's the entire model. The interesting part is the discipline of applying it honestly.

Core domain

Core Domain — the subdomain that gives your business its competitive advantage. The part of the system you cannot afford to be merely "okay" at. It is what your product would still need to be the best in the world at, even if you fired half the team and rewrote everything else.
Do not call something "core" because it's hard, important, fun, or got the most engineering hours in the last quarter. "Core" is a market position, not an engineering opinion. The litmus test is competitive: if your competitor copied this exactly, would you lose?

Core is rare. Most companies have one core subdomain. Some have two. Almost none have five.

Your core is where the senior engineers go. Where the deepest tests live. Where you accept five-month projects that nobody else would attempt. Where the code has to be well-modeled, because it will outlive every other module in the repo. The core domain is the only place where reinventing the wheel is justified — because off-the-shelf wheels are too round, too generic, too easy for competitors to also buy.

What's core for whom?

  • For Spotify: music recommendation and playback quality. (Not "having a music library" — everyone has one.)
  • For Uber: ETA prediction, dispatch, surge pricing. (Not "showing a map" — Google Maps does that.)
  • For a tax-filing app: tax computation correctness. (Not "scanning receipts" — that's commodity.)
  • For a fitness app: workout planning, progressive overload, recovery modeling. (Not "tracking weight" — you can do that with a Notes app.)

Notice how narrow each one is. "Spotify is great because it does music." No, Spotify is great because of recommendations. The rest of the experience exists to deliver that core well.

Supporting subdomain

Supporting Subdomain — necessary work that is custom to your business but does not differentiate you in the market. You have to build it because no off-the-shelf product fits well enough. But you don't have to be the best at it; you only have to be good enough to support the core.
Do not call supporting work "secondary" or "lesser." It's just not where the moat is. A bad supporting subdomain can sink the company; an excellent one rarely wins it. Aim for "competently boring."

Supporting subdomains are the unglamorous middle child. Examples:

  • Admin dashboards for the operations team.
  • Internal reporting and metrics for finance.
  • The CMS your marketing team uses to push promotions.
  • Bulk-import tools for migrations.
  • The customer-support agent's view of a user's history.

You probably can't buy these — they're too specific to how your business works. But you also shouldn't over-invest in them. The right move is usually: junior or mid-level engineers, simple architecture, ship fast, accept some tech debt, refactor only when it actively hurts.

The most common mistake with supporting subdomains is treating them like core. Spending six months perfecting an admin dashboard nobody outside the company sees is, strategically, a misallocation. The same six months on the recommendation engine could have been the difference between staying competitive and being acquired.

Generic subdomain

Generic Subdomain — necessary work that is identical (or nearly so) at every company. Authentication, payment processing, email delivery, push notifications, file storage. Solved problems with mature vendors and open-source options.
Do not say "we'll just write our own — it's not that hard." That sentence has launched a thousand half-built auth systems. Generic does not mean trivial; it means somebody else has already paid the price of doing it right.

Generic subdomains are where teams hemorrhage time. Auth alone could fund a small startup. Building it yourself in 2026 is almost always wrong because:

  • The threat surface (OAuth flows, SSO, MFA, recovery, SCIM, audit logs, breach response) is enormous.
  • Mature vendors (Auth0, Firebase Auth, AWS Cognito, Clerk, Supabase) have spent thousands of engineer-years on it.
  • It rarely appears in the things customers care about. Nobody picked your app for "great password reset flow."

The recipe for generic subdomains is brutal and clarifying: buy, integrate behind an ACL, move on. The ACL (chapter 4) is what lets you swap vendors later without burning the codebase.

"We need it to be perfect, so we'll build it ourselves." That sentence about a generic subdomain is a five-alarm fire. The vendor's solution is almost certainly more correct than yours will be after a year of work, because they only do that one thing and you have a whole product to ship. The right argument is the opposite: "this is generic; we'll buy the vendor and put our senior engineers on something we can win."

The simple test: "if a competitor copied this exactly, would we lose?"

This is the entire model in one sentence. Apply it to any feature, module, or subdomain you can name. The answer puts it in a bucket:

"If a competitor copied this exactly, would we lose?" Bucket Investment level
Yes — this is our advantage Core Senior engineers, deep tests, custom build, multi-quarter horizon
No, but we still need it — nobody sells exactly this Supporting Mid-level engineers, simple, ship fast, refactor when it bleeds
No — everyone has one of these Generic Buy a vendor, wrap in ACL, never touch again

If you're tempted to argue that "well, our auth is special because we have B2B SSO and SCIM and..." — stop. Auth0 also handles B2B SSO and SCIM. Generic does not mean simple. It means someone else has built this exact thing, and they have a sales team motivated to keep up with your needs.

Subdomain vs bounded context (the same confusion as last chapter)

You met the distinction in chapter 3 and we promised we'd come back to it. Here's the short version:

Subdomain
A piece of the BUSINESS PROBLEM. "We need to recommend music to listeners." "We need to verify identities." "We need to process payments." Lives in slide decks, OKRs, and business strategy meetings. Has a strategic classification: core / supporting / generic.
Bounded Context
A piece of the SOLUTION. ":recommendations:domain" ":onboarding:domain" ":payments:domain" Lives in the build graph, the codebase, and the team org. Has a model, language, team, and an enforced boundary.

In an ideal world, one subdomain maps to one bounded context. In reality:

  • Sometimes one subdomain spans two contexts (a legacy "Payments" module and a new "PaymentsV2" module both serve the Payments subdomain).
  • Sometimes one context serves two subdomains (a "Notifications" context delivering both core marketing pings and generic password-reset emails).
  • Sometimes the mapping is messy because the org has been through three reorgs since the system was designed.

The classification — core, supporting, generic — applies to subdomains. Then you ask: which contexts implement this subdomain? Those contexts inherit some of the strategic weight, but the classification itself is a business decision, not a code decision.

A worked example: classifying a fitness app

Let's say you ship a fitness app. The features are:

  • Sign up, log in, password reset, MFA.
  • Personalized workout plans, progressive overload, recovery modeling.
  • User profile (name, photo, bio, friends).
  • Tracking weight, body measurements, mood.
  • In-app purchases (subscriptions, paid programs).
  • Push notifications (workout reminders, social interactions, marketing).
  • Analytics for engagement, retention, churn.

Run each through the "if a competitor copied this exactly, would we lose?" test:

Workout Planning (programs, periodization, recovery) — CORE. This is why someone picks your app over the dozen alternatives. You hire your two best engineers here. You write the deepest tests here. You let nothing else block work in this module.

Tracking (weight, measurements, mood) — SUPPORTING. Necessary, custom to your data model, but a competitor copying it doesn't kill you. Mid-level engineers, simple Room schema, refactor only when needed.

Profiles & Social — SUPPORTING, leaning generic. You'll probably build this yourself, but with off-the-shelf libraries for image cropping and chat.

Auth — GENERIC. Use Firebase Auth or Auth0. Wrap in an ACL. Never look at it again unless billing changes.

Payments — GENERIC. Use Google Play Billing + RevenueCat or similar. Behind an ACL.

Push Notifications — GENERIC for transport (FCM), but the scheduling logic ("send a workout reminder if the user hasn't trained in 3 days") is SUPPORTING at minimum, possibly tipping into CORE if your retention story depends on it.

Analytics — GENERIC for the pipe (Mixpanel, Firebase Analytics), SUPPORTING for the dashboards your PMs read.

One core. Two-and-a-half supporting. Four generic. That's a healthy distribution. If your spreadsheet shows seven cores and one generic, you're either deluded or you're a unicorn. (Probably the first.)

Why this matters: where to invest your senior engineers

The classification is not academic. It changes your week:

// CORE work // Owner: senior engineer with deep domain stake // Architecture: full DDD treatment, aggregates, value objects, events // Tests: domain unit tests, property tests, scenario tests // Velocity: deliberately slow; correctness over throughput // Refactor cadence: continuous // SUPPORTING work // Owner: mid-level engineer or rotating ownership // Architecture: lightweight; simple Room schema, plain Kotlin classes // Tests: integration tests; pragmatic unit coverage // Velocity: ship fast, accept tech debt // Refactor cadence: when it bleeds // GENERIC work // Owner: vendor + a junior engineer to maintain the integration // Architecture: ACL only; vendor SDK does the work // Tests: contract test for the ACL boundary // Velocity: integrate once, then forget // Refactor cadence: only on vendor migration

The point is not that you write less-quality code in supporting subdomains. The point is that the shape of "good enough" is different for each. A perfectionist DDD treatment of an admin dashboard is over-engineering. A "let's just throw it together" treatment of your core recommendation engine is malpractice.

The Android shape: subdomains and Gradle modules

You don't tag subdomains in code with annotations. You tag them in the build graph — in your settings.gradle.kts — using the module path itself, and you reflect the classification in the dependency rules and the architecture inside each module.

// settings.gradle.kts — subdomain classification visible in the layout // CORE subdomain — the workout-planning brain include(":workout-planning:domain") // pure Kotlin, deep model include(":workout-planning:data") include(":workout-planning:feature") // SUPPORTING subdomains include(":tracking:domain") // simpler model, less ceremony include(":tracking:data") include(":tracking:feature") include(":profiles:feature") // single module — small enough // GENERIC subdomains — mostly vendor wrappers include(":auth:vendor") // Firebase Auth ACL include(":payments:vendor") // Play Billing + RevenueCat ACL include(":notifications:vendor") // FCM transport ACL include(":analytics:vendor") // Mixpanel ACL // :app — wires everything together

Notice three things. (1) The core subdomain gets the full :domain / :data / :feature trio — it deserves the architecture. (2) Supporting subdomains can collapse modules — :profiles:feature alone is fine if there's barely any logic. (3) Generic subdomains live as :vendor modules, signaling that they're thin wrappers around a third party. Anyone reading settings.gradle.kts can see the strategic shape.

Module naming carries the classification. If you want this discipline to outlive the engineer who introduced it, name your generic-subdomain modules with a :vendor or :integration suffix. New engineers reading the build graph immediately understand "oh, this is a wrapper, not a place to add new features." Conversely, your core subdomain doesn't need a special suffix — it should look like the heart of the codebase, because it is.

The "everything is core" trap

Every team you work with will, over time, reclassify everything as core. The recommendations engineer thinks recommendations are core (probably right). The auth engineer thinks auth is core ("our auth is special!"). The analytics engineer thinks analytics are core. The notifications team is sure their work is core because retention.

Each of them is partially correct: their work is important, hard, and load-bearing. None of them are doing the right test. The test is not "is this important to the company?" The test is "would a competitor copying this exactly cost us users?"

Beware the "we're all core" classification. If your spreadsheet ends up with five core subdomains, three things are happening: (1) you flinched on the litmus test, (2) you're spreading senior engineers too thin to actually win at any of them, and (3) you'll under-invest in the one that actually is core because it had to share the budget. Force-rank. Pick at most two cores. Demote the rest.

It helps to do the classification as a group, with a PM and a senior engineer in the room, and to write down the answer to the litmus test for each one. Forcing the words "if Acme copied our identity verification exactly, customers wouldn't notice" out of someone's mouth is what cures the "everything is core" delusion.

How classification reshapes hiring, prioritization, and tech choices

Once you've done the exercise, you have a one-page document that quietly answers a lot of expensive arguments:

Hiring

Job descriptions for engineers on the core subdomain emphasize depth and domain stake (e.g., "experience designing reinforcement-learning recommenders" or "comfortable with multi-week refactors"). Job descriptions for engineers on supporting subdomains emphasize delivery and breadth ("ship features fast across multiple modules"). Job descriptions for generic-subdomain work essentially do not exist — that work is a vendor + a maintainer.

Prioritization

When two PMs fight for the same sprint, the subdomain classification breaks the tie. A core-domain bug usually wins over a supporting-domain feature. A generic-subdomain "improvement" is almost always wrong — the answer is to live with the vendor's behavior or switch vendors.

Tech choices

Core-domain code is where you can justify exotic libraries, custom DSLs, KMP for shared models. Supporting-domain code uses the boring default stack (Room, Retrofit, Compose, Hilt, no surprises). Generic-domain code uses whatever the vendor SDK requires — you don't pick the stack, the vendor did.

Common mistakes

  • Choosing the most "interesting" subdomain instead of the most differentiating one. Engineers love a deep technical problem. The deep technical problem is not always the moat.
  • Treating generic stuff as bespoke. Auth, search, file upload, image processing — teams burn quarters here that should have been one Stripe-or-Firebase integration.
  • Treating bespoke stuff as generic. The other failure: assuming "we can just use the vendor's recommendation engine" when recommendations are your core. Off-the-shelf recommenders deliver everyone-else's experience.
  • Letting the classification rot. What was core in 2022 (a custom pricing engine) may be commodity in 2026 (the LLM that ate the pricing-engine market). Re-classify yearly.
  • Treating "we built it ourselves" as evidence it's core. Sunk cost. The decision to keep maintaining it is a fresh decision, made on today's facts.

Build vs buy vs outsource: the cheat sheet

Subdomain Build Buy Outsource
Core ✅ Always ❌ Never — would defeat the moat ❌ Never — this is your IP
Supporting ✅ Default ⚠️ If a vendor fits cleanly ⚠️ For one-off projects (migrations)
Generic ❌ Don't, unless you must ✅ Default ✅ For undifferentiated maintenance

The hardest cell is "supporting + buy." Sometimes a SaaS dashboard product really does fit your reporting needs. Sometimes it almost fits but not quite, and you'll spend more on customization than you'd spend building it. Decide deliberately, not by default.

A subdomain audit, in 30 minutes

30 minutes. Open settings.gradle.kts. A spreadsheet (or sticky notes).

  1. List every Gradle module in your app.
  2. For each module, write down which subdomain it serves. (Some modules serve multiple — that's a signal worth flagging.)
  3. For each subdomain, ask the litmus test out loud: "If a competitor copied this exactly, would we lose?" Be honest.
  4. Tag each subdomain: CORE, SUPPORTING, or GENERIC.
  5. Count how many cores you ended up with. If it's more than two, force-rank and demote the rest.
  6. For each GENERIC subdomain, write down: "Does a vendor solve this? If yes, why didn't we use them?" The answer is usually "we didn't think about it" — a fixable problem.
  7. For each CORE subdomain, count the senior engineers actively working there. If the answer is zero, you've identified a strategic mismatch worth raising in the next planning cycle.

Output: a one-page table with three columns (subdomain, classification, current investment) and a short list of "we should rebalance" items. Bring it to your next architecture review or 1:1 with your engineering manager. This document will outweigh a quarter's worth of feature debate.

When the classification changes

Subdomain classifications are not permanent. Three things commonly shift them:

  • Vendor commoditization. What you had to build in-house in 2018 (search, recommendations, feature flags, observability) is a vendor SaaS in 2026. Periodically scan: is anything we currently treat as core now genuinely available as a service?
  • Strategic pivot. The company decides its core is shifting from "best-in-class workouts" to "best community for fitness." Suddenly Profiles & Social moves from supporting to core, and Workout Planning may demote a tier.
  • Regulatory or compliance shift. Auth used to be generic. Then your enterprise customers demanded SCIM, SSO, audit logs, custom IDP integration. The vendor still handles 90%, but the 10% you have to build is now strategically meaningful. The classification may become "generic with a thin core wrapper."

Re-classify yearly. Don't wait for the architecture review to discover the moat moved.
