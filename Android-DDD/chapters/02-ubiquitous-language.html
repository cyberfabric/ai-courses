Chapter 2

Ubiquitous Language

Same word, same meaning, everywhere — and a one-page doc you can commit today

End of chapter 1, I asked you to find three terms in your codebase that mean different things to different people. If you actually did the exercise, you have the perfect raw material for this chapter. If you didn't — go do it before you finish reading. The whole point of ubiquitous language is that it's grounded in the actual mess your team has, not in textbook examples.

This chapter is about the single highest-leverage practice in all of DDD. It costs almost nothing to start, requires no new dependencies, no architectural rewrite, no buy-in from leadership. It's a markdown file. And the teams that actually maintain one ship fewer bugs, onboard new engineers faster, and stop having the same naming argument every quarter.

The translation tax

Sit in any product meeting and listen to the words flying past:

  • The PM says "user."
  • The designer says "member."
  • The backend engineer says "account."
  • QA says "profile."
  • The database column is subscriber_id.
  • The analytics event is visitor_signup_completed.
  • The Compose component is UserProfileCard, the Room entity is AccountEntity, and the Retrofit DTO is SubscriberDto.

Six words. One concept. Every conversation, PR, ticket, and bug investigation has a hidden translation step. Every new engineer pays it for months. Every cross-team meeting pays it again. Multiply by ten core concepts and a team of fifteen, and you've built a tax that compounds daily.

That tax is invisible because there's no line item for it. It just shows up as "this took longer than I thought" on every estimate, forever.

What "ubiquitous" actually means

Ubiquitous Language — one word per concept, used identically by everyone (PM, design, engineering, QA, support) and in every artifact (chat, tickets, code, database columns, analytics events, screens).
Do not say "domain glossary" (sounds like a wiki page nobody reads), "business language" (excludes engineers), or "naming convention" (sounds like a style guide). The word is ubiquitous — it's everywhere or it's broken.

The test is brutal and simple: pick a random class name in your code. Walk over to a PM and say it out loud. Do they recognize it? If yes, congratulations. If they say "wait, what's a SubscriberRecord?" — your language isn't ubiquitous. It's a private dialect spoken only by engineers, with translation overhead at every boundary.

Where the language lives

One file. At the root of your repo. Next to CLAUDE.md, README.md, and CONTRIBUTING.md.

your-android-app/ ├── README.md ├── CLAUDE.md ├── UBIQUITOUS_LANGUAGE.md <-- this is the one ├── settings.gradle.kts ├── app/ ├── domain/ ├── data/ └── feature/

That's the whole infrastructure. Markdown, version-controlled, reviewed in PRs like any other file. There's a sneaky benefit to this location: tooling like Claude Code automatically loads root-level docs into context on every task, which means once the file exists, every AI-assisted edit reads it for free. We'll go deep on that integration in chapter 11; for now, just know that "where it lives" matters not just for humans.

What goes in it

Each entry has the same five-part shape. Boring is the point — boring is what makes it scannable.

# UBIQUITOUS_LANGUAGE.md ## Trip **Definition.** A passenger's request to be transported from a pickup location to a dropoff location, fulfilled by a driver. A Trip exists from the moment the rider taps "Request" until it reaches a terminal state (Completed, Cancelled, or Expired). **Examples.** - A rider requests a Trip from home to the airport. - A driver accepts a Trip and is matched to it. - A Trip is Cancelled if no driver accepts within 5 minutes. **Do not say.** Ride, RideRequest, Order, Job, Booking, Journey. The word is *Trip* — in chat, tickets, code, and analytics. **Related.** TripStatus, Pickup, Dropoff, Fare, Driver, Rider. --- ## Pickup **Definition.** The location and instant at which a Driver is expected to meet the Rider. A Pickup has coordinates, an address, and an estimated arrival time. **Examples.** - "Pickup is at 221B Baker Street, ETA 3 minutes." - A Trip cannot start without a confirmed Pickup. **Do not say.** Origin, From, StartLocation, PickupPoint. **Related.** Trip, Dropoff, ETA, Address.

Every entry: definition, examples, "do not say", related terms. No more, no less. Resist the urge to add UML diagrams, ER models, or a section on "implementation details." Those drift faster than the language itself, and they pollute the doc with stale crap.

How to build one (the actual workshop)

Pair an engineer with a domain expert. PM, designer, support lead — whoever can answer "what does this word actually mean to the business?" Block 90 minutes. Bring snacks.

  1. List terms. Walk through one feature out loud. Every time someone uses a noun, write it on a sticky (or a Google Doc, or a Miro board). Don't filter. Twenty minutes of just dumping.
  2. Cluster synonyms. "User," "member," "account," "profile" — they go in one group. Pick the winner together. The PM gets the deciding vote because they speak to customers; if customers say "member," the code says Member.
  3. Define the winners. One sentence per term. If you can't define it without using another term you also need to define, that's fine — add the dependency to the list.
  4. Write the "do not say" list. The losing synonyms become the banned list. This is the most important section because it's what new engineers grep for.
  5. Commit. Open a PR titled "Initial UBIQUITOUS_LANGUAGE.md for the Trip feature." Get reactions. Ship.

You will not produce a complete language in 90 minutes. You'll produce ten to fifteen entries for one feature. That's plenty. The doc grows on every PR after that.

The naming compass

Once you have a language, the question becomes: how does it land in code? DDD has a tidy answer Eric Evans called the naming compass, though he didn't use those words. It's three rules:

Nouns
become classes — Trip, Pickup, Driver
Verbs
become methods — trip.cancel(), driver.accept(trip)
Adjectives
become flags or value objects — Premium, Cancelled, Confirmed

If you find yourself reaching for "Manager," "Helper," "Service," "Util," "Processor" or "Handler" — stop. Those are not domain words. They're the noises engineers make when they don't know what to call something. Find the actual domain noun or verb and use it.

The Manager smell. A class called UserManager is almost always one of three things in disguise: (1) a missing entity (it's actually User with the methods on it), (2) a missing repository (it's actually UserRepository), or (3) a missing domain service (it's a stateless operation that needs a real verb-based name like UserAuthenticator). When in doubt, the noun – or the verb – is the right answer; "Manager" never is.

The smells that reveal a missing language

Open your project's find-usages on these substrings. Each match is a "the team didn't have a word for this, so we made one up" moment:

  • Manager — SessionManager, NotificationManager (the Android one is fine; yours probably isn't), UserManager.
  • Helper — DateHelper, StringHelper, ValidationHelper. Helper to whom?
  • Util / Utils — the universal "I didn't know where to put this" suffix.
  • Processor — OrderProcessor, PaymentProcessor. The verb is hiding in there. chargePayment(order).
  • Handler — usually a missing event listener or callback. Sometimes a missing aggregate.
  • Service on something that has state — "service" should be stateless. If yours holds a cache, it's an entity or repository.
  • Info / Data / Object as suffixes — UserInfo, OrderData, RideObject. Pure noise.

None of these are illegal. Some have legitimate uses (Android's own InputMethodManager is genuinely a manager). But every match in your own code is a question: "is there a real domain word I should be using here instead?" The answer is "yes" surprisingly often.

Android-specific traps

Mobile has its own contamination problem: framework words leaking into domain code. Watch out for:

Framework leak
// In :domain class TripScreen( val trip: Trip, val activity: Activity, val onBackPressed: () -> Unit ) class CheckoutFragment(...) class CartView(...) class OrderActivity(...)
Domain words only
// In :domain class Trip( val id: TripId, val pickup: Pickup, val dropoff: Dropoff ) { fun cancel(): Trip = ... } // :feature talks about Screens, :domain doesn't.

"Screen," "view," "fragment," "activity," "intent" (the Android kind), "viewmodel" — these are presentation concepts. They have no business in your :domain module. If you find them there, you've smuggled UI thinking into the model. Push it back out.

The reverse is also true: domain words belong inside ViewModels and Composables, not invented UI vocabulary. A screen showing trip status should be called TripStatusScreen, not StatusViewerScreen. Same word, every layer.

A worked example: the Trip glossary

Let's go back to the ride-sharing app from chapter 1. The team had six lookalike classes. Here's what their UBIQUITOUS_LANGUAGE.md looked like after the disambiguation workshop:

RidePickupRequest — the lifecycle of a passenger's intent to be picked up, from tap to terminal state. Owned by the Trip context. A RidePickupRequest is created by the Rider, accepted by a Driver, and reconciled by Billing.
Do not say "Order" (that's Billing's word for the same lifecycle, post-completion), "Ride" (ambiguous — there is no noun for "the ride itself"), "Job" (that's how the Driver app refers to an accepted RidePickupRequest), or "Booking" (a marketing word, not a domain word).

Notice the language box doesn't pretend the other words don't exist. It says: "yes, those words exist; here's where each is legitimate; here's the canonical word in this context." The team didn't try to ban "Order" globally — the Billing context legitimately uses it. They scoped the language to a context. (Foreshadowing chapter 3.)

How language drift happens — and how to stop it

Even good languages decay. Here's how, and what to do about it:

Drift mode #1: the new feature

A new engineer joins, ships a feature, doesn't read UBIQUITOUS_LANGUAGE.md. Six new words enter the codebase. Nobody on the review catches it because the reviewer was focused on logic, not vocabulary.

Fix: add a single line to your PR template — "Did this PR introduce any new domain terms? If so, update UBIQUITOUS_LANGUAGE.md in this PR or open a follow-up ticket." That's the entire intervention. Most drift dies right there.

Drift mode #2: the API change

Backend renames a field. Mobile follows. Suddenly the ubiquitous word is whatever the backend team picked this week. The doc lags.

Fix: when an API DTO disagrees with the language, the DTO is wrong (from your perspective). Map it at the boundary. The mapper file becomes a useful artifact: it's the literal translation layer between two contexts. We'll cover this as the anti-corruption layer in chapter 4.

Drift mode #3: the marketing pivot

Marketing decides the product is now called "GoNow" instead of "RideHail." The ubiquitous language updates, you do a search-replace across the repo, and life moves on. This one is fine — it's the system working as intended.

Treat the language doc like a contract. When the doc and the code disagree, one of them is wrong. Decide which. Either update the doc or rename the code. Don't let them silently diverge — that's how you end up with five different words for "Trip" again.

The naming compass, applied to a real feature

Let's run the compass on a checkout flow and see what falls out. Suppose your domain expert says:

"A shopper builds a cart, then places an order. Once the order is paid, it's confirmed. A confirmed order can be shipped. A shipped order can be delivered. The shopper can cancel an order any time before it's shipped."

Run the compass over that paragraph:

  • Nouns → classes: Shopper, Cart, Order, Payment, Shipment, Delivery.
  • Verbs → methods: cart.addItem(), cart.placeOrder(), order.pay(), order.confirm(), order.ship(), order.cancel().
  • Adjectives → states / value objects: Confirmed, Paid, Shipped, Delivered, Cancelled — modeled as a sealed class OrderStatus.

That paragraph just gave you the entire skeleton of your feature. Notice what's not in there: no "Manager," no "Service," no "Helper," no "Controller." The domain expert didn't use those words because they're not domain words. If you start writing OrderManager, you've already drifted.

// Compass-driven, not framework-driven sealed class OrderStatus { object Pending : OrderStatus() data class Paid(val at: Instant) : OrderStatus() data class Confirmed(val at: Instant) : OrderStatus() data class Shipped(val tracking: TrackingNumber) : OrderStatus() data class Delivered(val at: Instant) : OrderStatus() data class Cancelled(val at: Instant, val reason: CancellationReason) : OrderStatus() } class Order( val id: OrderId, val shopper: ShopperId, val items: List<OrderItem>, val status: OrderStatus ) { fun pay(at: Instant): Order = require(status is OrderStatus.Pending).let { copy(status = OrderStatus.Paid(at)) } fun cancel(at: Instant, reason: CancellationReason): Order = require(status !is OrderStatus.Shipped && status !is OrderStatus.Delivered).let { copy(status = OrderStatus.Cancelled(at, reason)) } }

Read that out loud. "Order pay at... order cancel at, reason." It sounds almost exactly like the domain expert's sentence, because it is. That's the goal.

The PR-template intervention

This is so cheap it feels like cheating. Add to .github/PULL_REQUEST_TEMPLATE.md:

## Domain Language - [ ] No new domain terms in this PR, OR - [ ] New terms have been added to UBIQUITOUS_LANGUAGE.md, OR - [ ] A follow-up ticket has been opened: #_____ ## Anti-vocabulary check Did I introduce any of: Manager, Helper, Util, Processor, Handler, Info, Data (as suffixes)? If so, why not a domain word?

Two checkboxes. They take ten seconds to fill. They surface 90% of language drift before merge. The first time someone in code review says "what does OrderProcessor do that isn't already on Order?" the culture has shifted.

A short refactor: pick one ambiguous class

Theory is cheap. Let's do one rename. Pick a class in your codebase named with a generic suffix — UserManager, OrderProcessor, PaymentHelper, anything. Walk through this:

// Before class CheckoutHelper( private val cartRepo: CartRepository, private val payments: PaymentService, private val analytics: Analytics ) { suspend fun checkout(cart: Cart, paymentMethod: PaymentMethod): Result<Order> { // 80 lines of validation, charging, persistence, eventing } } // After class PlaceOrder( private val cartRepo: CartRepository, private val payments: PaymentGateway, private val analytics: DomainEventPublisher ) { suspend operator fun invoke( cart: Cart, paymentMethod: PaymentMethod ): Result<Order> { // same 80 lines, but now the file name says what it does } }

The class is now named after a verb in the domain language ("place an order"). The dependency on PaymentService became PaymentGateway — a less ceremonial word, more honest about what it does. The function is invoked as placeOrder(cart, method), which reads like the business sentence.

This rename took one minute. It will pay back every time someone greps for "where do orders get placed?" — and instead of finding seventeen call sites of checkoutHelper.checkout, finds one verb that matches the language.

Why this matters more on Android than you think

Backend codebases get language by accident — schema, API contracts, admin tools all force vocabulary alignment. Mobile codebases get language only on purpose. Nothing about Android forces you to name Trip as Trip instead of Ride. The compiler won't care. The lint rules won't care. Production won't care, until the day the marketing copy says "Your trip starts soon" and the in-app banner says "Your ride is on its way" and a customer screenshots both for a 1-star review.

How Claude Code uses the language doc

Sneak preview — the full chapter on this is chapter 11. When Claude Code starts in a repo, it auto-loads root-level documentation into its context. UBIQUITOUS_LANGUAGE.md is one of those files. From that moment on, every code-generation task implicitly knows your domain words.

That means: ask Claude to "add a status badge to the trip card" and it writes TripStatusBadge, not RideStatusBadge. Ask it to "add a checkout button" and it generates PlaceOrderButton using your verb. The doc becomes the leverage that propagates the language across every change you make. It also becomes the leverage for the AI-driven refactor we'll do in chapter 12.

Exercise: write your first three entries

Thirty minutes. Today.

  1. Create UBIQUITOUS_LANGUAGE.md at the root of your repo.
  2. Pick three core terms from your codebase — the ones from your chapter 1 exercise are perfect. (If you skipped that, pick three nouns that appear in five or more places.)
  3. For each, write the five-part entry: definition, examples, "do not say", related, plus a one-sentence note on which Gradle module owns it.
  4. Open a PR. Title: "Add initial UBIQUITOUS_LANGUAGE.md."
  5. In the PR description, tag a PM or designer. Ask: "Does this match how you talk about these things?"

The PR is the point. The entries will be wrong — that's expected. The conversation in the PR comments is the actual workshop, asynchronously.

What's next

You now have a vocabulary. The next obvious question is: where does it apply? When the Billing team says "Order" and the Sales team says "Order" and they mean different things — who's right? The answer is "both, in their own context," and that's exactly what bounded contexts are for.
