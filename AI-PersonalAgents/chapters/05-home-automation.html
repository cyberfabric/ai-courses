Home & IoT Automation

Smart home meets smart agent — natural language control for lights, climate, and security

Why AI Agents Beat Traditional Home Automation

In the last chapter you taught your agent who you are — your schedule, your diet, your budget, your boundaries. Now it's time to give it control of the physical world around you.

You probably already have some smart home devices. Maybe a smart bulb, a thermostat, a robot vacuum. And you probably control them through 3-4 different apps, each with its own interface, its own routines, its own frustrations.

Traditional smart home automation works like a vending machine: press button B7, get exactly B7. "When motion detected in hallway AND time is after 10 PM, turn on hallway light at 20% brightness." It works, but it's rigid. You can't say "make it cozy for movie night" to an IFTTT rule.

An AI agent changes the game. Instead of programming rigid if/then rules, you describe what you want and the agent figures out how.

Traditional Automation
Rule 1: At 7 PM, set living room lights to 40% Rule 2: At 7 PM, set thermostat to 21°C Rule 3: At 7 PM, close blinds Rule 4: At 7 PM, turn on TV (4 rules, zero flexibility, same thing every night)
Agent-Driven Automation
"Make it cozy for movie night" Agent checks: current temperature, sunset time, who's home, what was last watched. Adjusts lights, blinds, thermostat, and TV accordingly. Different every time based on context.

The difference is intent vs. instruction. You express intent ("make it cozy"), the agent translates that into the right instructions for right now. On a hot summer evening, "cozy" means AC on and blinds closed against the sun. On a cold winter night, "cozy" means heater up and warm lighting. Same word, different actions. That's what an AI agent brings to your smart home.

The Foundation: Home Assistant

Before your agent can control anything, it needs a translator — something that speaks to all your devices regardless of brand. That translator is Home Assistant.

Home Assistant is an open-source home automation platform that connects to over 2,000 device types. It's the Rosetta Stone of smart homes. Your Philips Hue bulbs, your Nest thermostat, your Xiaomi vacuum, your IKEA blinds — Home Assistant talks to all of them through a single API.

The Architecture

Here's how the pieces fit together:

You → message your agent via Telegram → OpenClaw understands intent → calls Home Assistant API → controls your devices

You never open a device app. You never touch a routine editor. You just text.

Connecting OpenClaw to Home Assistant

OpenClaw has a built-in Home Assistant plugin. Here's how to wire them together:

  1. Make sure Home Assistant is running on your network (Raspberry Pi, mini PC, or a VM)
  2. Create a Long-Lived Access Token in Home Assistant: Profile → Security → Long-Lived Access Tokens → Create Token
  3. Add the integration to OpenClaw's config:
integrations:
  home_assistant:
    enabled: true
    url: "http://192.168.1.100:8123"
    token: "eyJ0eXAi...your-long-token-here"
    discover_entities: true
  1. Restart OpenClaw. It will discover all your Home Assistant entities automatically.

Check the logs — you should see something like:

🏠 Home Assistant connected
   Discovered 47 entities:
   - 12 lights
   - 3 climate devices
   - 2 locks
   - 8 switches
   - 6 sensors
   - 4 media players
   - 12 automations
No Home Assistant yet? The easiest way to start is Home Assistant OS on a Raspberry Pi 4/5. It's a dedicated appliance — flash the image to an SD card, boot it, and you have a smart home hub in 10 minutes. If you just want to experiment, Home Assistant also runs in Docker on your laptop.

Natural Language Device Control

With the connection established, you can now control your entire home through chat. No more app-switching. No more remembering which app controls which device.

It's too warm in here
Current living room temperature: 26°C. Outside it's 22°C and dropping. I can: 1. Turn on the AC to 23°C 2. Open the windows (smart blinds up) to let the evening breeze in 3. Both — AC now, switch to windows in 30 min when it's cooler outside What sounds good?
Option 3, smart
Done! AC set to 23°C now. I'll open the blinds and turn off the AC at 9:30 PM when it should be 20°C outside. I'll let you know when I switch over.

Notice what happened: you didn't say "set the thermostat to 23." You described how you felt ("too warm") and the agent used context — inside temperature, outside temperature, time of day — to offer intelligent options. That's the difference between a remote control and an agent.

How Entity Discovery Works

When OpenClaw connects to Home Assistant, it pulls a list of all entities: light.living_room, climate.bedroom_ac, lock.front_door. The LLM uses these entity names to understand what's available.

To make this work smoothly, give your entities human-friendly names in Home Assistant. The LLM handles "Living Room Lamp" much better than "light.hue_color_lamp_1_a4f2."

Spend 15 minutes renaming your Home Assistant entities to plain English names. It's a one-time investment that dramatically improves how well the agent understands your requests. "Bedroom AC" is better than "climate.midea_192_168_1_45".

Scene Creation Through Conversation

Scenes are the real superpower. Instead of controlling one device at a time, you describe a vibe and the agent orchestrates multiple devices to create it.

We're having friends over for board games tonight. Can you set the mood?
Board game night! Here's what I'm thinking: 🔆 Living room lights to 70% (bright enough to read cards, not harsh) 🔆 Kitchen lights to 50% (snack zone accessible) 🌡️ Thermostat to 22°C (comfy with extra people generating heat) 🔇 TV off (no distractions) 🎵 Spotify: "Chill Instrumental" playlist at low volume on the living room speaker Should I set this up? I'll save it as a "Board Game Night" scene so you can request it with one message next time.
Perfect, go for it. But make the music a bit louder
All set! Lights, thermostat, and TV adjusted. Music playing at 35% volume on the living room speaker. Saved as "Board Game Night" scene. Next time just say "board game night" and I'll set everything up instantly. Have fun! 🎲

The agent just created a multi-device scene through conversation, saved it for reuse, and adjusted on the fly when you gave feedback. Try doing that with a Home Assistant automation YAML file.

Building Scenes Incrementally

You don't need to define every scene upfront. Over time, your agent learns from your requests:

  • Week 1: "Dim the lights and turn on the TV" — agent does it manually
  • Week 2: "Movie night" — agent remembers last time, suggests saving as a scene
  • Week 3: "Movie night" — one message, everything happens instantly
  • Week 4: "Movie night but Sam's not here" — agent adjusts (skip the extra blanket reminder, different snack suggestion)

Morning and Evening Routines

Routines are scheduled scenes that run automatically. Your agent can orchestrate the entire transition from sleep to awake (and back) without a single tap.

The Perfect Morning Routine

Add this to your OpenClaw config as a scheduled workflow:

schedules:
  morning_routine:
    cron: "0 6:45 * * 1-5"  # Weekdays at 6:45 AM
    action: |
      Start my morning routine:
      1. Gradually brighten bedroom lights over 10 minutes
      2. Set thermostat to 22°C
      3. Start the coffee machine
      4. After 10 min: read me today's weather and calendar
      5. Turn on bathroom lights and fan

At 6:45 AM on weekdays, your bedroom lights start dimming up like a sunrise. By 6:55, coffee is brewing, the house is warm, and your agent sends you a Telegram message: "Good morning! 14°C and sunny. You have a dentist appointment at 2 PM and Sam's birthday dinner at 7. Coffee's ready."

On weekends? Nothing happens. The agent respects your standing order: no interruptions before 10 AM on Saturday and Sunday.

Evening Wind-Down

schedules:
  evening_routine:
    cron: "0 22 * * *"  # Every day at 10 PM
    action: |
      Start evening wind-down:
      1. Dim all lights to 20%
      2. Set thermostat to night mode (19°C)
      3. Lock the front door (confirm if already locked)
      4. Check all windows (report if any are open)
      5. Remind me if Pixel hasn't had evening meds
      6. Set phone to Do Not Disturb (if supported)

The evening routine is especially valuable because it catches things you'd forget — did you lock the door? Is a window open when rain is forecast? Did Pixel get her 8 PM medication?

Presence-Based Automation

One of the most impactful automations is presence detection. Home Assistant can track whether you're home based on your phone's connection to WiFi. Your agent uses this to:

  • When everyone leaves: Turn off lights, pause AC/heating, set robot vacuum to clean, arm security
  • When someone arrives: Turn on porch light, adjust climate, disarm security, send welcome notification
  • When you're asleep: Reduce thermostat, lock doors, night lighting only
integrations:
  home_assistant:
    presence:
      track_devices:
        - "device_tracker.alex_phone"
        - "device_tracker.sam_phone"
      away_actions: "Turn off all lights, set AC to eco mode, start vacuum"
      home_actions: "Welcome lighting, restore AC to comfort mode"

Security Routines

Your agent can serve as an intelligent security layer on top of your smart locks, cameras, and sensors.

Motion Detected
→
Agent Checks Context
→
Expected? Ignore
→
Unexpected? Alert

The key difference from a dumb motion sensor is context. A regular camera sends you 47 alerts a day when the cat walks by. Your agent knows Pixel's schedule, knows when the mail carrier usually comes, and only alerts you for genuinely unusual activity.

# Security context in system prompt
Security awareness:
- Front door should be locked after 10 PM and when no one is home
- Motion on the porch between 10 AM-12 PM weekdays is likely the mail carrier — don't alert
- Pixel triggers the hallway motion sensor constantly — ignore indoor motion from that sensor
- If a window opens while we're away, alert immediately
- If the smoke detector triggers, alert ALL household members instantly
Your AI agent is NOT a replacement for a real security system. It's a smart filter on top of your existing devices. For actual security (break-ins, fires), rely on dedicated alarm systems with professional monitoring. Your agent is great for convenience (lock checks, activity filtering) but not for 911-level emergencies.

Maintenance Reminders

Your home has invisible maintenance tasks that are easy to forget — until something breaks. Your agent can track them all.

# Home maintenance schedule in system prompt
Home maintenance tracking:
- HVAC filter: replace every 3 months (last replaced: Feb 15)
- Smoke detector batteries: check every 6 months (last: Jan 1)
- Water filter: replace every 4 months (last: March 10)
- Dryer vent: clean yearly (last: December)
- Gutter cleaning: twice a year, spring and fall
- Fridge coils: clean every 6 months
- Robot vacuum brushes: replace every 2 months

Your agent calculates the next due date for each task and reminds you at the right time. No more "when did I last change the filter?" guessing games.

When your agent reminds you about a maintenance task and you complete it, just text "done" and the agent updates the date. The next reminder auto-adjusts. It's like having a building superintendent who never takes a vacation.

Guest Mode

Having friends or family stay over? Your agent can temporarily adjust the entire house.

Guest mode activated for this weekend! Here's what I've adjusted: 🌡️ Thermostat up to 23°C (your parents prefer it warmer, noted from last visit) 🔆 Hallway night lights on from 10 PM - 7 AM (navigation in unfamiliar house) 🔒 Front door: auto-lock disabled during daytime (so they can come and go) 🔇 Morning routine: delayed to 8 AM instead of 6:45 AM 📱 Guest WiFi network enabled (password: WelcomeHome2026) I'll turn everything back to normal Sunday evening after they leave. Extended guest mode through Monday evening. I'll restore normal settings Tuesday morning. Enjoy the visit!

Notice how the agent remembered a preference from the last visit (parents liking it warmer). Over time, your agent builds a profile for recurring guests without you having to explain every time.

Handling Failures Gracefully

Smart home devices go offline. WiFi drops. Bulbs need resetting. Batteries die. A good agent doesn't just fail silently — it tells you what happened and suggests a fix.

Turn off all the lights
Done! All lights off except: ⚠️ Kitchen ceiling light (Hue bulb) — not responding. It may have lost WiFi connection. Try: toggle the physical switch off and on. If it still doesn't respond, it might need re-pairing in the Hue app. Want me to remind you tomorrow if it's still offline?

This is miles better than the standard smart home experience where you tap "off" in an app, nothing happens, and you have no idea why.

Add a standing order to your system prompt: "If a device doesn't respond, always tell me which device failed, why it might have failed, and suggest a fix. Never silently skip a failed device." This turns device failures from invisible headaches into actionable notifications.

Smart Home Privacy: What You Need to Know

When you connect an AI agent to your smart home, you're creating a detailed record of your daily patterns — when you wake up, when you leave, when you sleep, who visits. This data is sensitive.

  • Keep Home Assistant local. Don't expose it to the internet. Access it via VPN or local network only
  • Voice assistants record you. If you use Alexa or Google Home alongside your agent, know that those devices send audio to the cloud. Your OpenClaw agent doesn't — it only processes text messages
  • Camera footage is personal data. If your agent references camera feeds, make sure recordings stay on local storage, not cloud services
  • Device logs reveal patterns. Your light/lock history shows when you're home or away. Don't share Home Assistant access carelessly
  • Use a local LLM for home control if possible. Sending "unlock the front door" through a cloud API is less ideal than processing it locally

Practical Tips for a Reliable Smart Home Agent

  • Name entities clearly — "Living Room Lamp" not "light.0xa4f2b1"
  • Group devices by room in Home Assistant — makes "turn off the bedroom" work naturally
  • Set reasonable defaults — "dim" means 30%, "bright" means 100%, define these in your prompt
  • Always confirm destructive actions — locking doors, disabling alarms, turning off heating in winter
  • Have physical overrides — light switches, manual locks. Never depend 100% on automation
  • Test scenes before relying on them — "movie night" should work perfectly before you have guests over
  • Monitor your agent's actions — OpenClaw logs every Home Assistant command. Review weekly at first
