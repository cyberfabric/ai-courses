Learning, Media & Travel

Reading lists, travel itineraries, and content recommendations — your cultural concierge

Your Personal Librarian, Critic, and Travel Agent

In the last chapter you put your agent in charge of your health. Now let's tackle the fun stuff: the books you want to read, the shows you want to watch, the skills you want to learn, and the trips you want to take.

These are the parts of life that enrich you — but they often fall through the cracks. You hear about a great book and forget the title by the time you're at a bookstore. A friend recommends a show and it vanishes from memory. You dream about a trip to Japan but never move past the dreaming phase because planning feels overwhelming.

Your agent becomes three things at once: a librarian who remembers every book, article, and podcast you've ever mentioned; a critic who learns your taste and recommends what you'll actually enjoy; and a travel agent who turns "wouldn't it be cool to visit..." into a fully planned itinerary.

The Reading and Learning Agent

Your agent can maintain a personal reading log — books, articles, courses, podcasts — and track your progress over time.

The agent didn't just log a book — it tracked your yearly goal, made personalized recommendations based on what you rated highly, avoided suggesting something you've already read, cross-referenced with a friend's recommendation, and reminded you about book club. That's an engaged librarian, not a database.

Article Summarization

You come across an interesting article but don't have 15 minutes to read it right now. Send the link to your agent.

📰 "The Science of Sleep Stages" — 12 min read, summarized: Key points: • Deep sleep (stages 3-4) is when your body physically repairs — prioritize it over total hours • REM sleep handles memory consolidation — cutting sleep short mainly cuts REM • The 90-minute cycle means 7.5 hours is better than 8 if it means waking mid-cycle • Blue light suppresses melatonin for up to 2 hours — the "no screens before bed" rule is real • Consistent wake time matters more than consistent bedtime Relevant to you: Your average sleep last week was 7.2 hours. Bumping to 7.5 would complete 5 full cycles. Also, your 10 PM quiet hours rule (from Chapter 7) aligns perfectly with the blue light recommendation. Save to your Obsidian knowledge base?
Add this to your system prompt: "When I send a URL, summarize it in under 100 words with key takeaways. If it's relevant to something we're tracking (health, finance, travel), connect it to my personal data. Offer to save important articles to my knowledge base."

Learning Path Creation

Want to learn something new? Your agent can create a structured plan, track daily progress, and adjust based on how it's going.

Travel Planning: From Dream to Itinerary

This is where your agent goes from helpful to genuinely impressive. Travel planning is a perfect agent task because it requires synthesizing many inputs: dates, budget, preferences, logistics, weather, visa requirements, and personal taste.

Let's plan that Japan trip. October, 10 days, budget around 5000 BGN per person

Pace: Packed days or relaxed with free time? 2. Focus: Culture & temples, food, nature, city life, or a mix? 3. Accommodation: Hotels, traditional ryokans, or mix? 4. Must-sees: Anything specific on your bucket list? 5. Sam's priorities? Any must-haves for them? Budget check: 5,000 BGN/person (~¥400,000) for 10 days is comfortable for mid-range travel in Japan. Perfect! The agent balanced both your interests (Akihabara + baseball for you, temples + ramen for Sam), suggested a ryokan experience it knows you'd enjoy based on the Shōgun excitement, kept it under budget, and offered to go deeper on any section.

The "Travel Binder" in Your Pocket

Once your itinerary is set, your agent becomes your travel companion during the trip. Ask it anything:

  • "We're at Shibuya and hungry. Find us a vegetarian-friendly ramen place within walking distance"
  • "How do I say 'no shellfish please' in Japanese?" — アレルギーがあります。えびなしでお願いします
  • "It's raining tomorrow. What indoor activities are near our hotel?"
  • "We're ahead of schedule. Anything cool within 30 minutes of Kyoto station?"
  • "Just spent 3,200 yen on lunch" — automatically logged to your travel expense tracker

Every question gets an answer that accounts for where you are, what you've already seen, your dietary restrictions, and your budget. A human travel agent couldn't do this unless they were standing next to you 24/7.

Packing Lists

Your agent generates a packing list based on the destination, weather forecast, activities planned, and trip length:

# Automatic packing list generation
When I confirm a trip, generate a packing list based on:
- Destination weather forecast (check 3 days before departure)
- Planned activities (hiking needs different gear than city touring)
- Trip duration
- Any special events (fancy dinner = dress clothes)
- My known preferences (I always forget phone charger)
Send the list 3 days before departure with checkboxes.
Add a "things I always forget" section to your system prompt: "When generating packing lists, always include: phone charger, adapter plug, toothbrush, allergy medication, and a photocopy of my passport. I forget at least one of these every trip." Your agent compensates for your known weaknesses.

Post-Trip: Closing the Loop

The trip doesn't end when you land. Your agent can help with the aftermath:

  • Expense summary: "Total Japan trip: 4,250 BGN per person. Under budget by 750 BGN! Biggest expense: hotels. Biggest surprise: we spent 400 BGN on convenience store snacks."
  • Trip journal: Agent compiles your messages, photos, and expense logs into a trip summary in Obsidian or Notion
  • Rating and memory: "Rate the trip?" — saved for future reference. Next time you plan a trip, the agent knows you rated Japan 10/10 and what you loved
  • Recommendations for others: "Mila asked about Japan tips" — agent generates a personalized recommendation list based on your experience

Integration with Obsidian and Notion

All this data — books, articles, media, learning progress, travel logs — can flow into a personal knowledge base. Think of it as your second brain.

integrations:

      book_note: "Templates/Book.md"

Your Taste Profile Is Surprisingly Revealing

Your reading list, watch history, and learning interests paint a detailed picture of who you are — your political leanings, health concerns, relationship status, financial literacy, and intellectual curiosity. This data is routinely sold by streaming platforms and bookstores.

  • Keep your media log local. Obsidian is better than cloud-based tools for this kind of personal data
  • Be mindful of article URLs. Sending an article about "dealing with anxiety" to a cloud LLM for summarization creates a record. Use a local model for sensitive topics
  • Travel plans reveal absence. Your itinerary shows exactly when your home is empty. Don't share detailed travel plans on social media, and keep your agent's travel data local
  • Learning paths reveal goals. "Learning Spanish" is innocent. "Learning about divorce law" is not something you want in a cloud provider's dataset

Exercise: Plan Your Next Trip

Use your agent to plan a real upcoming trip (or a dream trip you've been thinking about):

  1. Start the conversation: Tell your agent where, when, how long, and budget. Example: "Plan a long weekend in Plovdiv, May 2-4, budget 600 BGN for two"
  2. Answer the agent's questions about pace, interests, and priorities
  3. Review the itinerary: Does it include transportation, accommodation, activities, and food? Is the budget realistic?
  4. Request changes: "Swap the museum for a wine tasting" or "We need a vegetarian dinner option on Saturday"
  5. Ask for a packing list based on the weather forecast
  6. Add it to your calendar: Ask the agent to create calendar events for the trip

Bonus: Log a book you recently finished with a rating, and ask for a recommendation. Then log a show you've been watching. After both, ask "What should I read or watch next?" and see how the agent combines both media types into one recommendation.
