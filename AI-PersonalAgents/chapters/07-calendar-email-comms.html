Chapter 7

Calendar, Email & Communication

Email triage, calendar management, and social coordination on autopilot

The Communication Tax

In the last chapter your agent learned to track your money. Now let's tackle something even more valuable: your time.

The average person spends 2-3 hours per day on personal communication overhead. Not meaningful conversations — just the logistics. Scheduling a dentist appointment. Replying to that "when are you free?" text. Scanning 40 emails to find the 3 that matter. Coordinating dinner plans across a group chat where nobody can agree on a date.

This is the communication tax. You pay it every day, and you barely notice because it's scattered across tiny 2-minute tasks. But add them up and it's a part-time job you never applied for.

Your agent is about to become your executive assistant for personal communications.

Email Triage: From 40 Emails to 4 Actions

You wake up, open your inbox, and there are 40 new emails. Most people start scrolling from the top, reading each one. That's like going through your mail by reading every flyer, catalog, and coupon before finding the electric bill.

Your agent does it differently. It reads everything, categorizes it, and presents you with a prioritized summary.

Setting Up Email Access

OpenClaw connects to your email through IMAP (for reading) and SMTP (for sending drafts). Here's the config:

integrations:
  email:
    enabled: true
    provider: gmail         # gmail, outlook, or imap
    email: "alex@gmail.com"
    # For Gmail: use an App Password (not your regular password)
    app_password: "xxxx xxxx xxxx xxxx"
    check_interval: 15      # Check every 15 minutes
    imap_server: "imap.gmail.com"
    smtp_server: "smtp.gmail.com"
Use an App Password, not your real password. Gmail and Outlook support App Passwords — special tokens that only grant email access, not full account access. Generate one at myaccount.google.com → Security → App Passwords. This way, your agent can read email without having the keys to your entire Google account.

Draft Responses: Your Voice, Their Effort

The agent drafts replies matching your tone, not some corporate template. This is where the persona work from Chapter 4 pays off.

Generic AI Reply
Dear Mom, Thank you for your kind invitation. I would be delighted to attend Easter lunch. I anticipate arriving at approximately 12:00 PM. Warm regards, Alex
Nobody texts their mom like this. The agent hasn't learned your voice.
Your Voice
Yes! We'll be there around noon. We'll bring dessert! 😊
Casual, warm, matches how you actually write to your mom. This is what good persona prompting produces.

To get this right, add tone instructions per relationship in your system prompt:

# Communication tone rules
Email/message tone by relationship:
- Family (Mom, Dad, sister): warm, casual, use emojis occasionally
- Close friends (Mila, Stefan, Dani): very casual, jokes OK, short messages
- Acquaintances: friendly but neutral, no emojis
- Service providers (dentist, plumber): brief, professional, to the point
- Unknown senders: do not draft a reply, just summarize

CRITICAL: Never send any reply without my approval first.
Always show the draft and wait for "send" or edits.
The "never send without approval" rule is non-negotiable. An agent that sends a poorly-worded reply to your mom on your behalf will cause more problems than it solves. Always review before sending — you can make it a one-word approval ("send" or "ok"), but the human must always be in the loop.

Smart Calendar Features

Your agent doesn't just add events — it thinks about the logistics around them:

  • Travel time: Adds buffer before events based on location distance
  • Preparation reminders: "Dentist tomorrow — remember to floss extra well tonight"
  • Conflict detection: "You just accepted dinner at 7, but you have book club at 7 on the first Thursday"
  • Recurring awareness: Knows your gym schedule, volunteer shifts, and book club without you mentioning them each time
Add a calendar context section to your system prompt: "My recurring events: gym Mon/Wed/Fri 7:30-8:30 AM, volunteer shift every other Saturday 10 AM-1 PM, book club first Thursday 7 PM. Never schedule over these unless I explicitly ask." The agent will automatically protect these time slots.

Social Coordination

Coordinating plans with multiple people is one of life's most annoying tasks. The back-and-forth of "When are you free?" across three different chats is enough to make you cancel the plan entirely.

The "Find a Time" Hack

Instead of managing the coordination yourself, delegate it to your agent:

I want to have dinner with Mila and Stefan this weekend. Find a time that works
Checking your calendar... You're free: • Saturday evening (after 2 PM, volunteer shift ends at 1) • Sunday all day I don't have Mila and Stefan's calendars, but I can draft a message for your group chat: "Hey! Want to grab dinner this weekend? I'm free Saturday evening or Sunday. What works for you two?" Want me to send this to your group chat? Once they reply, I'll find the overlap and suggest a restaurant.
Send it
Sent to "Weekend Crew" group chat! I'll watch for their replies and let you know once we have a time. Meanwhile, want me to shortlist a few restaurant options? Budget preference?

When Mila replies "Saturday works!" and Stefan says "Sunday better for me, but Saturday is fine too," your agent spots the overlap and suggests: "Saturday evening it is! All three of you are free. Here are 3 restaurant options under 30 BGN per person..."

You went from "ugh, coordinating dinner" to "dinner is booked" with three text messages.

Communication Boundaries

Being always connected doesn't mean being always available. Your agent can enforce boundaries that your willpower alone can't.

# Communication boundaries
Quiet hours: 10 PM to 8 AM (weekdays), 10 PM to 10 AM (weekends)

During quiet hours:
- Hold all non-urgent notifications
- Only alert me for: messages from family containing words like
  "emergency," "hospital," "urgent," or "help"
- Queue everything else for my morning digest

Focus blocks:
- When I say "focus mode," hold ALL notifications for 2 hours
- When the block ends, send a summary of what I missed

Vacation mode:
- When I say "vacation mode," auto-reply to all personal emails:
  "I'm on vacation until [date]. I'll reply when I'm back!"
- Only alert me for genuine emergencies from family
The quiet hours feature is a game-changer for sleep quality. Your phone still receives messages (so you have them in the morning), but your agent only wakes you up for genuine emergencies. The filter isn't based on who sent it — it's based on content analysis. A text from Mom saying "Great dinner tonight!" at 11 PM gets held. A text from Mom saying "Dad fell, we're at the hospital" gets through immediately.

The Context Bridge

One of the most powerful things your agent does is connect information across platforms. It sees your email, your calendar, your chat messages, and your notes. No human assistant has this kind of cross-platform vision.

Example: The Context Bridge in Action

You get an email from Stefan: "Hey, can we grab coffee tomorrow?" Your agent checks your calendar, sees a 2-hour gap between 3-5 PM, and drafts a reply: "Sure! How about 3:30? There's that new place near Oborishte."

Then it adds a tentative calendar event, checks the weather for tomorrow (outdoor seating?), and reminds you that you owe Stefan 25 BGN from last week's dinner (from Chapter 6). One email triggers a cascade of useful actions across four different systems.

Communication Privacy: Eyes on Your Inbox

Giving your agent email access means an AI is reading your personal correspondence. Take a moment to think about what's in your inbox: password resets, bank statements, medical results, personal conversations. This is deeply private data.

  • Use App Passwords, not your real account password. Revoke them instantly if compromised
  • Limit what the agent reads. Most email providers let you filter by label or folder. Only grant access to your Inbox, not Sent or Drafts
  • If using a cloud LLM, your email subjects and bodies are sent to the API. Consider using a local LLM for email triage, or only send summaries, not full content
  • Never store email credentials in plain text. Use environment variables: app_password: ${GMAIL_APP_PASSWORD}
  • Review sent messages. Keep the "never send without approval" rule. Periodically check your Sent folder to confirm only approved messages went out
  • Calendar access is less risky but still reveals your daily patterns, locations, and social network. Keep it local if possible

Exercise: Set Up Your Communication Agent

Get email triage and calendar management running:

  1. Connect your email using the IMAP/SMTP config above (Gmail users: create an App Password first)
  2. Connect your calendar (Google Calendar or CalDAV)
  3. Add the communication prompt section to your system prompt
  4. Test email triage: Send "check my email" and verify the categorization makes sense. Approve or edit at least one draft reply
  5. Test calendar: Ask "what's my week look like?" and verify it shows your real events
  6. Test scheduling: Ask the agent to schedule something next week. Check that it avoids your protected time slots
  7. Test the context bridge: Ask "do I have anything with Mila coming up?" — see if it checks both email and calendar
  8. Set up quiet hours: Configure the boundary rules and test by sending a non-urgent message during quiet hours (it should be held)

The real test: use this setup for a full day. At the end, count how many times you opened Gmail or your calendar app directly. The goal is zero.
