Chapter 4

Prompt Engineering for Personal Agents

System prompts, standing orders, and teaching your agent about your life

Why Prompting Agents Is Different

In the last chapter you set up a working agent and had your first conversation. It responded, checked the weather, set reminders. But it felt a bit... generic. Like talking to a helpful stranger at a hotel front desk instead of a friend who knows you.

That's about to change.

When you prompt a regular chatbot, the worst that happens with a vague instruction is a mediocre answer. You shrug, rephrase, try again. But when you prompt an agent — something that can actually act on your behalf — vague instructions cause real-world consequences.

Tell a chatbot "buy something nice for dinner" and you get recipe suggestions. Tell an agent the same thing and it might order $80 of sushi from the most expensive restaurant in town. The stakes are different because agents do things.

The Prompting Analogy

A system prompt is like a job description for a personal assistant. When you hire someone to help around the house, you don't just say "help me." You tell them your schedule, your preferences, your budget, what's off-limits, and how you like things done. A system prompt does exactly the same thing — except the assistant never forgets, never has a bad day, and works 24/7.

The System Prompt: Your Agent's DNA

Every message you send to your agent is a user prompt. But there's a hidden prompt that runs before every conversation — the system prompt. It defines your agent's personality, boundaries, and standing instructions.

In OpenClaw, the system prompt lives in your config.yaml:

agent:
  name: "Jarvis"
  system_prompt: |
    You are Jarvis, a personal AI assistant for Alex.
    You help with daily life tasks: groceries, scheduling,
    health tracking, and home management.
    You are friendly but concise. You never make purchases
    without explicit approval.

That pipe character (|) lets you write multi-line text. And you should — a good system prompt for a personal agent is typically 20-50 lines long.

Think of the system prompt as the "background music" of every conversation. Your agent reads it before every single message you send. It shapes everything — tone, decisions, boundaries, priorities. Get this right and your agent feels like it truly knows you. Get it wrong and you'll be constantly correcting it.

Crafting a Persona

Your agent's persona isn't just cosmetic. It affects how it communicates, how proactive it is, and how much hand-holding it provides. Think about three dimensions:

1. Tone

How formal or casual do you want the conversation to feel?

Casual Tone
You speak like a close friend. Use casual language, contractions, and light humor. Keep responses short — I'm usually texting from my phone. No need for "Dear" or "I hope this helps."
Great for everyday personal use. Feels natural in a messaging app.
Professional Tone
You communicate clearly and efficiently. Use proper grammar, numbered lists for multi-step answers, and bullet points for options. Be helpful but not chatty.
Better if you prefer structured, no-nonsense interactions.

2. Proactiveness

Should your agent just answer questions, or should it anticipate needs?

Proactive
When I mention an upcoming event, proactively suggest related tasks. If I say "birthday party Saturday," suggest a gift, remind me to RSVP, and ask about transportation.
Reactive
Only do exactly what I ask. Don't suggest additional tasks or follow-ups unless I specifically request ideas.

3. Detail Level

Does "plan dinner" mean a full recipe with shopping list, or just "how about tacos?"

There's no wrong answer — it depends on your personality. Some people want a chatty AI buddy. Others want a terse, efficient tool. The key is to be explicit about what you want.

Context Injection: Teaching Your Agent About YOU

Here's where things get personal. A system prompt without context about your life is like a GPS without your destination. Your agent needs to know the details that make its suggestions relevant.

What to Include

# Personal context — add to your system prompt
You are helping Alex, who lives in Sofia, Bulgaria.

Household:
- Lives with partner Sam and a cat named Pixel
- Sam is vegetarian, Alex eats everything
- Pixel needs medication at 8 AM and 8 PM daily

Diet & Health:
- Alex is lactose intolerant (can handle small amounts of cheese)
- No shellfish allergy but dislikes shrimp
- Trying to eat under 2000 calories on weekdays
- Gym on Mon/Wed/Fri mornings

Schedule:
- Works 9 AM to 6 PM, Mon-Fri
- Every other Saturday: volunteer shift at animal shelter (10 AM - 1 PM)
- Monthly book club: first Thursday of the month, 7 PM

Budget:
- Weekly grocery budget: 150 BGN
- Dining out budget: 80 BGN/week
- No single purchase over 100 BGN without asking first

Look at how much this changes the conversation:

Plan dinners for this week
Here's your dinner plan for this week: Monday: Grilled chicken with roasted veggies (Sam gets portobello mushroom steaks instead) Tuesday: Veggie stir-fry with tofu — works for both of you Wednesday: Pasta with marinara and a side salad (dairy-free sauce) Thursday: Book club night! Quick pre-meeting meal: Buddha bowls with quinoa Friday: Homemade pizza — use dairy-free cheese on your half, regular on Sam's Estimated grocery cost: ~95 BGN (under your 150 BGN budget). Want me to generate the shopping list?

Without context, you'd get generic meal suggestions. With context, the agent accounts for Sam's vegetarianism, your lactose intolerance, your calorie goals, Thursday's book club, and your weekly budget. That's the difference between a chatbot and a personal agent.

The "New Roommate" Approach

Imagine a new roommate just moved in. What would you tell them in the first week so they don't accidentally cause problems? That list is your agent's context injection:

  • "Don't use the front-loading washer after 10 PM — it shakes the floor and wakes the neighbor."
  • "The fridge door doesn't close properly unless you push it twice."
  • "Pixel bites strangers but is fine once she smells your hand first."
  • "We alternate who takes out the trash — this week is mine."

These tiny personal details are what make your agent actually useful. The more specific your context, the fewer corrections you'll need.

The "Standing Orders" Pattern

Standing orders are always-on instructions that your agent follows without being asked. Think of them as guardrails that prevent the agent from doing something you'd never approve.

# Standing orders
Rules you must ALWAYS follow:
1. Never spend money without explicit approval
2. Never share personal information with third parties
3. Always confirm before deleting anything
4. If a reminder involves medication, mark it as HIGH priority
5. When suggesting restaurants, exclude chains — I prefer local places
6. If I ask for "something quick" for dinner, it means under 30 minutes prep
7. Weekend mornings are sacred — no reminders before 10 AM on Sat/Sun
8. Always mention allergens when suggesting food (lactose, shellfish for guests)
9. When I say "cheap," I mean under 30 BGN for a meal for two
10. If in doubt about anything, ASK — don't guess
Rule #10 is the most important rule you'll ever write. An agent that guesses wrong can cause real problems. An agent that asks first is mildly annoying but safe. Always include an explicit "when uncertain, ask me" instruction.

Why Standing Orders Matter

Without standing orders, your agent makes assumptions based on its training data — which reflects the "average" person, not you. "Pick a restaurant" without standing orders might suggest a trendy chain 30 minutes away. With standing orders, it picks that cozy local Italian place around the corner that's under budget.

Think of standing orders as your agent's common sense — except you get to define what "common sense" means for your specific life.

Structured vs. Natural Language Instructions

You've seen two styles in this chapter already: structured (numbered lists, YAML-like) and natural language (sentences and paragraphs). When should you use which?

Structured (Use for Rules)
Budget rules: - Groceries: max 150 BGN/week - Dining: max 80 BGN/week - Single purchase limit: 100 BGN - Always show running total when adding items
Clear, unambiguous, easy for the LLM to parse. Best for hard limits and factual data.
Natural Language (Use for Personality)
I like it when you're a bit playful in your responses — throw in a joke when suggesting weekend plans, but stay serious when it comes to money or health stuff. Don't overdo the emojis, but one or two per message is fine.
Nuanced, hard to express as a bullet list. Best for tone, personality, and soft preferences.

The best system prompts use both: natural language for the persona section, structured format for rules and facts. Think of it as a resume — the personal statement is prose, but the skills section is bullet points.

Priority Hierarchy: When Instructions Conflict

What happens when your own rules contradict each other? "Save money" vs "book the best hotel." "Eat healthy" vs "I want comfort food." Your agent needs to know which instruction wins.

# Priority hierarchy (highest to lowest)
1. SAFETY: Never do anything that could harm health or security
2. BUDGET: Respect financial limits unless I explicitly override
3. PREFERENCES: Follow dietary, scheduling, and lifestyle preferences
4. CONVENIENCE: Optimize for ease and time savings
5. QUALITY: Within budget, prefer quality over cheapness
Book me a hotel in Plovdiv for this weekend. I want something nice.
I found 3 options in Plovdiv for Fri-Sun: 1. Hotel Evmolpia — boutique, Old Town, 140 BGN/night (280 total) ⭐ Great reviews, walking distance to everything. Exceeds your usual per-purchase limit of 100 BGN. 2. Guest House Plovdiv — charming, central, 75 BGN/night (150 total) ✓ Within budget, good reviews, 10-min walk to Old Town. 3. Hostel Old Town — basic but clean, 35 BGN/night (70 total) Budget-friendly but shared facilities. Based on your priorities, I'd recommend #2 — it's the best balance of quality and budget. But you said "something nice," so if you want to splurge, #1 is worth it. Want me to book one?
Go with #1, it's a special weekend
Noted! I'll look up booking details for Hotel Evmolpia, Fri-Sun. Since it's over your usual 100 BGN single-purchase limit, just confirming: you approve 280 BGN total for this booking? 👍

See what happened? The agent flagged the budget conflict, presented options ranked by the priority hierarchy, and still asked for confirmation before spending over the limit. That's a well-prompted agent.

Few-Shot Examples: Show, Don't Tell

Sometimes the best way to teach your agent how to respond is to show it. Few-shot examples in your system prompt demonstrate the exact format and style you expect.

# Example interactions (include in system prompt)

Example — grocery list request:
User: "I need stuff for tacos"
You: "Taco night! Here's your list:
🌮 Tortillas (corn, 8-pack)
🥩 Ground beef 500g (or black beans for Sam)
🧀 Cheddar cheese (dairy-free option for you: nutritional yeast)
🍅 Tomatoes x3, lettuce, red onion
🌶️ Jalapeños, lime x2, cilantro
🫘 Refried beans, 1 can
🫙 Salsa and sour cream (oat-based for you)
Est. cost: ~35 BGN. Add to this week's grocery list?"

Example — schedule check:
User: "Am I free Saturday afternoon?"
You: "Saturday afternoon looks clear! You have your shelter
volunteer shift 10 AM - 1 PM, but after that you're free.
Want me to block off time for something?"
Two to three examples are enough. More than five and your system prompt gets bloated. Pick examples for your most common interaction types: lists, scheduling, and meal planning cover about 80% of daily personal agent use.

Prompt Templates for Common Tasks

Here are ready-to-use prompt snippets you can add to your system prompt for the most common personal agent tasks:

Meal Planning

When planning meals:
- Always account for all household members' dietary needs
- Suggest a mix of quick (under 30 min) and involved recipes
- Include estimated cost per meal
- Generate a consolidated shopping list at the end
- Flag if total exceeds the weekly grocery budget
- Default to 5 dinners (Mon-Fri) unless told otherwise

Email Triage

When triaging email:
- Categorize as: URGENT / NEEDS REPLY / FYI / SPAM
- Summarize each email in one sentence
- For NEEDS REPLY, draft a response matching my casual tone
- Never auto-send — always show me the draft first
- Group by category, urgent first

Shopping Lists

When managing shopping lists:
- Keep a running list I can add to incrementally
- Group items by store section (produce, dairy, pantry, etc.)
- Show estimated total cost
- Remember recurring items I buy weekly (milk, bread, eggs, bananas)
- When I say "the usual," add all recurring items

Travel Research

When helping with travel:
- Always consider my budget constraints first
- Prefer direct flights under 4 hours, trains for shorter distances
- I like window seats, aisle for flights over 4 hours
- Hotel preferences: central location > amenities > price
- Build itineraries with mix of must-see and off-the-beaten-path
- Include walking time between activities
- Always note if a destination requires a visa

Debugging Bad Agent Behavior

Your agent will do dumb things sometimes. Before blaming the LLM, check your prompt. Nine times out of ten, bad behavior comes from unclear instructions.

Common Problems and Prompt Fixes

Problem Likely Cause Prompt Fix
Agent is too verbose No length guidance Add: "Keep responses under 100 words unless I ask for detail"
Ignores dietary restrictions Restrictions buried in a long prompt Move dietary info to the top, under a clear heading
Suggests expensive options No budget context Add explicit budget limits with the "cheap/moderate/splurge" definitions
Too many follow-up questions Overly cautious "ask before acting" rule Narrow the rule: "Ask before spending money or deleting data. For everything else, use your best judgment."
Forgets preferences Relying on memory instead of system prompt Put essential preferences in the system prompt, not just in past conversations
Responds in wrong language No language instruction Add: "Always respond in English unless I write in another language"
Vague System Prompt
You are a helpful personal assistant. Help me with daily tasks and be nice.
This tells the agent nothing about YOU. It will give generic answers to everyone because it has no personal context to work with.
Specific System Prompt
You are Jarvis, personal assistant for Alex in Sofia. Alex lives with Sam (vegetarian) and cat Pixel. Alex is lactose intolerant, gyms Mon/Wed/Fri, has a 150 BGN/week grocery budget. Be casual and concise. Never spend money without asking. Weekend mornings: no reminders before 10 AM.
In just 3 lines, the agent knows: who you are, your household, health constraints, schedule, budget, tone preference, and key boundaries. Every response will be tailored to this context.

The Iterative Approach: Start Simple, Refine Often

Don't try to write the perfect system prompt on day one. It's an iterative process, like tuning a recipe.

Start Simple
→
Use for 3 Days
→
Note Annoyances
→
Fix the Prompt
→
Repeat

Here's a realistic evolution:

  1. Week 1: Basic persona + name + household members. "Help me, be casual."
  2. Week 2: Add dietary restrictions and budget after the agent suggests a $60 dinner. Add "ask before spending."
  3. Week 3: Add schedule context after it suggests a gym session during book club. Add standing orders for weekend mornings.
  4. Week 4: Add few-shot examples after the grocery list format annoyed you three times. Fine-tune tone.

After a month, your system prompt is battle-tested and deeply personal. No one else's prompt would work for you — and yours wouldn't work for them. That's the sign of a well-tuned agent.

Your System Prompt Is Sensitive Data

Your system prompt contains your schedule, dietary needs, budget, household members, and daily patterns. Treat it like a diary entry:

  • Don't share it publicly — it reveals a lot about your life
  • If using a cloud LLM, your system prompt is sent with every API call. Be aware of your provider's data retention policy
  • For maximum privacy, use a local LLM (Ollama) so your prompt never leaves your machine
  • Back it up — losing a well-tuned system prompt after weeks of refinement is painful. Keep a copy outside your agent's config

A Complete System Prompt Example

Let's put it all together. Here's a full, production-ready system prompt you can adapt for your own life:

# System Prompt for Jarvis — Alex's Personal Agent

## Who You Are
You are Jarvis, a personal AI assistant for Alex. You're casual, concise,
and slightly witty — like a helpful friend, not a corporate chatbot.
Use short paragraphs. One or two emojis per message is fine, no more.

## About Alex
- Lives in Sofia, Bulgaria with partner Sam and cat Pixel
- Works 9-6 Mon-Fri (don't schedule personal tasks during work hours)
- Gym: Mon/Wed/Fri mornings at 7:30 AM
- Volunteer shift: every other Saturday, 10 AM - 1 PM at animal shelter
- Book club: first Thursday of each month, 7 PM

## Household Rules
- Sam is vegetarian — always include veggie options for shared meals
- Pixel needs meds at 8 AM and 8 PM — HIGH priority reminders
- No loud activities after 10 PM (apartment rules)

## Diet & Health
- Alex: lactose intolerant (small cheese amounts OK), dislikes shrimp
- Weekday calorie target: under 2000 kcal
- "Something quick" = under 30 min prep time

## Budget
- Groceries: 150 BGN/week max
- Dining out: 80 BGN/week max
- No single purchase over 100 BGN without asking me first
- "Cheap" = under 30 BGN for two people

## Standing Orders
1. Never spend money without explicit approval
2. Never share personal info with third parties
3. Always confirm before deleting anything
4. Medication reminders = HIGH priority, always
5. Prefer local restaurants over chains
6. No reminders before 10 AM on weekends
7. When unsure, ASK — don't guess

## Response Style
- Keep messages under 100 words unless I ask for detail
- Use bullet points for lists, numbered steps for instructions
- Always show estimated cost for purchases and meal plans
- When suggesting multiple options, recommend your top pick
Copy this template, replace the personal details with your own, and paste it into your config.yaml under agent.system_prompt. Then spend the next week observing and refining. The best system prompt is the one you've iterated on for a month.

Exercise: Write Your Personal System Prompt

Time to create the system prompt that turns your agent into your agent. Follow these steps:

  1. Start with the template above — copy it and open it in your editor
  2. Replace all personal details — name, location, household, diet, schedule, budget
  3. Write 5 standing orders that reflect your personal boundaries
  4. Add 2 few-shot examples for your most common requests (meals? scheduling? lists?)
  5. Paste it into config.yaml and restart OpenClaw
  6. Test with these messages:
    • "Plan dinners for this week" — does it account for your dietary needs?
    • "Am I free Saturday?" — does it know your recurring schedule?
    • "Buy me a [something expensive]" — does it ask for approval?
    • "Remind me about [something] on Sunday at 8 AM" — does it respect weekend rules?
  7. Note what went wrong and fix it. Repeat daily for a week.

By the end of the week, your agent should feel like it truly knows you. If someone else tried to use it, the responses would make no sense for their life — and that's exactly the point.
