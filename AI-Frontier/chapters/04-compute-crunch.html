Chapter 4

The Compute Crunch

Stargate’s 10 GW, Vera Rubin, custom silicon — and why the binding constraint of 2026 is electricity, not algorithms

As of April 2026, the bottleneck on AI progress has stopped being algorithms and started being infrastructure. Stargate — OpenAI, Oracle, and SoftBank’s joint venture — has 10 half-million-square-foot buildings under construction in Abilene, Texas, racing toward 1 GW of operational capacity by mid-2026 and 10 GW total across the program [Data Center Frontier]. xAI’s Colossus 2 in Memphis crossed 2 GW of capacity and 555,000 GPUs in January 2026 — with Phase 1 going from groundbreaking to online in 122 days, smashing every prior industry timeline by ~80% [Introl, Jan 2026]. NVIDIA’s Vera Rubin platform is in full production, with frontier-lab deployments scheduled for H2 2026 and a stated 5× performance over Blackwell [NVIDIA, Mar 2026]. OpenAI’s custom Titan inference chip — co-designed with Broadcom, built on TSMC N3, fed by Samsung HBM4 — is targeted for first deployment in December 2026 with a 90% inference-cost reduction goal [FinancialContent, Jan 2026]. The IEA projects 1,100 TWh of global data-center electricity consumption in 2026 — roughly Japan’s entire national draw, an 18% upward revision from December 2025 [IEA Energy and AI report]. The numbers are so large they stop sounding like data centers and start sounding like national-scale industrial buildouts. They are.

The last three chapters were about models — what they can do, how they reason, who builds them open vs closed. This chapter is about the wall those models keep slamming into. By April 2026, every frontier lab has the same basic problem: there isn’t enough electricity to run the model they want to train next.

This is a story about gigawatts, transformers, real-estate-scale construction, custom silicon, and a quiet crisis on the grid that doesn’t make headlines until somebody pulls the plug. By the end of the chapter you’ll have a clear-eyed picture of why “compute” in 2026 means substations and high-voltage interconnects more than it means GPUs.

From algorithm bottleneck to infrastructure bottleneck

For the first decade of the deep-learning era, the binding constraint was clever architecture: better attention, better optimizers, better training tricks. From 2018 to about 2023, the second binding constraint was data: enough high-quality tokens to feed ever-larger models.

By 2026, both of those are still important — but neither is the thing that determines whether GPT-6 or Claude Opus 5 happens on schedule. The thing that determines that is whether you can plug it in.

Every one of those numbers is a warning light. Per the IEA’s Energy and AI report, global data-center electricity demand is on track to hit 1,100 TWh in 2026 — an 18% upward revision from estimates published just four months earlier. PJM, the grid operator covering 13 US states, saw its capacity-clearing price for the 2026–27 delivery year settle at $329.17/MW, up from $28.92/MW in 2024–25 [arXiv 2509.07218]. Data-center growth is the named cause.

The infrastructure bottleneck looks different from the algorithm bottleneck. Algorithm bottlenecks get solved by smart people in conference papers. Infrastructure bottlenecks get solved by power-purchase agreements, environmental impact assessments, transformer suppliers in Wisconsin, and county commissioners in Texas. The talent pool that fixes the second problem is not the same as the one that fixed the first.

The Stargate program

Stargate is the single most ambitious AI-infrastructure project in history. It’s a joint venture between OpenAI, Oracle, and SoftBank with a stated commitment of $500B over four years and a target of 10 GW of US AI compute capacity [Stargate LLC, Wikipedia]. That price tag is bigger than the inflation-adjusted Apollo program and the Manhattan Project combined.

Abilene: the flagship

The flagship site sits in Abilene, Texas. As of late 2025 the first two of ten planned half-million-square-foot buildings were operational and powering early model training. The remaining six are slated to finish by mid-2026, with construction proceeding 24/7 and 5,000+ workers on site [Big Country Homepage, 2026]. Larry Ellison personally cited “ten half-million-square-foot buildings” in a public update.

Today the campus draws about 300 MW, already surpassing every other AI-dedicated data center on the planet. By mid-2026 the buildout is projected to cross the 1 gigawatt threshold — the first time a single AI campus has done so [Tom’s Hardware]. The full Stargate buildout will deploy 450,000 NVIDIA GB200 GPUs at this site alone [DCD, 2025]. Add the next-gen Vera Rubin systems coming H2 2026 and the GPU count grows considerably.

Five new sites

OpenAI announced five additional Stargate sites across the US: in New Mexico, Ohio, and three other locations chosen for grid access and tax incentives [OpenAI, 2025]. With Oracle’s 4.5 GW partnership layered on top of the original 5 GW commitment, the program credibly clears its 10 GW target by 2027 if the grid cooperates [OpenAI Stargate × Oracle].

What used to be measured in megawatts is now measured in gigawatts. A 1 GW data center draws roughly the electrical power of a million American homes. Two of the entries above are already past that threshold. By the end of 2026 most of them will be.

xAI’s Colossus 2 deserves a special call-out. The Introl analysis notes that the 122-day ground-to-online record for Colossus Phase 1 cut the standard industry deployment time by nearly 80%. Elon Musk has shown that, given political will and a willingness to ignore some traditional permitting timelines, the wall-clock from groundbreaking to running training jobs can be measured in months instead of years. Other operators are taking notes.

NVIDIA Vera Rubin: the H2 2026 buildout

If Stargate is the where, Vera Rubin is the what. Announced in detail at CES 2026 and now in full production, the NVIDIA Vera Rubin platform is the silicon substrate that the next generation of frontier clusters will run on [NVIDIA Newsroom, CES 2026].

It’s not one chip; it’s seven, designed to act as a single supercomputer:

  • Vera CPU — the host processor
  • Rubin GPU — the compute engine
  • NVLink 6 Switch — intra-rack interconnect
  • ConnectX-9 SuperNIC — rack-to-rack networking
  • BlueField-4 DPU — data-path offload
  • Spectrum-6 Ethernet switch — cluster-scale switching
  • Groq 3 LPU (newly integrated) — specialized inference accelerator

The performance claim

NVIDIA claims Rubin delivers 5× the AI performance of Blackwell at the platform level [Inside HPC, Jan 2026]. On large mixture-of-experts models, the company says Rubin can match Blackwell’s training time using one-quarter the GPUs at roughly one-seventh the cost per processed token [NVIDIA, Mar 2026].

If even half of those numbers hold up under independent benchmarking, frontier training compute is about to get a step-function reset. A run that took 90 days on Blackwell could take ~25–30 days on Rubin at lower power.

Who’s deploying

NVIDIA names Anthropic, Meta, Mistral AI, and OpenAI as frontier-lab customers planning Vera Rubin deployments in H2 2026. The first cloud providers to offer Vera Rubin instances are AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft, and Oracle, plus the NVIDIA Cloud Partner tier (CoreWeave, Lambda, Nebius, Nscale) [Yahoo Finance / NVIDIA CES 2026].

Translation: starting roughly Q3 2026, every major frontier lab will be either training on or transitioning to Rubin silicon. The ones that aren’t are running custom in-house chips.

The custom-silicon shift

For most of the deep-learning era, “buying compute” meant “buying NVIDIA.” That assumption broke decisively in 2025–26.

OpenAI Titan

Announced October 13, 2025 in a $10B partnership with Broadcom, OpenAI’s Titan inference chip is targeted for first deployment in December 2026 [FinancialContent, Jan 2026]. The relevant numbers:

  • Process node: TSMC N3 (3nm) for Titan 1; A16 (1.6nm) for Titan 2 in 2027 [TrendForce, Jan 2026]
  • Memory: Samsung HBM4, exclusive supply deal [Technetbook, Mar 2026]
  • Targeted deployment: 10 GW of Titan-based capacity by 2029 (yes, gigawatts; that’s the unit now) [Maginative]
  • Stated goal: 90% reduction in inference cost vs general-purpose GPUs — per Richard Ho, OpenAI’s VP of Hardware

Titan is an inference chip, not a training chip. That distinction matters: if 90% of OpenAI’s eventual cost is serving tokens to users (not training), shaving 90% off inference reshapes the entire economic model. The Titan strategy is essentially “let NVIDIA train, but don’t let NVIDIA inference.”

The custom-silicon shift is not a fashion. It’s a structural answer to what BAML analysts have called “the Nvidia tax” — the gross margins NVIDIA captures from being the only viable game in town. When that tax is on the order of 60–70% gross margins on every GPU, every hyperscaler eventually concludes the math says build your own.

The flip side: building custom silicon is brutally hard. You need world-class chip architects, multi-billion-dollar tape-outs, multi-year design cycles, and software stacks (compilers, kernel libraries) that took NVIDIA two decades to perfect. The labs and clouds doing it have time, money, and existential motivation. Smaller players are still buying NVIDIA.

The grid problem

You can buy the GPUs. You can pour the concrete. You cannot, in 2026, reliably get the megawatts.

The interconnection wait

Connecting a new gigawatt-scale load to the US power grid currently has a 5–7 year typical interconnection queue in most utility territories. That’s how long it takes to study impacts, build transmission, and approve the connection. AI buildouts cannot wait that long, so two workarounds have emerged:

  • “Behind-the-meter” on-site generation. Operators are siting natural-gas turbines on the data-center premises — effectively building their own mini power plants. Stargate Abilene has used gas turbines as bridge power; Colossus 1 famously deployed a fleet of mobile gas generators on day one.
  • “Energy island” campuses. Locate the data center where the power already is — near retired or idle generation, near nuclear plants, near hydroelectric capacity — rather than trying to bring the power to the demand. Stargate’s Texas siting follows this logic; so does Microsoft’s reactivation of the Three Mile Island Unit 1 nuclear plant.

The PJM signal

If you want a single number that proves the grid is stressed, look at PJM Interconnection’s capacity-market clearing prices. The 2026–27 delivery year cleared at $329.17/MW-day — up ~10× from the $28.92 price for 2024–25 [arXiv, 2025]. PJM has explicitly cited rapid data-center growth as a major contributor.

That price increase ripples through every electricity bill in 13 US states. AI’s compute crunch is showing up on residential utility statements, which is also showing up in town halls and local elections.

Moratoriums and pushback

Per [Tech-Insider analysis], at least three US states are likely to impose moratoriums on new data-center construction by 2027, with Virginia (the densest concentration of US data-center load) being the most-cited. The Netherlands has already implemented a 9-month moratorium on new hyperscale permits. Local utilities in Ohio, Georgia, and Arizona are starting to require multi-year power-supply commitments before they’ll even study a new load.

The political math is straightforward: voters notice when their electric bill goes up 30% and a data center goes in next door. Whether that math turns into binding constraints is the open question of mid-2026.

Transformers and switchgear: the hidden bottleneck

Even with the megawatts available, a more boring crisis is brewing: physical transformer and switchgear shortages. Lead times for the high-voltage transformers that step grid power down to data-center voltage have stretched from ~52 weeks pre-2023 to 2–3 years in 2026. The same is true of the breakers, panels, and battery backup systems inside the building.

You can have all the GPUs and all the gigawatts and still not be able to plug them together because there isn’t a transformer to put between them. This is the kind of problem that doesn’t make TechCrunch headlines but determines who hits 2026 deadlines.

The 100 kW rack

Inside the data center, the racks themselves have been redesigned around AI density. A traditional enterprise rack draws 10–14 kW. A 2026 frontier-AI rack draws 80–120 kW — roughly an order of magnitude more [EnkiAI, 2026].

The reasons:

  • Individual GPUs now draw up to 1.2 kW each (vs ~300W a few years ago)
  • 72-GPU dense racks (NVIDIA NVL72-class) pack 72 chips into a single rack
  • NVLink switches and network fabric eat additional kilowatts

Air cooling stops working above roughly 30–40 kW/rack. Above that, the only viable approach is liquid cooling — either direct-to-chip cold plates or full immersion. The Dell’Oro Group reported the data-center liquid-cooling market nearly doubled in 2025 to roughly $3B and is projected to hit $7B by 2029 [Schneider Electric].

Direct-to-chip cooling has won as the default for new builds in the 50–150 kW range. Immersion is reserved for the most extreme deployments where every milliwatt of pumping efficiency matters.

Practical consequence: nearly every existing data center on Earth that was built before 2023 is obsolete for frontier AI work. The cooling, electrical distribution, and floor loading aren’t up to spec. You can’t retrofit a 30 kW/rack facility to 100 kW/rack and have it work. You build new.

The economic moat just became an infrastructure moat

For most of the 2010s, the AI moat was talent: hire the right researchers, write the right paper, win. From 2020 to 2024, the moat shifted to compute: have a billion dollars in NVIDIA H100s, win.

By April 2026 the moat has shifted again. It is now infrastructure: the ability to break ground on gigawatt-scale construction projects, to lock in 10-year power-purchase agreements with utilities, to negotiate behind-the-meter generation deals, to secure transformer and HBM supply two years out.

That moat is not climbed by being smart. It’s climbed by being capital-rich and politically connected. The list of organizations that can credibly run that play is short: OpenAI/Microsoft/Oracle, Google, Meta, Anthropic/AWS, xAI, and (with national-scale support) DeepSeek/Alibaba/ByteDance/Tencent on Huawei silicon.

That’s eight, maybe ten organizations on the planet. Twenty years ago anyone with a few servers could enter the AI race. Today the cost of admission is closer to a national infrastructure budget.

The contrarian read

Every gigawatt above is a bet that AI will eventually pay for itself. So far, the financial evidence is thin. Big tech is on track to spend roughly $1.1 trillion on AI capex from 2026 to 2029, with total AI spending projected at $1.6T over the same window [Tech-Insider]. Meanwhile, $500B per year is being committed to AI infrastructure in 2026–27, against ~$12B in annual US consumer AI revenue [NPR, Nov 2025]. An MIT report found that despite $30–40B of enterprise GenAI investment, 95% of organizations are getting zero measurable return. A National Bureau of Economic Research study published February 2026 found 90% of firms reporting no productivity impact from AI, even as their executives projected 1.4% productivity gains and 0.8% output gains. Microsoft has guided to ~$25B in AI-related revenue for FY2026 against $97.7–150B of capital expenditure — a payback period that, on conservative assumptions, stretches well beyond a decade. The arithmetic is what it is. Either the revenue side accelerates a lot, or the gigawatts being poured today look very different in retrospect.

None of this proves a bubble. But the shape of the curves — massive infrastructure spend, modest current revenue, optimistic projected revenue, herding behavior across all hyperscalers at once — is a shape we’ve seen before. The 2000s telecom buildout poured fiber that served the 2010s internet boom beautifully — ten years late, after most of the original investors were wiped out. AI infrastructure may turn out to be similar: durably useful, financially brutal for the people who funded it.

Or it may not. Reasonable people disagree. What’s certain is that the people pouring the concrete today are betting more than they can afford to lose, on a thesis the market hasn’t yet validated.

The efficiency-first alternative path

Not everyone is racing to gigawatt-scale. A serious counter-trend in 2025–26 is the efficiency-first thesis: smaller, sparser, faster models that fit on less hardware. Mixture-of-experts (Chapter 3’s recurring theme) activates 13–49B parameters out of 1.6T totals — inference cost tracks the active count, not the total. On-device models from Apple, Google (Gemini Nano), and the Phi/Llama-on-Edge ecosystem run useful workloads on a phone’s neural engine at zero cloud cost. Distillation pipelines turn frontier models into 3–7B specialists that run on a single consumer GPU. Quantization (4-bit, 2-bit, sometimes 1.58-bit) shrinks inference memory by 4–10×. The efficiency stack isn’t at the frontier on capability — but it’s 100× cheaper, runs without an interconnection queue, and is good enough for a remarkable percentage of real workloads. If the gigawatt thesis stalls (regulatory, financial, or physical), the efficiency thesis is the obvious successor — and it’s already winning the on-device, edge, and cost-sensitive segments.

The efficiency path doesn’t need Stargate. It doesn’t need Vera Rubin. It doesn’t need Titan. It needs better quantization research, smarter post-training, and a willingness to live within the capability ceiling that small-model architectures impose. That ceiling moves up every year too.

Looking forward

By December 2026, expect: (1) the first single-campus AI cluster to operate above 1 GW — Stargate Abilene is the favorite, Colossus 2 is already there at 2 GW if you count the full Memphis footprint. (2) Vera Rubin-based instances live in production at AWS, Azure, GCP, and Oracle, with at least one frontier model trained on Rubin-class compute. (3) OpenAI’s Titan inference chip in initial deployment, providing ~10% of OpenAI’s production inference capacity by year-end. (4) At least one US state passing a binding moratorium or restrictive permitting regime on new data-center construction. (5) PJM and similar capacity-market prices remaining elevated, pushing residential utility costs up by 5–15% in affected territories. (6) Liquid cooling standard on every new build above 50 kW/rack — air-cooled AI racks are already extinct at the frontier. (7) The first “gigawatt-scale failure” — a major buildout delayed 12+ months by transformer or grid issues. Probably-won’t-happen by EOY 2026: a fully-permitted, fully-online 10 GW single-program buildout. Stargate’s 10 GW target slips into 2027 by most realistic forecasts.

Probably won’t rot:
  • The shift from algorithm-bound to infrastructure-bound progress
  • The dual-stack reality: NVIDIA + multiple custom-silicon plays in parallel
  • The 100 kW/rack threshold and the liquid-cooling mandate that follows
  • The 5–7 year grid-interconnection queue (this is regulatory, not technical — it moves slowly)
  • The political backlash arc as residential power costs rise alongside AI capex
  • The structural tension between $500B/year AI infra spend and modest current revenue

The five-second summary

The compute crunch in one breath

The bottleneck on AI in 2026 isn’t the model — it’s the megawatts. Stargate’s $500B program targets 10 GW, with Abilene crossing 1 GW by mid-year. xAI’s Colossus 2 is already at 2 GW and 555,000 GPUs in Memphis. NVIDIA’s Vera Rubin platform launches H2 2026 with 5× Blackwell performance; OpenAI’s Titan inference chip targets December 2026 with a 90% inference-cost reduction goal. Custom silicon (Titan, TPU v7, Trainium, Maia, MTIA, Ascend) is now mainstream — the “NVIDIA tax” finally has alternatives. Global data-center electricity hits ~1,100 TWh in 2026, ~5th-largest consumer-equivalent if it were a country. Frontier racks draw 100+ kW each, mandating liquid cooling. PJM capacity prices are up 10× from 2024–25; three US states will likely impose moratoriums by 2027. The economic moat is now an infrastructure moat — eight to ten organizations on Earth can credibly play. And the contrarian counter-fact: $500B/year of capex is chasing ~$12B of consumer revenue, with 95% of enterprise AI projects reporting zero return. The compute crunch is real. Whether the gigawatts pay back is the bet of the decade.
