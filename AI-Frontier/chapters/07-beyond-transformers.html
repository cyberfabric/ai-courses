Chapter 7

Beyond Transformers: SSMs and Hybrids

Mamba-3, Jamba, Titans, diffusion text — the architectures eating the Transformer’s lunch from below, and where the original still rules

As of April 2026, the “all-Transformer” assumption that defined frontier AI from 2017 to 2024 is dead at the architectural level — but the obituaries you read on Twitter are still wrong. Hybrids won. Pure Mamba models hit a recall wall (“Achilles’ Heel of Mamba,” NeurIPS 2025 spotlight [Goomba Lab, 2025]), pure Transformers hit a quadratic-cost wall, and the production frontier has converged on interleaved attention + state-space + sparse routing. Mamba-3 shipped from CMU/Princeton/Together/Cartesia in March 2026 with half the state size of Mamba-2 and better downstream accuracy [MarkTechPost, Mar 2026]. Jamba 1.5 from AI21 scaled hybrid Mamba–Transformer to 398B total / 94B active parameters with a 256K effective context. Qwen3-Next (Sep 2025) and Qwen 3.5 (Feb 2026) ship Gated DeltaNet + Gated Attention hybrids in production at Alibaba scale [NVIDIA, 2026]. DeepSeek V4 Preview (April 24, 2026) leans on Compressed Sparse Attention + Heavily Compressed Attention to run 1M-token contexts at 27% of V3.2’s FLOPs and 10% of its KV cache [DigitalApplied, Apr 2026]. Google’s Titans (Dec 2025) reintroduced test-time memorization — the model rewrites its own weights as it reads. And Inception Labs Mercury 2 (Feb 2026) shipped a commercial diffusion LLM doing 1,109 tok/sec on an H100 [BusinessWire, Feb 2026]. The frontier is no longer one architecture. This snapshot rots fast, the patterns last.

Welcome to Part III of the course: Beyond LLMs. Part I told you what shipped, Part II told you where it’s going. The next three chapters explore the architectures, paradigms, and embodiments that aren’t the dominant Transformer-LLM-text-prediction stack — the alternatives that may, or may not, define the next era.

This chapter is about the architectural alternatives under the hood. The next two are about the alternative frames — world models (Ch 8) and embodied AI (Ch 9). All three pair real momentum with a contrarian dose of “but… not yet.”

Companion: This chapter builds on AI-DeepDive Ch 7 (The Full Transformer) for attention basics and AI-DeepDive Ch 13 (Architectures Compared) for the broader family tree. If you don’t remember why attention is O(n²), jump back — the rest of this chapter is built on top of it.

The problem the Transformer can’t solve

Let’s start with what we’re running away from. The Transformer’s attention mechanism is famously powerful: every token can “see” every other token directly. That’s why it’s good at recall — ask GPT-5.4 about a name from token 3 of a 100K-token doc and it can usually find it.

The catch: that “every-to-every” computation costs O(n²) in both compute and memory. Double the context, quadruple the cost. At 1M tokens, naive attention requires roughly a trillion attention scores per layer. Multiply by tens of layers and tens of heads, and you’re burning whole GPU racks just to read.

The KV cache is the second wall

It gets worse at inference time. Modern LLMs cache the “keys” and “values” of every prior token (the KV cache) so they don’t recompute attention for already-seen tokens. The KV cache grows linearly with context but is memory-bandwidth-bound — you’re shoveling gigabytes per second from VRAM into compute units. By 1M tokens, the KV cache for a frontier-class dense model can hit hundreds of GB per request.

This is why “1M context” remains marketing-speak for many models. The window opens at 1M, but the per-token serving cost is so high that the provider quietly throttles you.

Three escape routes

The post-Transformer field organizes itself around three different bets on how to escape the quadratic cliff:

  1. State Space Models (SSMs) — compress history into a fixed-size state, propagate it linearly. Mamba, Mamba-2, Mamba-3.
  2. Hybrids — mostly SSMs or linear attention, with a thin layer of full attention sprinkled in to preserve recall. Jamba, Qwen3-Next, Kimi Linear, DeepSeek V4.
  3. Diffusion text — abandon left-to-right token prediction altogether. Generate text in parallel, then refine. Mercury, LLaDA.

A fourth bet — test-time memory (Titans) — is technically orthogonal to the others, but it’s the most surprising 2025–2026 development and we’ll cover it last.

State Space Models: the linear-time intuition

Forget the math for a second. Here’s the intuition.

A standard attention layer is like a librarian with perfect recall: ask about any token in the input and they hand it to you in full fidelity. Beautiful. Expensive.

An SSM is like a meditator. As input streams in, they hold a fixed-size mental summary. New token? Update the summary. Older tokens? Already folded into the summary — you can’t pull them out verbatim, but their gist is still there. Cost is linear in sequence length, no matter how long.

What “selective” means

The original Mamba (Dec 2023) added a key trick: the meditator chooses what to remember. Each new token can modulate the state-update parameters based on its content — the model learns to compress aggressively on filler words and faithfully on names, numbers, code identifiers, etc. This was the “selective state space” breakthrough that put SSMs on the map.

Mamba-3 — the March 2026 update

The newest entry, Mamba-3, came out of a CMU–Princeton–Together AI–Cartesia collaboration on March 18, 2026. It introduces three changes designed for “inference-first” deployment [arXiv:2603.15569]:

  • Exponential-trapezoidal discretization — a smarter way of stepping the continuous SSM through discrete tokens, improving stability.
  • Complex-valued state updates — lets the state “rotate” through different patterns, not just attenuate.
  • Multi-Input Multi-Output (MIMO) formulation — the state has multiple coupled channels that talk to each other, not just one.

The headline numbers, from the Princeton blog:

The thesis: SSMs aren’t a curiosity. They’re the efficient backbone for the next decade of long-context, low-latency inference — provided you stop trying to use them as drop-in Transformer replacements.

The Achilles’ heel. A NeurIPS 2025 spotlight paper titled “Achilles’ Heel of Mamba” demonstrated that pure Mamba and Mamba-2 architectures systematically fail on synthetic copy and recall tasks that Transformers solve trivially [Goomba Lab, 2025]. The cause is not implementation: it’s structural. The fixed-size state is compressed; you cannot uncompress an arbitrary past token. Empirically, Mamba layers maintain recall accuracy for 4–8 stacked layers before needle-in-a-haystack performance degrades sharply. This is why pure Mamba won’t replace Transformers at the frontier — and why every winning 2026 architecture is a hybrid.

Pure SSMs (Mamba family). The bet: linear-time everything. Wins on edge inference, tiny models, audio/genomics, and anywhere recall doesn’t matter much. Loses on five-shot MMLU, code retrieval, long-document QA. Best as a backbone component, not a replacement.

The hybrid sweet spot

If pure Mamba can’t recall and pure attention can’t scale, the obvious answer is to mix them. The question is the mixing ratio.

The 2025–2026 finding: you don’t need much attention. One attention layer per ~7–8 SSM (or linear-attention) layers is enough to plug the recall hole, while keeping ~90% of the cost savings.

Jamba 1.5 — the open-weight hybrid pioneer

AI21’s Jamba family pioneered the production hybrid. The architecture interleaves Mamba and Transformer layers in a 1:7 ratio (one attention layer for every seven Mamba layers), then sprinkles MoE into a subset of layers for capacity. Jamba 1.5 scales this to 398B total / 94B active across 72 layers [AI21, 2026].

The result: an open-weight model with a 256K effective context window that runs at a fraction of dense-Transformer cost. Jamba is the first model to ship a hybrid at this scale and prove that the architecture doesn’t silently fall apart under real workloads.

Qwen3-Next and Qwen 3.5 — hybrid-MoE at Alibaba scale

Qwen3-Next shipped in September 2025 as Alibaba’s “ultra-efficient” preview, with a follow-up Qwen 3.5 (397B-A17B MoE) in February 2026 [StableLearn, 2026]. Architecture highlights:

  • 48 layers, with every 4th layer using GQA attention; the rest use Gated DeltaNet, a linear-attention variant.
  • Ultra-Sparse MoE activating only 3.7% of parameters (3B of 80B for the smaller variant) per token.
  • Multi-Token Prediction — predict several tokens per step, decoded in parallel.

The Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B model became NVIDIA’s reference example for hybrid-MoE optimization on H100/H200 stacks [NVIDIA, 2026].

Kimi Linear and the Gated DeltaNet wave

Moonshot’s Kimi Linear uses the same Gated DeltaNet + Gated Attention recipe to ship a 262K native context open-weight model. The pattern across multiple Chinese open-weight labs in 2026 is striking: nearly every major release uses some hybrid attention design, almost none use pure dense Transformers anymore.

DeepSeek V4 — the most aggressive 2026 hybrid

DeepSeek V4 Preview shipped on April 24, 2026 with a hybrid attention scheme that’s arguably the most aggressive in production [dasroot.net, Apr 2026]:

  • Compressed Sparse Attention (CSA) — small chunks of tokens are summarized into “summary tokens,” and each new token attends only to the top-k most relevant summaries instead of every prior token.
  • Heavily Compressed Attention (HCA) — even more aggressive compression for very long contexts.
  • Hash routing in the first three MoE layers (deterministic by token ID), then learned routing afterwards.
  • FP4 storage for MoE expert weights — 4-bit precision in memory, decoded to higher precision on load.

The result: at 1M-token context, V4-Pro uses 27% of single-token inference FLOPs and 10% of the KV cache of V3.2 [DigitalApplied, 2026]. Same model class, an order-of-magnitude better serving economics.

This is the architecture eating frontier production deployments in 2026.

Diffusion text: throwing out left-to-right

SSMs and hybrids still generate text the “normal” way: left to right, one token at a time. Diffusion language models throw that assumption out too.

The core idea, in one paragraph

Diffusion models for images (Stable Diffusion, Midjourney, etc.) start with random noise and iteratively denoise it into a coherent image. Diffusion language models do the same with text: start with a sequence of masked tokens, and denoise them in parallel over a few iterations, refining the whole sequence at once instead of generating token-by-token.

The win: parallelism. A standard autoregressive LLM has to wait for token n before it can produce token n+1. A diffusion LLM produces a whole sequence per denoising step, and only needs ~10–30 steps total. On GPUs, that’s a 10× throughput opportunity.

Mercury and Mercury 2 — the commercial diffusion frontier

Inception Labs — founded by Stanford, UCLA, and Cornell researchers behind foundational diffusion work — released Mercury, the world’s first commercial-scale diffusion LLM, in 2025. Mercury Coder Mini and Coder Small hit 1109 and 737 tokens/sec respectively on a single H100, outperforming speed-optimized frontier models by up to 10× while matching their quality [arXiv:2506.17298].

Mercury 2 shipped February 2026 as a reasoning-focused diffusion LLM — described as “5× faster than leading speed-optimized LLMs, with dramatically lower inference cost” [BusinessWire, Feb 2026]. Inception is reportedly deploying these models at Fortune 500 customers as the autoregressive-vs-diffusion bake-off begins to play out in real procurement decisions.

LLaDA — the open-weight diffusion alternative

LLaDA (Large Language Diffusion with mAsking) is the most prominent open-weight diffusion LLM. As of April 2026 you can download LLaDA-8B-Instruct and LLaDA-8B-Base from HuggingFace. Its architecture is BERT-like (bidirectional attention, no causal mask), does not use a KV cache, and processes the whole sequence at every denoising step.

What diffusion text unlocks

  • Speed. 5–10× throughput at comparable quality — matters most for agents in tight inner loops.
  • Bidirectional editing. The model can rewrite tokens in the middle of the sequence, not just append. Format conformance and infill become trivial.
  • Built-in error correction. Each denoising step lets the model “fix” tokens it generated in earlier steps — a structural mitigation for some kinds of hallucination.
  • Controllable generation. Want JSON in a specific schema? Mask the values, fix the keys, and denoise — the schema is enforced architecturally.

But it’s still small-scale. The diffusion-LLM frontier as of April 2026 tops out around ~10B parameters in the open and undisclosed-but-modest sizes commercially. No diffusion LLM has shipped at 500B+ active parameters or matched GPT-5.4 / Claude Opus 4.7 on hard reasoning benchmarks. Diffusion may yet eat the latency-bound segment of the market — high-volume agents, low-stakes summarization, real-time coding suggestions — without ever displacing the autoregressive flagship for “hardest 5% of queries.” Treat 2026 diffusion-LLM hype the way you treated 2023 SSM hype: real, but smaller than the headlines.

Diffusion language models. The bet: parallel generation beats sequential. Wins on throughput and on use-cases where bidirectional rewriting matters (code edits, structured outputs, format-constrained generation). Loses on the very long, very open-ended generation tasks autoregressive LLMs are still designed around. Watch the next 12 months — if a major lab ships a 100B+ diffusion model that matches GPT-5-class quality, the architectural debate gets a lot more interesting.

Titans: when the model rewrites itself

The most architecturally surprising 2025–2026 development came not from a startup but from Google Research. On December 4, 2025, the Titans paper landed (NeurIPS 2025 poster), proposing a radically different memory model: let the model update its own weights at inference time.

The intuition: short-term and long-term memory

Standard Transformers have one memory store: the KV cache, which is essentially a transcript of recent attention. Titans adds a second store: a small neural network — literally an MLP — whose weights are updated as the model reads. Think of it as the model writing notes in a separate notebook as it processes the input.

The “surprise” mechanism

Titans only writes to its long-term memory when something surprising happens — when the new input meaningfully diverges from what the model already knows. This is measured via a gradient signal: high gradient = high surprise = worth committing to memory [arXiv:2501.00663].

Combined with attention, Titans handles context windows above 2 million tokens while keeping linear-time inference. On the BABILong benchmark (long-context reasoning) it outperforms much larger models including GPT-4, RecurrentGemma-9B, and Llama-3.1-70B [eTavrian, 2025].

Why this is a big deal

Every Transformer LLM you’ve used is, at inference, a frozen function. It has parameters that were trained months ago, and a temporary working memory in the KV cache. Titans dissolves that line: parameters can change now, in the middle of your conversation, in response to what you said.

Google followed up Titans with the MIRAS framework — a more general theoretical foundation for what they’re calling “continuously learning” architectures. None of it is in production at GPT/Claude/Gemini scale yet. But it is the most concrete answer the field has produced to one of LLMs’ oldest complaints: “why can’t the model actually learn from this conversation?”

Test-time learning architectures (Titans, MIRAS). The bet: the line between training and inference is a relic. Wins on tasks needing genuine in-context adaptation (long documents, persistent agents, lifelong learning). Loses on safety predictability — a model that rewrites its own weights at inference is harder to audit, version, and roll back. Watch this space; it’s the most interesting architectural research direction since the original Transformer.

Where the Transformer still wins (and why)

Despite all of the above, GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.7, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and Grok 4 — the four highest-grossing model APIs in the Western market — remain predominantly dense or sparse Transformers. That’s not nostalgia. It’s a deliberate choice.

Recall-heavy workloads

Anything that requires verbatim retrieval of arbitrary past tokens — long-document Q&A, multi-doc legal analysis, codebase-wide refactoring — benefits from explicit attention. Hybrid models close most of this gap; pure SSMs do not.

Hardest-of-hardest reasoning

On the upper end of the GPQA Diamond, Humanity’s Last Exam, and FrontierMath distribution, dense or moderately-sparse Transformers + extended thinking still hold. The structural advantage of full attention shows up most when the model is doing many compositions over many entities.

Fine-grained instruction following

On benchmarks like IFBench, dense Transformers with strong post-training still dominate. Hybrids are catching up, but the post-training recipe for hybrids is less mature.

“The Transformer is dead” has been wrong every year since 2022. SSMs were going to kill it (2023). Diffusion text was going to kill it (2024). Mamba-2 was going to kill it (early 2024). RetNet was going to kill it (also 2023, briefly). What actually happened: the Transformer absorbed what it needed (sparse attention, MoE, linear attention where useful) and the alternatives became its components, not its replacement. The field that talks loudest about replacing Transformers is also the field that keeps releasing hybrids that contain Transformers. Be skeptical of the next “Transformer killer” headline. The asymptote is hybrids, not displacement.

The multimodal twist: Mixture-of-Mamba and friends

Everything above was about language. The story gets more interesting once you cross modalities.

One of the more striking 2025–2026 results is Mixture-of-Mamba — an architecture that takes the SSM idea and adds modality-aware sparsity. The intuition: when you’re processing a vision token, you don’t need the same state-space parameters as when you’re processing a text token. Mixture-of-Mamba routes each token through a modality-specific SSM block, similar to how MoE routes tokens through experts.

The result: the same total compute as a unified Mamba, but markedly better cross-modal performance — and the architecture extends cleanly to any number of modalities (image, audio, text, code) without ballooning the per-token cost.

The vision-language hybrid MaTVLM (ICCV 2025) is another data point: replace most of the Transformer blocks in a vision-language model with Mamba blocks, keep a few attention layers, and you get comparable accuracy at a fraction of the inference cost — particularly relevant for video, where token counts are enormous.

Expect the same pattern as we saw with text-only LLMs: pure Mamba wins on edge / mobile multimodal, hybrids win on long-context multimodal, and dense Transformers cling to the highest-fidelity tasks (e.g., expert-level image reasoning at frontier scale).

What changes for training, not just inference

Most of this chapter has focused on inference — what happens when you serve the model. The training story is its own thing, and it’s the reason hybrids took longer to ship at scale than the architectural papers suggested.

Hybrid post-training is genuinely harder

Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) and the more modern post-training pipelines (constitutional AI, GRPO, etc.) were all developed assuming dense Transformers. The reward models, the PPO variants, the value heads — all designed around the attention pattern. When you swap in Mamba layers, the gradient flow changes, the credit assignment changes, and standard recipes silently underperform.

This is why early hybrids (2024-era Jamba 1.0, RWKV) were strong on raw next-token prediction but underwhelming on chat benchmarks: the post-training hadn’t caught up. By 2026, the recipe is maturing — DeepSeek V4’s per-domain expert RL approach (a separate specialist trained for math, coding, agent tasks, etc., each with its own reward model) is partly an accommodation to hybrid post-training quirks.

Stability is non-trivial

SSMs are sensitive to the discretization scheme. Pure Mamba-1 had stability issues at long contexts that Mamba-2 partly fixed, that Mamba-3 fixes more carefully via exponential-trapezoidal discretization. The Lyapunov stability analysis (NeurIPS) is a useful theoretical lens: a model whose state can “blow up” under adversarial input is not safe to deploy, no matter how good the benchmarks look.

Hardware co-design matters more than for Transformers

Transformers map cleanly to dense matmul, which is what every accelerator since 2017 has been optimized for. SSMs need scan primitives (associative scan, parallel scan) that older accelerators don’t do well. Mamba-3’s “inference-first” design philosophy is largely about being kind to GPU memory hierarchy and bandwidth — you can’t just pretend the hardware doesn’t exist. NVIDIA has shipped first-class kernels for Mamba and hybrid stacks; AMD and custom AI chips are still catching up.

The on-device frontier

Frontier-class flagship models live in data centers. The other half of AI — the half running on your phone, your laptop, your earbuds — has very different architectural constraints. And this is where the post-Transformer architectures shine first.

On a phone-class NPU with maybe 8–16 GB of RAM and strict power budgets:

  • KV cache memory at long context becomes the binding constraint long before raw compute does.
  • Latency matters in milliseconds, not seconds.
  • Battery means every joule per token counts.

SSMs are nearly purpose-built for this regime. A 3B Mamba-3 derivative can serve thousands of tokens of context on-device with stable memory use, where a same-size Transformer would either OOM or take too long. Expect 2026’s on-device assistants — the ones that actually run locally, not the marketing ones — to be predominantly hybrid, often with a heavier Mamba component than their data-center cousins.

Edge-first architectures. The bet: bring inference closer to the user. Wins on privacy, latency, offline use, and per-token cost (zero serving cost on-device). Loses on raw quality — a 3B on-device model is not a 1.6T cloud model, and never will be. The interesting question for 2026 is which user experiences are good enough at 3B that the privacy/latency/cost wins justify shipping them locally instead of via API. Expect dictation, summarization, drafting, retrieval against personal data, and live translation to all flip on-device by year-end.

Forecast: end of 2026 in architectures

By December 2026, three predictions look relatively safe and one is genuinely uncertain.

Safe bets:

  • Every major Chinese open-weight release will be a hybrid. The pattern is already universal. Expect Qwen 4, GLM 5+, MiniMax M3+, Kimi K3+ to all use linear/sparse attention as the dominant layer with Transformer attention sprinkled in.
  • At least one Western frontier lab will ship a hybrid in production. Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and Meta have all published hybrid research. By Q4 2026, expect at least one of the named flagship APIs to quietly become a hybrid — user-invisible, but with a step-change in serving cost at long context.
  • Diffusion text will ship at >30B parameters with a public benchmark sweep. Inception Labs or a peer will publish results that force the autoregressive crowd to take diffusion seriously for at least latency-sensitive workloads.

Genuinely uncertain: Whether a Titans-class test-time-memory architecture ships at frontier scale. Google has the research; the question is whether the safety story is mature enough to deploy a model that rewrites itself.

What may rot fastest: the specific version numbers (Mamba-3, Mercury 2, Qwen 3.5, DeepSeek V4 Preview), the specific FLOP and KV-cache reduction numbers, the specific benchmark margins, and the layer ratios. What will remain true: the O(n²) wall, the SSM recall-vs-throughput trade-off, the structural advantage of attention for in-context retrieval, the fact that hybrids consistently beat “pure” alternatives, and the fact that diffusion text trades sequential coherence for parallelism. Memorize the patterns; let the version numbers age.

What this means for you, the builder

If you’re writing application code, the architectural details rarely show up in your prompts. But they show up everywhere in your serving costs and your latency budget. A few practical implications:

  • Don’t pay for context you don’t use. If your prompt is <8K tokens, the choice between hybrid and dense Transformer barely matters. If your prompt is >200K, it matters enormously — one architecture will cost you 5× the other.
  • Latency-sensitive? Try diffusion. Mercury 2 (commercial) and LLaDA (open) are real options for inner-loop agents and real-time UX, where 5× throughput translates directly to 5× better p99.
  • Long-context recall ≠ long-context window. A hybrid model with a 1M window will open the window but quietly compress earlier tokens. If you need exact recall at token 700,000, run an evaluation, don’t trust the spec sheet.
  • The provider may swap your architecture out from under you. If GPT-5.4-Turbo or Claude Opus 4.7 quietly becomes a hybrid in Q3 2026, you’ll see latency improve and behavior shift slightly on edge cases. Build evals you can re-run automatically.

Try this. Pick a long-context task you actually have — say, a 200K-token document QA. Run it through three models with three different architectures: a dense Transformer (e.g. Claude Opus 4.7), a hybrid (e.g. DeepSeek V4-Flash), and a diffusion LLM (e.g. Mercury 2 if you have access, or a fast small Mamba-based model otherwise). Measure: time-to-first-token, total cost, and answer correctness on 20 needle-in-haystack questions sprinkled across the document. The differences will surprise you — in either direction.

The bigger pattern

Step back from the model names and you can see something that’s easy to miss in the version-number churn: the era of the “one true architecture” is ending.

From 2017 to roughly 2023, the field was a Transformer monoculture. Bigger Transformer, more data, longer training, win. By 2025 the cracks were obvious: serving costs at long context, recall fragility, sequential-decoding latency. By 2026 the post-Transformer ecosystem isn’t a single competitor — it’s a menu of architectures, each better at some axis (recall, throughput, cost, persistence, parallelism), composed into hybrids tuned per use-case.

This is healthier than monoculture, but it raises the cost of building. You can’t just “pick the SOTA model” anymore. You have to know what you’re optimizing for and pick the architecture that shapes that curve.

The takeaway

The Transformer didn’t die. It absorbed its competitors. SSMs became its long-context backbone. MoE became its capacity multiplier. Sparse attention became its default. Diffusion became its fast-lane sibling. Test-time memory may become its life-long-learning extension. The frontier of 2026 is a menagerie, not a single beast — and the architectures you ignore today will be the production defaults you depend on by 2027.
