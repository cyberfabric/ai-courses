Chapter 9

Embodied AI: Foundation Models for Robots

Helix, π0, Figure 03, Optimus Gen 3, NEO, Apollo, Digit, Unitree — what’s deployed, what’s demo-ware, and which 2026 production promises will hold

As of April 2026, embodied AI is having its “foundation model moment” — finally, after a decade of false starts. Tesla Optimus Gen 3 production is scheduled to start late July 2026 at Fremont via a Model S/X line conversion, with a first-gen target of 1M units/year and a Texas Gigafactory line aimed at 10M/year by 2027 [Electrek, Apr 2026]. Figure 03 — partnered with OpenAI for AI — runs Helix, the first Vision-Language-Action (VLA) model executed entirely on embedded onboard GPUs, manufactured at the BotQ facility (12,000 units/year). Apptronik Apollo ($5B valuation, $935M raised) is operating at Mercedes-Benz Berlin and Kecskemét, plus Jabil, with full-fleet 2026 deployment in plan [Robozaps, 2026]. 1X NEO (Norway) opened preorders with first customer deliveries 2026. Agility Digit — the no-head, backward-leg humanoid — is in Amazon pilots handling intralogistics. And the Chinese price war is fully on: Unitree R1 ships at $4,370 domestically (~$6,800 international), G1 at $13,500 base, vs. Boston Dynamics / Tesla prototypes at $50k–$150k [SCMP, 2026]. The volume gap is even more striking: Unitree shipped >5,500 humanoids in 2025, vs. ~150 each from Tesla, Figure, and Agility — a 36× ratio [TechCrunch, Feb 2026]. And yet: Bain, McKinsey, and Gartner all warn the demo-to-deployment gap is huge. Most humanoids still need ~2-hour batteries, run in highly structured environments, and rely heavily on human supervision. Gartner predicts fewer than 20 firms scale to real production by 2028. This snapshot rots fast, the patterns last.

This chapter is about new embodiments — AI that has a body, walks around your factory floor, and tries (often unsuccessfully) to fold a t-shirt.

By the end you’ll know what a Vision-Language-Action model actually is, the 2026 humanoid roster (Western and Chinese), where deployments are real vs. theatrical, and which of the “mass production by H2 2026” promises are credible.

The shift: from task-specific to foundation

If you remember anything about robotics from before 2024, remember this: every robot was hand-coded for its task. Pick-and-place at a specific station with specific parts, taught via demonstration or hard programming. Move the part 3 cm to the left? Re-teach the robot. New product variant? Re-teach the robot. Different lighting? Probably re-teach the robot.

Then a generation of Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models arrived, and the picture flipped. A VLA takes vision (camera feed), language (a natural-language instruction like “put the red block in the blue bin”), and outputs actions (joint angles, gripper commands, end-effector trajectories). One model. Many tasks. Often zero re-teaching for variations within distribution.

This is the same pattern that played out in NLP from 2018 to 2022 — from per-task models to one foundation model that does everything. Robotics is now in its 2020. Two more years of this and the field looks very different.

Why VLA models work now and didn’t before

  • Pre-trained vision-language backbones (CLIP, SigLIP, PaliGemma) come with strong perception out of the box.
  • Cross-embodiment data — projects like Open X-Embodiment pooled robot trajectories from many labs, many robot bodies. One model learns transferable manipulation skills across hardware.
  • Action generation matured — diffusion policies and flow-matching produce smooth high-frequency continuous actions instead of brittle discrete commands.
  • Simulation got real — Cosmos and friends (see Ch 8) generate hundreds of thousands of physics-grounded synthetic trajectories per real demonstration.

The VLA roster you should know

Two VLA models dominate 2026 conversation. They’re built differently and reflect different bets.

π0 (Pi-Zero) — Physical Intelligence

π0 from startup Physical Intelligence (announced late 2024, expanded through 2025–2026) is a generalist VLA designed for cross-embodiment skill transfer. The architecture:

  • PaliGemma backbone (built from SigLIP vision encoder + Gemma language model)
  • Action expert trained on robot trajectories from Open X-Embodiment
  • Flow-matching action head producing continuous actions at 50 Hz
  • Diffusion policy structure for action generation
  • Trained on data from 8 different robot embodiments — single-arm, dual-arm, varied grippers

The bet: a single VLA can drive any robot body. Physical Intelligence is selling the model and policy stack, not the hardware.

Helix — Figure AI

Helix (Figure AI, Feb 2025) is the opposite philosophy: a generalist VLA specifically tailored for humanoid robots, optimized to ship in their actual product. Architecture:

  • Dual-system design — a slow component handles images + text instructions; a fast component produces high-frequency motor actions. (Same idea as Kahneman’s System 1 / System 2; also adopted by NVIDIA’s Groot N1.)
  • Runs entirely onboard on embedded low-power GPUs — no cloud, no remote inference. This is the breakthrough that made Helix “commercially deployable.”

Wins on flexibility (one model, many SKUs), data efficiency (pre-train on web video, fine-tune on a few hundred demonstrations), and language interface (humans give instructions in English). Loses on edge cases (long-tail dexterity), repeatability (a precision welder is still better at welding), and predictability (a deterministic program never has a bad day).

The 2026 humanoid roster (Western)

Tesla Optimus Gen 3

Optimus is the most-watched and most-hyped humanoid in 2026. The production plan:

  • Late July 2026 — production starts at Fremont via Model S/X line conversion (4-month conversion).
  • First-gen line targets 1M units/year.
  • Second-gen line in Gigafactory Texas aims at 10M/year by 2027.
  • $20B CapEx committed to robotics.
  • Feb 2026 reveal: 50-actuator Gen 3 hands — the dexterity story Tesla has been promising since Optimus Gen 1.

Whether these targets land on time is the open question. Optimus Gen 2 was supposed to ship in volume in 2025 and didn’t. Tesla has a long history of optimistic timelines. But Musk’s capital commitment is real and the production-line photos are real.

Figure 03

Figure shipped Figure 03 built at the BotQ facility (12,000 units/year). The OpenAI partnership (announced 2024) provides the foundation models behind the scenes; Helix is the on-robot VLA. Figure’s pitch is “humanoid for the workplace, today” — pilot deployments in BMW factories and undisclosed logistics partners.

1X NEO

Norwegian-origin, OpenAI-backed (Sam Altman is an investor). The first soft-clad consumer-facing humanoid — designed to feel approachable in homes rather than menacing in factories. Preorders open, first deliveries 2026. NEO Gamma (the new generation) trained using NVIDIA Cosmos Predict + Cosmos Transfer (see Ch 8).

Apptronik Apollo

$5B valuation, $935M raised total (including a recent $520M round to scale production with Jabil) [Robot Report, 2025]. Real deployments:

  • Mercedes-Benz Digital Factory Campus (Berlin) — moving components between stations
  • Mercedes-Benz Kecskemét plant (Hungary) — logistics shuttling, quality checks
  • Jabil — manufacturing partnership and pilot deployments

2026 plan: scale commercial production with Jabil, expand from pilots to full-fleet deployments.

Agility Digit

The contrarian one. Digit doesn’t look human — no head, backward-bending legs — because it’s optimized for one job: warehouse intralogistics. Amazon pilots ongoing, where Digit handles repetitive, low-skill, physically demanding tasks (totes, bins, parts). The lesson Agility teaches: you don’t need humanoid form for humanoid jobs.

The Chinese price war (and why volume matters)

The most disruptive humanoid story of 2025–2026 isn’t any Western flagship. It’s Unitree, a Chinese startup, shipping radically cheaper humanoids in radically larger volumes.

Volume tells the real story

Unitree filed for a $610M Shanghai IPO in early 2026 [Rest of World, 2026]. The company is profitable and scaling. There’s also Noetix, Booster Robotics, and a long tail of Chinese entrants competing on price. The CNBC framing — “China’s humanoid robots go from viral stumbles to kung fu flips in one year” — captures the velocity [CNBC, Feb 2026].

Cost-first, scale-first humanoids. The bet: ship cheap, ship now, iterate in the wild. Unitree’s strategy is consumer electronics, not capital equipment. Wins on volume (more deployed robots = more data = faster improvement), price elasticity (whole new buyer categories at $5k), and rapid iteration (no need to be perfect on day one). Loses on dexterity ceiling, regulatory friction in Western enterprise procurement, and on tasks where reliability matters more than per-unit cost.

The demo-to-deployment gap

Now the contrarian half of the chapter. Every Bain, McKinsey, and Gartner report on humanoid robots in 2025–2026 says some version of the same thing: the demo videos are dazzling, the deployments are humbling.

What the demos hide

  • Staged environments. Demo backgrounds are controlled; lighting is fixed; objects are pre-placed. Real factories aren’t.
  • Remote supervision. Many viral “autonomous” demos have a tele-op operator off-camera making split-second corrections. The official term is “shared autonomy”; the practical term is “a human is helping.”
  • Cherry-picked takes. The 30-second demo is the best of dozens of attempts. Failure rates aren’t published.

Where the dexterity ceiling actually is

From the AI CERTs analysis: dexterity and fine-motor control remain in “relatively earlier stages” with real gaps in tactile sensitivity and precision. Many demos sidestep dexterity by using simple claws or hinged grippers rather than multi-fingered manipulation. Tesla’s Feb 2026 reveal of 50-actuator Gen 3 hands is the most aggressive industry response — but these are not yet shipping in volume on production-line robots.

Gartner’s number

Gartner predicts fewer than 20 firms will scale humanoids to actual production by 2028. That’s out of hundreds of humanoid startups currently raising capital. Most of the field will not survive the next two years.

Reality vs. roadmap. Read the “mass production by H2 2026” promises with one eye on the McKinsey crossing-the-chasm framing. Most humanoid robots in 2026 are doing the same things forklifts and conveyor belts already do, at higher cost, with worse uptime, and with a tele-op pilot in another room helping out when things go sideways. The technology is real and accelerating — but the commercial story is years behind the marketing story. If a humanoid pitch deck doesn’t mention battery life, autonomy hours, supervision ratio, or unit economics, those are exactly the omissions that will determine whether the company ships in 2027.

Where embodied AI is actually delivering value today

Strip out the hype, and there are real, deployed places where embodied AI is generating ROI in 2026.

  • Warehouse intralogistics — Agility Digit at Amazon, Apollo at Mercedes warehouses. Repetitive, structured, low-dexterity. Solved-ish.
  • Manufacturing kitting and material handling — Apollo at Mercedes, Figure 03 at BMW. Same pattern: structured, predictable, well-defined.
  • Quality inspection on production lines — humanoids carrying cameras and reasoning about whether a part looks right.
  • Hazardous-environment work — nuclear inspection, offshore platform maintenance, hot-zone logistics. The robot doesn’t need 8 hours of battery if its alternative is sending humans into danger.
  • Research / data collection at home — 1X NEO sells (in part) on the data flywheel: every home unit generates training data for the next generation.

Notice what’s not on this list: cooking dinner. Folding laundry. Caring for elderly parents. Handling kids. These are the use cases marketing decks promise and that 2026 robots cannot reliably do. The dexterity, safety, and judgment requirements are an order of magnitude harder than warehouse picking.

The data flywheel: why volume is destiny

One of the most underrated stories in 2026 humanoid robotics is the data flywheel. Every deployed robot generates training data — sensor readings, motor commands, success/failure traces, edge cases. More deployed robots = more data = better next-generation model = more deployments.

This is the same pattern that gave Google Search its lock-in (more queries = more click data = better ranking) and Tesla Autopilot its competitive lead (more cars on the road = more driving data = better model). It applies to embodied AI even more sharply because physical-world data is fundamentally scarce — you can’t scrape it from the internet.

Now look at the volume numbers from earlier in this chapter through that lens. Unitree shipped 36× more units than Tesla, Figure, and Agility combined in 2025. Unitree is also, by definition, gathering 36× more in-the-wild physical data. That data flywheel is the biggest reason Western robotics companies should be nervous about Chinese competition — not the cheaper unit price.

Tesla’s counter-strategy is to deploy in their own factories first, where they fully own the data, and to push hard on simulation (with NVIDIA Cosmos and similar) to compensate for limited real-world deployment scale. Whether sim-to-real transfer is good enough to substitute for actual deployment volume is one of the most important open questions in the field.

How VLAs actually generate actions

One technical detail worth knowing because it’s how the field is making rapid progress: action chunking.

Older robot policies output one motor command per inference step. Run inference at 10Hz, you get 10 motor commands per second. Slow, jerky, and each command is myopic — the policy can’t plan ahead.

Modern VLAs (π0, Helix, OpenVLA-OFT) output an entire chunk of future actions per inference step — typically 20–100 commands at once. The policy says “here’s the next 1 second of motion in detail” rather than “here’s the very next 100 millisecond command.” The result is smoother motion, faster effective control rate, and the model can reason about longer horizons.

Combined with diffusion-policy or flow-matching action heads (which are good at generating coherent multi-step trajectories), action chunking is the under-the-hood reason 2026 humanoid videos look meaningfully smoother than 2024 ones. It’s not bigger models — it’s a smarter action representation.

Safety, regulation, and the home question

Most of this chapter has discussed factory and warehouse deployment. The bigger long-term question is can a humanoid live in your home?

The factory bar is “don’t hurt the trained workers who know to stay clear and can hit the e-stop.” The home bar is “don’t hurt the toddler who runs into the room unexpectedly while you’re carrying a knife.” These are different problems, and the home bar is staggeringly higher.

The regulatory gap

As of April 2026, there is no global standard for “humanoid robot safe to operate around humans.” The EU Machinery Regulation (entered force 2027) will be the first attempt; the US has nothing equivalent. Most current deployments use ISO 10218 (industrial robots) or ISO 13482 (personal-care robots), neither of which was designed for general-purpose humanoids that move around freely.

The 1X bet

1X NEO is the most aggressive consumer-home play. Soft-clad exterior. Conservative motion profiles. Designed to look approachable. The pitch: ship to early adopters, generate data, iterate. The risk: a single high-profile injury sets back the entire consumer-humanoid category by years.

This is why most of the industry is starting in factories — the safety case is bounded, the operators are trained, the environment is controlled, and the insurance premiums are tractable. Home is the destination; factory is the runway.

Insurance is the underrated gating factor

Few people writing about humanoids in 2026 mention insurance, and that’s a tell. Every commercial deployment requires liability coverage. Insurers price coverage based on actuarial data — data that doesn’t yet exist for humanoid robots. So either the deployer self-insures (only viable for very large operators like Amazon and Mercedes), or premiums are astronomical. The first humanoid platform to publish reliable safety statistics — meaningful injury rates per million operating hours — will unlock entire categories of deployment that today are uninsurable at any reasonable price.

What this means for you, the builder

Even if you’re not building robots yourself, embodied AI shapes 2026 in ways that touch software builders too.

  • VLA models are opening up to software developers. π0 is publicly downloadable. OpenVLA is open-source. Building with these on a tabletop arm (e.g., a $5k cobot) is now a weekend project, not a PhD prerequisite.
  • NVIDIA Cosmos + simulation means you can train a policy without owning hardware. The skill graph for “robotics engineer” is shifting toward “ML engineer who knows physics.”
  • The data infrastructure problem is wide open. If you’ve worked on data labeling or LLM evaluation, the same problems exist for VLA — with even less tooling. Whole companies will be built on “LangSmith for robots”-class infrastructure.
  • Watch the integration layer. The interesting business in 2027 isn’t building humanoids — it’s building the workflow software that makes one humanoid useful in 50 different factories without 50 custom integrations. SAP/ServiceNow for embodied AI.

Try this. Pick a 5-minute manual task you do regularly — loading the dishwasher, sorting mail, packing a backpack. Watch yourself do it. Count the distinct sub-skills (pinch grip, palm grasp, two-handed coordination, identify-this-object, plan-the-stacking-order, etc.). Now imagine teaching a humanoid to do it. Which sub-skills are well within 2026 capability? Which are clearly out of reach? You’ll quickly develop a much better intuition for “easy demo” vs. “hard product” than any spec sheet can give you.

Forecast: the rest of 2026

By December 2026, four predictions look reasonably safe and one is wide open.

Reasonably safe:

  • Tesla Optimus Gen 3 starts production but misses the volume target. “Production starts in late July” will land. “Hits 1M units in year one” almost certainly will not. Expect tens of thousands, not millions, by year-end.
  • Apollo, Figure 03, and Digit each cross 1,000 deployed units. Real factory and warehouse deployments scale from pilot to limited fleet.
  • Unitree ships >30,000 humanoids in 2026. The IPO funds a new factory. China continues to dominate volume.
  • Helix and π0 fork the field. Humanoid makers will pick a side: build your own VLA (Tesla, Figure) or buy an off-the-shelf one (most of the rest).

Wide open: whether any humanoid achieves credible consumer-home deployment outside controlled beta. NEO is the most likely; the safety, dexterity, and trust requirements are still daunting.

What may rot fastest: all the version numbers (Optimus Gen 3, Figure 03, NEO Gamma), the unit-shipment counts, the price points (Unitree will probably be cheaper by Q4), and the Mercedes/Amazon/BMW pilot status. What will remain true: the demo-vs-deployment gap, the dexterity ceiling, the safety/regulatory drag, the structure-of-environment requirement, the Chinese cost advantage, and the fact that “humanoid form” is often the wrong form for the actual job (Digit’s no-head design wins on its merits). Memorize the patterns, let the price tags age.

The bigger pattern (closing Part III)

Embodied AI is the part of the 2026 frontier that’s easiest to film and hardest to ship. The hype is doing more harm than good in some quarters — deals are getting cut on demo videos, valuations are getting set on YouTube views, and the gap between “impressive demo” and “reliable product” has rarely been wider.

And yet: the underlying technology is real. VLA models work. Foundation models for action exist. Onboard inference is now possible. Cross-embodiment learning is a reality. Chinese volume is creating a data flywheel nobody else has. Tesla’s capex is real. Apollo at Mercedes is real. Digit at Amazon is real. The path from here to genuinely useful general-purpose humanoids is not blocked — it’s just much longer than the demos suggest.

The takeaway

Robotics is having its foundation-model moment. VLAs (Helix, π0, Groot N1, Cosmos Policy) are real. Tesla, Figure, 1X, Apptronik, Agility, and Unitree are all shipping units — in radically different volumes, at radically different prices, with radically different definitions of “deployed.” The 2026 humanoid market is a mix of genuine progress and theater. Read the demos with the same skepticism you read AI benchmark numbers: impressive, but show me the production logs.
