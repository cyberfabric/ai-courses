Chapter 6

What to Expect by End of 2026

Lab signals, expert forecasts, and the scaling-laws debate — stitched into a clear-eyed timeline for the next eight months

As of April 2026, the year is roughly one-third over and the loudest forecasters all agree on one thing: H1 2026 will look small compared to H2 2026. Morgan Stanley calls it “a transformative leap” driven by an “unprecedented accumulation of compute” at top US labs [Fortune, Mar 2026]. Stanford HAI’s 2026 AI Index finds capability is still accelerating, not plateauing — SWE1-bench Verified rose from 60% to near 100% of the human baseline in a single year, and frontier models gained 30 percentage points on Humanity’s Last Exam [Stanford HAI 2026]. Sam Altman has confirmed that GPT-6 pretraining finished March 24, 2026, with launch “weeks-to-months away” [FindSkill, Apr 2026]. Claude Opus 4.7 shipped April 16; Claude Mythos is anticipated Q2 2026; Gemini 3.1 Ultra is expected before mid-year. Analysts are calling Q2 2026 “the most competitive quarter in AI model history”. Big-five hyperscalers are projected to spend $720B on AI capex in 2026 alone, while a major forecast pegs combined OpenAI + Anthropic + xAI annualized revenue at $110B by December. And yet: Morgan Stanley’s same forecast warns of a 9–18 GW US power shortfall through 2028, Ilya Sutskever has declared “pretraining as we know it will end,” and the gap between “wins gold at IMO” and “reads analog clocks at 50.1%” is the new shape of progress. The frontier is jagged, not smooth.

This is the synthesis chapter for Part II. Chapters 4 and 5 covered infrastructure and agents — the where and what. This chapter covers when: a clear-eyed forecast for the rest of 2026, weighing lab signals, expert predictions, and the scaling-law status against the contrarian wall.

By the end you’ll have a calibrated mental model of what to expect by Christmas, what probably won’t happen by then, and which forecasts to take seriously vs which to discount.

The expert tour: who’s saying what

Six high-credibility forecasters dominate the 2026 conversation. Their predictions overlap heavily, which is itself useful information.

Stanford HAI — the data view

The 2026 AI Index is the closest thing to a neutral, measurement-based snapshot. Headline findings:

  • Capability is accelerating, not plateauing. The “capabilities winter” narrative from late 2024 didn’t survive contact with the data.
  • SWE1-bench Verified: 60% → ~100% of human baseline in one year
  • +30 percentage points on Humanity’s Last Exam (a benchmark designed to be hard for AI)
  • Models match or exceed humans on PhD-level science, competition math, and multimodal reasoning
  • The frontier is jagged. The same model wins gold at IMO and reads an analog clock correctly only 50.1% of the time
  • 73% of AI experts expect positive workforce impacts; only 23% of the American public agrees — a 50-point sentiment gap

Morgan Stanley — the breakthrough thesis

Morgan Stanley’s “Intelligence Factory” report calls for a major AI breakthrough in H1 2026, driven by gigawatt-scale compute concentration at frontier labs [Morgan Stanley Institute, 2026]. The blunt corollary: a US power shortfall of 9–18 GW through 2028 — a 12–25% deficit on the power needed to run the compute that’s already on order. For scale: 18 GW is roughly 18 large nuclear plants.

The bank’s “15-15-15” dynamic captures the hyperscaler economics: 15-year data-center leases at 15% yields, $15 per watt of net value creation. Bitcoin miners are converting their facilities into HPC; gas turbines and fuel cells are filling the gap while the grid catches up.

Ajeya Cotra — the calibrated forecaster view

The Planned Obsolescence forecast is the most-cited specific prediction set in the AI-safety community [Cotra, 2026 predictions]. Two of her concrete numbers:

  • 95% confident that a major company runs an AI shopping agent completing >100,000 transactions by year-end 2026
  • Combined annualized revenue of OpenAI + Anthropic + xAI projected to reach $110B by December 2026 — up from estimates in the $30–40B range a year earlier

Time / Davos 2026 — the deployment view

The Time / Davos 2026 panel and PwC’s 2026 AI Business Predictions converge on a different framing: 2026 is the “year of deployment” rather than the year of new capability. The shift: from experimentation and demos to the messy reality of integration, governance, and ROI accountability.

Jakob Nielsen — the UX view

Nielsen’s 18 Predictions for 2026 emphasize the AI shopping agent (95%), the “ChatGPT moment for medicine,” and the rise of high-frequency “AI economic dashboards” that track productivity gains and losses in near-real-time.

Hopkins-Bloomberg — the policy view

The Hopkins Bloomberg Center calls 2026 a pivotal year for US AI governance, with executive orders, agency-level rules, and the first test cases of the EU AI Act’s general-purpose-AI provisions all colliding in a single calendar year.

The scaling-laws debate

Here’s where the experts disagree most. The big question: is pre-training scaling still working, or has it hit a wall?

The “scaling has slowed” camp

Ilya Sutskever stated at NeurIPS 2024 that “pretraining as we know it will end” and that the 2010s were the age of scaling, but we’re now “back in the age of wonder and discovery” [Masood analysis, 2026]. The argument:

  • Data scarcity. Chinchilla-optimal training for a 1T-parameter model needs ~20T tokens. High-quality internet text exists in the 10–50T token range depending on how you count. The largest models are now training on more tokens than uniquely exist.
  • Diminishing returns. Pre-training compute is doubling, but base-model perplexity gains are sub-log. The marginal token costs more for less.
  • The recipe broke. “Pour internet text into a model and scale up” stopped being the whole answer. Synthetic data, RL, post-training, and reasoning compute now contribute more than naive scaling.

The “scaling is alive” camp

Stanford HAI’s 2026 AI Index pushes back hard: capability is still accelerating, the “wall” narrative isn’t supported by the benchmark data. The argument:

  • New scaling axes. Test-time compute (Chapter 2), MoE sparsity (Chapter 3), synthetic-data pipelines, and RL-on-trajectories all scale. Pre-training is one axis among many.
  • Doubling continues. METR’s 7-month task-length doubling (Chapter 5) is the cleanest evidence the curve isn’t flat.
  • Gigawatt clusters arrive H2 2026. The first cluster-scale test of the “is scaling alive?” question has not yet happened — the gigawatt training runs are still ahead.

The synthesis

Both camps are partially right. Here’s the synthesis that most working researchers would sign off on as of April 2026:

Scaling axis Status Outlook H2 2026
Pre-training tokens Diminishing returns Slows further; data wall is real
Pre-training compute Still helps but sublinear Slows; cost-per-quality-unit rises
RL post-training Now rivals pre-training cost Continues scaling fast
Synthetic data pipelines Promising but model-collapse risk Standard practice; hybrid w/ human curation
Test-time compute Linear gains (so far) Continues; reasoning models commoditize
MoE sparsity Standard practice Pushes total params higher with stable serving cost
Multimodality Default in frontier models Audio + video reach parity with text

The summary: pre-training scaling has slowed, but “scaling AI” has not. The compute keeps going somewhere. It just goes into RL, reasoning, and tool-use trajectories instead of the next pre-training run.

The hybrid-future thesis

One under-appreciated forecast cuts across all the experts: the future isn’t bigger LLMs — it’s LLMs plus everything else. The hybrid stack:

Foundation LLMs
+
Classical AI
(planners, solvers)
+
Simulators
(physics, code)
+
World models
(spatial, embodied)
+
Programmatic glue

Stanford HAI explicitly notes this in the 2026 Index: the systems winning at hard tasks aren’t pure LLMs anymore — they’re LLMs orchestrating classical AI components, calling solvers, querying world models, executing programmatic loops. The “LLM as everything” aesthetic of 2023–24 has been quietly replaced by the “LLM as central conductor” aesthetic.

The risks to the forecast

Every prediction in this chapter could be wrong. Here’s the catalog of how:

Grid bottlenecks

Morgan Stanley’s 9–18 GW shortfall is real. If the grid doesn’t deliver, the gigawatt-cluster training runs that the breakthrough thesis depends on simply won’t happen on schedule. The first major buildout slip is already overdue.

Regulatory shock

An EU AI Act enforcement action that imposes meaningful fines on a frontier lab, or a US-side executive order shifting the landscape, could rewrite Q4 2026 in a single news cycle.

Cost crunch

The $720B capex vs $110B revenue gap (Chapter 4’s contrarian framing) doesn’t close in 2026. If investor patience runs out before EOY, capex curves get cut and the “breakthrough” thesis collapses on funding rather than capability.

Model collapse from synthetic data

Research warns of “model collapse” when models train heavily on synthetic data — gradual quality degradation as variance shrinks [arXiv 2503.19551]. Most labs have already moved to hybrid synthetic-plus-curated-human data, but if the next generation leans harder on synthetic alone, quality regressions could ship.

Geopolitical shock

The Huawei + DeepSeek dual-stack reality (Chapter 3) means a Taiwan / TSMC disruption hits the whole industry — and a US-China escalation could reshape export controls overnight.

The “wall” reasserting itself

If the first gigawatt-scale training run lands and the resulting model isn’t materially better than the half-gigawatt-scale predecessor, the “scaling has hit a wall” thesis wins decisively. That outcome would change every forecast in this chapter.

The contrarian read

Every prediction in this chapter could be wrong, and several probably will be. Forecaster track records on AI are mixed at best: the 2024 predictions of “AGI by 2026” from credentialed sources look silly today; the 2022 predictions of “LLMs are a dead end” look sillier. Prediction error in this domain runs both ways. Also: the experts named above have systematic biases. Stanford HAI is mostly funded by frontier labs. Morgan Stanley is selling a thesis to clients. Lab spokespeople have product launches to support. Even the most calibrated independent forecasters (Cotra, Nielsen) have been wrong before and will be wrong again. The right epistemic posture for this chapter isn’t “here’s what will happen” — it’s “here’s what people are saying, here’s how confident they are, and here’s the spread of plausible outcomes.” If the breakthrough thesis is right, H2 2026 will be one of the most consequential periods in AI history. If it’s wrong, we’ll spend a year talking about an “AI winter” that turns out to be just a slowdown. Either way, the boring answer is the safest: read the lab post-mortems quarterly, recalibrate, and don’t bet your career on any one prediction in this chapter.

Worth pulling out one more contrarian pattern. The shifting narrative on AGI from Stanford’s 2026 expert survey shows growing consensus that superintelligent AI is likely not around the corner — and, more importantly, that it may not matter that much. The conversation has quietly shifted from “when will AGI arrive?” to “what does AI being economically transformative without being ‘AGI’ look like?” If you read AI Twitter expecting a singularity in November 2026, you will be disappointed in a way that the actual researchers won’t be.

The headline forecast

Stitching everything together, here’s the calibrated forecast for December 2026: (1) GPT-6, Claude Mythos / Opus 5, Gemini 3.1 Ultra / Gemini 4, Llama 5, DeepSeek V5 all shipped within the year — Q2 is the most competitive quarter in AI model history. (2) First single-campus >1 GW AI cluster operational (Stargate Abilene most likely; Colossus 2 already there if you count the full Memphis footprint). (3) Big-5 hyperscaler AI capex ~$720B; combined OAI/Anthropic/xAI annualized revenue approaching $110B but probably falling short. (4) METR doubling continues to ~4-hour autonomous coding tasks at 50% success on private benchmarks. (5) 40% of enterprise apps embed agentic workflows; Gartner’s 40% project-cancellation prediction also tracking. (6) First major regulatory enforcement action under the EU AI Act’s GPAI rules. (7) Tesla Optimus, Figure 03, 1X NEO all in some form of constrained mass production — the “1000 demos, 0 deployments” pattern from Chapter 9 partially broken. (8) The “scaling wall” debate not resolved — H2 2026 will give us the first gigawatt-cluster data points but probably not enough to settle the question. (9) The hybrid-future thesis dominates — foundation models + classical AI + simulators + world models + programmatic glue is the agreed direction of travel. Probably-won’t-happen by EOY 2026: AGI by any defensible definition, fully autonomous 8-hour workflows in production, a frontier-lab IPO at the rumored valuations, or a politically-binding global AI treaty. Those are 2027+ questions, if they happen at all.
