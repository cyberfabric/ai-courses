Chapter 8

World Models & JEPA

LeCun’s bet, V-JEPA 2, VL-JEPA, Genie 3, NVIDIA Cosmos — predicting states of the world instead of next tokens, and what that buys you

As of April 2026, the “world model” thesis — that LLMs are not the path to general intelligence and that predicting abstract states of reality beats predicting next tokens — has gone from a Yann LeCun monologue to a $1B+ funded research program with shipped models. AMI Labs (LeCun’s post-Meta venture) closed a $1.03B seed at a $3.5B valuation on March 10, 2026, explicitly built around JEPA [MIT Tech Review, Jan 2026]. V-JEPA 2 (Meta, June 2025) was pre-trained on over 1 million hours of internet video and hit 77.3% top-1 on Something-Something v2 motion-understanding, with 65–80% pick-and-place success on novel objects when paired with a robot [Meta AI, 2025]. VL-JEPA (Dec 11, 2025, arXiv:2512.10942) at just 1.6B parameters beats CLIP, SigLIP2, and Perception Encoder on 16 video benchmarks and outperforms GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and Gemini 2.0 on WorldPrediction-WM (65.7% vs frontier-LLM scores) [arXiv:2512.10942]. DeepMind’s Genie 3 — previewed in August 2025, publicly launched January 29, 2026 — turns text prompts into 720p / 24 fps interactive worlds you can navigate in real time, with environments staying consistent for “several minutes” [DeepMind, 2026]. And NVIDIA Cosmos (Predict 2.5, Transfer 2.5, Reason 2, Cosmos Policy) is now the de-facto world-foundation-model layer for robotics, used by 1X for the NEO Gamma humanoid [NVIDIA News, 2026]. And yet: no world model has had its “ChatGPT moment.” Genie 3 is gated behind a $250/month tier. V-JEPA 2’s pick-and-place is impressive in lab demos and humbling in factories. The thesis is real; the deployment isn’t. This snapshot rots fast, the patterns last.

This is the alternative-paradigm chapter of Part III. Where Chapter 7 covered different shapes of token predictors, this chapter covers something more radical: the bet that predicting tokens is the wrong frame entirely. World models predict what the world will look like, not what the next word will be.

By the end of this chapter you’ll know what JEPA actually does, why LeCun believes LLMs are a dead end for general intelligence, what world models like Genie 3 and Cosmos already enable, and where the whole research program is still “almost ready” (it has been for years).

LeCun’s thesis, in one paragraph

Yann LeCun — Turing Award winner, ex-Chief AI Scientist at Meta — has been saying the same thing in slightly different words since 2022: large language models cannot reach human-level intelligence. They are useful tools, but they fundamentally model language about the world, not the world itself. They have no robust internal representation of physical causality, no ability to predict the consequences of actions, and no way to plan in any meaningful sense beyond imitating planning that appeared in their training data [Fenxi, 2026].

The remedy, in LeCun’s framing: build systems that predict abstract representations of the world — not pixels, not tokens, but the essence of what comes next in some compressed, learnable latent space. That’s JEPA: Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture.

Why LLMs fall short, in LeCun’s words

  • No causal model. An LLM can describe gravity but cannot predict that the ball will fall if you don’t catch it — not from first principles, only by retrieval.
  • No action-consequence loop. “If I rotate this cup 30 degrees, what does the scene look like?” is a question text-trained models answer with confidence and frequent error.
  • Generative noise. Pixel/token generation forces the model to commit to specific details — the exact shade of blue, the exact word — that aren’t actually predictable. JEPA argues you should predict the concept, not the surface.

The contrarian betting odds

In January 2026 LeCun left Meta to start AMI Labs, raising $1.03B at a $3.5B valuation in March 2026 — one of the largest seed rounds in AI history [BuildMVPFast, 2026]. The pitch: human-level AI built on world models, not LLMs. Investors are betting (literally) that he’s right; the rest of the field is mostly betting (with code and capex) that he’s wrong.

The 5-year pattern. LeCun has been declaring “LLMs are a dead end” since at least 2022. JEPA was first proposed in 2022. I-JEPA shipped in 2023. V-JEPA in 2024. V-JEPA 2 in 2025. VL-JEPA in late 2025. Each iteration is genuinely better than the last. None have produced a model that an end-user would prefer to GPT-class for any common task. The world-models thesis may yet be vindicated — and may yet be the next decade’s defining paradigm shift — but if you’re a builder making product decisions in 2026, “world models will replace LLMs” should still read as a research bet, not a near-term roadmap. As “Critiques of World Models” (arXiv:2507.05169) argues, even the definition of what counts as a “world model” is contested.

What JEPA actually does

Strip away the manifesto and JEPA is a self-supervised training scheme. The intuition is surprisingly clean.

The setup

Take an input — an image, a video clip, a paragraph. Mask part of it. Now ask the model: “predict the missing part.”

So far, this sounds like every other masked-prediction technique (BERT, MAE, etc.). The JEPA twist: the prediction is in embedding space, not pixel space.

The architecture

JEPA has two encoders:

  • Context encoder — takes the visible part of the input, produces an embedding.
  • Target encoder — takes the masked-out (true) part, produces a target embedding.

Then there’s a small predictor network that takes the context embedding and tries to predict the target embedding. The loss is the distance between predicted and actual target embeddings.

Critically, the model never tries to reconstruct the actual pixels or tokens. It only has to predict the essence of what was masked — because that’s what the embedding captures.

Why this matters

Consider video prediction. If you train a model to predict the literal next frame, it has to commit to:

  • Where exactly the leaves on the tree will move
  • What exact pattern the clouds will form
  • What specific direction the bird’s wing will be at

Most of these details are genuinely unpredictable. A pixel-prediction loss penalizes the model for getting them wrong, even though no possible model could get them right. The result: blurry, washed-out, low-fidelity predictions — the model averages across all plausible futures.

JEPA predicts in embedding space, where these unpredictable details have already been compressed away. The model only commits to what is actually predictable: the rough motion of the camera, the trajectory of the bird, the broad weather pattern. Crisper predictions, less wasted capacity.

V-JEPA 2 in more depth

V-JEPA 2 is the most production-relevant model in the family as of April 2026. Released June 11, 2025, the model:

  • Was pre-trained on over 1 million hours of internet video without any human annotation
  • Achieves 77.3% top-1 on Something-Something v2 motion understanding
  • Reports state-of-the-art on human action anticipation
  • Powers 65–80% task success on robot pick-and-place involving novel objects and settings [Robot Report, 2025]
  • Code and checkpoints are openly available for commercial use

The shift this represents is significant: a model that learned about physical dynamics by watching YouTube can transfer that knowledge to a real robot arm without ever being shown that specific arm or those specific objects.

VL-JEPA — the “lean and surprising” one

VL-JEPA (December 2025) is the result that made even some of LeCun’s critics raise eyebrows. At just 1.6B parameters, it:

  • Beats CLIP, SigLIP2, and Perception Encoder on average across 8 video classification + 8 video retrieval datasets
  • Matches classical VLMs (InstructBLIP, QwenVL) on GQA, TallyQA, POPE, POPEv2 visual question answering
  • Achieves 63.9% / 65.7% top-1 on WorldPrediction-WM (base / SFT) — beating GPT-4o, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and Gemini 2.0 at the time of submission [HuggingFace papers, 2025]
  • Uses 50% fewer trainable parameters than equivalent token-space VLMs in controlled comparison
  • Supports selective decoding that reduces decoding operations 2.85× with similar quality

The pattern: a small JEPA model can dominate large frontier LLMs on tasks that require predicting what happens next in the visual world. On generic chat? Still no contest, the LLMs win.

The JEPA family. The bet: predict in embedding space, not pixel/token space. Wins on physical reasoning, video understanding, prediction tasks, sample efficiency. Loses on open-ended generation, conversational fluency, anything that requires producing specific language. Best as a backbone for perception and planning, with an LLM as the language interface on top — not as a replacement for the LLM.

Genie 3: when the world model becomes a holodeck

If JEPA is the “abstract embedding” flavor of world model, DeepMind’s Genie 3 is the “literal pixel simulation” flavor. Both are world models in the broad sense; the trade-offs are entirely different.

What it does

Type a prompt. Genie 3 generates an interactive 3D world you can walk through in real time, at 720p and 24 fps. The environment stays consistent for several minutes — you can turn around, the wall is still where it was, the object you placed on the table is still on the table. Visual memory extends back roughly one minute [WaveSpeed AI, 2026].

That “several minutes of consistency” is the breakthrough. Earlier world models (Sora 2 for video, Genie 2 for short interactive scenes) produced impressive single shots that fell apart on extended interaction. Genie 3 is the first that persists, which is what makes it a candidate building block for embodied AI training, simulators, and games.

The release pattern

  • August 2025 — research preview, published demos and a paper
  • January 29, 2026 — public availability via Project Genie, gated behind the AI Ultra ($250/month) tier in the US for users 18+ [The Register, Jan 2026]

Why DeepMind is calling it a stepping stone to AGI

The DeepMind framing, repeated in TechCrunch coverage, is that an agent that can imagine a coherent simulated world in which to test plans is an agent that can plan. Train an embodied agent in millions of cheap simulated environments — with Genie-3-generated terrain, physics, opponents — and you get a generalist that scales without needing physical robots for every experiment.

It’s a few minutes, not infinite. Genie 3 is a real achievement, but read the spec sheet carefully. “Several minutes of consistency.” “Visual memory back to about one minute.” “720p, 24 fps.” A real game world is hours of consistent state, every interaction durable, all NPCs persistent. A real training environment for robotics is hundreds of hours of stable, physics-accurate playback. We are not there. We are at “impressive demo, gated behind a $250/month subscription, useful for narrow applications.” Watch what changes by Q4 2026 — if persistence reaches an hour, the game changes.

NVIDIA Cosmos: world models as a platform

The most quietly significant world-model story of 2025–2026 isn’t a single model — it’s NVIDIA building a platform of them and giving them away.

NVIDIA Cosmos is a family of world foundation models for “Physical AI” (NVIDIA’s preferred term for robotics + autonomous vehicles + simulation). The 2026 lineup:

ModelPurpose
Cosmos Predict 2.5Generates realistic future world states from multimodal inputs (image + text + action).
Cosmos Transfer 2.5Photoreal, physics-based simulation for synthetic-data generation. Turn one real video into thousands of variations.
Cosmos Reason 2Spatiotemporal understanding + chain-of-thought reasoning for physical scenarios.
Cosmos PolicyRobot control policy, post-trained from Cosmos Predict-2; SOTA on LIBERO and RoboCasa benchmarks [Robot Report, 2026].

Who’s using it

The customer list, per NVIDIA’s March 2026 newsroom [NVIDIA News, 2026]:

  • 1X — using Cosmos Predict + Cosmos Transfer to train the NEO Gamma humanoid robot
  • Skild AI — augmenting synthetic datasets via Cosmos Transfer
  • Neura Robotics, Virtual Incision, Waabi, XPENG, Uber — among first wave of adopters

The strategic move

Cosmos models ship under an open model license. NVIDIA is doing for world models what they did for CUDA: making the platform free, then selling the GPUs that run it. By shipping the foundation, they make every robotics company a customer.

NVIDIA Cosmos & world-model platforms. The bet: ship the foundation model layer for physical AI, capture the inference revenue. Wins on adoption (open license + free models = fast diffusion across robotics startups). Loses on differentiation (everyone gets the same baseline; the model itself stops being a moat). The pattern echoes the open-weights LLM story from Chapter 3 — foundation as commodity, deployment as the value.

JEPA vs Genie vs Cosmos: three flavors of world model

It’s easy to miss that “world model” means three quite different things in 2026. They aren’t competing for the same use cases.

FlavorWhat it predictsBest use caseExample
Embedding-space (JEPA)Abstract representations of masked contentPerception backbones, planning agents, sample-efficient learningV-JEPA 2, VL-JEPA
Pixel/scene generativeLiteral next frames of an interactive worldGame-like simulators, training environments, immersive UXGenie 3
Physical-AI platformPhysics-grounded futures, control policiesSynthetic data for robots, control policies, safety simulationNVIDIA Cosmos

A given product might use all three. A humanoid robot at a Tesla Optimus competitor might use a JEPA-style backbone for perception, Cosmos for synthetic data and simulation, and a Genie-3-style world model for high-fidelity training environments — with an LLM on top translating natural language commands into concrete plans. The architectures don’t replace each other, they layer.

The training problem nobody talks about: representation collapse

JEPA looks elegant on paper. In practice, it has a famous failure mode that took years to address: representation collapse.

If both the context encoder and the target encoder can output any embedding, the easiest way to minimize the prediction loss is to make them both output the same constant vector. Loss = 0, model learned nothing. This is the JEPA equivalent of an LLM learning to always predict “the.”

The standard fix — and it’s genuinely fiddly — is to use an exponential moving average (EMA) of the context encoder’s weights as the target encoder. The target encoder is updated slowly, the context encoder is updated by gradients, and the asymmetry prevents collapse. This is the same trick that BYOL, MoCo, and other self-supervised methods use, and JEPA inherited it.

The deeper issue: you cannot easily diagnose what a JEPA-style model has learned, because the embedding space has no human-readable semantics. A pixel reconstruction model gives you visual debugging output. An LLM gives you text. A JEPA model gives you a 1024-dimensional vector you have to probe with downstream tasks. This makes JEPA harder to debug, harder to evaluate, and harder to trust in production — all real reasons why the “LLM crowd” remains skeptical of the paradigm even when the benchmarks favor it.

The hybrid future: LLMs as the language layer

Even the most aggressive world-model proponents (LeCun included) don’t imagine throwing LLMs out. The dominant realistic 2026–2030 vision is something like:

User input
→
LLM (intent & language)
→
World model (simulate & plan)
→
Action / output

The LLM handles the parts it’s good at: parsing what the user wants, communicating back, formulating tasks. The world model handles the parts it’s good at: predicting how the world will respond to candidate actions, scoring plans against simulated outcomes, grounding the LLM’s output in physical plausibility.

You can read VL-JEPA’s positioning this way: it isn’t trying to chat with you, it’s trying to ground a chat-capable system in something that knows physics.

By end of 2026, three things look likely and one looks uncertain.

Likely:

  • At least one major frontier-LLM lab will ship a JEPA-style perception backbone as part of their multimodal stack — quietly, as an internal architectural choice rather than a product line.
  • NVIDIA Cosmos becomes the de-facto layer for robot-training synthetic data, the way CUDA became the de-facto compute API.
  • Genie 3 (or a competitor) hits the “hour of consistency” threshold, opening up the first plausible “text-to-game-world” consumer products.

Genuinely uncertain: Whether AMI Labs ships a model in 2026. LeCun’s pitch deck implies a 5–10 year horizon to human-level AI. Whether anything ships at all within 2026 is open.

Where the LLM crowd pushes back

The world-models thesis has serious critics. The “Critiques of World Models” paper and a steady drumbeat of OpenAI / Anthropic-aligned researchers raise three counter-arguments worth knowing.

1. LLMs are quietly building world models too

The mechanistic-interpretability community has shown LLMs developing internal representations of board states, geographic space, character locations, and physical attributes — all without explicit world-model training. The LLM critics argue: maybe scale + RLHF + tool use is producing world models incidentally, and the right move is to keep doing that while it works.

2. Embedding-space prediction has its own failure modes

If JEPA predicts in a learned embedding space, that embedding can have its own blind spots, biases, and shortcuts. “Predict the embedding” doesn’t magically solve grounding — it just moves where the grounding has to happen.

3. The benchmarks are still narrow

VL-JEPA beating GPT-4o on WorldPrediction-WM is striking but narrow. World models excel on their benchmarks. The day a JEPA-class model wins on a generally-respected mixed benchmark like Humanity’s Last Exam or GPQA Diamond — not just the prediction-flavored ones — the conversation will shift. We are not there.

The “ChatGPT moment” question

Every world-model proponent eventually gets asked the same question: where’s the ChatGPT moment? When does a world model become so obviously useful that a non-technical user installs it and tells their friends?

Genie 3 is the closest candidate so far — consumer-facing, gated, with a real product (Project Genie) sold to real users. But $250/month, US-only, 18+, and “a few minutes of consistency” is a long way from “everyone’s grandmother is using it.”

V-JEPA 2 and VL-JEPA are research wins that show up in robotics demos and benchmark tables. They are not yet products an end-user touches. Cosmos is platform infrastructure for robot makers, not a consumer model. None of them have produced a moment where the broader public goes “oh, that’s what world models are for.”

The honest 2026 answer: the world-model ChatGPT moment may not arrive in 2026 at all. It may arrive bundled with embodied AI — Chapter 9 — when a humanoid robot ships at scale and the world model is the silent thing that keeps it from walking into walls. Or it may take longer.

What this means for you, the builder

Practical implications for someone shipping AI products in 2026:

  • If you’re building anything with video understanding, V-JEPA 2 is now a credible open-weight backbone. It’s on GitHub, commercially licensed, and outperforms many CLIP-class models for less compute.
  • If you’re training agents, NVIDIA Cosmos for synthetic data is becoming the cheapest way to scale training environments without physical hardware.
  • If you’re building consumer experiences with simulated worlds, Genie 3 via Project Genie is the closest thing to a turn-key API — but expensive, US-only, and Ultra-tier-gated.
  • If you’re building anything chat-based, world models are not yet a substitute. Use the best frontier LLM, supplement with retrieval, and watch the JEPA space — in 12 months it may be relevant to your stack too.

Try this. Pull V-JEPA 2 from facebookresearch/vjepa2. Run it on 30 seconds of your own phone video. Ask it to anticipate the next few seconds of motion. Compare what comes out to what your favorite VLM (GPT-4o-class, Claude Opus 4.7, Gemini 3.1 Pro) does on the same clip with a “describe what happens next” prompt. The differences are illuminating — one model has watched a million hours of physical motion, the other has read the internet about it. Different failures, different strengths.

The bigger pattern

Step back and the world-model story is the same story we’ve been seeing all of Part III: the era of the “one true paradigm” is ending.

Chapter 7 said: stop assuming the Transformer is the whole architecture — hybrids beat purebreds. Chapter 8 says: stop assuming token prediction is the whole paradigm — world models beat token predictors at predicting the world, while token predictors beat world models at producing language. Chapter 9 (next) will say: stop assuming AI lives only on a server — the most dramatic 2026 development may be foundation models that walk into your kitchen.

Through all of it, a healthy mental model is: menagerie, not monoculture. JEPA, Genie, Cosmos, hybrid Transformers, diffusion text, test-time memory, on-device SSMs, and frontier LLMs will all coexist. The interesting builder skill is no longer “pick the SOTA model” — it’s knowing which kind of model is the right shape for which kind of problem.

The takeaway

World models predict the world; LLMs predict language about the world. The 2026 frontier is starting to use both, deliberately, where each is strongest. JEPA is real research with real results — and still a research bet. World models won’t replace LLMs in 2026. They will, in 2026, start to complete them.
