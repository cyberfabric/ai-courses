Chapter 3

Open vs Closed: The Race Tightens

DeepSeek, Qwen, Kimi, GLM — where the gap collapsed, where it didn’t, and what “open” actually means in 2026

As of April 2026, the open-weight frontier is genuinely competitive with closed labs on most benchmarks — and ahead on a few. DeepSeek V4-Pro shipped April 24, 2026 with 1.6T parameters (49B active), 1M-token context, on Apache 2.0, and was day-zero compatible with Huawei’s Ascend 950 chips, end-to-end Chinese stack, no Nvidia inside [Fortune, Apr 24, 2026]. GLM-5 currently leads Chatbot Arena at 1451 ELO, with Kimi K2.5 at 1447, both above every closed-lab entry [BenchLM, 2026]. Kimi K2.5 hits 99% on HumanEval and 98% on MATH-500. Google flipped Gemma 4 to Apache 2.0 on April 3, 2026 [WinBuzzer]. And yet: on SWE-Bench Pro — the private-codebase variant of the production coding benchmark — closed Claude Opus and GPT-5 still beat the strongest open models. The race tightened. It didn’t end.

Last chapter we explored reasoning models. Whether they were closed (GPT-5.4 Thinking, Opus 4.7, Gemini Deep Think) or open (DeepSeek-R1, GLM-5.1) was a footnote. This chapter is where the geography stops being a footnote.

Five Chinese labs — DeepSeek, Qwen (Alibaba), Kimi (Moonshot), GLM (Zhipu), and to a lesser extent the Western open-weight pack of Mistral and Llama — simultaneously reached frontier quality in the last 18 months. That hadn’t happened before. It’s the most important architectural and geopolitical shift in the LLM era so far.

Where the gap collapsed

For most of LLM history, open-weight models trailed closed labs by 12–18 months on capability. That gap is now measured in weeks for many tasks. Here’s where the open side has caught up — and on some benchmarks, passed — closed.

Knowledge: MMLU is essentially over

MMLU (massive multitask language understanding) was the dominant knowledge benchmark for years. Open weights now cluster at 88–94% alongside every closed flagship. Kimi K2.5 sits at 92.0% — tied with the strongest closed labs [Let’s Data Science, 2026]. The benchmark is saturated; the gap is statistical noise.

Graduate science: GPQA Diamond

Kimi K2.5 reaches 87.6% on GPQA Diamond [OpenLM.ai] — within striking distance of the closed flagship cluster (94.1–94.4%). A few percentage points still separate them, but the trajectory is clear.

Human preference: Chatbot Arena

This is the most striking number in the chapter. The open-weight ELO gap on Chatbot Arena was 8.04% in January 2024 and is now 1.70% as of February 2025 [BenchLM]. GLM-5 currently sits at 1451 ELO — the highest of any model on the leaderboard, including all closed entries. Kimi K2.5 at 1447 is statistically indistinguishable.

Read that twice. The model that humans prefer most in blind A/B comparisons in April 2026 is open-weight, Chinese, and Apache-2.0-adjacent. That fact would have been impossible to predict from where the field was 24 months ago.

Where closed still leads

The leaderboard tells a triumphalist open-weight story. Real production work tells a different one. Here’s where closed labs still hold a meaningful lead.

SWE-Bench Pro: private-codebase coding

SWE-Bench Verified (the public coding benchmark) has been getting clobbered by everyone — GLM-5 at 77.8%, Kimi K2.5 at 76.8%, GLM-4.7 at 73.8% [AkitaOnRails, Apr 24, 2026]. Closed-side leaders sit at 83.5–87.6%. A real but narrow gap.

Move to SWE-Bench Pro — the private-codebase variant where the model has not seen the test cases — and the picture changes.

Source: CodeAnt SWE-Bench Scores 2026. Two takeaways:

  • Even closed leaders drop dramatically when moving from public to private codebases. SWE-Bench Verified at 80%+ collapses to under 25% on Pro’s held-out subset. The eval/production gap from Chapter 1 is real and measurable.
  • The strongest open models are not yet reporting numbers on this benchmark at all — which is its own data point. When the public benchmark is saturated, vendors enthusiastically report; when the private benchmark exists, the silence is loud.

Long-horizon agentic work

Closed labs — especially Anthropic — still hold a meaningful lead on multi-step agentic benchmarks like Terminal-Bench Hard, τ²-Bench, and MCP-Atlas (Opus 4.7 at 77.3%). Open-weight models exist that handle agentic loops, but production-grade reliability over 5+ hour autonomous tasks is still rare in the open camp.

Latency at scale

Hosted closed APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) have spent years optimizing inference infrastructure. Self-hosting an open-weight 1.6T MoE model at production latency is a serious engineering project. The gap is operational, not capability-driven, but it’s real.

The Huawei + DeepSeek strategic shift

Until April 2026, every frontier LLM in the world was trained on Nvidia hardware. Then DeepSeek V4 shipped and the assumption broke.

End-to-end Chinese stack

DeepSeek V4 is optimized for Huawei Ascend 950PR and 950DT processors, with day-zero compatibility achieved on release [Fortune, Apr 24, 2026]. The supporting software is Huawei’s CANN (Compute Architecture for Neural Networks) — functionally equivalent to Nvidia’s CUDA. Together, that’s a fully functional, production-grade AI stack with zero American software dependencies [OguneSecurity, 2026].

The market response was immediate. Alibaba, ByteDance, and Tencent placed bulk orders for Ascend 950PR chips — hundreds of thousands of units — and the chip price rose 20% in the weeks before the release [Reuters via Investing.com].

Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang reportedly described the shift as “a disaster” for US chip dominance [36kr EU, 2026].

What this changes

Three durable consequences, separate from any one product launch:

  • Export controls have a workaround. US chip export restrictions to China assumed Nvidia was the only viable training stack. That assumption is now wrong. The control regime needs a redesign.
  • Frontier compute is no longer monolithic. Two parallel hardware ecosystems — Nvidia/CUDA and Huawei/CANN — now coexist at the frontier. Vendor lock-in just became a real choice, not a default.
  • Open weights gained strategic weight. A Chinese open-weight model on Chinese hardware is durably independent of US policy in a way that a closed-API frontier model never can be. For sovereign customers (governments, regulated industries), that’s a structural advantage.

We’ll dig into the compute-and-energy story in the next chapter. The geopolitical fracture is one half of why compute became the binding constraint of 2026.

The licensing landscape (and what “open” actually means)

“Open weights” sounds like a uniform thing. It isn’t. There’s a real spectrum from genuinely permissive to open in name, restricted in practice.

License Used by Commercial use Redistribution Notable restrictions
Apache 2.0 DeepSeek V4, Gemma 4, Mistral (some) ✅ Unrestricted ✅ Yes, including derivatives None — explicit patent grant
Llama Community Llama 3, Llama 4 ✅ below 700M MAU ✅ With attribution Commercial license required above 700M monthly active users
Qwen / Kimi / GLM custom Alibaba, Moonshot, Zhipu ⚠️ With conditions ⚠️ With conditions Restrictions on certain commercial deployments, fine-tuning for competing services, and acceptable use
Closed (API only) OpenAI, Anthropic, xAI, Google flagships ✅ via API only ❌ No weights released Subject to API terms; can be revoked

License sources: Logan Thorneloe’s license breakdown, MindStudio on Gemma 4 Apache 2.0, CallSphere on open-weight enterprise use.

Read the actual license before committing. “Open weights” on a model card does not mean Apache 2.0. Some custom community licenses prohibit using the model to train competing models, prohibit certain redistributions, or require running through approved channels. If you’re building anything commercial on top of an open-weight model, treat the license like you would a commercial vendor contract.

Open weights ≠ open source

“Open weights” means you can run the model. It does not mean you can audit how it was made. The training data is closed, the post-training recipe (RLHF data, preference rewards, safety filters) is closed, the architectural ablations and hyperparameter searches are closed. You can fine-tune the released weights, but you cannot reproduce the model from scratch — not even close. Custom community licenses also restrict transparency around fine-tuning pipelines and instruction-tuning methods. In other words: open-weight models give you reproducibility of inference, not reproducibility of training. The audit story remains incomplete.

This matters for at least three audiences:

  • Regulators. The EU AI Act’s general-purpose-AI rules require documentation of training data sources. An “open-weight” release that doesn’t come with a training data card doesn’t satisfy that requirement.
  • Security researchers. You cannot meaningfully audit for backdoors, data poisoning, or biased instruction tuning without seeing the post-training pipeline. The weights tell you what the model does; they don’t tell you why.
  • Enterprise legal teams. If a model was trained on copyrighted data, that risk transfers to anyone who deploys the weights commercially. Open weights distribute the model. They don’t distribute the indemnification.

There’s a second contrarian thread worth flagging. Many of the top open-weight models from Chinese labs achieved frontier MMLU/GPQA scores suspiciously soon after each new closed-lab release. The strongly-suspected-but-rarely-admitted answer is distillation from closed-model outputs — using GPT-class models as “teachers” for synthetic training data. If true, it means the open frontier is downstream of the closed frontier in a deep way. The capability lead in raw research still belongs to whoever has the strongest closed model that month.

Why did the gap close so fast?

If you’d been told in 2023 that by 2026 a Chinese open-weight model on Chinese hardware would lead Chatbot Arena and ship Apache 2.0, you’d have called it implausible. Five things changed:

  • Distillation got really good. Synthetic data generated by frontier closed models, filtered for quality, became a viable training signal. You didn’t need original data scale; you needed teachers.
  • MoE architectures changed the economics. Activating 49B of 1.6T parameters per token means inference cost roughly tracks the active count, not the total. Open-weight labs could ship enormous models without Nvidia-scale inference budgets.
  • Chinese national investment compounded. Strategic investment in compute, recruiting, and infrastructure paid off in unison. Five labs, not one, hit the frontier together.
  • Curated datasets beat raw scale. Smaller, higher-quality, instruction-tuned datasets outperformed the “more tokens” strategy. Some labs trained frontier-level models on under 5T tokens by being ruthless about data quality.
  • Open eval and rapid iteration. The open community ships, evaluates, ships again on weekly cadence. Closed labs ship quarterly. The compounding rate is faster on the open side.

Practical advice: when to choose which

Choose open-weight when:

  • Privacy / data residency matters. Self-hosting means no data leaves your infrastructure.
  • Sovereignty / regulatory. Government, defense, regulated industries that can’t depend on a foreign API.
  • Fine-tuning is core to your moat — closed APIs limit how deeply you can customize.
  • Cost at scale. Above ~10M queries/day, self-hosted MoE inference often beats API pricing.
  • Auditability/control at the inference level — you control the binary that runs.
  • Long-term independence from any single vendor’s pricing, deprecation, or policy changes.

Choose closed (API) when:

  • Best-in-class agentic tasks. Long-horizon, multi-tool, production-reliability work still favors closed.
  • Operational simplicity. No GPU management, no inference infra, no model updates.
  • Latency SLAs. Hosted APIs have deeper inference optimization than most teams can match.
  • Lower volume. Below ~1M queries/day, API economics typically beat self-hosting.
  • Bleeding edge. The very newest models still ship closed-first.
  • Compliance / indemnification. Closed vendors offer commercial indemnification that open licenses don’t.

Most serious teams are now multi-model by default. A closed flagship for the hardest tasks, an open-weight model for high-volume and privacy-sensitive ones, and a router in between. The right answer is rarely “commit to one side.”

Looking forward

By Q4 2026, expect the open-weight side to credibly close the SWE-Bench Pro gap on private codebases (we’ll see the first open model in the 65–70% band). Expect at least one major Chinese lab to ship a model that reaches the closed-side agentic frontier (Terminal-Bench Hard, τ²-Bench) within 5 percentage points. Expect Apache 2.0 to become the default open-weights license — the custom-community-license fashion of 2024 is fading fast (Gemma 4’s flip on April 3, 2026 is the canary). Expect at least one major Western lab to release a frontier-class open-weight model partly in response to the Chinese momentum — the strategic incentive to not be visibly behind on openness is now too high. And expect the Huawei stack to be deployed at gigawatt scale by at least one Chinese hyperscaler before December.

What might NOT happen by Q4: a fully open-source release (weights + training data + pipeline + post-training recipe) from any frontier lab. That’s still a 2027+ question, if it happens at all.

What rots vs what lasts

Rots fast in this chapter:
  • The specific model versions (DeepSeek V4-Pro, Kimi K2.5, GLM-5.1, Qwen 3.5) — expect quarterly bumps from each lab.
  • The Chatbot Arena ELO numbers (1451, 1447) — this leaderboard reorders weekly.
  • The exact MMLU/GPQA/MATH scores — saturated benchmarks will be replaced.
  • The Huawei chip generation (Ascend 950PR / 950DT) — the next generation is already taped out.
Probably won’t rot:
  • The five-Chinese-lab structure of the open-weight frontier.
  • The Apache 2.0 vs custom-community-license divide as the meaningful licensing question.
  • The “open weights ≠ open source” distinction.
  • The dual-stack reality (Nvidia/CUDA + Huawei/CANN) for at least the next several years.
  • The pattern of distillation making the open frontier downstream of the closed frontier on raw research progress.

The five-second summary

Open vs closed in one breath

The open-weight side — mostly Chinese, mostly MoE, mostly running on either Apache 2.0 or custom community licenses — is now genuinely competitive on knowledge, math, and human preference, and ahead on Chatbot Arena. The closed side still leads on production coding (especially private codebases) and long-horizon agentic work. The Huawei + DeepSeek combination broke the assumption that frontier AI requires Nvidia silicon. “Open” remains a spectrum, not a binary — weights are released, but training data, pipelines, and post-training recipes stay closed. Most serious teams now run multi-model. The race tightened. It didn’t end.
