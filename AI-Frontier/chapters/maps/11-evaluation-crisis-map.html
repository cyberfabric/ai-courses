Chapter 11

The Evaluation Crisis

Memory Map
📏

The Big Idea

By April 2026, the public benchmarks the field built its leaderboards on are saturated, contaminated, and Goodharted. Lab numbers diverge from production performance by tens of points. The right response is not to abandon evaluation — it’s to own a held-out private eval set, run behavioral and calibration tests, and pair automated evals with structured human review. This snapshot rots fast, the patterns last.

🚨 The Five Failure Modes

📈
1. Saturation
Top six models bunched within 1–3 points on MMLU/GPQA. No headroom = no signal. Anything older than two years has saturated.
🧪
2. Contamination
Test data in training corpora. Overlap rates 1–45%. Decontaminating GSM8K drops some scores 13 points.
🎯
3. Goodhart’s Law
When a leaderboard becomes a target, it stops measuring capability. Labs train against the eval, then post the score.
🛡️
4. Safetywashing
Safety benchmarks correlate with raw capability. Bigger model wins the “safety” eval without being safer.
🌉
5. Deployment Gap
Lab 92% ↔ production 50–70%. Long tail, real users, real tools — all absent from the lab number.
🪞
Bonus: Eval-incentive trap
From Ch 10: 9 of 10 major benchmarks reward confident-wrong over honest abstention. Compounds with Goodhart.

🛠️ The Real-Evaluation Toolkit

  1. Held-out private eval set

    50–500 items from your real distribution. Hash it. Never publish. Run on every model release.

  2. Time-bounded tests

    Use benchmarks released after the model’s training cut-off. Date-overlap = score-suspect.

  3. Behavioral tests

    Score abstention, citation, format preservation, refusal — not just aggregate accuracy.

  4. Red-teaming

    Adversarial multi-turn dialogues by skilled attackers. Rolling campaigns, not one-off pre-launch reviews.

  5. Calibration tests

    Reliability diagrams, Brier score, ECE. A miscalibrated 90% model is more dangerous than a calibrated 80%.

  6. Production telemetry

    Logs are the ultimate eval. Sample failures, label them, feed back into the eval set.

200 items

Why it works: humans aren’t Goodharted. They notice fluent confabulation that benchmarks reward. Cost: ~100 person-hours per cycle — cheap for a model purchase decision, expensive for CI. Right architecture: hybrid — automated for the common case, human for the high-stakes go/no-go.

📚 Key Terms

Saturation
When top models score within the eval’s noise floor of each other — no headroom to compare.
Contamination
Test data (or close paraphrases) appearing in training data. Overlap rates 1–45% in 2026 frontier models.
Indirect contamination
Open-weight model inheriting memorization from a closed teacher model via distillation, even with no direct test-set leakage.
Goodhart’s law
“When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.” Applies to every benchmark labs care about.
Safetywashing
(Hendrycks et al.) Safety benchmarks correlated so heavily with capability that scoring well measures size, not safety.
Held-out private eval set
50–500 labeled items from your real distribution, never published. The only number you can trust for your domain.
Calibration
How well the model’s confidence matches its accuracy. A 90% accurate model with bad calibration is worse than 80% with good calibration.
Vibes-eval
Structured human evaluation panels (200 items, 3+ reviewers, multi-axis rubric). Often outperforms automated benchmarks at predicting deployment outcomes.
IAISR 2026
International AI Safety Report 2026 — Bengio + 100 experts, 30 countries, Feb 2026. Most authoritative single document on field-wide risk-management.

🧠 Quick Quiz

Why is “92.7% on MMLU” close to noise in 2026?

Saturation: top six models within 1–3 points of each other and of the ceiling, inside the eval’s own noise floor. Plus probable contamination. The number doesn’t separate models.

What three questions should you ask about any benchmark claim?

(1) When was the benchmark released? (2) Is the test split publicly available? (3) Has the model been retrained since? “Old, public, yes” = treat as marketing.

Why did GSM8K scores drop 13 points after decontamination?

Those points were memorized test items, not learned reasoning. Decontamination removes the test items from the eval (or paraphrases them), and the “capability” that depended on memorization disappears.

What is safetywashing?

Safety benchmarks that correlate so heavily with raw capability that bigger models score better without being meaningfully safer. The metric measures size in a safety jersey.

Why does vibes-eval often beat automated benchmarks at predicting deployment?

Humans aren’t Goodharted. They notice fluent confabulation, hidden uncertainty, and stylistic tricks that benchmarks reward. With structure (rubric, multiple reviewers, IRR tracking) it’s rigorous, not vibes in the dismissive sense.

Key Takeaways

  • Public benchmarks in 2026 are saturated, contaminated, and Goodharted — the headline number tells you less than ever.
  • Anything older than two years (MMLU, HellaSwag, MATH-500) is mostly saturated; new benchmarks contaminate within months.
  • Goodhart’s law + binary-grading trap: labs train against the leaderboard, the leaderboard rewards confident-wrong, and confidence-while-wrong is what gets shipped.
  • Safetywashing means many “safety scores” mostly measure raw capability — one number is never enough for a deployment go/no-go.
  • Lab numbers and production numbers diverge by tens of percentage points. Long tail, real prompts, real tools — absent from lab evals.
  • Live benchmarks (LMSys, LM Council, Artificial Analysis, METR) help but don’t fix Goodhart — Arena ELO is itself optimized for.
  • Real evaluation = held-out private eval set + behavioral tests + calibration + red-teaming + production telemetry. No single number.
  • Vibes-eval done right (200 items, 3 reviewers, multi-axis rubric) often beats automated benchmarks at predicting deployment outcomes.
  • IAISR 2026 (Bengio, 100 experts, Feb 2026) is the field’s most honest acknowledgment that evaluation has not kept pace with capability.
  • This snapshot rots fast, the patterns last.
