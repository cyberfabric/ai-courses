Chapter 13

Reading the Frontier Without the Hype

Memory Map
🧭

The Big Idea

By the time you read this, half the version numbers in this course will be wrong. The framework for staying current is more durable than any specific source list. Triangulate across independent sources, discount lab marketing, wait for reproduction, and invest in taste, evaluation skill, and skepticism muscle — the parts that age well. This snapshot rots fast, the patterns last.

Triangulate across at least three. Claims that survive triangulation have a chance of being real.

🏛️ Credibility Hierarchy (Trust → Skepticism)

  1. Independent third-party evaluations

    METR, Arena, Artificial Analysis, LM Council, IAISR 2026 panel. No financial stake in any single model winning.

  2. Reproduced research

    Independently re-run by a separate group, ideally with code. Rare; gold when found.

  3. Original preprints with code & data

    Minimum viable transparency. Read carefully.

  4. Lab post-mortems & technical reports

    Honest accounts of what didn’t work. The most undervalued primary source in 2026.

  5. Independent journalism

    The Information, WSJ tech, FT AI desk. Editors and named sources.

  6. Curated newsletters with editorial independence

    The Batch, Import AI, Turing Post, AI Snake Oil.

  7. Lab marketing

    Reliable for “a thing was launched.” Not for relative ranking.

  8. Mainstream press headlines

    Often a generation behind on technical claims.

  9. Social media

    Useful for early signals; never the basis for a decision.

🚫 The Five Demo-Trap Heuristics

📅
Announcement ≠ availability
“Coming soon” can mean weeks or quarters. Wait for general access.
🎬
Demo ≠ product
Demos are marketing. Production behavior is the deployment gap (Ch 11).
🔢
Headline number ≠ comparable
Different prompt, different split, different scoring rules.
🛡️
Capability claim ≠ safety claim
“Solves X” tells you nothing about adjacent failure modes.
🐦
Researcher tweet ≠ lab position
Personal forecasts ≠ lab-endorsed roadmap.

📚 Key Terms

Triangulation
Pulling a claim from at least three source layers and noting what they agree (and disagree) on.
Credibility hierarchy
Ordering of source types from most to least trustworthy for capability/safety claims about frontier models.
Reproducibility floor
The empirical state of code/data sharing in AI research: ~5% share code, <33% share test data.
AI snake oil
(Narayanan & Kapoor) AI that doesn’t work as advertised and probably can’t. The default skepticism frame.
Demo trap
Inferring product behavior from demo videos. Demos are cherry-picked; production is the long tail.
Identity-protective updating
Subconsciously discounting evidence against your stated AI-optimist or AI-skeptic position.
METR Time Horizon
METR’s metric for the task length a model completes at 50% success. Doubling = 130.8 days post-2023 (Jan 2026 estimate).
AAII Index
Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index v3 — aggregate of 10 challenging evals, with cost/quality/latency dashboards.
Lab post-mortem
Public account of what didn’t work. Highest signal-to-noise in the AI information ecosystem; usually skipped.

🧠 Quick Quiz

What’s the first question to ask about a benchmark headline?

When was the benchmark released? If it predates the model’s training cut-off by more than a year, assume contamination affects the score.

Why are lab post-mortems undervalued?

They’re written after the marketing cycle, contain numbers labs would never show during a launch (cost burn, failure rates), and reveal structural reasons something didn’t work. Most readers skip them for the next launch.

What’s the difference between marketing, research, and roadmap signaling from a lab?

Marketing = “a thing exists” statement, ignore relative claims. Research = method & limitations, not headline numbers. Roadmap signaling = what they’re telling investors and competitors, not next quarter’s ship list.

What does METR’s Time Horizon 1.1 say about post-2023 progress?

Doubling time of agent task length is 130.8 days (≈4.3 months) — faster than their previous estimate. Note: a curve fit, not a forecast; the y-axis is 50%-success, which is a research bar not a deployment bar.

Which skills age well in this turbulent period?

Taste, evaluation design, system design with imperfect components, cost/latency/quality intuition, statistical literacy, domain expertise, architecture-pattern fluency, skepticism muscle. NOT specific prompt tricks, benchmark scores, or framework APIs.

What’s the contrarian point of this chapter?

Even the “trustworthy” sources rot. Eval orgs can be captured; newsletters get acquired; safety reports get gamed. The skill being taught is not which sources to trust — it’s how to keep deciding which sources to trust.

Key Takeaways

  • The frontier moves fast; the framework for staying current is more durable than any specific source list.
  • Triangulate across at least three of the four source layers (lab marketing, mainstream press, social media, research preprints).
  • Independent evals (METR, Arena, Artificial Analysis, LM Council, IAISR 2026) are the closest thing to ground truth.
  • Reproducibility floor: ~5% of AI papers share code; <33% share test data. Treat any unreproduced claim as preliminary.
  • Subscribe to 3–5 newsletters across different lenses; pair a daily triage feed with depth (The Batch, Import AI, AI Snake Oil).
  • Run every benchmark headline through the skepticism filter: release date, public split, prompt template, reproduction, variance, cost, independent confirmation.
  • Lab post-mortems are gold — read them when they happen. The signal-to-noise is the highest in the entire ecosystem.
  • Update beliefs proportionally: 1 demo = preliminary; multi-lab reproduction = strong; production data = strongest.
  • Invest in skills that age well: taste, evaluation design, system design with imperfect components, cost/latency/quality intuition, skepticism muscle.
  • Even the “trustworthy” sources rot — the durable skill is how to keep deciding which sources to trust, not which list to memorize.
  • This snapshot rots fast. The patterns last. Build accordingly.
