The Big Idea

Q2 2026 is the most competitive quarter in AI model history (GPT-6, Claude Mythos, Gemini 3.1 Ultra all in the same eight-week window). Pre-training scaling has slowed; RL, test-time compute, and synthetic data are still scaling. The frontier is jagged — superhuman on IMO problems, 50% on analog clocks. Every prediction has plausible failure modes. Read forecasts as spreads, not facts.

📜 Key Terms

Jagged frontier
The pattern where a model wins gold at IMO and reads analog clocks at 50.1%. Capability is uneven across task types.
Intelligence Factory model
Morgan Stanley’s framework for forecasting AI capability against compute, energy, and capital availability.
Scaling wall
The thesis that pre-training has hit diminishing returns and that bigger models on the same recipe stop helping. Half-true.
Data wall
~10–50T tokens of high-quality human text exist; Chinchilla-optimal training for 1T-param models needs ~20T tokens. Largest models now need more unique data than exists.
Model collapse
Quality degradation when models train heavily on their own synthetic outputs — variance shrinks, diversity erodes.
Test-time compute
Spending more inference cycles per query (reasoning models). A scaling axis that’s still working.
Hybrid future
LLMs as central conductor for classical AI, simulators, world models, and programmatic loops — not LLMs alone.
15-15-15 dynamic
Morgan Stanley’s shorthand for hyperscaler economics: 15-year leases at 15% yields, $15/watt of net value created.

🧪 Quick Quiz

What does Stanford HAI say about “scaling has hit a wall”?

The data doesn’t support it. Capability is still accelerating: SWE1-bench Verified went 60→100% of human baseline in a year, with +30 percentage points on Humanity’s Last Exam. The narrative is wrong; the picture is uneven, not flat.

Why is Q2 2026 considered the most competitive quarter in AI model history?

GPT-6, Claude Mythos, and Gemini 3.1 Ultra are all expected to ship within roughly the same eight-week window. That kind of frontier-lab convergence has never happened before.

What is the “jagged frontier”?

The pattern where the same frontier model is superhuman on some tasks (IMO gold, PhD-level science) and sub-toddler on others (analog clocks at 50.1%). Capability isn’t a single dimension; benchmark numbers don’t generalize.

How is “scaling AI” different from “scaling pre-training”?

Pre-training scaling has slowed (data wall, diminishing returns). But RL post-training, test-time compute, synthetic data, MoE sparsity, and multimodality are all still scaling. Compute keeps going somewhere.

What’s the calibrated way to read this chapter?

As spreads of plausible outcomes, not as facts. Every forecaster has biases. Every prediction has plausible failure modes. The honest version is: “here’s what people are saying and how confident they are,” not “here’s what will happen.”
