Chapter 9

Fine-Tuning: LoRA & QLoRA

Making AI models your own — when generic isn't enough, but retraining from scratch is insane

The One-Sentence Version

Fine-tuning takes a pre-trained model and trains it further on your specific data so it learns your style, your format, or your domain expertise.

In Chapter 4, you learned RAG — giving the model access to your data at query time. RAG is about what the model sees. Fine-tuning is about how the model behaves. They solve different problems, and knowing when to use which (or both) is one of the most important decisions in AI development.

Most developers will never need to fine-tune a model. RAG and prompt engineering handle the majority of use cases. But when they don't — when you need the model to consistently follow a specific format, use domain-specific language correctly, or behave in a way that no amount of prompting achieves — fine-tuning is the answer. And thanks to LoRA and QLoRA, it's gone from "you need a GPU cluster" to "you can do this on a single GPU over lunch."

Why Fine-Tune?

General-purpose models like GPT-4 or Llama are trained to be helpful across all topics. But sometimes you need a specialist, not a generalist. Here are the signs that prompt engineering and RAG aren't enough:

  • Consistent format: You need every response in a specific JSON schema, a particular report template, or a defined structure. Prompting gets you 90% there; fine-tuning gets you 99%.
  • Domain language: Medical, legal, or technical terminology that the model gets subtly wrong. Fine-tuning teaches it the correct usage.
  • Brand voice: Every response should sound like your company — not generic, not ChatGPT-ish. Fine-tuning on examples of your actual writing style works remarkably well.
  • Specific behavior: "Always respond in bullet points," "never use markdown," "always include a confidence score." When system prompts are unreliable, fine-tuning makes the behavior default.
  • Smaller, faster model: A fine-tuned 7B model that's specialized for your task can match a general 70B model on that specific task, at 10x the speed and 1/10th the cost.

The Old Way vs The New Way

Full Fine-Tuning (The Old Way)

Traditional fine-tuning updates every weight in the model. For a 7B parameter model, that means modifying 7 billion numbers. This requires:

  • Multiple high-end GPUs (80+ GB VRAM total)
  • Days of training time
  • Hundreds or thousands of dollars in cloud GPU costs
  • A complete copy of the model for each fine-tune (14+ GB per version)

This is still how frontier models are trained, but for most developers it's impractical and unnecessary.

LoRA: The Breakthrough

Lo
LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)
Instead of modifying all 7 billion weights, LoRA adds tiny "adapter" matrices alongside the frozen model. Only the adapters are trained — typically 0.1-1% of the model's parameters.

The insight is elegant: most of the knowledge in a pre-trained model is already useful. You don't need to change the whole brain — you just need to add a small, specialized module that adjusts the model's behavior for your use case.

Think of it like this: the base model is a fully trained chef. LoRA doesn't retrain the chef from scratch. It gives them a recipe card with your specific preferences — "less salt, always use fresh herbs, plate it this way." The chef's core skills are unchanged; they just adapt their output to your taste.

0.1-1% Parameters trained (vs 100% for full fine-tuning)
10-100 MB Adapter size (vs 14+ GB for full model)
1 GPU Minimum hardware (16 GB VRAM for 7B models)

QLoRA: Even More Accessible

QL
QLoRA (Quantized LoRA)
Combines LoRA with quantization: the base model is loaded in 4-bit precision (75% less memory), while the LoRA adapters are trained in full precision. Same quality, much less VRAM.

QLoRA is what made fine-tuning truly accessible to individual developers. With QLoRA, you can fine-tune a 7B model on a single consumer GPU with 16 GB VRAM. You can even fine-tune 13B or 30B models on a 24 GB GPU. Before QLoRA, this was unthinkable.

Method VRAM for 7B Model VRAM for 13B Model Quality
Full Fine-Tuning ~60 GB ~100+ GB Best (but often overkill)
LoRA ~16 GB ~28 GB Excellent (very close to full)
QLoRA ~8 GB ~16 GB Very good (slight trade-off)
For most developers, QLoRA is the right choice. The quality difference between QLoRA and full LoRA is negligible for most tasks, and the memory savings are massive. Start with QLoRA; only move to full LoRA if you can measure a quality gap.

The Fine-Tuning Workflow

Prepare Data → Choose Base Model → Configure LoRA → Train → Merge & Test

Step 1: Prepare Your Data (The Hard Part)

This is where most fine-tuning projects succeed or fail. You need examples of the behavior you want — input/output pairs in the right format:

# Training data format (JSON Lines)
{"messages": [
    {"role": "system", "content": "You are a medical documentation assistant."},
    {"role": "user", "content": "Patient presents with persistent cough for 3 weeks."},
    {"role": "assistant", "content": "Assessment: Chronic cough (>3 weeks duration).\nDifferential: Post-nasal drip, asthma, GERD, ACE inhibitor side effect.\nRecommended workup: Chest X-ray, spirometry, trial of PPI."}
]}

You typically need 100-1000 high-quality examples. The emphasis is on quality — 200 perfect examples beat 5000 sloppy ones. Each example should demonstrate exactly the behavior you want the model to learn.

Data preparation is genuinely 70-80% of the work in fine-tuning. Collecting, cleaning, and formatting examples takes far longer than the actual training. Budget your time accordingly. If you don't have good training data, fine-tuning won't help — garbage in, garbage out.

Step 2: Choose a Base Model

Pick a model from HuggingFace (Chapter 2) that's closest to what you need. Common choices:

  • Llama 3.1 8B Instruct — great all-rounder for most fine-tuning tasks
  • Mistral 7B Instruct — strong at following instructions, Apache 2.0 license
  • Phi-3 Mini — when you want a tiny, fast model for a focused task
  • CodeLlama — if your task is code-related

Always start with an Instruct model (not Base). Instruct models already know how to follow instructions and have conversations. You're teaching them your specific style, not starting from zero.

Step 3: Configure and Train

Here's what the training code looks like with the popular trl and peft libraries:

from peft import LoraConfig
from trl import SFTTrainer
from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, AutoTokenizer, BitsAndBytesConfig

# Load model in 4-bit (QLoRA)
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained("meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct",
    quantization_config=BitsAndBytesConfig(load_in_4bit=True))

# Configure LoRA — these are the tiny adapter layers
lora_config = LoraConfig(r=16, lora_alpha=32, target_modules=["q_proj", "v_proj"],
                          lora_dropout=0.05, task_type="CAUSAL_LM")

# Train
trainer = SFTTrainer(model=model, train_dataset=dataset,
                      peft_config=lora_config, max_seq_length=512)
trainer.train()  # Takes 30 min to a few hours depending on data size
trainer.save_model("my-custom-adapter")

The output is a small adapter file (10-100 MB) that you can load on top of the base model at inference time. You don't modify the original model at all — you just add your adapter.

Step 4: Merge and Test

After training, you can either:

  • Load the adapter at runtime — keep the base model and adapter separate. Useful when you have multiple adapters for different tasks.
  • Merge the adapter into the base model — create a single model file. Simpler to deploy, slightly faster inference.

Then test extensively. Fine-tuned models can be great at what you trained them on but worse at everything else (this is called catastrophic forgetting). Make sure your model still handles general tasks acceptably.

RAG vs Fine-Tuning: The Decision Tree

This comes up so often that it deserves a clear framework. We touched on this in Chapter 4; here's the detailed version:

Use RAG When

• The model needs ACCESS to data it doesn't have • Your data changes frequently • You need to cite sources • The answer exists in your documents • "What does our API do?" → RAG finds the docs • Setup: hours, no GPU needed

Use Fine-Tuning When

• The model needs to BEHAVE differently • You need consistent format/style/tone • Domain-specific language or terminology • Making a smaller model match a larger one on specific tasks • "Always respond like our brand" → fine-tune • Setup: days, GPU needed

The Decision Flowchart

"Does the model know the answer but says it wrong?" → Fine-tune (it's a behavior problem)

"Does the model not know the answer at all?" → RAG (it's a knowledge problem)

"Both?" → Use both. Fine-tune for behavior + RAG for knowledge. This is the most powerful combination.

Common Fine-Tuning Use Cases

Use Case What You're Teaching Training Data Examples
Structured output Always respond in a specific JSON format 200+ input/output pairs in your schema
Brand voice Match your company's writing style 500+ examples of your actual content
Domain expertise Medical, legal, or technical terminology 1000+ domain-specific Q&A pairs
Code style Follow your team's coding conventions 500+ code samples in your style
Classification Categorize inputs consistently 500+ labeled examples

Platforms and Tools

You don't have to set up everything from scratch. Here are the main options:

HF
HuggingFace + PEFT/TRL
The open-source standard. Full control, free (you provide the GPU). Best for developers who want to understand every step.
AX
Axolotl
A wrapper that simplifies fine-tuning configuration. Define everything in a YAML file instead of writing Python. Great for quick iteration.
OA
OpenAI Fine-Tuning API
Upload your data, click a button, get a fine-tuned GPT model. No GPU needed but costs money and you're locked to OpenAI. Simplest option.
TG
Together AI / Anyscale
Cloud platforms for fine-tuning open-source models. You get the convenience of a managed service with the flexibility of open models.

Cost and Time: What to Expect

Approach Hardware Time Cost
QLoRA on your GPU 1x GPU (16+ GB VRAM) 1-4 hours Free (electricity only)
QLoRA on cloud GPU 1x A100 or similar 1-4 hours $2-15
OpenAI fine-tuning None (managed) Minutes to hours $5-50+ (depends on data size)
Full fine-tuning (7B) 4x A100 GPUs Hours to days $50-500+

The numbers that surprise most people: QLoRA fine-tuning of a 7B model on 500 examples takes about 1-2 hours on a single GPU and costs under $10 on cloud. That's not a typo. Fine-tuning went from "enterprise only" to "side project budget" in the span of two years.

Connection to Android Development

Fine-tuned models connect to Android in two ways:

  • Backend specialization: Fine-tune a model for your app's domain (e.g., cooking, fitness, legal advice). Run it on your backend. Your Android app calls the same API as always, but the responses are better-tailored to your users because the model has been fine-tuned for your specific use case.
  • On-device models: Fine-tune a small model (1-3B) with LoRA, merge the adapter, quantize the result, and deploy it on-device. This is how specialized on-device AI features work — a small model that's been fine-tuned to be excellent at one specific task (text classification, entity extraction, intent detection).

The on-device path is particularly exciting for Android. A fine-tuned 1B model that does one thing really well can run in real-time on modern phones and provide AI features without any network connection.

  • You need consistent output format/style that prompting can't reliably achieve
  • Domain-specific language or terminology that the model gets wrong
  • You want a smaller, cheaper model that matches a larger one on your specific task
  • You have high-quality training data (100+ examples minimum)
  • Your use case is well-defined and repeatable
  • Your problem is knowledge access, not behavior — use RAG instead
  • Prompt engineering hasn't been fully explored yet — try that first
  • You don't have good training data (and can't create it)
  • Your needs change frequently — fine-tuning is a snapshot; RAG is live
  • A cloud API already gives you good enough results
RAG, Fine-Tuning, or Both?

For each scenario below, decide whether you'd use RAG, fine-tuning, or both. Justify your choice:

  1. A customer support chatbot that needs to know your product's documentation and always respond in a friendly, specific brand voice.
  2. A code assistant that should follow your team's coding conventions (naming, architecture patterns) when generating code.
  3. A medical note generator that needs to use correct terminology and follow a specific template, using the patient's chart data.
  4. An app that classifies user feedback into categories (bug, feature request, complaint, praise).
  5. A search feature that finds relevant articles from your company's blog when users ask questions.

Answers: (1) Both — RAG for docs, fine-tune for voice. (2) Fine-tuning — it's a behavior/style issue. (3) Both — fine-tune for format/terminology, RAG for patient data. (4) Fine-tuning — classification is a behavior task. (5) RAG only — it's a knowledge access problem.
