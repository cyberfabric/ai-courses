Chapter 14

Debugging Background-Work Restrictions on Modern Android (15 / 16)

Your background sync just stopped. Nothing crashed. The OS just decided.

The Bug You Can’t Reproduce on Your Dev Machine

You ship a release. The crash dashboard is clean. But three weeks in, support tickets pile up: “My nightly sync stopped working.” “Notifications stopped appearing after I rebooted.” “The app just doesn’t download in the background anymore.”

You can’t reproduce any of it. Your test device works fine. Your QA emulator works fine. Then you notice: every reporter is on Android 15 or 16. And they all updated to your latest release that targets Android 15+.

Welcome to the world of silent background-work restrictions — the bug class where the OS quietly refuses to run your code, sometimes throws an exception you have to look hard to find, and other times just… doesn’t fire your job. No crash. No log line on a happy-path device. Just a feature that’s broken for a slice of your users.

Chapter 13 was about a crash you can see in logcat the moment it happens. This chapter is about restrictions that are usually invisible until you know what to grep for. The same diagnostic loop applies — signature → AI diagnosis → root cause → reusable skill — but you have to know where the signatures live.

The Restriction Landscape: Android 12 → 16

To debug modern background-work bugs you have to hold the timeline in your head. Each version added a new restriction, and the restrictions stack — if you target Android 16, you inherit every restriction from 12 onward.

Version What changed The error you’ll see
Android 12 Apps in background can no longer start foreground services (with limited exceptions) ForegroundServiceStartNotAllowedException
Android 13 POST_NOTIFICATIONS runtime permission required; user can revoke notifications even from FGS Notification simply doesn’t appear; no exception
Android 14 Foreground services must declare a foregroundServiceType in the manifest; new shortService type added MissingForegroundServiceTypeException
Android 15 BOOT_COMPLETED restrictions on most FGS types; 6-hour cumulative timeout on dataSync & mediaProcessing; new mediaProcessing type ForegroundServiceStartNotAllowedException from BOOT_COMPLETED; RemoteServiceException on timeout
Android 16 JobScheduler quotas now enforced even when concurrent with a foreground service or in the active standby bucket Jobs silently delayed / skipped; setImportantWhileForeground() ignored

If “my background sync stopped” reports start trickling in after a release, the first question is: did this release bump targetSdk? If yes, the next question is: which version’s restrictions just turned on for me? Almost every modern background bug starts with that two-question funnel.

Restriction #1: BOOT_COMPLETED Cannot Start Most FGS Types

This is the single most common silent regression in 2026. The pattern was solid for years: an app registers a BOOT_COMPLETED receiver, and after every reboot it kicks off a foreground service to resume sync, replay queued notifications, or restore state. On Android 15+, that pattern explodes.

The BOOT_COMPLETED Crash Signature

2026-04-22 09:14:08.221  4521  4521 E AndroidRuntime: FATAL EXCEPTION: main
2026-04-22 09:14:08.221  4521  4521 E AndroidRuntime: Process: com.example.shop, PID: 4521
2026-04-22 09:14:08.221  4521  4521 E AndroidRuntime: android.app.ForegroundServiceStartNotAllowedException:
2026-04-22 09:14:08.221  4521  4521 E AndroidRuntime:     Service.startForeground() not allowed due to mAllowStartForeground false:
2026-04-22 09:14:08.221  4521  4521 E AndroidRuntime:     service com.example.shop/.SyncService
2026-04-22 09:14:08.221  4521  4521 E AndroidRuntime:     BG-FGS-start not allowed: BOOT_COMPLETED restricted for type dataSync
2026-04-22 09:14:08.221  4521  4521 E AndroidRuntime:     at android.app.Service.startForeground(Service.java:803)
2026-04-22 09:14:08.221  4521  4521 E AndroidRuntime:     at com.example.shop.sync.SyncService.onCreate(SyncService.kt:42)
2026-04-22 09:14:08.221  4521  4521 E AndroidRuntime:     at com.example.shop.boot.BootReceiver.onReceive(BootReceiver.kt:18)

What Claude Code Finds

(1) The exception name — ForegroundServiceStartNotAllowedException — pinpoints the class instantly. (2) The message includes the magic phrase “BG-FGS-start not allowed: BOOT_COMPLETED restricted for type dataSync” — this is the OS telling you exactly which restriction fired. (3) The stack trace shows BootReceiver.onReceive → SyncService.onCreate → the failed startForeground. (4) Restricted types from BOOT_COMPLETED on Android 15+: dataSync, camera, mediaPlayback, mediaProjection, phoneCall, and microphone (which was already restricted from Android 14). (5) Allowed types from BOOT_COMPLETED: location (GPS-related), connectedDevice, health, remoteMessaging, specialUse, and systemExempted. (developer.android.com/about/versions/15/behavior-changes-15, 2026-04-26)

How to Test It Without Bumping Your targetSdk

You don’t want to wait until users hit it. Force the restriction on for your build with the app-compat framework:

# Force the BOOT_COMPLETED restriction on for your package on any Android 15+ device:
adb shell am compat enable FGS_BOOT_COMPLETED_RESTRICTIONS com.example.shop

# Reboot the device:
adb reboot

# Watch for the crash:
adb logcat -s AndroidRuntime ActivityManager *:E

# When you’re done testing:
adb shell am compat disable FGS_BOOT_COMPLETED_RESTRICTIONS com.example.shop

Root Cause Pattern: BootReceiver That Starts a Sync FGS

Almost every legacy Android codebase has one: a BroadcastReceiver that listens for android.intent.action.BOOT_COMPLETED and calls ContextCompat.startForegroundService(...) to launch a sync, queue replay, or session restoration job. On Android 15+ devices the second your app rebuilds with targetSdk = 35 or higher, that pattern crashes the moment the user reboots their phone. The fix is rarely “keep the FGS” — the right move is to migrate the post-boot work to WorkManager.

Fix: Replace BootReceiver-Triggered FGS with WorkManager

// BEFORE: crashes on Android 15+ at first reboot
class BootReceiver : BroadcastReceiver() {
    override fun onReceive(context: Context, intent: Intent) {
        if (intent.action == Intent.ACTION_BOOT_COMPLETED) {
            ContextCompat.startForegroundService(
                context, Intent(context, SyncService::class.java)
            )
        }
    }
}

// AFTER: enqueue a one-time WorkManager job after boot
class BootReceiver : BroadcastReceiver() {
    override fun onReceive(context: Context, intent: Intent) {
        if (intent.action == Intent.ACTION_BOOT_COMPLETED) {
            val request = OneTimeWorkRequestBuilder<SyncWorker>()
                .setConstraints(
                    Constraints.Builder()
                        .setRequiredNetworkType(NetworkType.CONNECTED)
                        .build()
                )
                .build()
            WorkManager.getInstance(context).enqueueUniqueWork(
                "post-boot-sync",
                ExistingWorkPolicy.KEEP,
                request
            )
        }
    }
}

class SyncWorker(ctx: Context, params: WorkerParameters) : CoroutineWorker(ctx, params) {
    override suspend fun doWork(): Result = withContext(Dispatchers.IO) {
        // ... your sync logic ...
        Result.success()
    }
}

Why this is the right move: WorkManager handles network constraints, retries, and battery state for free; it doesn’t need to be a foreground service for short post-boot work; and it side-steps every BOOT_COMPLETED restriction the OS adds going forward. (developer.android.com/develop/background-work/services/fgs/restrictions-bg-start, 2026-04-26)

Restriction #2: The 6-Hour dataSync / mediaProcessing Timeout

Android 15 added a cumulative ceiling: any dataSync or mediaProcessing foreground service can run for at most 6 hours in a rolling 24-hour window. The two pools are tracked separately, but each is shared across all of your app’s services of that type. Burn six hours of dataSync across two services, and the next startForeground with that type throws ForegroundServiceStartNotAllowedException with the message “Time limit already exhausted for foreground service type dataSync.” (developer.android.com/develop/background-work/services/fgs/timeout, 2026-04-26)

The timer resets when the user brings your app to the foreground. Until then, you have to live within the budget — or, more importantly, shut down gracefully when the OS calls onTimeout().

The onTimeout Callback You Must Implement

class BulkSyncService : Service() {

    override fun onTimeout(startId: Int, fgsType: Int) {
        // The OS just told us our 6-hour budget is up.
        // We have only a few seconds to call stopSelf() before the
        // system kills us with a RemoteServiceException.
        Log.w("BulkSync", "FGS timeout fired (type=$fgsType). Persisting checkpoint.")
        persistCheckpoint()
        stopSelf(startId)
    }

    private fun persistCheckpoint() {
        // Save where we got to, so the next sync can resume.
    }
}
If you don’t implement onTimeout(), or if you take longer than a few seconds to call stopSelf(), the system kills you with: android.app.RemoteServiceException: "A foreground service of type dataSync did not stop within its timeout: ...". This crashes your process. Implementing the callback is not optional in Android 15+.

Forcing the Timeout in Test

# Enable the time-limit behavior even if your app doesn’t target Android 15:
adb shell am compat enable FGS_INTRODUCE_TIME_LIMITS com.example.shop

# Shorten the dataSync window to 2 minutes for local testing:
adb shell device_config put activity_manager data_sync_fgs_timeout_duration 120000

# Same for mediaProcessing:
adb shell device_config put activity_manager media_processing_fgs_timeout_duration 120000

# Reset back to the 6-hour default when you’re done:
adb shell device_config delete activity_manager data_sync_fgs_timeout_duration

Without this trick you’d have to wait six hours of real wall-clock time to see your onTimeout handler fire. With it, you can write a deterministic instrumentation test that flushes a long-running sync and verifies you handle the callback.

Restriction #3: Android 16 Job Quotas Concurrent with FGS

Pre-Android-16, JobScheduler quotas were paused while a foreground service was running or while the app was in the active standby bucket. That gave you an unspoken loophole: keep an FGS alive and you could run unlimited jobs concurrently with it. Android 16 closes that loophole. Jobs now adhere to their runtime quota even when concurrent with an FGS, and even in the active bucket. (developer.android.com/about/versions/16/behavior-changes-all, 2026-04-26)

The symptom: a job that always ran to completion before now stops mid-execution and gets re-scheduled on Android 16. Worse, JobInfo.Builder#setImportantWhileForeground(boolean) — which used to extend a job’s runtime when the app had an FGS — is silently ignored in Android 16. Calling it doesn’t throw; it just doesn’t do anything anymore.

The New Debugging APIs

Android 16 also gave us two new JobScheduler APIs that finally tell you why a job didn’t run:

val scheduler = getSystemService(JobScheduler::class.java)

// Why is my job pending right now?
val reasons = scheduler.getPendingJobReasons(jobId)
// Returns an int array of PENDING_JOB_REASON_* constants.

// What's the recent history of pending reasons?
val history = scheduler.getPendingJobReasonsHistory(jobId)
// Returns the most recent constraint changes — great for "why was it
// pending five minutes ago?" investigations.

Wire these into your in-app debug menu and you can stop guessing whether it’s the network constraint, the battery state, or the quota that’s holding the job back.

The “My Background Sync Just Stopped” Diagnostic Workflow

This is the workflow you reach for when a user reports the silent failure mode: nothing crashed, but my sync isn’t running.

📱 Reproduce on Android 15/16
→
📋 dumpsys jobscheduler
→
🔋 Battery Historian
→
🔍 Filter by your package
→
🤖 /diagnose-background-restriction

Step 1: dumpsys jobscheduler

# Capture the full JobScheduler state for your app:
adb shell dumpsys jobscheduler > jobscheduler.txt

# Or filter immediately:
adb shell dumpsys jobscheduler | grep -A 30 com.example.shop

Look for sections labeled Pending jobs:, Active jobs:, and most importantly the last execution result per job — reasons like STOP_REASON_QUOTA, STOP_REASON_TIMEOUT, STOP_REASON_DEVICE_STATE, or STOP_REASON_USER_UI_STOPPED tell you exactly which policy fired.

Step 2: Battery Historian

You’ve already used Battery Historian in Chapter 8 for power debugging. Same tool, different filter: capture a bug report after a 24-hour window where the user reports the issue, and look at the JobScheduler and Foreground Services swimlanes. You’ll see exactly when each FGS started, how long it ran, when it was killed, and why.

Step 3: Standby Bucket Sanity Check

# What bucket is the app actually in?
adb shell am get-standby-bucket com.example.shop
# active | working_set | frequent | rare | restricted

# Force it back to active for testing:
adb shell am set-standby-bucket com.example.shop active
A surprising fraction of “background sync stopped” reports turn out to be the device parking the app in rare or restricted. The app isn’t broken — the OS just decided you’re not popular. The fix is usually about user habit (open the app daily) rather than code, but verifying the bucket as the very first diagnostic step saves a lot of pointless code archaeology.

The Predictive Back-Gesture Side Quest

Worth a quick mention because it’s in the same “modern OS quirks that bite background flows” bucket. Android 14 introduced predictive back gestures behind a manifest opt-in (android:enableOnBackInvokedCallback="true"); Android 15 fixed the long-standing Pixel-only back-gesture quirk where opting in caused some OEMs to swallow the gesture entirely.

Why it matters here: many BroadcastReceivers and Service-driven UI flows assumed the legacy onBackPressed() path. If your foreground-service notification opens an Activity that uses the legacy back path on a Pixel that then loses the gesture, users report it as “the sync notification doesn’t close.” The fix is to migrate to OnBackInvokedDispatcher — treat that migration as part of your modern-OS readiness audit, not a separate project.

Building the /diagnose-background-restriction Skill

Create .claude/commands/diagnose-background-restriction.md:

# /diagnose-background-restriction — Background Restriction Triage

Take a logcat excerpt + dumpsys output and identify which Android
background-work restriction fired.

## Input
- A logcat excerpt (must include any exception/error lines).
- The output of `adb shell dumpsys jobscheduler <package>`.
- Optional: the user’s `targetSdkVersion` from build.gradle.
- Optional: the device’s Android version.

## Detection Heuristics
Match against these signatures, in priority order:

1. **BOOT_COMPLETED FGS restriction** — logcat contains
   `ForegroundServiceStartNotAllowedException` AND
   `BG-FGS-start not allowed: BOOT_COMPLETED restricted for type <type>`.
2. **6-hour FGS timeout** — logcat contains
   `RemoteServiceException` AND
   `did not stop within its timeout` OR
   `Time limit already exhausted for foreground service type`.
3. **Missing FGS type (Android 14+)** — logcat contains
   `MissingForegroundServiceTypeException`.
4. **Generic background-FGS-start (Android 12+)** — logcat contains
   `ForegroundServiceStartNotAllowedException` without a BOOT_COMPLETED
   marker.
5. **Job-quota silent skip (Android 16+)** — no exception in logcat,
   but dumpsys shows `STOP_REASON_QUOTA` or
   `STOP_REASON_DEVICE_STATE` for the user’s job.
6. **Standby-bucket throttling** — dumpsys shows the app in
   `RARE` or `RESTRICTED` standby bucket.

## Analysis Steps
1. Identify which restriction fired (use the highest-priority match).
2. Cite the Android version that introduced it.
3. Name the offending component (Service, Receiver, Job ID).
4. Suggest the fix in priority order:
   a) For BOOT_COMPLETED FGS: migrate to WorkManager.
   b) For 6-hour timeout: implement onTimeout(); checkpoint state.
   c) For missing FGS type: add foregroundServiceType to manifest.
   d) For Android 16 job quotas: drop setImportantWhileForeground;
      use getPendingJobReasonsHistory to see why the job is pending.
   e) For standby bucket: surface to user; review usage patterns.
5. Provide the exact `adb` command to reproduce locally.

## Output Format
- **Restriction class:** <name> (introduced in Android <version>)
- **Offending component:** <Service/Receiver/Job>
- **What the OS told you:** <quoted log line or dumpsys field>
- **Recommended fix:** <1–3 sentence remediation>
- **Reproduce locally:** <adb command>
- **Verify fix:** <dumpsys field or logcat absence to check>

This is your one-stop skill for the entire modern-restriction taxonomy. When a teammate pastes a confusing dumpsys, you don’t play guess-the-restriction — the skill tells you which one fired, why, and the migration path.

The Migration Playbook: Drop FGS or Keep It?

Half the work in this space is deciding whether a piece of background work even needs to be a foreground service in 2026. The decision matrix:

Use case Right tool Why
Long-running music playback mediaPlayback FGS User-initiated, user expects an ongoing notification, no time limit applies.
Live navigation with GPS location FGS User actively driving; OS treats GPS-driven location as exempt.
Active phone call (VoIP) phoneCall FGS Required for the OS’s call-state integration; not subject to the 6-hour limit.
Resuming sync after reboot WorkManager BOOT_COMPLETED can’t legally start most FGS types on Android 15+; WorkManager is the correct API.
Periodic backup / cloud upload WorkManager with constraints You’ll bust the 6-hour dataSync ceiling on a slow connection; WorkManager handles retry & constraints natively.
One-shot “process this video” up to 6 hours mediaProcessing FGS, with onTimeout() The right type for video transcoding; just budget for the timeout.
Quick on-demand task < 3 minutes triggered by user shortService FGS Newer FGS type with stricter limits but lighter manifest requirements.

Before / After: A Real Migration Score Card

Before: Legacy BootReceiver + Long-Running Sync FGS

Crashes after first reboot on Android 15: ~3% of daily active users (one per session)

Silent timeouts on overnight sync: ~8% of users hit the 6-hour ceiling

Top complaint: “App stopped syncing after I rebooted my phone”

Diagnostic burden: 1–2 days per support ticket; no signature in Crashlytics for the silent case

Code: BOOT_COMPLETED → startForegroundService(SyncService.class)

After: WorkManager + Checkpointed mediaProcessing FGS

Crashes after first reboot on Android 15: 0% (BOOT_COMPLETED no longer starts an FGS)

Silent timeouts: 0% (sync resumes from checkpoint via OneTimeWorkRequest)

How This Connects to the Rest of the Course

Modern OS restrictions are a debugging surface, not just an API change. The signatures here plug into skills you already have:

  • Use /analyze-crash (Ch 2) on the BOOT_COMPLETED FGS exception — the “BG-FGS-start not allowed” message is one of the most pattern-matchable Android errors that exists.
  • Use /analyze-anr (Ch 3) when an FGS that didn’t stop in time turns into a watchdog kill.
  • Use /analyze-battery (Ch 8) on the same Battery Historian capture you took to debug the silent sync — the wake-lock metric introduced in Ch 8 and the FGS timeouts here are deeply linked.
  • Use /triage-crash (Ch 11) on Crashlytics clusters of ForegroundServiceStartNotAllowedException — they’re almost always the BOOT_COMPLETED pattern and are usually fixable in a single PR.
  • Add /diagnose-background-restriction (this chapter) to your team’s .claude/commands/ so the on-call rotation can triage a confusing dumpsys without paging the senior Android dev.

Exercise: Simulate, Diagnose, Fix

  1. Wire up a deliberately-broken pattern. In a side project, register a BOOT_COMPLETED receiver that calls ContextCompat.startForegroundService on a dataSync-typed Service. Set targetSdk = 35.
  2. Force the restriction on a real Android 15+ device (or 16 emulator) without bumping the user’s manifest: 
    adb shell am compat enable FGS_BOOT_COMPLETED_RESTRICTIONS <your-package>
adb reboot
  3. Capture the crash: adb logcat -d -t 500 *:E > restriction.log.
  4. Capture the system state: adb shell dumpsys jobscheduler > jobscheduler.txt.
  5. Run your /diagnose-background-restriction skill on the two captures. Verify it correctly identifies the BOOT_COMPLETED restriction and recommends a WorkManager migration.
  6. Apply the fix from this chapter: replace the FGS launch with a OneTimeWorkRequest enqueued through WorkManager. Reboot. Confirm the sync still happens via WorkManager.getInstance(context).getWorkInfosByTagLiveData(...) or the in-app debug menu.
  7. Bonus: repeat the loop for the 6-hour timeout. Use: 
    adb shell am compat enable FGS_INTRODUCE_TIME_LIMITS <your-package>
adb shell device_config put activity_manager data_sync_fgs_timeout_duration 60000
    Run a long-running sync, watch onTimeout() fire after 60 s, verify your checkpoint persists, and confirm a follow-on OneTimeWorkRequest resumes from where you stopped.

By the end of this exercise you have hands-on familiarity with the two restriction patterns that produce ~80% of the modern-Android background bugs, and you’ve verified your /diagnose-background-restriction skill against real captured signals.
